## ARTICLE 2 — The truth about A-List seed in New Zealand
**URL:** /lawn-tips/the-truth-about-a-list-seed-in-new-zealand/
**Date:** May 2026

**Key points:**
- A-List is NZ's premium certified seed programme run by NZLA and other seed breeders
- Only 65% of a blend needs to be A-List varieties — 35% can be certified non-A-List
- The 35% non-A-List component can change batch to batch without notice
- This is a problem for lawns wanting consistency of appearance and performance
- NZLA's position: they don't lead with A-List branding because the system doesn't guarantee full-blend consistency
- NZLA maintains batch records that can be cross-referenced with official certification documentation
- Customers who want consistency should ask specifically about batch composition

## ARTICLE 3 — The Right Way to Apply Lawn Chemicals: mL per 100m² Explained
**URL:** /lawn-tips/the-right-way-to-apply-lawn-chemicals-ml-per-100m2-explained/
**Date:** March 2026

**Key points:**
- The correct system is mL per 100m² — NOT mL per litre
- Dosing by mL per litre causes overdosing because water volume changes but product rate doesn't
- Water is just the carrier — it dilutes the product for even application but doesn't change the active ingredient dose
- How to calculate: measure your lawn area → use the label rate (e.g. Grub+ at 15ml/100m²) → multiply by your area → divide by water volume needed for coverage
- Worked example: 15ml/100m² over 350m² = 52.5ml product. Mix into enough water to cover the area evenly (e.g. 16L) = 52.5ml in 16L
- NZLA App has an application helper tool that does this calculation automatically
- Key rule: always calculate mL per 100m² first, then work out water volume needed for that area

## ARTICLE 4 — Understanding Growing Degree Days (GDD's)
**URL:** /lawn-tips/understanding-growing-degree-days/
**Date:** February 2026

**Key points:**
- GDD formula: (Max temp + Min temp) ÷ 2 − Base temp = daily GDD
- GDD accumulates over time to track biological development
- Base temperatures: 0°C (US standard), 6°C (NZ cool season grasses), 10°C (NZ warm season grasses)
- 200 GDD between PGR applications (using base 6°C for cool season)
- NZLA App has a GDD tracker — set a custom start date per region
- GDD accumulates continuously — not reset when a threshold is hit
- Useful for timing PGR reapplication, pre-emergent herbicide windows, and disease pressure monitoring
- Each region in NZ accumulates GDD at different rates — a Northland lawn hits 200 GDD much faster than a Southland lawn in spring

## ARTICLE 5 — Lawn Care 101: Getting the Basics Right
**URL:** /lawn-tips/lawn-care-101-getting-the-basics-right/
**Date:** November 2025

**Key points:**
- Three non-negotiables: mowing, irrigation, slow-release fertiliser
- Mowing: follow the 1/3 rule (never remove more than 1/3 of leaf blade), keep blades sharp, mow frequently enough that you're never removing too much at once
- Irrigation: deep and infrequent (2-3 times per week, not daily), early morning is best, never irrigate at night (fungal risk)
- Fertiliser: slow-release only — avoid agricultural and fast-release products. Consistent feeding = consistent colour
- If lawn is more than 50% weeds, renovate before spending money on maintenance products
- PGR is only for lawns that have the basics right — don't apply PGR to a lawn that isn't being mowed and fed properly

## ARTICLE 6 — Grass Grub Treatment Timing in New Zealand: When to Apply Grub+
**URL:** /lawn-tips/grass-grub-treatment-timing-in-new-zealand-when-to-apply-grub/
**Date:** November 2025

**Key points:**
- Adult grass grubs emerge in November and lay eggs in the soil
- Eggs hatch approximately 2 weeks after adults emerge
- Larvae feed from November through to March
- Best timing: late September or October (before adults emerge, while larvae from previous season are still present and active)
- Summer applications (December, January, February) still effective — larvae are actively feeding
- Grub+ active ingredient lasts 6 months in the soil
- Grub+ is the only NZ product that doesn't require repeat applications within a season
- Apply at 15ml/100m², water in after application
- If you see birds pecking at the lawn, turf pulling up like carpet, or brown patches — grass grubs are likely already present

## ARTICLE 7 — Should You Apply Slow Release Fertiliser in the Rain?
**URL:** /lawn-tips/should-you-apply-slow-release-fertiliser-in-the-rain/
**Date:** October 2025

**Key points:**
- Damp foliage causes fertiliser burn — salt in granules releases onto wet leaf surface and scorches
- Light rain is not enough — need consistent soaking rain (6-10mm minimum) to wash granules off the leaf and into the soil
- Heavy rain increases leaching risk, especially on sandy soils
- Wet fertiliser in a spreader can clog and cause uneven application, and damages the spreader
- One exception: liquid wetting agents can be applied during or just before heavy rain — this is actually ideal timing for wetting agents
- Best practice: apply fertiliser when foliage is dry and rain is forecast 12-24 hours later, or irrigate after applying

## ARTICLE 8 — Wetting Agent for Lawns in New Zealand: Liquid Vs Granular
**URL:** /lawn-tips/wetting-agent-for-lawns-liquid-vs-granular/
**Date:** October 2025

**Key points:**
- Liquid wetting agents are superior to granular for NZ lawns
- Granular wetting agents use zeolite as a carrier — must be watered off before actives can work, causing delay and uneven coverage
- Liquids coat soil evenly and start working immediately after application
- Quality check: good liquid wetting agents are thick, not watery — thin products lack concentration
- Three surfactant types: Penetrant (breaks surface tension, gets water into dry/compacted soil), Retainer (slows drainage, holds moisture in root zone), Spreader (even distribution across and through soil)
- Three-way (3W) products contain all three types — best all-round result
- NZLA Wetter 3W: three-way product — fastest and longest-lasting results
- NZLA Penetrate: pure penetrant — ideal for very dry, compacted, or hydrophobic soil
- NZLA Wetter: pure retainer — ideal for sandy soils and coastal lawns
- Application: 08 nozzle produces coarser droplet for better soil contact
- Apply monthly from early September through summer
- Must be watered in: 6-10mm irrigation after application to move product into soil profile
- If no irrigation: apply just before steady rainfall — don't leave sitting on foliage (can cause staining on decks/concrete if tracked inside)
- Best time to start: early September — soil moisture still adequate from winter rain, helps product move deeper before dry months

## ARTICLE 9 — Lawn Herbicides in New Zealand: Choosing the Right Product for Your Grass Type
**URL:** /lawn-tips/lawn-herbicides-in-new-zealand-choosing-the-right-product-for-your-grass-type/
**Date:** October 2025

**Key points:**
- NZLA recommends single-species lawns over blends for precise herbicide control
- Mixed species lawns limit which herbicides can be used safely

### Perennial Ryegrass
- Meso (mesosulfuron): safe at seeding time as pre-emergent; prevents up to 46 broadleaf weeds; reapply only after 4-6 weeks AND 2 mows post-germination; can cause temporary bleaching; also works as post-emergent broadleaf control; works as pre-emergent barrier when watered in
- Etho (ethofumesate): safe at seeding; targets Poa annua and some broadleaf weeds; can tank-mix with Meso at seeding; apply earlier after germination than Meso; two-stage Poa control: water in first application (kills germinating seed) then follow-up application on leaf surface 14 days later (kills remaining plants); can act as pre-emergent when watered in every few months; overuse risk: resistance
- NZLA Gold: best broadleaf herbicide for home lawns — widest range of species, safe on established rye
- NZLA BWC: professional-grade, restricted to PCBU/commercial use from March 2025; online purchase requires verification at checkout

### Tall Fescue
- Same compatibility as ryegrass
- Meso and Etho safe at seeding as pre-emergents, and post-emergent after 2 mows / 4-6 weeks
- Etho can punch slightly harder on fescue if lawn is under stress — apply when turf is actively growing and soil is moist
- NZLA Gold: safe and consistent for ongoing broadleaf control
- NZLA BWC: professional-grade option for larger/commercial properties

### Fine Fescue
- DO NOT use Meso at seeding — reduces seedling density, causes thin/patchy establishment
- Meso safe as post-emergent after 2 mows / 4-6 weeks post-germination
- No safe pre-emergent at seeding time for pure fine fescue
- NZLA Gold: safe on established fine fescue (>6 months old); some mild bleaching possible but fades
- NZLA BWC: commercial use option for established fine fescue
- NZLA Halo: fine fescue can tolerate this (selective for foreign grass control); minimum 6 months old before applying; stand-down period of 6-8 weeks — cannot seed after application during this window

### Kikuyu
- MSF 600: for certain broadleaf and grassy weeds; safe on well-established Kikuyu at correct rates
- NZLA BWC: can use alongside MSF 600 for heavy weed pressure; PCBU-only

### Couch
- MSF 600: check label specifically lists couch as safe — some formulations are not suitable for couch
- NZLA BWC: effective for broadleaf and grassy weeds on established couch; PCBU-only

## ARTICLE 10 — Granular Lawn Fertiliser in New Zealand: Why Not All Fertilisers Are Equal
**URL:** /lawn-tips/lawn-fertiliser-nz/
**Date:** October 2025

**Key points:**
- Most NZ fertiliser market is agricultural-grade products rebagged for lawn use, or low-quality coatings
- PCSCU (Polymer-Coated Sulphur-Coated Urea): the gold standard — dual coating controls hydraulic release; each granule coated in sulphur then sealed with polymer; consistent release regardless of microbial activity; performance consistent across different NZ climates
- NZLA sources PCSCU from the USA — not Chinese-manufactured prills
- Low-quality Chinese PCSCU: coating is inconsistent and brittle — when it cracks, slow-release ends instantly
- Nitrogen inhibitors: chemical additive sprayed onto standard urea to slow release slightly — NOT the same as polymer coating; only extends release by days or weeks, not months; some brands misleadingly market inhibitor-treated urea as "slow release"
- Methylene urea (MU): common in NZ including from professional turf suppliers; relies on soil microbial activity to release nitrogen; only works well when soil is warm (microbes active); fails in cooler regions or winter; misleading "3-4 month" release claims are under ideal conditions only
- Key signals of cheap fertiliser: short-lived results, surge of growth then crash, uneven colour, top growth with no root development, increased disease pressure, burn
- Signs of quality slow-release: steady colour over months, even growth, deeper roots, fewer fungal issues
- NZLA's granular range uses the same PCSCU technology used on NZ golf courses, sports fields, and professional turf

## ARTICLE 13 — PGR – The Lawn Hack Professionals Swear By
**URL:** /lawn-tips/pgr-the-lawn-hack-professionals-swear-by/
**Date:** August 2025

**Key points:**
- Active ingredient: trinexapac-ethyl (same as Primo Maxx in pro turf)
- Primary purpose: deeper roots and turf resilience — mowing reduction is a side effect
- Slows upward canopy growth; redirects energy to roots and lateral spread
- Cool season (ryegrass, tall fescue, fine fescue): start August-September, reapply every 4-6 weeks through to March-April
- Warm season (Kikuyu, couch): start late spring, continue through summer when actively growing
- Application rate: 15-30ml/100m² in 4-5L water
- Apply to dry lawn, morning or evening preferred
- Leave at least 6 hours either side of mowing
- Effect lasts 4-6 weeks depending on weather and growth conditions
- Slight yellowing after first application is normal — tank-mix with Iron+ or apply separately to offset
- Can tank-mix with liquid fertilisers or fungicides (test small patch first)
- Compatible grasses: Ryegrass, Tall Fescue, Fine Fescue, Kikuyu, Couch
- Tip: apply before going away on holiday — return to a lawn that hasn't surged
- On Kikuyu and couch: also slows creeping stolons that invade garden beds and driveways
- Does not stop growth — slows it. Lawn still grows, just at a more manageable pace

## ARTICLE 14 — How Blockade Works: Stopping Weeds Before They Start
**URL:** /lawn-tips/how-blockade-works-stopping-weeds-before-they-start/
**Date:** August 2025

**Key points:**
- Active ingredient: 480g/L Prodiamine (same active as Syngenta's Barricade)
- Mode of action: inhibits cell division in root tips of germinating weed seeds — stops roots and shoots from developing; works in soil before weeds break surface
- Does NOT kill existing weeds — strictly pre-emergent; once a weed is visible, Blockade won't touch it
- "Stops germination" is a simplification — it stops seedlings from establishing, not the seed from waking up
- Protection duration: up to 6 months
- Timing: apply before weeds germinate — early to mid-spring as soil temps rise, or autumn before winter annuals emerge
- Watering in: critical — 6-10mm irrigation within 7 days; irrigation preferred over rainfall (more consistent)

**Application rates:**
- Established turf: 30ml/100m² (upper end of label range)
- Garden beds and ornamental areas: up to 80ml/100m²

**Where it can be used:**
- NZ label: registered for Kikuyu and Couch (warm-season grasses)
- Cool-season grasses (Ryegrass, Fine Fescue): not on NZ label; registered overseas (USA); off-label use in NZ is at user's discretion
- Garden beds: safe around ornamentals; pull mulch back before applying so product binds to soil, not mulch; replace mulch after watering in
- Garden paths/driveways: can tank-mix with glyphosate — glyphosate kills existing weeds (knock-down), Blockade creates residual barrier; leave 24 hours undisturbed for glyphosate to translocate before irrigating Blockade in
- Lawns with new seed: DO NOT use Blockade before or during seeding; will prevent grass seed germination same as weed seeds; wait until lawn is fully established (mowed at least 3 times, strong even cover)
- Pets: keep off treated area until spray is dry and product is watered in; once area dry, safe for pets

## ARTICLE 15 — Sowing Seed, Soil Temps & Irrigation
**URL:** /lawn-tips/sowing-seed-soil-temps-irrigation/
**Date:** April 2025

**Key points:**
- Soil temperature matters more than air temperature
- Measure soil temp at 10cm depth (turf industry standard)
- NZLA App shows soil temperature data

**Ideal soil temps and germination times (at 10cm depth):**
- Fine Fescue: 12-18°C, 7-21 days
- Perennial Ryegrass: 8-25°C, 5-14 days
- Tall Fescue: 12-25°C, 7-21 days
- Couch: 18-30°C, 10-21 days
- Kikuyu: 20-30°C, 10-28 days

**Watering during germination:**
- 3-4 times daily, light bursts to keep top 2-3cm damp (not soaked)
- Once seedlings up: reduce to once daily, then every 2-3 days; increase depth to push roots down

**Hot/windy weather:**
- Soil can dry out in under an hour
- Run irrigation every 45-60 minutes during peak heat; short 2-3 minute bursts
- The wet towel trick: hang wet towel on line when irrigation starts; if it dries before next cycle, intervals are too long

**Seasonal guidelines:**
- Early Spring: 3-4x daily, watch morning soil temps
- Late Spring: 2-3x daily then taper, soil holds warmth
- Summer: hourly if hot/windy; best for Couch and Kikuyu
- Autumn: 3-4x daily; ideal for Ryegrass and Fescues
- Winter: rarely needed; too cold for most species to germinate

## ARTICLE 16 — NZLA Product Safety
**URL:** /lawn-tips/nzla-product-safety/
**Date:** April 2025

**Key points:**
- Foliar fertilisers: not classified as dangerous goods; non-toxic to humans, pets, animals; keep off lawn until dry (not for safety, but to prevent tracking residue onto hard surfaces and dilution of product)
- Herbicides, fungicides, insecticides: stronger, EPA-regulated; follow label exactly (legal requirement); general rule: keep kids and pets off for 24 hours after application unless label says otherwise
- PPE: varies by product — read the label; don't assume it's the same across products
- SDS (Safety Data Sheets): available for every NZLA product — contact via FAQ page
- Section 11 of SDS: covers toxicity data — useful for pet owners
- GHS labelling: NZ has shifted from HSNO to Globally Harmonised System; look for pictograms (exclamation = irritant, health hazard symbol = serious effects)
- Granular fertilisers: not toxic; pets may sniff or eat them; keep pets off for a few hours after application; if dog tends to eat things off the lawn, consider foliar instead
- NZLA Azoxy: 250g/L azoxystrobin; look up active ingredient for detailed studies and real-world use cases

## ARTICLE 18 — Which NZLA Grass Seed is Best for Your Lawn?
**URL:** /lawn-tips/nzla-lawn-seed-an-overview/
**Date:** January 2025

**Key points:**

**NZLA Perennial Ryegrass:**
- High foot traffic tolerance; quick recovery
- Moderate-high water requirement
- Optimal growth temp: 10-20°C; tolerates down to -5°C; struggles above 30°C without water
- Best in full sun; tolerates light shade
- Four varieties: Standard Rye (blends well with other species), Dark Rye, Midnight Dark Rye (deeper colour but foreign grasses more visible), RSR Rye (modest spreading ability via stolons, good for high-traffic sports areas)

**NZLA Tall Fescue:**
- High foot traffic tolerance
- Moderate water requirement; deep roots = drought tolerant
- Optimal temp: 18-24°C; tolerates heat to 32°C if watered; cold to -10°C
- Tolerates partial shade (minimum 4 hours sun daily)
- Two varieties: Standard Tall Fescue (clump-type, best looking, no spreading) and Elfin Tall Fescue/ETF (produces rhizomes, spreads to fill bare patches, can be mown shorter, slightly lower maintenance)
- Jon's opinion: standard tall fescue is the better-looking grass

**NZLA Fine Fescue:**
- Moderate foot traffic; not for high-use areas
- Low water requirement; drought-tolerant
- Optimal temp: 15-24°C; tolerates down to -5°C; brief heat to 28°C
- Best in shade; excellent under trees or near buildings
- Less fertiliser and mowing needed; struggles in hot or wet conditions

**NZLA Couch:**
- High foot traffic; good recovery
- Low-moderate water once established
- Optimal temp: 25-35°C; goes dormant below 10°C (may brown in winter); NOT recommended for South Island
- Full sun only — poor in shade
- Fine leaves, dense growth; requires regular dethatching

**NZLA Kikuyu:**
- Excellent foot traffic tolerance
- Moderate-high water
- Optimal temp: 20-30°C; goes dormant below 10°C; NOT recommended for South Island
- Full sun only — struggles significantly in shade
- Fast-growing; requires frequent mowing; aggressive stolons require edging

## ARTICLE 20 — Lawn Renovation Guide
**URL:** /lawn-tips/lawn-renovation-guide/
**Date:** January 2025

**8-step process:**

**Step 1 — Weed control:**
- If >50% weeds or undesirable grasses, kill entire lawn with glyphosate (non-selective)
- Seed safe within days of glyphosate; delay seeding until vegetation visibly dead
- May need second glyphosate application for Kikuyu, couch, paspalum
- Soil test 3-4 weeks before renovation (Hills Labs)

**Step 2 — Mow, thatch, cultivate:**
- Mow closely (~20mm), remove all clippings and debris
- Remove thatch only in cool weather with adequate moisture — not in heat or drought
- Core aerate to loosen soil, allow air/water/nutrients in, create partial seedbed

**Step 3 — Drag, top dress, drag:**
- Drag with heavy mat or chain link fence to break up cores
- Apply screened topsoil or sand to fill low spots
- Level and drag again to fill core holes

**Step 4 — Fertiliser:**
- NZLA Starter Fertiliser at 1.5kg/100m²
- High P and N for rapid establishment; additional P and K if soil test recommends

**Step 5 — Seeding:**
- Divide seed into two equal lots; sow at right angles to each other
- Sunny open areas: NZLA Perennial Ryegrass
- Partial shade/partial sun: NZLA Elfin Tall Fescue
- Heavy shade: 100% Fine Fescue

**Step 6 — Roll and water:**
- Lightly roll for seed-to-soil contact
- Keep moist until well established
- Pre-emergent for Rye and Tall Fescue: apply Meso and/or Etho on day of seeding (not after germination)
- DO NOT use pre-emergents with 100% Fine Fescue

**Step 7 — First mow:**
- When grass reaches 75mm; just take the tops off
- Light water after first mow to reduce shock

**Step 8 — Follow-up fertiliser:**
- 6 weeks after seeding: NZLA Starter or NZLA All Seasons
- Consider NZLA Wetter for soil moisture retention

**Ongoing care:**
- 1/3 rule for mowing height
- Irrigation: 2-3 deep waterings per week (20-30mm each); early morning
- Tuna can method: time how long to collect 1.5cm in a tuna tin; use that as irrigation run time

## ARTICLE 21 — Everything You Need To Know About Thatch
**URL:** /lawn-tips/everything-you-need-to-know-about-thatch/
**Date:** December 2024

**Key points:**
- Thatch: layer of organic material between soil and grass (dead and living stems, roots, shoots)
- Thin layer (<12-13mm): beneficial — insulates roots, retains moisture
- Excessive thatch: prevents water/air penetration, harbours disease and pests, impedes nutrients
- Causes of thatch buildup:
  - Higher mowing height = more top growth = more organic matter deposited
  - Fast-release fertilisers: surge of soft growth faster than decomposition can keep up; NZLA doesn't sell fast-release for this reason
  - Grass species: rhizome/stolon-producing species (Kentucky bluegrass, creeping red fescue, creeping bentgrass) form more thatch; ryegrass and tall fescue are bunch-type grasses — minimal thatch
  - Low soil pH (<5.5): inhibits decomposing microorganisms; clay/sandy soils also have fewer microbes
  - Compaction: restricts air and water movement, limits microbial activity
- Grass clippings: research shows leaving clippings does NOT significantly increase thatch (blades decompose easily; thatch forms from slower-decomposing roots and stems)
- Prevention: appropriate species selection, liming, core aeration, proper fertiliser use
- Physical removal: required when thatch layer reaches 25mm or more; vertical mower / dethatching machine; only in cool weather with adequate moisture
- NZLA products that promote microbial activity: Nurture and Restore (12-month shelf life; only buy for the coming season)
- Coring: improves microbial activity, improves soil structure, reduces thatch buildup over time

## ARTICLE 24 — Lawn Coring
**URL:** /lawn-tips/lawn-coring/
**Date:** August 2024

**Key points:**
- Best timing in NZ: August and September (early spring — grass actively growing and recovers quickly)
- Benefits: improved soil aeration, enhanced root growth, increased water absorption, better nutrient uptake
- Equipment: mechanical core aerator (hire or buy) or manual coring tool (NZLA sells a manual aerator)
- Process:
  1. Mow lawn shorter than usual first
  2. Water lawn thoroughly 1-2 days before (softens soil)
  3. Core entire lawn; aim for soil plugs 7.5-10cm apart
  4. Leave soil plugs on lawn to break down naturally (or rake if top-dressing with sand — use mower/leaf blower to remove plugs first)
  5. Fertilise after coring (NZLA All Seasons); overseed thin/bare patches
  6. Water regularly post-coring to support recovery
- If top-dressing with sand: remove cores before top-dressing; sand fills the holes for better drainage improvement

## ARTICLE 26 — Ready Lawn Aftercare
**URL:** /lawn-tips/ready-lawn-aftercare/
**Date:** May 2024

**Key points:**

**Before turf is laid:**
- Apply NZLA Starter Fertiliser to soil at 1.5kg/100m² before installation
- Turf must be laid day it is received for best results

**Ryegrass aftercare:**
- Saturate soil on install day; maintain consistent moisture for 14-21 days
- Morning watering; avoid ponding (water heats up and scorches)
- Mow frequently at highest setting during establishment (40-50mm initially; 35mm minimum)
- Once established: regular mowing height 30mm; use catcher
- 4-6 weeks post-installation: All Seasons granular + can start foliar range
- Broadleaf weed control: NZLA Gold (Triumph Gold)

**Tall Fescue aftercare:**
- Same establishment watering as ryegrass
- Initial mow: 7-10 days after install at 40-50mm; subsequent mows at 40mm minimum (some cultivars 50mm minimum — check with supplier)
- Once established: 40-50mm minimum mowing height
- 4-6 weeks: All Seasons + foliar range
- Weed control: NZLA Gold

**Fine Fescue aftercare:**
- Irrigate immediately on installation; set up sprinklers while laying
- Hot weather: also lightly water during 2pm-4pm for first 7-10 days to cool surface temp
- Evapotranspiration in summer: 4-6mm/day minimum water replacement
- Establishment mowing: 35mm; once established can reduce to 25-35mm
- 4-6 weeks: All Seasons + foliar range
- Weed control: NZLA Gold

**Kikuyu aftercare:**
- Saturate on install; consistent moisture 14-21 days; morning watering
- Initial mowing: 40-50mm; subsequent mows 35mm minimum
- Once established: 20-30mm mowing height
- 4-6 weeks: All Seasons + foliar range
- Weed control: Banvine or MSF600 every 6 months
- Spring scalp: closely mow in early spring then top dress All Seasons; promotes finer, leafier summer turf and eliminates need for verticutting

**Couch aftercare:**
- Saturate on install; consistent moisture 14-21 days
- Winter: warm-season — may discolour or go dormant; regains colour when warm conditions return
- Initial mowing: 35-40mm during establishment
- Once established: 25mm optimal; mow every 5-6 days at minimum 30mm
- 4-6 weeks: All Seasons + foliar range
- Weed control: Banvine (bi-annual applications; couch sensitive to certain herbicides — check label)

## ARTICLE 27 — Winter Foliar Feeding
**URL:** /lawn-tips/winter-foliar-feeding/
**Date:** May 2024

**Key points:**
- Winter foliar feeding purpose: supply essential nutrients when root uptake is limited by cold soil temperatures
- Roots have reduced ability to take up nutrients from soil in winter (lower temps, decreased metabolic activity)
- Foliar feeding bypasses roots — direct absorption through leaf
- Benefits: less nutrient leaching (bypasses soil), quick uptake even in cold conditions, improved winter hardiness (especially K and P), nutrients stored in plant tissue for use when dormancy ends
- CAUTION: do NOT push excessive growth in winter by over-fertilising with N
  - Cold temperatures cause cell walls to harden off naturally
  - Pushing growth forces cells to expand against hardened walls = weakened, damaged cell structure
  - Result: stretched, disease-prone grass that can't handle cold
  - Some minimal winter growth is normal and fine; excessive is harmful
- Last granular fertiliser: apply 6-8 weeks before anticipated winter dormancy; don't apply again until spring
- NZLA Application Guide has specific timing recommendations by region

## ARTICLE 28 — Understanding Lawn NPK
**URL:** /lawn-tips/understanding-lawn-npk/
**Date:** August 2023 (updated November 2025)

**Key points:**

**Nitrogen (N):**
- Essential for chlorophyll production (green colour) and strong roots/stems
- Usually highest percentage in NPK ratio
- Deficiency: yellow, thin, weak turf
- Excess: too-rapid growth, increased disease and pest susceptibility

**Phosphorus (P):**
- Essential for root development and energy transfer (photosynthesis)
- Moves slowly through soil; used sparingly
- Deficiency: stunted growth, yellow or purple leaves
- Low percentage in professional turf fertilisers (including NZLA All Seasons and All Seasons N+)

**Potassium (K):**
- Regulates water movement within plant; stress tolerance (heat, drought)
- Helps plant use nitrogen more efficiently
- Deficiency: weaker plants, more susceptible to disease and pests

**Micronutrients:**
- Iron: chlorophyll production, cellular function, improves colour
- Calcium: stimulates stems and roots, supports metabolism and enzyme activity
- Manganese: chlorophyll creation; deficiency = grey/tan spots; excess = brown spots
- Zinc: metabolic regulation, chlorophyll production

**NZLA's position:** Sources highest-quality US-made PCSCU globally; aware that cheaper Chinese PCSCU and alternative slow-release technologies are used by other brands; quality over profit margins

## ARTICLE 30 — Dog Urine and Your Lawn
**URL:** /lawn-tips/dog-urine-and-your-lawn/
**Date:** August 2023

**Key points:**
- Dog urine contains high levels of nitrogen — causes burn (yellow, brown, dead patches) from excess nitrogen
- Smaller dogs: urine can have opposite effect — small dark green patches indicate lawn is low in nitrogen; fix with consistent All Seasons feeding for uniform colour
- Burn pattern: yellow/brown patch in centre with ring of dark green growth around edge (nitrogen stimulates at perimeter but burns at concentration)
- Prevention: dilute urine spots with water immediately after dog urinates
- Training dog to use specific area is the most effective long-term solution
- Products added to dog's water bowl to prevent burn: NZLA's community poll (May 2023, NZ Lawn Addicts Facebook group) showed 97% reported they didn't work
- Fertiliser consideration: higher-N products (All Seasons N+ or fast-release) increase burn risk; switching to standard All Seasons (less upfront N, more slow-release N) has helped customers

## ARTICLE 31 — How to Water Your Lawn
**URL:** /lawn-tips/how-to-water-your-lawn/
**Date:** August 2023 (updated November 2025)

**Key points:**
- Best time to water: early morning, 5am-7am (time to soak in before heat; less evaporation)
- Avoid midday watering (evaporates too fast)
- Evening watering risk: water on foliage overnight = humidity = fungal disease
- Cool season (rye, fine fescue, tall fescue): 2-3x per week, 2.5-4cm total per week
- Warm season (Kikuyu, couch): 1-2.5cm per week; more drought-tolerant
- Daily watering creates shallow root system — avoid it; train roots to go deep by irrigating infrequently but deeply
- Tuna can test: place empty tuna cans around lawn while irrigating; when can has 1-1.5cm water, you've watered enough; time how long it takes — use that time consistently
- Multiple tuna cans = also identifies coverage gaps in irrigation system
- Avoid watering in windy conditions (uneven coverage)
- Wetting agents help: NZLA Wetter (liquid) and Wetter GR (granular); apply monthly September to April; prevent dry patch and hydrophobic soil

## ARTICLE 32 — How to Fix a Waterlogged Lawn
**URL:** /lawn-tips/how-to-fix-a-waterlogged-lawn/
**Date:** August 2023 (updated November 2025)

**Key points:**
- Excessive moisture: reduced turf quality, poor drainage, moss and weed development
- Identify soil type first: clay (holds water) vs sandy (drains fast); soil test helps
- NZLA Aerate: soil conditioner for clay/silt soils; molecules attracted to clay and silt particles, making soil more stable with more space between particles; improves air:water ratios; allows water to reach roots and drain excess from top layer
- NZLA Penetrate: soil penetrant; releases water from surface and accelerates movement through soil profile; speeds soil dry-down; drives water downward off surface
- Tank-mix: NZLA Aerate + NZLA Penetrate can be combined when soil has both clay/silt properties

**Drainage improvement methods:**
- Core aeration: removes plugs, creates channels for air and water; do annually on problem areas
- Surface grading: adjust grade so water drains away from buildings; gradual slope ideal
- Drainage systems: French drains or perforated pipes for severe cases
- Topdressing: thin layer of sand, compost, or sand-soil mix; fills low areas, creates smoother surface
- Avoid heavy traffic on clay soil (worsens compaction)
- Watering: deep and infrequent — never frequent shallow watering (promotes shallow roots and waterlogging)
