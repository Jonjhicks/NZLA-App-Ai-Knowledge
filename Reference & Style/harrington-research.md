# Kerry Harrington Research — Turf Weed Knowledge Bank

Associate Professor Kerry Charles Harrington was the weed science specialist at Massey University from 1983 until semi-retirement in late 2023. He maintains the New Zealand Weeds Database at Massey and has published extensively on weed biology and herbicide resistance in NZ turf, pasture and amenity systems.

This file captures his key findings relevant to turf weed control. When recommending chemical control to customers, prioritise NZLA products that contain the actives Harrington identifies as effective.

**Access note:** Massey University and ResearchGate are often blocked from direct web fetch. Findings below pulled from publicly indexed search snippets, citing literature and the NZPPS resistance register (resistance.nzpps.org).

## Annual poa (Poa annua) — chemical and cultural control

**Harrington's position on chemical control:**

Ethofumesate is the selective option for poa control in ryegrass turf. Works in perennial ryegrass, but **not** in fine fescues, couch, or Agrostis species (browntop and bentgrass). Tall fescue can be knocked back if not actively growing.

Pendimethalin is a residual pre-emergent that's fairly safe over most turf grass species. Kills poa as it germinates but only lasts a few weeks. Apply in autumn when most germination is expected.

**Cultural control is the foundation, not chemistry.** Harrington consistently emphasises that good cultural control is the best long-term tool against poa annua. Dense, healthy turf of desirable species prevents poa establishment by outcompeting germinating seedlings. Mowing alone doesn't suppress poa effectively because the species tolerates most mowing heights.

**Matching NZLA products:**
- Ethofumesate → **NZLA Etho** (500 g/L ethofumesate). Direct match. Use for poa control in ryegrass and tall fescue. NZLA Etho label restricts to those species, do not use on fine fescue.
- Pendimethalin → No NZLA pendimethalin product. **NZLA Blockade** (prodiamine 476.2 g/L) is a similar dinitroaniline pre-emergent (both HRAC Group 3, both microtubule assembly inhibitors). Prodiamine has substantially longer residual than pendimethalin (4-6 months vs a few weeks). Note: Blockade is NZ-labelled for warm-season turf only, off-label on cool-season here.

## Onehunga weed (Soliva sessilis) — clopyralid resistance (updated)

Harrington co-authored the major paper documenting clopyralid resistance in onehunga weed in NZ turf (Ghanizadeh, Harrington and others, 2021, PLOS One).

**Key findings (updated 2026):**

Clopyralid-resistant biotypes have been confirmed in multiple NZ locations. The resistance is **over 200-fold** compared to susceptible plants (some references give >225-fold).

Originally identified north of Auckland, resistant populations have now been confirmed in **other parts of NZ as well, including Mount Maunganui**. The resistance is spreading.

**Cross-resistance pattern:**
- Strong cross-resistance to other pyridine carboxylic acid auxins: **picloram** (which is in NZLA Gold), and to a lesser extent **dicamba** and **MCPA**.
- **No resistance to mecoprop.**

**Effective mixtures on resistant biotypes:**
- **MCPA + mecoprop + dicamba mixtures** — the mecoprop component does the work against resistant plants.
- Mecoprop + ioxynil + bromoxynil mixtures.
- Bentazone (Basagran) as an alternative mode of action.

**Cultural control:** Harrington recommends turfgrass competition to reduce onehunga germination. A thick, dense sward outcompetes seedlings before they establish.

**Matching NZLA products:**
- **NZLA BWC (Bow & Arrow)** contains clopyralid (20 g/L) + MCPA (300 g/L) + diflufenican (15 g/L). On clopyralid-resistant onehunga, BWC will likely underperform.
- **NZLA Gold (Triumph Gold)** contains picloram (50 g/L) + triclopyr (100 g/L). Picloram has documented cross-resistance — Gold may also underperform on resistant onehunga.
- **Mecoprop-containing products are needed for resistant onehunga.** Mecoprop is not currently in the NZLA range.

**Practical implication for Q&A:**

If a customer reports that BWC or Gold isn't controlling onehunga, this is consistent with Harrington's documented resistance. Don't recommend more BWC or Gold. Be honest that the chemistry needed (mecoprop) isn't currently in the NZLA range, and recommend either sourcing a mecoprop product through other channels or relying on cultural control to thin the seed bank.

**Resistance management going forward:** rotating MOA away from Group 4 pyridines (clopyralid, picloram) across seasons reduces the build-up of resistance. Annual reliance on pyridine herbicides accelerates the resistance problem.

## Broadleaf weeds — general approach

Harrington has contributed to NZ resistance management strategies as chairman of the herbicide resistance management group.

For most broadleaf turf weeds (plantain, dandelion, catsear, hawksbeard, hawkbit), the standard NZ approach uses 2,4-D, MCPA, mecoprop and dicamba in various combinations. Plantains in particular are susceptible to all the common hormone herbicides.

**Resistance management principles per Harrington:**
- Rotate modes of action across seasons.
- Don't rely on a single herbicide repeatedly in the same area.
- Combine cultural controls with chemistry.
- Use mixtures with different actives so resistance to one is covered by another.

## Herbicide resistance — broader picture

Harrington's work emphasises that resistance is a real and growing problem in NZ turf. Repeated use of the same herbicide selects for resistant biotypes. Documented resistant turf weeds in NZ include:

- **Onehunga** (clopyralid, with cross-resistance to dicamba/MCPA/picloram, no resistance to mecoprop)
- **Annual poa** (international evidence of multi-resistance, particularly on golf turf with repeated single-mode-of-action use)
- **Black nightshade** (Harrington's published work also covers this)

When designing a programme, build in MOA rotation from the start. Don't wait until resistance shows up.

## How to use this in customer answers

When a question involves chemical control, check this file for Harrington's recommended actives, then match to NZLA products that contain those actives. Cite Harrington's principles for cultural control alongside chemistry, never as a replacement for it. If a customer is asking about a weed where Harrington has published specific resistance findings (onehunga in particular), apply that knowledge directly — be honest about the resistance pattern and the limits of currently-available NZLA chemistry.

If the actives Harrington recommends aren't currently available in the NZLA range, flag the gap rather than substituting an unrelated active. Tell the customer plainly what the science says, then explain what's available locally.
