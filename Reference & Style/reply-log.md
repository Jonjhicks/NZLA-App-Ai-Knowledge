## Q — Moss in Kikuyu — 2026-04-17 — Auckland

> Hey team, I have moss growing in places that the Kikuyu hasn't had a chance to grow yet. I have been using all the NZLA products religiously but because of all the rain we have had in Auckland lately? How should I tackle this?

### A

Kikuyu's heading into its slow season now. Soil temps in Auckland are starting to drop toward the 15°C mark where kikuyu slows right down, and below 10°C it basically stops. Any bare patch right now isn't going to fill in from the kikuyu until spring fires up properly, probably late September into October.

Moss shows up because conditions are right for it. You've had heaps of rain recently, which is ideal conditions for moss. Low light, waterlogged soil, no competing grass.

Hit it with NZLA Moss. 200 mL per 10 L of water. The active ingredient works on contact, turns the moss black within a few days. Once it's crisped off, rake it out. Don't skip the raking, dead moss left in place just holds moisture and the next lot sets up shop on top of it.

Then take a look at the spot itself. Is it shady? Does water pool there after rain? Does a screwdriver push in easily, or does it take some force (that's compaction). Moss is almost always a symptom of one of those. If compaction's the driver, pulling a few cores from that area will help, though the kikuyu still won't properly respond until spring.

Come late spring once soil temps are right, a feed of NZLA All Seasons N+ will close those gaps fast.

## Q — Moss control general — 2026-05-05

> What's the best way to get on top of moss in my lawn?

### A (Jon's final, definitive style for moss replies)

Moss is always the result of the conditions. It normally shows up in damp and shaded areas, and compacted, boggy soil contributes to it. Killing the moss without fixing the underlying issue and it'll just come back.

NZLA Moss at 200 mL per 10 L of water, sprayed over the affected areas. You need a high water volume so the product gets right down onto the moss at soil level, not sitting up on the leaf canopy, a lot of people get confused by the high water volume.

Pull some cores through the affected area if you can, just to open it up and let it breathe. NZLA Aerator is good for this if you have one.

Cut back any trees or branches blocking sun over the winter months. I removed a whole tree because of this in one part of my lawn. The missus was not very happy.

Mowing height matters too. Shorter turf thins out, and a bit more canopy helps with coverage. For ryegrass that's 40 to 60 mm over winter.

Make sure you're not still following your summer irrigation plan. I'm in Christchurch and my irrigation's down to once a week now. Stay on a summer schedule and you're putting too much water on, which keeps the soil damp for longer than it should be, and that's exactly what moss wants.

Sometimes you can do all the bits and pieces you want, but if you've got fence lines causing shade, you can either extend the garden beds or accept it and put down more seed in spring. That's what I've got to do with a couple of patches in my own garden.

NZLA Penetrate is a great product for moving moisture away from the top part of the soil profile, you'll find that will also help, it won't cure it but it will help it coming back after applying NZLA Moss and Aerating, leaving the lawn longer etc.

## Q — Robot mowers — 2026-05-29

> Robot lawn mower: Do they improve your lawn or create new problems? Considering purchasing a robot lawn mower - interested in the impact that it has on the lawn over time.

### A

The lawns I've seen running a robot mower full-time usually look better over time, not worse, and there's a simple reason for it.

A robot cuts little and often, sometimes every day. That constant light tipping pushes the grass to tiller and spread sideways instead of stretching up, so you end up with a denser, finer sward. The clippings are tiny and get dropped straight back, so the nutrients in them, nitrogen especially, go back into the soil instead of into your green waste bin. Over a season that's a real feed you're getting for nothing.

So yes, done right, it improves the lawn.

The problems are mostly about setup and conditions, not the machine itself. The big one is wheel tracking. A robot tends to follow similar paths and it'll mow in the wet because it doesn't know any better. On soft or poorly drained ground that can leave wear lines and a bit of compaction where it keeps running.

Cutting height matters too. Set it too low and you'll get the same scalping stress you'd get from any mower, just more often. Keep cool-season grass like ryegrass and fescue up around 30 to 50mm. Warm-season lawns like kikuyu and couch can sit lower.

Because it doesn't collect, you can't lift clippings out if you get a disease run through the lawn. The cutting itself isn't a big disease driver as long as the blades stay sharp, but worth knowing you lose that option.

One that's easy to forget here: hedgehogs. Robot mowers running at night injure them, and we've got plenty about. Set it to mow during the day and that's sorted.

On feeding, don't let the clipping return talk you out of a proper program. It genuinely cuts the nitrogen you need to put on, but it doesn't give you the potassium and iron that keep colour and hardiness up. A balanced feed like All Seasons a few times across the year still earns its place.

Get the height right, mow in daylight, and keep an eye on tracking if your ground's soft. Do that and a robot mower is one of the better things you can put on a lawn.

## Q — Fusarium / Microdochium diagnosis — 2026-05-29

> [Image of white cottony mycelium on grass blades, late autumn] What is this? what causes it and how do I control it?

### A

That white cottony webbing across the blades is fungal mycelium. Now, that bit can be a little misleading on its own, because dollar spot throws out mycelium too and the two can look similar on a damp morning. But given the time of year, cold and wet heading into winter, I'd say this is almost definitely Fusarium, also called Microdochium patch, or the early stages of it.

What causes it is pretty simple. It's a cold weather disease that gets going when the grass stays wet and the air sits still. Long dewy mornings, shade, poor drainage, no airflow, and soft growth from a late hit of nitrogen, especially common with people using fast-release fertilisers late in the season.

First, fix the conditions. Knock the dew off in the mornings, even just dragging a hose or a brush across the lawn makes a difference because you're getting the leaf to dry faster. Hopefully you're not watering at night, you should hardly be watering at all this time of the year.

A lot of people make the mistake of throwing nitrogen at it, because that isn't the best idea this time of the year. If you've had a late hit of fast-release on it, that combined with the conditions would do it.

For the spray, get Fungus Pro on as soon as you can. It's a contact, so it knocks the mycelium down where it touches it and protects the healthy grass around the patch straight away, and with Fusarium the sooner you get onto it the better. Just know it sits on the surface and washes off, so you'll be reapplying through the wet months.

Then back it up with Azoxy. That one's systemic, so it moves into the plant and cleans up the infection a contact can't reach, and it gives you longer between sprays. Running the two together also stops the fungus building any resistance.

## Q — Rust diagnosis — 2026-05-29

> [Image showing orange-yellow pustules on grass blades] I can see rust

### A

Yep, that's rust. Those orange pustules will rub off on your fingers or turn your shoes orange when you walk through it, dead giveaway.

What it's really telling you is the lawn's growing slowly and it's a bit underfed. Rust takes hold when the grass isn't ticking over, so it's common on young lawns, hungry lawns, and anything sitting in dew with poor airflow. Late summer and autumn is its usual run, and it'll happily carry on while growth is slow.

Now here's where the standard advice needs a bit of care. The usual fix for rust is feed it to get it growing and mow the infected tips off. That's spot on in the growing season. But you're heading into winter, and a hit of nitrogen now does two things you don't want, it forces soft growth right when Fusarium pressure is climbing, and the cold means the lawn won't really respond anyway. So I wouldn't go chasing it with nitrogen this time of year.

What I would do: keep mowing and catch the clippings. Every cut takes infected leaf and spores off the lawn, which is the most useful thing you can do right now.

Sort the leaf wetness. Knock the dew off in the mornings, make sure you're not watering late in the day, and open up airflow if anything's crowding it.

Feed light, with iron. All Seasons gives it a gentle lift and the iron firms the plant up without pushing the soft growth a big nitrogen feed would. If you want to even the colour and give it a small nudge, Liquid Boost at 200 to 500 mL per 100m² is a foliar option.

On a home lawn rust is mostly cosmetic and it tends to grow out on its own once the grass picks up speed in spring. If it's running really hard and you want to knock it back, Fungus Pro (thiram) is the one for rust on label.

## Q — Pale uneven new lawn going into winter — 2026-05-29

> [Image of new lawn, mottled colour, autumn] What's going on here?

### A

Good news first, there's nothing wrong with that lawn that should worry you. No disease, no insect damage, no dead patches. What you're looking at is uneven colour on a young lawn heading into the cold, which is about as normal as it gets.

A couple of things are going on at once. The grass is cool-season, and as the soil cools off it slows right down and loses some of its punch in the colour. That's just what they do this time of year. On a newish lawn it shows up more because the plant's still filling in and the starter fertiliser that got it going has largely run its course.

Those lighter and darker stripes running down the lawn are worth a closer look. If they line up with your mowing passes, it's just the grass laying different directions catching the light, nothing to fix. If they don't, it's more likely uneven fertiliser spreading. Easy to sort next time by splitting the rate and going over it in two directions.

What I wouldn't do is throw a big hit of nitrogen at it to green it up. That forces soft growth right when the disease pressure is climbing. The better move is iron. It greens the lawn up without pushing growth, and it hardens the plant against the cold.

All Seasons at 2 kg per 100m² is the granular pick, it's got the iron and potassium for this time of year without overdoing the nitrogen. If you want to even the colour up faster and more evenly than a granular will, Liquid Boost sprayed at 200 to 500 mL per 100m² is 15 percent nitrogen with 6 percent iron, and because it's a foliar spray it goes on dead even.

## Article — Autumn & winter lawn fungus? Start with your fertiliser — 2026-05-29

> Long-form blog post for newzealandlawnaddicts.com, prompted by repeated forum posts about fungal issues stemming from fast-release fertiliser use heading into winter.

### A

It's like clockwork in the Facebook group this time of year, the same fungal issues start popping up in people's lawns.

And more often than not, it isn't one of our customers posting. I'm not going to pretend fertiliser is the whole story. Winter disease is mostly driven by the conditions, cool temperatures, long dews, damp, shade, poor airflow and drainage. That's the bulk of it. But fertiliser is the one part of that picture you actually control, and the lawns struggling are often running on cheap, high quick-release nitrogen with little or no potassium behind it. A lot of it gets sold in a bag marked 'lawn fertiliser', which doesn't help anyone.

This post isn't about pushing one product over another, it's just to explain why this happens. Can you get away with these fertilisers at certain times of the year? Sometimes, yes, particularly if you're spoon feeding regularly. But it's still not ideal and often creates other problems down the line, or when conditions aren't great, which is most of the time in New Zealand.

I know I sound like a broken record at times, but I can't emphasise this enough: alongside mowing regularly, fertiliser choice is probably one of the most important Lawn Care 101 fundamentals. In fact, it's such a big deal that it forms part of our Lawn Care 101 guide.

The right fertiliser doesn't just feed growth. It influences plant health, stress tolerance, disease pressure, nutrient availability, and how the lawn performs through the colder months.

There are some key times during the year where issues start identifying themselves, autumn, winter and peak summer being the obvious ones.

When you put a fast-release fertiliser down, the lawn gets a big slug of available nitrogen all at once. It greens up quickly and pushes soft top growth, then burns through it and drops back off. That soft, lush growth is the problem heading into winter. This matters most for the cool-season patch diseases, Fusarium being the main one we see going into a damp winter. Those are the ones that get worse when the lawn's carrying too much nitrogen. Cool, damp, slow growth is the environment they thrive in, and a hit of fast-release nitrogen on top just feeds them.

It works the other way for some diseases too. Rust, dollar spot and red thread actually show up more on hungry, underfed lawns, so this isn't as simple as less nitrogen always being better. It's about matching the feed to the season, and going into winter that means easing off the nitrogen rather than spiking it. I do still find red thread is often a result of using a fast release. People think it works like a long-term slow release, when it doesn't.

Growth forced by a nitrogen spike is weaker than steady growth. It holds moisture longer, tears more easily when you mow, and those torn edges are open doors for fungal pathogens to get into the leaf. The same flush that makes the lawn look great for a week or two is what leaves it more exposed when the weather turns.

When a lawn's already showing disease, the instinct is to throw a hit of fast-release at it, push fresh growth and grow the infected tissue out. A measured feed can genuinely help a lawn recover, that's fair enough. But a big fast-release spike into cold, damp conditions is the wrong tool. A few weeks later it wears off, the lawn drops back into stress, the conditions are still cold and damp, and you're back where you started, having treated the symptom.

Fast-release also drives the plant hard into leaf growth at the expense of roots and carbohydrate reserves. Heading into winter that's backwards. You want the lawn slowing down naturally and hardening off, building the reserves to carry it through the cold, not being pushed into soft growth it can't support.

The word "fast" isn't really the issue. What does the damage is the surge of available nitrogen, and fast-release is what delivers it all at once. A quality slow-release meters the same nitrogen out steadily, so the plant gets consistent feeding instead of a spike then a trough. That consistency is what builds a lawn that can handle stress.

So what should you be putting down this time of year? Hopefully you've all applied a slow-release fertiliser by now to carry the lawn through winter. Then move to foliar fertilisers through winter, keeping an eye on the amount of nitrogen in each one. Once you're properly into winter, the lawn's response to granular fertiliser slows right down anyway as soil temperatures keep dropping. I personally move to soil health products like Root Health, Humic & Restore, then rotate in Charger, Liquid Starter and Iron.

It's the same story every year. The fungal posts start rolling in, and when you dig into it there's usually either no proper granular programme there at all, or it's built on cheap, high quick-release nitrogen. Then come the fungicides and the rescue products, everyone chasing symptoms, when sorting the conditions and the fertiliser base would have taken a good chunk of the pressure off in the first place.

A quality slow-release granular won't make your lawn disease-proof. Nothing will, the weather has the final say. But it builds a stronger plant that handles stress a lot better once conditions turn against it.

## Q — Should I mow my lawn in winter? — 2026-05-29 (sample knowledge bank Q&A)

> Should I mow my lawn in winter?

### A

Yes, just less often. Lawns still grow through winter, especially up north, just slowly. Skipping it entirely means it gets away on you, then you take too much off in one go come spring and the lawn doesn't like that.

How often depends on where you are. Cool-season ryegrass and fescue in most of the South Island might only need a cut every 3 to 4 weeks through the depths of winter. Auckland and the upper North Island, more like every 2 to 3 weeks. Watch the lawn, not the calendar.

Lift the height up. 40 to 60 mm for ryegrass over winter. Longer grass photosynthesises better in low light, holds carbohydrate reserves better, and shades out moss.

Don't mow when there's a frost on the grass, you'll bruise the blades and leave footprints in the lawn for weeks. And don't mow when it's wet enough that the mower's making tracks. Sharp blades matter more in winter than summer too, a tear is a doorway for fungal pathogens in cool damp conditions.

## Comment — Grass seed and striping — 2026-05-29

> @Nick M NZLA Dark Rye and MDR are definitely darker, so the stripes tend to stand out more. (Forum thread on whether premium ryegrass cultivars give better stripes.)

### A (Jon's version after edits)

NZLA Dark Rye and MDR are definitely darker, so the stripes tend to stand out more. But I think a lot of people put too much emphasis on the grass type itself for producing stripes.

I've seen people running MDR and Dark Rye whose lawns still look average because the basics just aren't being followed properly. Mowing frequency, fertiliser, watering, sharp blades, they all play a massive role.

You see it a lot in the Facebook group where someone posts a really nice lawn and people ask "what grass is that?" thinking they can just sow that seed and instantly get the same result, when really it's the whole programme and consistency behind it.

That said, if all the basics are dialled in, then yeah, MDR and Dark Rye are probably about on par and definitely give some of the darkest striping.

To be fair, even lighter coloured ryegrasses like NZ Perennial Rye can still look unreal when they're healthy, well fed and maintained properly. That grass stripes up mint.

I'd say RSR is the worst one for striping (still not bad), but it has its own benefits over the others.

## Q — Pythium vs Microdochium diagnosis — 2026-06-04

> Comment on Pythium blight blog post by Hayden Sheat:
> With the conditions being so specific (temperature range) would it be unlikely to see this in a tall fescue lawn now - I have something that looks similar pythium blight and hoping it's not pythium

### A

Hayden, your instinct's right. Pythium foliar blight is summer-only for almost all of NZ, you need day temps over 30, night temps over 20, and high humidity sitting for 15-plus hours. None of that's happening in June. Tall fescue is also less susceptible than perennial rye, which makes the odds even lower.

Cool-temperature Pythium root rot sits in waterlogged cool soils rather than humid summer air. It is a thing, but it's much less common than the warm-season foliar version and the symptoms are different. Root rot is a slow yellowing decline rather than the rapid greasy patches you'd see with foliar blight.

What's actually likely on a tall fescue lawn this time of year showing disease-like symptoms is Microdochium patch (still called Fusarium patch by a lot of people). Cool wet conditions are exactly when it turns up. Small circular patches, often with a salmon-pink ring of mycelium on the active edge when the lawn's wet in the morning, that's the classic look.

To work out what you're actually dealing with, where are you in the country, can you post a close-up of the affected area plus a wider shot, is the soil sitting wet, and has anything been applied recently. More detail and I can point you in the right direction.

## Q — Pre-em timing — 2026-03-15 — Christchurch

> When should I be putting down prodiamine for summer grass weeds? I've heard different things from different people.

### A

For Canterbury, you want prodiamine down before soil temps at 50 mm depth climb past about 12°C and stay there. Usually that's late September into early October down your way, a week or two later than up north. Crabgrass and summer grass start firing once soils warm, so you're trying to get the barrier in place before germination, not after.

Second application around mid-December keeps the barrier active through the worst of summer, since prodiamine starts losing efficacy after roughly 12 to 16 weeks depending on rainfall and irrigation.

Water it in within a few days of application. Without that, you're not getting the barrier you think you're getting.

One thing worth flagging — prodiamine won't touch anything that's already germinated. If you're seeing seedlings come through, you're into post-em territory and that's a different conversation.
