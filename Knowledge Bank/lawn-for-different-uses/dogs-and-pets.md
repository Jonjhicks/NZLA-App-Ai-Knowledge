# Lawn With Dogs and Pets

Dogs cause two distinct types of damage: mechanical wear from running and digging, and chemical burn from urine. Understanding which you're dealing with changes how you respond.

## Species Selection

The right species makes a measurable difference in how well a lawn survives heavy dog use.

**NZLA RSR Ryegrass** is the standout choice for dog lawns. It's stoloniferous (above-ground spreading runners) so it actively self-repairs worn areas. Standard ryegrass stays where it was seeded and relies on tillering alone.

**Elfin Tall Fescue** has rhizomatous (underground) spreading. It's slower to repair than RSR but handles dry summers and shaded areas better, and tolerates urine slightly better than ryegrass.

**Couch** and **kikuyu** in the upper North Island recover aggressively from wear but are not very urine-tolerant.

No grass species is fully immune to dog urine damage. The difference between species is degree, not immunity.

## Managing Wear

Dogs are creatures of habit. The fence-line patrol, the entry point sprint, the spot where they lie in the sun — these tracks get hammered on the same line day after day. No lawn holds together under that kind of concentrated wear indefinitely.

Cultural management to support recovery:
- Mow at 40 to 50 mm rather than lower. More leaf means more capacity for photosynthesis and faster recovery.
- Heavier fertility programme than you'd run for a purely ornamental lawn. NZLA All Seasons three to four times a year supports the growth rate needed for continuous recovery.
- Overseed worn tracks in autumn. NZLA RSR Ryegrass or Perennial Rye into scarified or lightly raked soil, with NZLA Starter worked in. Even without addressing the cause, autumn seeding-and-over-seeding keeps coverage reasonable.

For spots that won't recover no matter what you do (gate entry, fence-line corner), the practical move is often to put a physical surface there — pavers, stepping stones, or a small mulched area — rather than continuously repairing lawn that has no chance.

## Urine Damage

The characteristic pattern: dead centre patch, dark green ring around it. Urine delivers a concentrated nitrogen load that burns the grass at the point of contact while fertilising the surrounding area. Female dogs produce more damage than males because their squat posture concentrates the deposit; larger dogs produce more damage than small ones.

**Repairing urine spots:**

1. Flush with water as soon as possible after urination. Pour 10 to 20 litres over the spot to dilute the urine salts. If caught within an hour or two, significant damage can be prevented. After 24 to 48 hours, the damage is done.

2. Wait 2 to 4 weeks after the initial burn. Trying to reseed immediately into urine-saturated soil won't work. Light recovery from the edges is possible without intervention if the damage was mild.

3. Rake out the dead material. Remove dead grass and lightly cultivate the surface — a garden fork or hand rake. The soil needs to be loose and the dead material gone before seeding.

4. Apply NZLA Starter granular lightly to the bare area and rake in.

5. Sow seed at a heavy rate — 40 to 50 g/m² for a patch repair to get fast coverage. NZLA RSR Ryegrass or Perennial Rye matched to the existing lawn. Cover with a light topping of quality topsoil or compost.

6. Water lightly and frequently. 2 to 3 times daily until germination (5 to 10 days). Once seedlings reach 30 to 40 mm, reduce to standard watering.

7. Push recovery with NZLA Liquid Starter at 7 to 10 days post-germination, and again at 17 to 20 days. First mow when seedlings are 70 to 80 mm.

For lawns with multiple dogs and constant urine spots, individual patch repair becomes unsustainable. A seasonal overseed of the main affected areas — scarify, reseed, feed — is more practical than chasing individual spots.

## Designated Dog Areas

The single most effective thing for protecting a good lawn from dog damage is giving the dog their own zone that isn't the main lawn. A section of the property set aside with mulch, pea gravel, artificial turf, or hard surface as the dog's primary toilet and exercise area keeps the main lawn in far better shape.

Most dogs adapt to a designated toilet area with a bit of routine. The payoff for the lawn is significant.

## Digging

Some dogs dig compulsively. There's no lawn management solution to a dog that digs — it's a dog behaviour problem, not a grass problem. Physical deterrents (rocks placed in favourite dig spots), additional exercise to reduce boredom, or a designated sandpit or digging zone are the relevant responses. The lawn part is just repair after the cause is addressed: rake, reseed, water, feed.

## Chemistry and Pet Safety

Check NZLA product labels for re-entry intervals before letting dogs back onto treated lawn.

NZLA Grub+ (for grass grub and other soil insects): keep dogs off the treated area until it's been irrigated in and the surface has dried. The product needs to be in the soil, not sitting on leaf surfaces.

Slug pellets: any slug bait containing metaldehyde is toxic to dogs. NZLA Worm Pellets use a different active (ferric phosphate or similar) — check the current label, but this is the far safer option for pet households.

For herbicide and fungicide applications, the label re-entry period is the guide. For most NZLA products this is 24 to 48 hours once dried, but always read the specific label.

## Realistic Expectations

Dogs and premium lawn don't coexist easily. A well-managed dog lawn will look tidy and functional. It won't look like a bowling green. The wear patterns, the occasional urine spot, the seasonal repair work — these are the cost of having dogs, not failures of lawn management. The right expectation is a lawn that looks good enough and holds together, with the rough spots managed rather than perfectly prevented.
