# Lawn Near Gardens and Planting Beds

The lawn edge nearest a vegetable garden is the one place where chemistry mistakes really sting. A bit of drift from a hormone herbicide can wipe out a bed of tomatoes. Get the setup right and you won't have to think about it again.

## The Risk Hierarchy

Not all NZLA products carry the same risk near food crops.

**NZLA Gold (picloram + triclopyr)** is the highest-risk product near vegetable gardens. Picloram is a persistent, soil-active herbicide that some crops — particularly tomatoes, capsicums, beans, peas, and potatoes — are acutely sensitive to. Clippings from Gold-treated lawn can't be composted if you're going to use that compost anywhere near vegetables; picloram residue persists in clippings for 12 to 18 months. Keep Gold applications at least 5 metres from any vegetable garden, and don't apply it where tree roots extend under the treated area.

**NZLA BWC (clopyralid + MCPA + diflufenican)** carries less residual risk than Gold but clopyralid does have some persistence. Keep a sensible buffer and don't compost clippings from recently treated lawn for 6 to 8 weeks.

**NZLA Halo (halosulfuron)** and **NZLA Meso (mesotrione)** both carry lower risk to vegetable crops than hormone herbicides. Standard buffer distances apply — don't spray them into the garden, but nearby use with good technique is manageable.

**NZLA Sticker (organosilicone adjuvant)** increases uptake and penetration of whatever it's mixed with. That cuts both ways: it makes herbicide more effective on target  and more damaging to non-target plants if drift occurs. Don't use Sticker in any spray mix you're applying near a vegetable garden.

## Drift Management

Drift is the main vector for cross-contamination. Fine droplets from a cheap or poorly calibrated sprayer can carry metres in light wind. On a still day with a flat fan nozzle producing medium-to-coarse droplets, the risk drops substantially.

Practical rules:
- Spray on calm days. Under 5 km/h is ideal, but watch for gusty conditions even when average wind speed is low.
- Spray with the wind blowing away from the garden, never towards it.
- Keep pressure low. Higher pressure produces finer droplets that stay airborne longer.
- Coarse nozzle tips reduce drift. A flat fan tip at 2 to 3 bar is safer than a hollow cone at 4 bar.
- Stay back 1 to 2 metres from the garden edge when spraying broadleaf herbicides. For Gold, stay back 5 metres.

## Run-off and Soil Movement

Run-off is less talked about than drift but still matters. Heavy irrigation-general or rain immediately after a fertiliser or herbicide application can push chemistry off the lawn and into adjacent garden beds. Time applications so there's no heavy rainfall forecast in the following 24 hours. When irrigating fertiliser in, apply enough to move it into the rootzone (6 to 10 mm) but not so much that it sheets off the surface.

## Clippings Handling

Clippings from recently treated lawn carry residue. For vegetable gardens, treat the compost pile as a potential residue sink:

- Clippings from Gold-treated lawn: don't compost for 12 to 18 months. Send to landfill.
- Clippings from BWC-treated lawn: don't add to the veggie garden compost heap for 6 to 8 weeks.
- Clippings from fertiliser-only treatment: fine for compost immediately.
- If unsure, send them to green waste rather than risk contaminating compost used on food crops.

## Physical Setup

The best protection is a physical buffer between the lawn and the garden. A path, a garden edge, stepping stones, or a mowing strip all create a break that reduces drift and makes mowing easier. Even a 300 to 500 mm path makes a meaningful difference to spray and clipping management.

In the buffer zone closest to the garden, hand-weeding instead of spraying is the low-risk approach. If  in that strip are the main problem, spot-treat carefully rather than blanket-spraying — a small pump sprayer with a hood shield gives much better control near garden edges.

## Organic Gardens

If the vegetable garden is run organically, the sensitivity goes up. Even a small amount of herbicide contamination in compost can carry enough residue to affect plants. For gardens under organic management, avoid herbicide use entirely in the adjacent lawn area. Cultural management — good fertility, dense turf, and hand-weeding the boundary strip — is the right approach. NZLA All Seasons feeds the lawn without adding chemistry risk.

## Practical Approach

For most New Zealand home gardens, the combination of good spray technique, sensible buffer distances, and awareness of which products are higher-risk handles nearly all situations. The problems happen when someone sprays on a windy day or adds treated clippings to the compost without thinking. Awareness is most of it.
