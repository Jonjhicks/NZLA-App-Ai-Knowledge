# Lawn for Kids and Play

A play lawn needs to take punishment and recover. Aesthetics are secondary. The most useful thing you can do is choose the right species and set a management programme that prioritises resilience over perfection.

## Species Choice

**NZLA RSR Ryegrass** is the right base for a kids' play lawn. Stoloniferous spreading means it actively fills worn areas rather than waiting for seed. It handles the combination of concentrated foot traffic, ball sport, running, and general chaos better than standard ryegrass.

Blending with tall fescue adds drought tolerance for summer when water restrictions hit and the kids are outside the most. A 70/30 or 60/40 RSR Ryegrass/tall fescue blend is a solid choice for most of the country.

Avoid fine fescue as the primary species for play lawns — it's low-wear-tolerance and not suited to this kind of use.

## Cultural Management

Mow at 40 to 50 mm. Taller grass cushions impact (relevant for falls), has more leaf area for photosynthesis, and recovers faster from wear than closely mown grass.

Fertility programme: NZLA All Seasons three to four times a year. Play lawns need the nutrition to keep growing and repairing. A once-a-year feed won't cut it.

Annual autumn overseed. Even if the lawn looks reasonable, an autumn overseed with NZLA RSR Ryegrass into scarified or lightly raked soil topped with NZLA Starter keeps density up through the following year. This is the single most useful annual maintenance step for a play lawn.

## Common Play Lawn Problems

**Trampoline:** The patch under a trampoline dies within a few months of consistent use. Between the shade, the core-aeration from constant movement, and the physical abrasion, grass can't survive under there long-term. Move the trampoline every 6 to 8 weeks during the season to spread the wear, or accept that it needs to go on a hard or mulched surface. Reseeding the dead patch in autumn after the trampoline comes in for winter works well — NZLA RSR Ryegrass with Starter, kept moist until germination.

**Paddling pools and sandpits:** Anything that sits on the lawn and blocks sunlight kills it underneath. Move them regularly — weekly during peak summer use if you want to maintain coverage. Overseed any dead patches in autumn.

**Football, cricket, soccer patterns:** The bowling crease, the centre of the pitch, the spot in front of the goal — these get hammered on the same point repeatedly. Autumn seeding-and-over-seeding of those specific zones with NZLA RSR Ryegrass and Starter rebuilds coverage before the following season.

**Bike tracks:** Repeated tyre paths compact and wear the lawn down to bare soil. A dedicated hard or mulched bike track is more practical than trying to maintain grass on a fixed line. Where a track has already formed, autumn reseed and — if the core-aeration is bad — a fork or core aeration before reseeding.

## Chemistry Safety Around Kids

Re-entry intervals on NZLA product labels tell you when it's safe to let kids back on the lawn. Follow them.

As a general guide, most products are safe once dry and the surface has had 24 to 48 hours. Insecticides like Grub+ need to be watered in and dried before re-entry. Always read the label for the specific product.

Practical approach: apply chemistry in the morning before school or on a day when kids are away. By the time they're back in the afternoon, the product has dried and it's a non-issue.

Don't store opened chemical containers where kids can access them. This is obvious but worth stating.

## Bee Stings

Bare feet on a lawn with clover or flowering broadleaf  is how most bee stings happen. Kids running and playing without shoes are at risk. For families where this is a genuine concern (allergies, young kids, bare-foot-always households), a NZLA BWC application in spring controls the broadleaf  that flower and attract bees. Keep on top of the programme and the risk drops significantly.

Slippery surfaces: wet lawn is slippery for running and ball play. Clover-rich lawn is more slippery than pure grass. Both are worth managing for an active kids' lawn. The BWC programme for clover control also addresses this.

## Designated Play Zones

The trampoline, the sandpit, the paddling pool spot, the cricket pitch — if these are in fixed locations, treating them as designated non-lawn surfaces from the start is easier than fighting the wear. A bit of planning with mulch, pavers, or artificial turf under the trampoline at installation prevents years of reseeding.

The main lawn stays nicer when it's not carrying the load of every fixed play structure on top of general play wear.

## Realistic Expectations

An actively used kids' play lawn will show wear, urine from pets, occasional bare patches, and seasonal recovery cycles. That's normal. The right management is focused on recovery speed, not on preventing all damage. With NZLA RSR Ryegrass, regular fertility, and an annual autumn overseed, a play lawn can look reasonably good most of the year even with heavy use. Perfect, not so much — and that's fine.
