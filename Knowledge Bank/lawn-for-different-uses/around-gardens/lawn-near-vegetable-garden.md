# What chemistry is safe near my vegetable garden?

Several considerations: herbicide drift, soil residual chemistry, run-off, and clippings handling.

Drift management:
- Spray on still days (under 5 km/h wind ideally).
- Use low-pressure spray with coarse droplet nozzles to reduce fine mist drift.
- Spray with the wind blowing away from the garden.
- Avoid spraying within 1 to 2 metres of garden edge with active vegetables.

Specific NZLA product considerations:

NZLA Gold (picloram + triclopyr):
- Picloram is highly damaging to vegetable crops, particularly tomatoes, capsicums, beans, peas, potatoes.
- Clippings from Gold-treated lawn can't be composted (residual persists 12 to 18 months).
- Stay well away from vegetable gardens (5+ metres) when applying Gold.
- Don't apply Gold within reach of tree roots that extend under the lawn.

NZLA BWC (clopyralid + MCPA + diflufenican):
- Clopyralid has some residual but less than picloram.
- Generally safer near gardens than Gold but still keep buffer.
- Some clippings residual - don't compost clippings from recently treated lawn for 6 to 8 weeks.

NZLA Halo (halosulfuron):
- Lower risk to most vegetable crops than hormone herbicides.
- Still maintain buffer distance.

NZLA Meso (mesotrione):
- Soil residual exists but lower risk than picloram.
- Standard buffer distance.

NZLA Sticker (organosilicone):
- Helps herbicide stick to target plants.
- Increases potential damage to non-target plants if drift occurs.
- Don't use Sticker in spray solutions near vegetable gardens.

Run-off considerations:
- Lawn applications shouldn't run off onto adjacent garden.
- Water in fertiliser carefully (not so much that nutrients wash off the lawn).
- Avoid herbicide application before heavy rainfall.

Clippings handling:
- Clippings from lawn treated with hormone herbicides shouldn't go in compost that's used on vegetables.
- Standard fertiliser clippings are fine for compost.
- When in doubt, send recent post-herbicide clippings to landfill.

Buffer distances:
- 2 metres minimum from vegetable garden for hormone herbicides.
- 5 metres ideal for higher-risk products (picloram).
- Some vegetables more sensitive than others - tomatoes, beans particularly vulnerable.

Practical setup:
- Physical barrier between lawn and garden (path, hedge, fence) helps reduce drift.
- Mow direction away from garden when possible.
- Catch clippings near garden edges rather than mulch-mow (less spread of chemistry residues into garden).

For gardens with sensitive crops:
- Avoid herbicide use in the lawn nearest the garden.
- Hand-weed lawn in the buffer zone.
- Spot-treat individual weeds with very careful application rather than blanket spray.

For organic vegetable gardens:
- Even minor chemistry contamination can break organic certification.
- Avoid herbicide use in adjacent lawn.
- Cultural lawn management (good fertility, dense turf) reduces weed pressure.

For typical NZ home gardens:
- Most NZLA products at label rate, applied carefully, don't cause garden damage.
- The risk is when application gets careless (windy day, drift, runoff).
- Awareness and careful application handle most situations.

Read product labels for specific advisory information about garden proximity. Some labels have explicit "do not apply within X metres of food crops" instructions.
