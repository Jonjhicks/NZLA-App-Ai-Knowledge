# Lawn Around a Pool

Pool-side lawn takes more punishment than most people expect. The chlorine or salt from splash water, the concentrated foot traffic on the same path every day, and the heat reflecting off surrounding paving all add up. Design the setup sensibly and the maintenance side is straightforward.

## The Splash Zone Problem

A small amount of chlorine from splash water isn't a problem for grass — it breaks down in the soil within hours. Where it does cause issues is in the narrow strip of lawn right at the pool edge, where splash is frequent and concentrated. Over time, chlorine accumulation stresses that zone and you get a consistently patchy strip of lawn that never quite recovers between repair attempts.

Salt-water pools are harder on the lawn than chlorine pools. Salt accumulates in the soil rather than breaking down, and the build-up in the splash zone gets worse over a season without intervention. Periodic deep flushing with fresh water helps dilute the accumulated salts — run a sprinkler on that zone for 30 to 60 minutes after a period of heavy pool use.

For hydrogen-peroxide pools, there's minimal lawn impact. Modern low-chlorine systems are also less damaging than older heavy-chlorination setups.

## Species Choice

Near a pool, species that self-repair and handle some salt stress are worth the extra consideration.

**NZLA RSR Ryegrass** handles wear and recovers from concentrated foot traffic better than standard perennial ryegrass. For the path between house and pool that gets hammered every summer, this makes a real difference.

**Elfin Tall Fescue** is rhizomatous and pulls through drought and summer stress well — useful for areas where pool paving reflects significant heat onto the lawn.

**Couch** in the upper North Island handles salt and heat well and self-repairs aggressively. For lifestyle properties around salt pools in Auckland or Northland, worth considering.

## Design Is the Real Fix

Most pool-lawn problems come from pushing the lawn too close to the water. A 1 to 2 metre hard surface border (paving, composite decking, or exposed aggregate) between the pool coping and the lawn edge eliminates the splash zone problem almost entirely. The lawn beyond that can be managed normally.

A defined path from the house to the pool — paving, stepping stones, or compacted gravel — takes the wear off the lawn and removes the trampled-strip problem. It also looks more intentional than a worn-out grass track.

For new pool installations, plan the lawn boundary as part of the design rather than trying to fit the lawn around the finished pool later. The drainage-and-soil-moisture from pool surrounds should direct water away from the lawn area, not onto it.

## Foot Traffic Patterns

The path between the house and the pool gets compressed into a narrow corridor. Even RSR Ryegrass can't cope with that volume of traffic on a single line without some degradation. Options:

- Install a hard path and remove the problem.
- Widen the informal route so traffic spreads across a broader area.
- Accept that a path will develop and reseed it each autumn when pool season ends.

The area directly at the pool steps or entry ladder also gets heavy use. Non-slip paving or a small hard-surface pad there is safer and more practical than trying to maintain a grass strip that gets constant wet feet and concentrated traffic.

## Fertilising and Chemistry Near Pools

The same drift and run-off principles that apply near vegetable gardens apply near pools, with one addition: some pool owners are particularly cautious about fertiliser or herbicide products washing into the pool water. From a lawn management perspective, avoid heavy applications immediately before pool use on a hot day when feet and hose-down water might carry material poolside. NZLA All Seasons granular fed according to label rates and watered in well carries minimal risk once it's in the rootzone.

## Algae and Slime

Constant moisture at the pool edge from splash water creates conditions where algae or slime mould can establish on the lawn surface. This is a drainage-and-soil-moisture and air-movement problem as much as a lawn problem. Improving drainage-and-soil-moisture in that zone and avoiding over-watering (the pool splash does some of the work) reduces it. Where NZLA Moss is used for algae in broader lawn situations, the setup near pools should still have adequate drainage-and-soil-moisture first.

## Old Pool Sites

An excavated and filled pool site often has highly variable soil — fill material mixed with the original topsoil, core-aeration from the backfill process, and sometimes debris. Subsidence over years can leave low spots. For establishing lawn over an old pool site: import quality topsoil to a 150 to 200 mm depth, address any drainage-and-soil-moisture issues, and treat it like a new lawn establishment with NZLA Starter and the appropriate seed blend.

## Realistic Expectations

Premium lawn right next to the water isn't really achievable. Chemistry exposure, concentrated traffic, and heat reflection from surrounding hard surfaces all work against it. The realistic goal is a tidy, functional lawn that looks good from the house and holds together through summer — with a proper hard-surface transition to the pool taking the worst of the load.
