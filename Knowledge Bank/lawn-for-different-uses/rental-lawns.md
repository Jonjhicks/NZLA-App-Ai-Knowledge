# Rental Lawns

Rental properties are a specific lawn care context. Tenants want a lawn that looks okay with minimal effort and cost. Landlords want one that's easy to maintain, won't be a dispute point at the end of tenancy, and holds value. Neither want to think about it much.

---

## For tenants

### What you're responsible for

Under NZ tenancy law, tenants are generally responsible for keeping the lawn mowed to a reasonable standard. What "reasonable" means isn't tightly defined — mowing it occasionally and not letting it go completely feral is the baseline.

Check your tenancy agreement. Some specify mowing frequency or lawn maintenance expectations.

### Minimum effective care

The minimum that keeps a rental lawn looking okay with the least possible effort:

**Mow it.** Regularly enough that you're never removing more than a third of the leaf. If the lawn gets ahead of you, mow high first, then a week later drop the height again. Never try to cut down a very long lawn in one pass — it stresses the grass and leaves a patchy result.

**Once-a-year fertiliser.** One application of All Seasons granular in spring (September/October) at 2 kg/100m² and the lawn will look better for the entire season. One bag typically covers 100 to 150 m². The cost is low, the result is significant.

**Basic weed control.** If the lawn is mostly your mowing isn't going to make it look much better. NZLA Gold applied to broadleaf  in autumn or spring kills the most common ones (dandelion, clover, plantain) and the grass fills back in. One application per year is usually enough maintenance.

That's it. Three things. The lawn won't win any awards, but it'll look after itself and won't become a dispute issue.

### What not to do

Don't apply anything strong or unfamiliar without knowing what you're doing — patchy application of the wrong product is harder to fix than . Stick to the basics.

Don't let the lawn go dry in summer to the point of dormancy and then try to revive it quickly before an inspection — this takes time. A basic watering schedule in summer is easier than emergency recovery.

## For landlords

### Setting up for low maintenance

The lowest-maintenance rental lawn is a dense, weed-suppressed turf mown at a reasonable height. The problem is that many rental properties have the opposite: a thin, weedy, irregular lawn that requires constant work to look acceptable.

The investment case for renovation: a one-off renovation of a poor lawn typically costs less than two or three years of tenant complaints and disputes. A dense, established lawn also photographs better and attracts better tenants.

### Renovation vs maintenance

If the lawn is less than 50% grass, plan to renovate at the end of a tenancy — oversow or full renovation depending on the state. The budget programme article covers minimum-cost renovation.

If the lawn is mostly grass but thin and lacking colour: a single application of All Seasons in spring and Gold for  will significantly improve it within one season.

### Choosing the right grass

For rental properties where someone else is doing the mowing: NZLA Perennial Rye is the most forgiving choice. It handles varied mowing heights, recovers well from neglect, and doesn't need close cutting to look reasonable.

RSR Ryegrass (stoloniferous) is worth considering for high-foot-traffic properties — kids, dogs, frequent use. It self-repairs in a way standard ryegrass doesn't.

## End of tenancy lawns

The standard test for lawn condition at end of tenancy is whether it has been maintained to approximately the standard it was at the start. Specific factors:

**Height.** A significantly overgrown lawn at end of tenancy is a valid dispute basis. Photographs from the start of tenancy are the benchmark.

**Weeds.** A lawn that was weed-free at start and is 40% dandelion at end is a maintenance failure. A lawn that had  at start hasn't necessarily deteriorated.

**Bare patches from dogs/urine.** Normal dog-use damage is wear and tear. Severe, unaddressed damage from an animal the tenant wasn't supposed to have is a separate matter.

**Renovation costs after tenancy.** Topdressing, reseeding, and weed control to restore a lawn to its original condition can reasonably be claimed from the bond where the deterioration exceeds normal wear and tear. Document the initial state thoroughly with photos and dates.
