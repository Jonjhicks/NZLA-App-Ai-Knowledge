# Bowling Green Management

Bowling green management is a specialist discipline. It's not home lawn care scaled down — it's a different category of work with different species, different equipment, different chemistry, and a completely different mowing regime.

## NZ's Two Green Types

New Zealand has a bowling green tradition that's unique internationally. The rest of the world plays on grass-based greens (browntop, bent). NZ developed its own tradition on cotula — a prostrate native daisy grown as a uniform, mat-forming playing surface.

**Cotula greens.** The traditional NZ bowling green. Leptinella squalida and Leptinella dioica — selected cultivars developed specifically for bowling green use. Prostrate growth habit lies flat without upright stems. Dense mat, tolerates mowing at 3 to 5 mm better than most grass species. Native to NZ, evolved for our conditions. Many older clubs maintain cotula greens as part of their heritage and local playing tradition.

Cotula management is specialised beyond browntop management. The herbicide range that's safe on cotula differs significantly from grass-safe products — using the wrong chemistry destroys the green. The Bowls Chemical Guide published by the NZ Sports Turf Institute is the essential reference for anyone managing a cotula green.

**Browntop greens.** Agrostis capillaris (browntop bent) selected cultivars. More common internationally. Mown at 5 to 8 mm depending on the standard sought. Some NZ clubs have converted from cotula to browntop for consistency, easier management, or to align with international playing standards.

Both have merits. The choice is made by the club committee, not the greenkeeper, but the greenkeeper manages the consequences.

## Mowing

Daily mowing during the playing season is the standard for a maintained club green. Not every other day, not when it looks long. Daily. At 3 to 5 mm for cotula, 5 to 7 mm for browntop.

Reel mower only. A rotary mower at these heights would tear the surface rather than cut it. The bedknife gap is set to the cutting height, the cylinder speed and ground speed determine the cut quality. Dull blades show immediately on the surface — uneven finish, tearing at the leaf tips, surface discolouration over the following days.

Blade sharpening and bedknife adjustment are routine maintenance, not exceptional events. On a well-run green they happen several times a season.

## Fertility

Fine turf at 3 to 7 mm can't handle the granular loads you'd apply to a home lawn. A single heavy feed that looks fine on a home lawn can burn a bowling green.

**NZLA Mini Prill** (SGN 150, fine particle granular) is designed for dense fine turf — the small granule size falls through the canopy to the soil surface rather than sitting on leaf tissue. It's the right granular format for greens use.

**NZLA Charger** and **Iron+** are the foliar programme tools. Light frequent foliar applications (every 2 to 3 weeks during the playing season) keep the green colour and quality consistent without the flush-and-crash response you get from heavy granular feeding.

The principle is small amounts often, not large amounts occasionally.

## Wetting Agents

Hydrophobic conditions destroy surface uniformity on a bowling green. A dry patch on a bowling green doesn't just look poor — it changes the roll of the bowl and makes play inconsistent. Wetting agent management is non-negotiable.

**NZLA Wetter** and **Wetter 3W** on a regular programme through the playing season. Monthly applications through the dry months. For greens with irrigation-general systems, this integrates naturally into the irrigation-general schedule. **NZLA Wetter GR** (granular) works for clubs without irrigation-general — apply, then rely on rainfall to activate.

## PGR Programme

**NZLA PGR** (trinexapac) on a regular application schedule reduces mowing frequency slightly, improves surface density, and produces a tighter, more consistent playing surface. At club-green intensity, PGR is a programme tool rather than an occasional application. Regular low-rate applications through the season maintain consistent effect. Results from a single application wear off after 3 to 5 weeks.

## Disease Management

Fine turf managed at low mowing heights under high maintenance intensity has high  pressure. The main diseases:

- Fusarium patch — autumn through winter. NZLA Fungus Pro preventative in April and August.
- Dollar spot — summer, particularly in humid conditions. NZLA Azoxy.
- Brown patch — warm humid summer conditions. NZLA Azoxy.

Rotate NZLA Azoxy and Fungus Pro across the season to reduce resistance risk. Don't rely on one product repeatedly.

For cotula greens, check any fungicide against the Bowls Chemical Guide before applying. Some standard turf fungicides are not safe on cotula.

## Annual Renovation

End-of-season renovation rebuilds what the playing season depletes. Timing depends on the playing season and climate — typically late summer through autumn for most NZ clubs.

- Heavy scarification removes thatch and organic accumulation.
- Core aeration relieves core-aeration and improves drainage-and-soil-moisture.
- Top dressing with appropriate material (specific green-grade material, consistent with the existing root zone profile).
- Overseeding any thin or damaged areas.
- Recovery programme through autumn before the next season starts.

This is the most physically intensive period of the greenkeeper's year and the foundation for the following season's surface quality.

## NZ Resources

The NZ Sports Turf Institute (NZSTI) is the authoritative technical body for bowling green management in New Zealand. They publish the Bowls Chemical Guide, which lists what's registered and safe for cotula and browntop greens. It's updated periodically and is the essential reference for approved chemistry — don't guess on products for a cotula green.

NZSTI training programmes, field days, and publications are the professional development pathway for club greenkeepers. The NZSTI national office can also provide technical advice on specific problems.
