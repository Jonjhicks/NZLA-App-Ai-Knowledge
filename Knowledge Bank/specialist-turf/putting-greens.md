# Home Putting Greens

A home putting green is a significant commitment — in construction cost, equipment, and daily management time. It's a genuine hobby-level investment for golfers who want to practice putting year-round. Done well, it's a standout feature. Done poorly, it's an expensive source of frustration.

## Construction Options

The construction standard determines the quality of surface achievable and the difficulty of maintenance. There are three realistic options.

**USGA specification (premium):** a layered construction with 300 mm of specific USGA-specification sand-based root zone over a 100 mm gravel drainage-and-soil-moisture layer. Engineered for excellent drainage-and-soil-moisture, consistent surface firmness, and true ball roll. Professionally installed. Cost: $200 to $400+ per square metre depending on size, shaping, and drainage-and-soil-moisture infrastructure. For a 50 to 100 m² home green, expect a $15,000 to $40,000 project. The surface you get is as close to club-quality as achievable at home.

**Modified construction (mid-range):** existing soil improved with significant sand incorporation (not top dressing — actual mixing to 150 to 200 mm depth) to create a more freely draining root zone. Better than standard lawn soil for drainage-and-soil-moisture and surface firmness but not USGA standard. DIY-possible for someone prepared to put in the groundwork. Cost: $50 to $150 per square metre in materials. A functional putting surface; the roll won't match USGA-spec but it's a genuine practice surface.

**Standard soil construction (entry level):** appropriate species established on existing soil. The cheapest option and the hardest to maintain. Drainage and surface consistency are limited by the soil. Fine for occasional putting practice, less suitable for serious golf improvement.

For anyone seriously considering a home green, the modified construction is the practical midpoint — significantly better than basic but doesn't require the full USGA project budget.

## Species Selection

**Browntop (Agrostis capillaris)** in selected cultivars is the most common NZ home putting green choice. Cool-season, handles the close mowing, produces a fine dense surface, and suits the NZ climate well for most of the country.

**Creeping bent (Agrostis stolonifera)** is the premium option — the species used on tournament golf greens internationally. Requires higher management intensity than browntop, more -prone, needs more water, but produces an exceptionally fine, true putting surface. For the committed home green enthusiast, creeping bent is the goal species.

**Fine fescue blend** as a lower-input alternative for home practice greens not aiming at premium quality. Less -prone than bent, handles some shade, produces a reasonable putting surface at 8 to 10 mm cutting height. Not fine enough for tournament-quality speed but functional for home practice.

## Daily Management

A home putting green during the playing season requires 30 to 60 minutes of daily attention. Less than that and quality degrades quickly.

**Mowing:** daily in season. Pedestrian greens mower (reel-type, cylinder mower specifically for greens use) at 5 to 8 mm for a home green, or 3 to 5 mm for maximum speed. A standard rotary mower will not work at these heights — a greens reel mower is essential equipment. This is a $1,000 to $3,000+ equipment investment for a new quality pedestrian greens mower.

**Rolling:** a light rolling 2 to 3 times per week firms the surface and improves trueness of roll. A 30 to 50 kg roller is adequate for a small home green.

**Fertility:** NZLA Mini Prill granular for occasional base nutrition — the small granule size drops through dense canopy to soil level. NZLA Charger and Iron+ for the foliar programme: light applications every 2 weeks during the season. Don't push heavy nitrogen — a soft lush surface gives slow, inconsistent roll.

**PGR:** NZLA PGR (trinexapac) on a regular programme improves surface density and reduces mowing frequency slightly. For a home green managed at this level, PGR is worth including.

**Wetting agents:** NZLA Wetter or Wetter 3W as a standard summer programme. Dry patch on a putting green produces inconsistent ball speed. Monthly application prevents it.

**Disease management:** fine turf at close mowing heights has elevated  pressure. Fusarium patch in autumn-winter, dollar spot in summer. NZLA Azoxy and Fungus Pro on rotation through the season. Don't skip  management on a premium home green — one fusarium outbreak can damage a large section of the surface.

## Is It Worth It?

For a golfer who plays regularly, practices consistently, and has the time and budget: yes. The improvement in short putting practice from daily green access is real. The construction and maintenance investment is substantial but so is the return for a committed golfer.

For someone with casual interest who wants a nice lawn feature: a standard home lawn with some ornamental design is much lower cost and maintenance. An under-maintained putting green looks worse than a good home lawn.

The honest question is whether you'll genuinely maintain it daily through the season. If the answer is uncertain, start with modified construction and a smaller area. A 30 m² well-maintained green is better than a 100 m² declining one.
