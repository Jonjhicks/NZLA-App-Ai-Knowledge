# What's the grass grub lifecycle?

Grass grub (Costelytra giveni, used to be C. zealandica) has a one-year cycle in most of NZ.

Adult beetles emerge from the soil in spring, usually October into November depending on region. They fly at dusk on warm calm evenings, mate, then the females burrow back into the soil to lay eggs. They don't feed much as adults, the damage is all from the larvae.

Eggs hatch a few weeks later. Tiny first-instar larvae start feeding on grass roots in late spring and early summer. They moult through three instars as they grow, getting bigger and feeding more heavily. By autumn they're at full size (15 to 20 mm), curl-shaped, white body with a tan head, and that's when the damage to the lawn is most visible.

Through winter, the larvae are in their third instar, still feeding but slower. They pupate in late winter to early spring, then emerge as adults the following spring to start the cycle again.

The best window for control is when larvae are small in late summer and early autumn. Once they're full-size grubs in late autumn through winter, treatment still helps but the root damage is already done.
