# Black Beetle

African black beetle (Heteronychus arator) is a significant turf pest in the upper North Island — Auckland and north particularly — though it can appear further south in warm seasons. The adult is a shiny black beetle, 12 to 15 mm long, hard-shelled, oval. It flies on warm evenings from late spring through summer. The adults aren't the damage problem.

## The damage comes from the larvae

After mating, females burrow back into the soil and lay eggs. Larvae hatch a few weeks later, grow through three stages (instars), and feed on grass roots for several months. They're C-shaped white grubs with a tan head capsule and three pairs of legs near the head. Full-size grubs reach 15 to 20 mm and look similar to grass grub larvae, though black beetle grubs tend to be slightly slimmer.

The feeding severs grass roots from below. The lawn yellows in irregular patches, and the turf in affected areas will lift away from the soil in your hand — there's nothing holding it down because the root system is gone. Birds (blackbirds, magpies, starlings) working a patch intensively are often the first visible sign. They're digging for grubs.

## One-year lifecycle

Adults emerge from soil in late spring (October to November). They fly, mate, and females return to the soil to lay. Eggs hatch into larvae from late spring. The larvae feed through spring and summer, peak damage shows up from late summer into autumn as the grubs reach full size. They overwinter as third-instar grubs, pupate in late winter, and emerge as adults the following spring.

## How to confirm

Dig a 30 cm square section of turf in an affected area and count what you find in the top 100 mm of soil. More than roughly 15 grubs per 0.1 m² (a square about the size of a dinner plate) is a meaningful population worth treating.

## Timing treatment correctly

The adults flying in late spring and early summer are the signal. Eggs are going in the ground. Target the larvae 6 to 8 weeks after peak adult flight — in most of the upper North Island that's late January through March. At this point the larvae are small, feeding near the surface, and much easier to kill than full-grown grubs.

Apply NZLA Grub+ (chlorantraniliprole 200 g/L) to a well-watered lawn and irrigate at least 6 mm straight after. The active needs to move through the thatch into the top of the soil where the grubs are. Application sitting dry on the surface won't reach them.

Maximum 1 application per year — label restriction. One well-timed application gives up to 6 months control.

Waiting until autumn when damage is obvious means the root system has already been chewed and recovery is slower. Early timing is the advantage.

## Adults aren't worth targeting

Squashing the beetles you see at night doesn't make a meaningful dent in the larvae that came from eggs already in the ground. Focus on larval timing, not adult count.

## Cultural side

Healthy, dense turf with good root depth tolerates some grub pressure better than a thin, stressed lawn. Good fertility through spring and summer (All Seasons programme), adequate irrigation-general, and correct mowing height all reduce susceptibility. But in a high-pressure year in the upper North Island, a Grub+ application at the right time is the reliable intervention.
