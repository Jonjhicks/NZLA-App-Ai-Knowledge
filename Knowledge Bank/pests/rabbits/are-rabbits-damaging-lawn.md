# Are rabbits damaging my lawn?

If you see rabbits regularly on the property, yes. They graze grass at night and early morning, can strip a small lawn quickly when populations are high.

Signs of rabbit damage:
- Closely cropped patches that look like they've been mown to ground level.
- Damage appears at the edges of the lawn near hedges, shrubs, fence lines (where rabbits enter).
- Rabbit droppings (small dark pellets in clusters) on or near the affected areas.
- Scratched soil where rabbits have dug shallow scrapes.
- New seedlings disappearing overnight.

Where rabbits matter:
- Lifestyle blocks and rural lawns adjacent to paddocks or scrub.
- Suburban lawns near reserves, parks, undeveloped land.
- New lawn establishment in rabbit territory.

What rabbits prefer:
- Young soft growth (new seedlings, freshly fertilised lawns).
- Clover and broadleaves in the lawn.
- Edges and corners where they can quickly retreat to cover.

NZ context:
- European rabbits (Oryctolagus cuniculus) are widespread pests in NZ, particularly Otago, Mackenzie, Canterbury.
- Populations vary year to year with disease (rabbit calicivirus) and management.
- Less of an issue in dense urban areas, more relevant on lifestyle blocks and rural-fringe lawns.

For typical NZ home lawn rabbit damage:
- No NZLA product for rabbit control - this is animal management territory.
- Physical exclusion (fencing) is the reliable control.
- Cultural management (removing cover near the lawn) reduces visits.
- Shooting and trapping for landowner-managed situations.

If you're not seeing rabbits but you're seeing rabbit-like damage, check for possums (also nocturnal grazers), pukeko in rural areas (graze grass), and pukeko-related birds. The damage pattern can look similar but the management is different.

For lifestyle block owners dealing with serious rabbit pressure, contact your regional council pest team or a rural pest control contractor. Rabbit calicivirus releases have been used to reduce populations in some regions.
