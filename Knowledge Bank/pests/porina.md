# Porina

Porina (Wiseana spp.) is a native NZ moth whose caterpillars cause serious turf damage through autumn and winter. It's a different animal to grass grub but the two are often confused. Knowing which you've got matters because timing of treatment differs.

## Porina vs grass grub

Porina larvae are caterpillars — long, segmented, grey-brown, up to 50 to 60 mm at maturity. They live in vertical burrows in the soil and come up at night to chew grass at the burrow entrance. The diagnostic sign is the small round holes (5 to 10 mm) visible at the soil surface. Damaged areas thin and bare off, with scattered burrow holes across the patches.

Grass grub larvae are C-shaped white beetles grubs with a distinct tan head capsule and three pairs of legs. They stay underground and sever roots. The turf in affected areas lifts easily because the root system is gone. No surface holes.

If you see surface holes and thin grass: porina. If the turf lifts like carpet and there are no holes: grass grub. Both can be present on the same lawn.

## Lifecycle

Adult moths emerge in spring and early summer, fly, mate, and lay eggs on the lawn surface. Eggs hatch into tiny caterpillars from summer onwards. Early larvae feed near the surface. As they grow, they excavate vertical burrows and feed at the entrance at night. Damage is most visible autumn through winter as the caterpillars reach peak size. By late winter they're up to 60 mm and deep in burrows. They pupate in spring and the cycle repeats.

Heaviest populations are typically in the South Island, Manawatu, Wairarapa, lower Waikato, and Taranaki. But porina pressure shows up across the country.

## Confirming the diagnosis

Part the grass in a damaged area early morning when it's damp. Look for burrow holes. To see the caterpillars themselves, pour a bucket of soapy water over the area — porina come to the surface quickly. The caterpillar shape immediately distinguishes them from grass grubs.

## Treatment

NZLA Grub+ (chlorantraniliprole 200 g/L). On label for porina. Up to 6 months control. Apply late September to early October and again in March.

Treat in autumn, March through May, when caterpillars are small and feeding near the surface. This is the critical window. Once they're full-grown and burrowing deep in late winter, surface-applied chemistry has a much harder time reaching them.

Application method for porina is slightly different from grass grub. Apply in late afternoon or evening so the active is present when caterpillars emerge at night. Then water in lightly. The active needs to be on the leaf surface and around the burrow entrances where the caterpillars feed. A heavy irrigation-general immediately after can wash it too deep. Light irrigation-general only.

If you've had porina before, treat as a preventative each autumn rather than waiting to see the damage again. The moths come back.

## Recovery

After porina damage, push recovery with NZLA Liquid Starter and oversow bare patches with NZLA Perennial Rye once the treatment is in place. Porina-damaged areas often have underlying core-aeration or poor drainage-and-soil-moisture that made them attractive. Address those conditions alongside the chemical treatment for longer-term results.
