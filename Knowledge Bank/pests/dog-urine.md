# Dog Urine Damage

Dog urine damage is one of the most common lawn complaints. The cause isn't toxins — it's nitrogen. Dog urine is concentrated nitrogen, and what happens to the lawn is exactly what happens when you apply too much nitrogen fertiliser in one spot.

More detail on this on the NZLA blog: newzealandlawnaddicts.com/lawn-tips/dog-urine-and-your-lawn/

## What it looks like

The classic pattern: a yellow or brown dead patch in the centre with a ring of darker green around the edge. The outer ring is getting a lower, stimulating dose of nitrogen that's spreading out from the spot. The centre got enough to burn.

Smaller dogs and lighter urinators sometimes cause the opposite — small dark green patches with no dead centre. This tells you the nitrogen isn't concentrated enough to burn, but it's enough to fertilise. If your lawn shows this pattern, the patches are a sign the lawn is running low on nitrogen overall. Consistent feeding with NZLA All Seasons evens the colour out.

## What actually causes the damage

Concentrated nitrogen in one spot. It's the same mechanism as spilling granular fertiliser — sodium and nitrogen salts in high concentration draw water out of the plant tissue and cause the cells to die. Females and squatting males cause the most damage because they deposit a full volume in one location. Dogs that walk and mark cause less because the volume is spread across multiple spots.

Acid or alkaline urine being the cause is a common theory but not accurate. Changing a dog's diet to alter urine pH doesn't reliably fix lawn damage for this reason.

## Immediate response

**Dilute it fast.** Water the spot heavily immediately after the dog urinates. The goal is to flush the nitrogen concentration down below the burn threshold before it has time to damage the tissue. 10 to 20 seconds with a hose over the spot is enough. This works well if you're watching and can respond within a few minutes. Impractical overnight.

## What doesn't work

Supplements added to the dog's water bowl or food claiming to neutralise urine pH — an NZLA Facebook group poll of 200+ lawn owners showed 97% reported no improvement. These products don't address the actual cause (nitrogen concentration).

## Longer-term management

**Train the dog to use a specific area.** The most effective long-term solution. Dogs can be reliably trained to use a section of lawn, a garden bed, gravel, or an artificial grass strip. It takes a few weeks of consistent training but is genuinely the fix.

**Fertiliser programme adjustment.** High-nitrogen programmes (particularly products with fast-release nitrogen or very high N rates) increase burn risk by raising the background nitrogen level in the soil. Switching to NZLA All Seasons with its slow-release PCSCU technology reduces the total nitrogen load in the soil at any given time. If the lawn is already high in N and the dog is adding more, the soil is closer to burn threshold across the whole area.

**Accepting some damage.** On a lawn with large active dogs, a degree of urine damage is realistic and the trade-off between lawn appearance and a happy dog is a genuine one. Focus on keeping the rest of the lawn performing well so the patches stand out less.

## Repairing damaged patches

Once the yellow-brown patch is there, the grass in the centre is dead and won't recover. Reseed once you're confident the nitrogen has diluted sufficiently — usually 2 to 3 weeks after the spot appeared, with regular watering over that period.

Water the spot well 3 to 4 times before reseeding to flush remaining nitrogen down through the profile.

Reseed with matching species at 30 to 40 g per m² and keep moist until established. NZLA Perennial Rye or the species matching the existing lawn. On areas with heavy, repeated dog traffic, NZLA RSR Ryegrass patches in better due to its stoloniferous spread.
