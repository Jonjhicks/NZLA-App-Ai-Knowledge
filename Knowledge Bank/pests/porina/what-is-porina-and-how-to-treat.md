# What is porina and how do I treat it?

Porina (Wiseana spp.) is a native NZ moth whose larvae cause significant turf damage. Unlike grass grubs (beetle larvae), porina larvae are caterpillars — long, segmented, brown or grey, growing up to 60 mm at maturity.

Lifecycle:

Adult moths emerge in late spring and summer, fly, mate, lay eggs on the lawn surface. Eggs hatch into small caterpillars from summer onwards. Larvae feed at the surface initially, then build vertical burrows into the soil and feed on grass at the burrow entrance, mainly at night. Damage builds through autumn and into winter. Pupation in spring.

Damage shows up as patches of dead or thin grass, often with visible holes (the burrows) about 5 to 8 mm wide. Bare patches with a scattered cluster of small round holes is the diagnostic sign. Heaviest damage is autumn through winter.

How to confirm: lift turf in an affected area and look for the caterpillars and their burrows. Larger porina can be 40 to 60 mm. Often easier to confirm by pouring soapy water over the affected area — porina come to the surface quickly.

Treatment: NZLA Grub+ (chlorantraniliprole 200 g/L). On label for porina and other surface-feeding caterpillars. Up to 6 months control. Maximum 1 application per year.

Apply when damage first appears, ideally autumn. For surface-feeding pests like porina, delay watering and mowing 24 hours after application so the chemical stays on the leaf and burrow entrances where the caterpillars feed.

Cultural side: heavy porina pressure often indicates an underlying issue (compaction, dry soil, thatch). Address the conditions alongside the treatment.
