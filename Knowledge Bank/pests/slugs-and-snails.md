# Slugs and Snails on Lawns

Slugs and snails don't damage established lawns. Mature grass is too tough for them to bother with. The issue is on new seedings where soft germinating seedlings are exactly what they prefer to eat.

## Where it matters

New lawn establishment — particularly autumn seedings (March to May) and spring seedings (September to November) when cool damp conditions favour high slug and snail activity.

Patch repairs and overseeded areas during the first two to three weeks after sowing.

Signs of slug damage on a new seeding: seedlings germinate normally then disappear overnight. Silvery slime trails across the soil surface in early morning. Bare irregular patches where seedlings have been eaten down to the soil level. If you see germination happening but seedlings vanishing, slugs are the usual culprit.

## Conditions that make it worse

Cool, damp autumn or spring weather. Shaded or poorly ventilated areas. Heavy organic top dressing laid over the seedbed gives slugs daytime shelter. Garden beds, shrubs, and damp ground covers within a couple of metres of the new lawn — those are the source populations.

Summer establishment (most of NZ) is lower risk. Hot dry conditions keep slug activity down and seedlings establish fast enough that the vulnerable window is short.

## NZLA products

**NZLA Tea Saponin** (liquid, 200 to 400 mL per 100m²) — camellia tea seed saponins irritate molluscs and reduce feeding activity on seedlings. Apply from day five to seven after sowing and repeat through the first three to four weeks of establishment as needed.

**NZLA Worm Pellets** (granular, 2 to 4 kg per 100m²) — same active chemistry in granular form. Slower release, slightly more persistent protection.

For typical home lawn establishment, one or two Tea Saponin applications through the vulnerable window handles most slug pressure. Severe infestations are uncommon.

## Additional measures

Clear leaf litter and garden debris from within two metres of the new seeding area. Trim back damp shrubs that harbour slug populations nearby. Don't apply heavy mulch over the seedbed during establishment.

Iron-based slug baits (ferric phosphate) are an effective complementary tool, particularly if surrounding garden beds have high snail populations, and are safe around pets and children.

## Once established

Once seedlings are four to six weeks old and through several mows, slug damage stops being an issue. The grass is mature enough to shrug off feeding. From that point, slugs and snails are part of the garden ecology and not a lawn management concern.
