# Are slugs and snails a problem on lawns?

Not on established lawns, no. Slugs and snails don't eat mature grass leaves at meaningful levels. The issue is on new seedings where the soft germinating seedlings are exactly what they prefer to eat.

Where slugs and snails matter:
- New lawn establishment, particularly in cool damp conditions where slug activity is high.
- Patch repair situations with young seedlings.
- Overseeded areas during the first 2 to 3 weeks.

How to spot the damage:
- Seedlings appear to germinate normally but then disappear overnight.
- Silvery slime trails visible across the soil surface in early morning.
- Bare patches in a new seeding where seedlings have been eaten down to soil level.
- Damage is patchy, not uniform across the seeding.

Conditions slugs and snails like:
- Cool damp weather (autumn and spring establishment).
- Shaded or damp areas.
- Heavy mulch or organic top dressing that gives them daytime shelter.
- Garden beds and shrubs near the lawn that harbour populations.

NZLA range:
- NZLA Tea Saponin (liquid) lists slugs and snails as secondary targets alongside its main use as a worm cast deterrent. The saponin chemistry irritates molluscs and reduces feeding activity.
- NZLA Worm Pellets (granular) - same active, same secondary effect on slugs and snails.

For lawn establishment situations, applying Tea Saponin or Worm Pellets in the few weeks after sowing reduces slug and snail damage to seedlings.

Other approaches:
- Iron-based slug baits (commercial slug pellets, ferric phosphate-based, are pet-safe and effective).
- Reduce slug habitat - remove debris, trim back damp shrubs around the new seeding.
- Time sowings to avoid peak slug seasons where possible.

For established lawns, no action needed. Slugs and snails are part of the wider garden ecology and don't damage mature turf at meaningful levels.
