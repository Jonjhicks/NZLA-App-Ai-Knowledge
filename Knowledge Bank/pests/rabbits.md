# Rabbits and Lawn Damage

Rabbits graze grass at night and early morning. High populations strip a lawn fast. This is primarily a rural and lifestyle block problem — dense urban lawns rarely see serious rabbit pressure, but any property near reserves, scrub, or undeveloped land can get hit.

## Identifying rabbit damage

Closely cropped patches that look mown to ground level, typically starting at the edges near hedges, fence lines, or shrubs where rabbits enter from. Rabbit droppings (small dark pellets in clusters) near the affected areas. Shallow soil scrapes. New seedlings disappearing overnight.

Rabbits prefer soft new growth — freshly fertilised lawn, clover and broadleaves, young seedlings during establishment. An established mature lawn tolerates moderate rabbit grazing better than a new seeding does.

## NZ context

European rabbits (Oryctolagus cuniculus) are a declared pest, most severe in Otago, Mackenzie, and Canterbury. They're less common in the upper North Island and denser urban areas. Populations fluctuate with  (rabbit haemorrhagic  virus, RHDV) and management.

If you're on a lifestyle block adjacent to farmed land or reserve in the South Island, rabbits are a real management issue. In suburban Auckland, less so.

## Exclusion is the reliable answer

Physical exclusion keeps rabbits out. Everything else reduces pressure but won't fully solve it.

Rabbit-proof fencing: 30 mm wire mesh, 900 mm above ground, 300 mm buried and bent outward at the base (rabbits dig). Permanent fencing for serious ongoing pressure. Expensive for larger areas but effective.

For new lawn establishment, temporary rabbit netting staked around the seeded area protects the vulnerable first 4 to 6 weeks. Once the lawn is mature and well-established, rabbits cause less damage and the netting can come down.

Clearing hedges, long grass, and  from fence lines removes daytime cover and makes the area less attractive. Rabbits prefer access to retreat quickly if threatened.

## Other approaches

Commercial repellent sprays (capsaicin-based, ammonia-based, blood meal) give short-term protection but need regular reapplication, especially after rain. Reliable as a short-term measure during establishment rather than as a permanent solution.

Cage traps and shooting are standard practice for rural property owners. Live cage traps must be checked regularly and dispatched correctly. Regional councils sometimes offer recreation shooting permits and rabbit control coordination in high-pressure areas.

Rabbit haemorrhagic  virus (RHDV) releases are managed at the regional level and have reduced populations in some areas. Not a property-level decision but relevant background if your regional council is active on this.
