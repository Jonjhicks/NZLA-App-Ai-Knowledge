# Are spittlebugs harmful to my lawn?

Not meaningfully. Spittlebugs feed on plant sap from grass stems but the damage per insect is small and most lawns tolerate them without visible decline.

Why the damage is limited:
- Each spittlebug nymph is small and feeds slowly.
- The protective foam restricts how many can occupy any one stem.
- The nymph stage is relatively short (a few weeks).
- The adults disperse and feed less intensively than the nymphs.

When it might matter:
- Heavy concentrated infestations on stressed turf can add up.
- Some specific susceptible grass species (kikuyu and certain warm-season grasses) can show yellowing under heavy spittlebug pressure.
- New lawns with limited tiller density are more vulnerable than established mature lawns.

Visual impact:
- The foam itself is the main complaint from customers, not actual damage.
- Once you knock the foam off (hose, rake, mow), the lawn looks normal underneath.
- The lawn doesn't yellow or thin from typical spittlebug presence.

What spittlebugs are NOT causing:
- Dead patches in the lawn (look for other causes - disease, dog urine, grub damage).
- Yellow patches (more likely fertility, disease, or other pest).
- Lawn thinning (usually fertility, mowing, or other underlying issue).

Predators that keep spittlebug populations in check naturally:
- Birds (particularly fantails and silvereyes).
- Spiders.
- Wasps.
- General predatory insects.

For typical NZ home lawn situations:
- Spittlebugs are cosmetic only.
- The infestation resolves on its own as the nymphs mature.
- Active control isn't usually justified.

If you're concerned about a heavy infestation:
- Hose the foam off to expose the nymphs.
- Mow the lawn (reduces tall stems they prefer).
- Address any underlying lawn stress that might be making the lawn more attractive to them.

NZLA range doesn't have a specific spittlebug product. Spending money on chemistry for spittlebugs isn't a good investment for the typical home lawn.
