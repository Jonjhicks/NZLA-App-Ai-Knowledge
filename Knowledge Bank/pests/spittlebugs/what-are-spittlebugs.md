# What are the foamy spit patches on my lawn?

Spittlebugs, also called froghoppers. Small sap-feeding insects (nymphs of various Cercopidae species) that produce a foamy mass around themselves on plant stems for protection from predators and dehydration.

What they look like:
- White foamy mass on grass stems, looks like spit.
- Inside the foam, a small green-yellow nymph 2 to 5 mm long.
- Often on taller grass and weed stems, less obvious on closely mown lawn.
- Adult froghoppers are small (5 to 7 mm), greenish or brown, jumping insects that leap when disturbed.

When you see them:
- Spring through early summer (October to December peak in NZ).
- More common on lawns that have grown taller or in lawns that include some grass weeds (yarrow, plantain stems).
- Often in shaded damp areas first.

What damage do they cause:
- Cosmetic mostly. The spit looks unpleasant but the actual feeding damage is minimal on grass.
- Heavy infestations on susceptible species can stress the plant a little.
- Not a real threat to lawn health in most situations.

NZ relevance:
- Common but rarely problematic. Most NZ lawn owners see spittle masses occasionally and ignore them.
- More noticeable on lifestyle blocks where some pasture grasses are mixed in.
- Adults can be seen jumping out of the grass when walked through.

Control:
- For typical home lawn use, no chemistry needed. The infestation passes within weeks as the nymphs mature.
- Hosing off the foam with water removes the protection and exposes the nymphs to predation and drying.
- Mowing regularly (weekly through their active season) reduces habitat.
- NZLA range doesn't have a specific spittlebug control product. Standard NZLA Grub+ may have some effect on contact but it's not the registered target list, and treating for spittlebugs alone isn't usually worth the cost.

For most NZ home lawns, spittlebugs are a cosmetic curiosity rather than a problem. Knock the spit off if it bothers you, leave it if it doesn't.
