# Grass Grubs

Grass grub (Costelytra giveni) is the most widespread turf pest in NZ and the main target for NZLA Grub+. It's a native beetle whose larvae feed on grass roots, and in a bad year it can destroy a lawn from the ground up before any visible damage appears at the surface.

## Lifecycle

Adults are small oval beetles (about 10 to 12 mm, reddish-brown) that fly at dusk on warm calm evenings in spring — October and November in most regions. They mate, then females burrow back into the soil to lay eggs. The adults don't feed much, the damage is all from the larvae.

Eggs hatch a few weeks later. First-instar larvae start feeding on grass roots from late spring. They grow through three instars over summer and autumn, getting progressively bigger and feeding harder. By autumn they're at full size — 15 to 20 mm, C-shaped, white-bodied with a tan head capsule and three pairs of small legs. That's when the root damage is at its worst and becomes visible above ground.

Through winter the grubs slow down. They pupate in late winter and emerge as adults the following spring to start the cycle again. One generation per year.

## How to identify grub damage

Three things to look for:

Irregular yellowing or browning patches appearing in late summer and autumn. Not following any pattern, showing up wherever grub populations are densest.

Turf that lifts like a carpet. The grubs sever the roots, so the grass loses its anchor. If you can grab a handful and pull it up easily, that's grub damage. Grab a section that looks healthy and pull on it too — if that also lifts without tearing, you've got a problem across the lawn, not just in the visible yellow patches.

Bird activity. Blackbirds, magpies, and starlings tearing up a lawn are not the problem — they're showing you where the grubs are. Persistent bird attention in the same areas, especially in autumn, is a reliable indicator.

To confirm, cut a spade square about 300 mm across in a damaged area and dig down 100 mm. Count the C-shaped white grubs. More than roughly 15 per 0.1 m² warrants treatment.

## Timing

NZLA recommends two applications per year, six months apart.

**First application: late September to early October.** This targets the soil before egg hatch and catches early first-instar larvae as they emerge. The active has residual soil activity, so applying ahead of hatch puts the product in place when the new larvae start feeding. This is the preventative approach — it stops damage before it starts.

Apply NZLA Grub+ (chlorantraniliprole 200 g/L) to a well-watered lawn and irrigate at least 6 mm immediately after. The active needs to move through the thatch into the top of the soil. Dry application sitting on the surface won't reach the grubs.

**Second application: March.** Six months after the spring treatment. Catches any remaining larvae and gives protection through the autumn period when larger grubs are at their most damaging.

Waiting until turf is obviously lifting in autumn to make your first application means the root damage is already done. The spring application is where you get ahead of the problem.

## Possums and birds as indicators

Possums digging up a lawn in autumn are almost always after grass grubs. They've learned that a suburban lawn in autumn is a reliable food source. Magpies, starlings, and blackbirds work the same areas for the same reason.

Fix the grub problem with a Grub+ programme timed correctly, and the possum and bird damage mostly resolves. Repairing the turf before the grub issue is sorted means doing the same repair again next autumn.

## Natural predators

Birds get some grubs. Hedgehogs eat them too. Neither will clear a meaningful infestation. Nematode-based biological controls exist in pasture research but aren't commercially viable for home lawn use in NZ.

## Prevention

If you've had grub problems before, treat as part of an annual programme. The adult beetles keep coming back — one season of treatment doesn't end the cycle. September/October and March each year keeps you ahead of them.

Dense, well-fertilised turf with good root depth handles some grub pressure better than a thin, stressed lawn. Healthy turf grows through light infestations that would destroy a neglected one. But in a high-pressure season, a Grub+ application is the reliable intervention regardless of lawn health.
