# Are sod webworms a problem in NZ?

Yes, in some regions and seasons. Less of a headline problem than grass grub or porina, but worth knowing.

Sod webworms are the larvae of small brown moths (Crambidae family). The larvae are about 20 to 25 mm at maturity, grey-brown with small spots, and live in silk-lined burrows or tunnels at the soil surface. They feed at night, chewing grass blades off near the base.

Damage shows up as small irregular brown patches starting at a few centimetres across and joining up into larger areas. Often confused with drought damage or fungal disease at first. The diagnostic giveaway is the silk-lined tunnel or the grass blade tips clearly cut off (rather than the wilted look of drought stress).

Heaviest pressure is late summer through autumn, particularly in the upper half of the North Island. The conditions that suit them are warm dry weather with stressed turf — drought-stressed lawns are particularly vulnerable.

How to confirm: morning soapy water flush. Mix dish soap with water in a watering can and pour over a suspected damaged area. Webworms (and other surface caterpillars) come up to the surface within minutes.

Treatment: NZLA Grub+ (chlorantraniliprole 200 g/L) is on label for surface-feeding caterpillars including sod webworm. Up to 6 months control. Maximum 1 application per year.

For sod webworms specifically (surface feeders), delay watering and mowing 24 hours after application so the active stays on the leaf where the caterpillars feed.

Cultural side: drought-stressed lawns are more vulnerable. Irrigation and balanced fertility reduce susceptibility.
