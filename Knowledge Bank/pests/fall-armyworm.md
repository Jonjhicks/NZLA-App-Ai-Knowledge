# Fall Armyworm

Fall armyworm (Spodoptera frugiperda) is a recent arrival in NZ. First detected in the Tauranga area in 2022, it's now established in the upper North Island and spreading. The damage is from caterpillars feeding on grass at night. A heavy infestation can strip a lawn rapidly — the name comes from how the caterpillars advance like an army through a field.

## What it looks like

Caterpillars are 30 to 40 mm when mature, brown-grey with darker stripes along the back and a distinctive inverted Y marking on the head. They feed on leaf blades at night and hide in the thatch and soil during the day.

Damage looks like the lawn has been aggressively and unevenly shaved — ragged leaf edges, patches of shortened grass, and in heavy cases large areas stripped down to the crown. The patches spread noticeably day to day when a population is active.

The adult moths fly at dusk and are unremarkable brown moths. Pheromone traps (fall armyworm specific) are available and will detect adults ahead of larval damage.

## Wide host range

Ryegrass, fescue, kikuyu, couch, paspalum, maize, sweetcorn. Pretty much anything that looks like a grass. Lush warm-season turf in summer is particularly vulnerable.

## Seasonality

Active during warm months only. This is a subtropical pest and NZ temperatures below about 10°C stop development.

Upper North Island (Auckland, Bay of Plenty, Northland, Waikato): November through April is the main activity window, with peak damage typically December to March.

Lower North Island and South Island: still establishing as of 2025. January through March if conditions allow. Cold winters limit overwintering, so populations in cooler regions may need annual re-establishment from northern sources.

Following an adult moth flight, allow 2 to 3 weeks for newly laid eggs to hatch and caterpillars to reach a treatable size.

## Speed matters

Fall armyworm populations can build and do significant damage within a few days. Monitoring through the active season is worth the effort. Check the lawn weekly during November to April and look for chewed leaf edges and advancing bare patches. If you've had armyworm before, set a pheromone trap through summer — trap catches tell you when the next generation is arriving.

## Treatment

Check the current NZLA Grub+ label for fall armyworm specifically before treating. The active (chlorantraniliprole) covers surface-feeding caterpillars, and fall armyworm is likely controlled by it, but confirm current label registration before applying for this target.

Apply in late afternoon or evening when caterpillars are coming up to feed. Water in lightly after — enough to move the active into the thatch, not a heavy irrigation-general that washes it down past the zone where caterpillars are feeding.

If a second wave follows 7 to 10 days after the first application, a follow-up treatment may be needed. Monitor the lawn through the active period.

## Reporting

This is still a relatively new biosecurity pest in NZ. If you're seeing damage consistent with fall armyworm for the first time in your area, collect a caterpillar sample and report it to MPI (0800 80 99 66). Photos and location help track spread.

## Lawn recovery

Once the active infestation is controlled, push recovery with NZLA Liquid Boost. Reseed bare patches with NZLA Perennial Rye once the pest threat has passed for the season.
