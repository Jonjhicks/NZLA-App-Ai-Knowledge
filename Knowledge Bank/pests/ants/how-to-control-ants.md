# How do I control ants in my lawn?

Depends on what you're trying to fix.

For occasional mounds on the lawn:
- Knock them flat as you mow. The ants will re-build but each rebuild costs them energy.
- Water the mound heavily, the colony often relocates if conditions become unfavourable.
- Don't apply chemicals over the whole lawn for a few mounds, the cost-benefit isn't there.

For concentrated nest activity destroying the lawn:
- Locate the nest entrance.
- Use a granular ant bait (hardware store, supermarket pet aisle) on or near the nest.
- The workers carry the bait back to the colony, queen and all are affected.
- Multiple bait applications over weeks may be needed.

For Argentine ant or other invasive species:
- These are problem species that don't respond well to standard ant baits.
- Professional pest control is the practical option.
- Council and biosecurity agencies sometimes coordinate area-wide treatments.

For ant trails coming into the house:
- Treat the entry point with household ant chemistry.
- Find and address the food source attracting them (crumbs, pet food, spilled sweet liquids).
- Lawn treatment doesn't help unless you can find and treat the source nest.

NZLA range doesn't have a registered ant control product. The chemistry used in standard lawn insecticides (NZLA Grub+) isn't designed for ant control, applying it for that purpose is off-label and won't work effectively.

For typical home lawn ant presence, leave them alone. They're part of soil biology. Beyond visible mounds in concentrated spots, the lawn isn't being damaged.

If ants are a genuine nuisance:
- Hardware store ant baits (Amgrow, Yates, others) handle most situations.
- Boric acid-based baits are reliable and have lower environmental impact than synthetic insecticides.
- For invasive species or chronic problems, professional pest control.

Be wary of chemical sprays for general ant control across a lawn. Most affect non-target beneficials (worms, beetles, springtails) more than they help with the ants.
