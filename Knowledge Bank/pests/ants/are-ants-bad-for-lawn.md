# Are ants bad for my lawn?

Mostly cosmetic. Ants themselves don't eat grass and don't damage the lawn directly. The issues are about nests and visible activity.

Mound-building ants:
- Create small mounds of fine soil on the lawn surface as they excavate underground tunnels.
- Mounds can be flattened by mowing but they re-build.
- Concentrated nest activity in one spot can smother the grass directly above.

Trail ants:
- Don't usually damage the lawn much.
- Can be a nuisance if the trail crosses paths or comes into the house.

Honeydew-tending ants:
- Some species farm aphids on garden plants for their honeydew secretions. The lawn isn't directly affected but the ants can be aggressive defending their farmed insects.

NZ context:
- Various native and introduced ant species. Argentine ant (Linepithema humile) is a documented pest in some parts of NZ but doesn't usually damage lawns directly.
- White-footed house ant, common in urban areas, mostly a household nuisance rather than lawn issue.
- Native ants generally less of a problem than introduced species.

When to do something about ants:
- Mound activity is destroying the lawn cosmetically in concentrated patches.
- Ants are coming into the house regularly and the lawn is the source.
- A nuisance species (biting ants, Argentine ants) is establishing.

When to leave them alone:
- A few mounds across a large lawn are part of normal soil biology.
- Ants are part of the soil food web, breaking down organic matter and aerating soil.
- They prey on other insects (some pest, some beneficial).

NZLA range doesn't have a specific ant control product. For nuisance ant control, hardware stores stock various household ant baits and dusts (boric acid, fipronil, others). For Argentine ant specifically, professional pest control is the practical solution.

For typical home lawn ant presence, no action needed.
