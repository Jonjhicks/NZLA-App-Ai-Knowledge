# Spittlebugs

The white foamy patches on grass stems are spittlebugs — the nymphs of froghoppers (Cercopidae family). They produce a protective foam around themselves to avoid predators and prevent drying out. It looks unpleasant. It's mostly harmless.

## What you're looking at

Inside each foamy mass is a small soft-bodied nymph, 2 to 5 mm long, green-yellow in colour. They feed by sucking sap from the grass stem they're sheltering on. The adults are small jumping insects (5 to 7 mm, greenish-brown) that scatter from grass in front of you when you walk through. They're the froghoppers.

Peak activity is spring through early summer (October to December in NZ). More common on lawns that have been let grow taller, and on lawns that include taller-stemmed  (plantain, yarrow) among the grass. Shaded damp areas show it first.

## Actual damage is minimal

Each nymph is small and feeds slowly. The foam limits how many can occupy one stem. The nymph stage is brief — a few weeks. By midsummer the foam has mostly gone and the lawn looks normal.

Spittlebugs are not causing dead patches, yellow patches, or thinning. If you're seeing serious lawn damage alongside spittlebug foam, something else is causing it (grub damage, dog urine). The spittlebugs are coincidental.

Very heavy infestations on susceptible warm-season species can stress individual plants slightly, but this doesn't produce visible decline on a properly maintained home lawn.

## What to do

Knock the foam off with a hose. Once the protective foam is removed, the nymphs are exposed to predators and drying. Mow regularly through the active season — it reduces the taller stems they prefer and disturbs the foam. The infestation resolves on its own within weeks.

Regular mowing, consistent irrigation-general, and normal lawn fertility are all you need. Chemical treatment for spittlebugs alone isn't a good investment for a home lawn.
