# Ants in Lawns

Ants don't eat grass. The damage they cause is about the mounds and nest activity, not direct feeding on turf. For most NZ home lawns, ants are a cosmetic issue rather than a genuine problem.

## What ants actually do to a lawn

The mounds are excavation spoil. As a colony digs tunnels and chambers underground, it pushes fine soil to the surface and piles it around nest entrances. Mowing flattens the mounds but doesn't disturb the colony beneath. They rebuild.

Concentrated nest activity in one spot can smother the grass above it over time. If a large nest is established under a patch of lawn, that patch can thin and bare off. Mound-building in one dense area is more of a problem than mounds scattered across a larger lawn.

Trail ants moving across the lawn cause little damage. Honeydew-tending ants (the ones farming aphids on nearby garden plants) don't affect the lawn directly.

## Why ants choose your lawn

Sandy or loamy soil is easy to dig. Lawns are stable and rarely cultivated. Underground temperatures are consistent. Nearby garden plants provide food chains (aphids, other insects). Once a colony is established and conditions stay favourable, it'll stay put for years.

Mound flushes often follow rain — the colony re-excavates collapsed tunnels. More surface activity in spring and summer as the colony is growing; quieter in winter as they go deeper.

## NZ species

Most are a nuisance rather than a turf problem. The Argentine ant (Linepithema humile) is the most problematic introduced species in some parts of NZ. It doesn't damage lawns directly but is aggressive and difficult to control. White-footed house ants are common in urban areas and usually a household issue rather than a lawn issue. Native ant species are generally low impact.

## When to act

A few mounds scattered across a lawn don't warrant any action. Ants are part of the soil food web and preying on other insects.

Treat only when mound activity is genuinely destroying the lawn in concentrated patches, or when ants are regularly entering the house from a lawn source.

## What works

For localised mound problems, water the nest heavily. The colony often relocates when conditions become unfavourable. Repeated mowing flattening costs them energy too. For a specific nest that won't shift, granular ant bait placed at or near the entrance works better than spray. Workers carry it back and the colony is affected over time.

For Argentine ant or other invasive problem species, professional pest control is the practical answer. Home treatments don't resolve established Argentine ant colonies effectively.

## NZLA range

NZLA Grub+ isn't designed for ant control and is off-label for that use. The chemistry won't work effectively on ants and applying it for that purpose isn't the right call. For ant management, hardware store ant baits handle most situations. Applying broad-spectrum insecticide across a lawn for a few ant mounds is hard to justify given the effect on worms, beetles, and other soil life.
