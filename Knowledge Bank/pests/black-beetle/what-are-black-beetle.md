# What are black beetle and how do I treat them?

African Black Beetle (Heteronychus arator) is a major NZ turf pest. The adult is a shiny black beetle about 12 to 15 mm long. The damage comes from the larvae (white C-shaped grubs about 15 to 20 mm), which feed on grass roots in spring.

Lifecycle in NZ:

Adults emerge from soil in spring and early summer, fly, mate, lay eggs in the soil. Eggs hatch into larvae from late spring. Larvae feed on grass roots through spring and into summer (the damaging stage). They pupate in late summer and emerge as adults the following spring. One generation per year.

Damage shows up as irregular yellow or brown patches in spring through to early summer. Affected turf lifts like a carpet because the roots have been eaten. Birds (blackbirds, magpies, starlings) often work the area pulling grubs out, which adds to the visible damage.

How to confirm: lift a 30 cm square spadeful of turf in an affected area. Cream coloured C-shaped grubs about 15 to 20 mm are typical. More than about 15 per 0.1 m² (a square the size of a dinner plate) warrants treatment.

Treatment: NZLA Grub+ (chlorantraniliprole 200 g/L). On label for African Black Beetle larvae. Up to 6 months control from a single application. Maximum 1 application per year — label restriction.

Apply in spring through early summer when larvae are mid-size and actively feeding. Irrigate ~6 mm immediately after application to move the chemical into the soil where the larvae are.

Cultural side: healthy thick turf handles minor grub pressure without intervention. Only treat where damage is significant or grub counts are above threshold.
