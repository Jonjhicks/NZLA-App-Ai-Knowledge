# Crickets in Lawns

Black field cricket is the main species affecting NZ lawns. The adults are large insects — up to 25 mm — and the distinctive chirping at night is usually the first sign of population build-up. Damage shows up in summer as irregular thinning patches where the crickets have been chewing grass stems and crowns at night.

## When they become a problem

Light populations are part of the landscape. A few crickets on the lawn aren't worth treating — they're food for birds and pose no meaningful threat to a healthy lawn.

The issue is when populations spike after a warm dry spring. Hawke's Bay, Northland, Auckland, Waikato and Bay of Plenty see the worst infestations in warm years. A heavy summer population in dry conditions can thin a lawn out noticeably within weeks, particularly on warm-season turf like kikuyu that's already under heat stress.

Small holes in the soil surface (burrow entrances) combined with patchy thinning and audible chirping on warm evenings confirms the problem.

## Seasonality

Cricket activity ramps up through late spring and peaks in January and February across most of the North Island. Eggs are laid in the soil through summer, nymphs emerge later in the season, and populations decline as temperatures drop through April. South Island infestations are rare — the cooler climate limits population build-up.

## Treatment

NZLA Grub+ (chlorantraniliprole 200 g/L) controls crickets. Apply to a well-watered lawn and water in straight after.

Best timing is as populations are building — late November into December gives the active time to work before peak numbers arrive. If you've had a serious cricket infestation one summer, treat the following early summer as a preventative rather than waiting for damage to show.

NZLA recommends two applications per year, six months apart: late September to early October, then again in March.

## Cultural side

Dense, well-watered turf is less vulnerable. Crickets do more damage on thin, dry, stressed lawns. Maintaining irrigation-general through dry spells and keeping the lawn healthy with the All Seasons programme reduces the impact when cricket populations are running. Healthy thick grass grows through moderate cricket pressure; thin stressed turf doesn't.
