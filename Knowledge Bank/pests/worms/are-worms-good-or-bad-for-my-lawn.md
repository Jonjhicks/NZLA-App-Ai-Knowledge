# Are worms good or bad for my lawn?

Good for most lawns. A nuisance for a specific subset.

What worms do well: aerate the soil naturally (each worm digs and refills tunnels constantly, opening soil structure), cycle organic matter, improve drainage and water infiltration, contribute to healthy soil biology, and concentrate nutrients into plant-available forms. Lawns with healthy earthworm populations generally have better soil structure and root development than lawns without.

So for general home lawns at standard mowing heights (30 to 50 mm), worms are working with you, not against you. Leave them alone.

Where they cause problems is on fine cut turf — bowling greens, golf greens, premium short-cut home lawns at 15 mm or below. The worm casts (small piles of digested soil pushed up to the surface) become a problem at low mowing heights. The casts get smeared by the mower, damage the cut quality, and create mud patches that don't drain.

For fine cut situations where casting is a genuine surface problem, NZLA's organic options are Tea Saponin (liquid, 200 to 400 mL per 100m²) and Worm Pellets (granular, 2 to 4 kg per 100m²). Both use camellia tea seed extract (saponins) which irritates worms and drives them deeper or off the surface. Organic, low environmental risk, and reversible — the worms come back over weeks once the saponin breaks down.

For normal home lawns, the right answer is "leave the worms alone." A few casts in winter aren't a problem worth treating. Mowing height of 30+ mm hides them completely. Be glad they're there — they're doing real work on your soil.
