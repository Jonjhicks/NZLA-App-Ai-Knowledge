# What are the muddy lumps on my lawn?

Worm casts. The little soil mounds at the surface are earthworms processing organic matter and pushing it up. In a healthy soil with decent organic matter, you'll see them especially in autumn and spring when worms are active in the top layer of soil.

They're not damage. They're a sign the soil is alive and biologically active, which is what you want underneath a lawn.

The downsides are cosmetic. Casts can flatten under the mower and smear, leaving muddy patches. They make a fine lawn look untidy. On greens-height turf they're a problem because they disrupt the surface, but on a home lawn at 30 to 50 mm cutting height they're not really an issue.

If they bother you visually, a light raking with a soft rake in dry conditions breaks them up and incorporates the soil back into the surface. Avoid mowing wet casts, that's when the smear happens.

Don't try to kill the worms. They're doing useful work and there's no acceptable home-lawn worm control anyway.
