# Sod Webworms

Sod webworms are the larvae of small brown moths (Crambidae family). Less common than grass grub or porina, but they show up in the upper North Island and can cause noticeable damage in warm-season turf and stressed lawns through summer and autumn.

## What they look like

Caterpillars 20 to 25 mm at maturity, grey-brown with small dark spots. They live in fine silk-lined tunnels in the thatch at the soil surface and come out at night to feed on grass blades by chewing them off near the base.

The adult moths are small and tan-coloured, flying low over the lawn at dusk. Lots of small moths disturbed when you walk across the lawn on a summer evening is a heads-up that webworm pressure may be building.

## Identifying the damage

Small irregular brown patches starting at a few centimetres across and merging into larger areas as populations grow. The grass blades are cut off cleanly near the base — a chewed-down appearance rather than the wilted look of drought stress. Fine silk webbing visible at the soil surface if you part the grass carefully in the morning.

Sod webworm damage is sometimes confused with drought damage or fungal . The soapy water flush test resolves the confusion fast: mix dish soap in a watering can and pour over the suspected area. Webworms and other surface caterpillars come to the surface within minutes.

## Other surface-feeding caterpillars

Three species produce similar night-feeding damage:

Sod webworms: 20 to 25 mm, grey-brown with spots, in silk tunnels. Peak pressure late summer to autumn.

Fall armyworm: 30 to 40 mm, brown-grey with dark stripes and a head marking. Feeds in groups and advances quickly through a lawn. More dramatic damage. Mainly upper North Island, summer to early autumn.

Porina: 40 to 60 mm, grey-brown, in vertical burrows. Leaves visible 5 to 10 mm holes at the soil surface. Autumn through winter.

Soapy water flush tells you which you're dealing with. The size and behaviour of what comes up is usually enough to identify the species.

## Treatment

NZLA Grub+ (chlorantraniliprole 200 g/L) is on label for surface-feeding caterpillars including sod webworm. Up to 6 months control. Apply late September to early October and again in March.

For sod webworm and other surface feeders, application timing matters: apply in late afternoon or early evening when caterpillars are coming up to feed. Water in lightly after. The active needs to stay near the surface and on the leaf where the caterpillars are feeding. A heavy irrigation-general washes it too deep. Avoid mowing for 24 hours after application.

## Cultural prevention

Drought-stressed lawns are more vulnerable than well-watered healthy ones. Consistent irrigation-general through dry spells and balanced fertility (All Seasons programme) keeps the lawn dense and resilient. Sod webworm pressure on a well-maintained lawn is far less damaging than the same population on a thin, struggling turf.
