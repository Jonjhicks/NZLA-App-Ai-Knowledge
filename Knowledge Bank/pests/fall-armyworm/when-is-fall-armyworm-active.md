# When is fall armyworm active in NZ?

Warm months. Fall armyworm is a tropical and subtropical pest, so its NZ activity tracks with warm summer conditions.

Upper North Island (Auckland, Bay of Plenty, Waikato, Northland):
- November through April is the main activity window.
- Peak damage typically December to March.
- May have a second generation in autumn under favourable conditions.

Lower North Island and South Island:
- Less established as of 2025, but spreading.
- January to March if conditions allow.
- Cold winters limit overwintering survival, populations may need re-establishment from northern source each year.

Why warm months:
- Fall armyworm development is temperature-driven.
- Soil temps above 18 °C support active populations.
- Below 10 °C, development effectively stops.

Triggers within the window:
- Warm wet conditions favour multiple generations.
- Drought stress on the lawn doesn't stop the pest, the caterpillars still feed but the lawn recovers more slowly.
- Following a moth flight (adult moths active at dusk and at night), egg laying can produce a damaging caterpillar generation 2 to 3 weeks later.

Monitoring through the season:
- Check the lawn weekly during the active window for signs of new damage.
- Watch for chewed leaf edges and patches of shortened grass.
- Set a pheromone trap if you've had armyworm before - the adults entering the trap signal a population increase.

Treatment timing:
- Apply NZLA Grub+ (check current label) when caterpillars are small and feeding actively, before significant damage shows.
- Late afternoon or evening application catches them when they come up to feed.
- Wait 7 to 10 days after a moth flight to target the new generation of caterpillars.

End of season:
- Cooler autumn weather slows armyworm activity.
- May (autumn) populations are usually the last damaging generation of the season in upper NI.
- No active management needed through winter, the threat resumes in spring.

For lawns in the upper NI armyworm zone, monitoring through summer is worth the effort. Catching the pest early when populations are small saves significant lawn damage compared to responding after heavy feeding has happened.
