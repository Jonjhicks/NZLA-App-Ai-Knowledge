# Earthworms in Lawns

Earthworms are good for most lawns. They aerate soil through constant burrowing, process organic matter, improve drainage-and-soil-moisture and water infiltration, and concentrate nutrients into plant-available forms. A healthy worm population is one of the better signs that the soil below a lawn is biologically active. For the vast majority of home lawns, the right answer is to leave them alone.

The case against worms is specific: worm casts on fine-cut turf. That's a real problem in a narrow context. Not generally.

## Worm casts

Casts are small mounds of digested soil pushed up to the surface as worms work through the profile. They're more visible in autumn and spring when worms are active in the top soil layer, and more obvious in wet conditions.

On a home lawn mowed at 30 to 50 mm, casts are a minor cosmetic issue at worst. They disappear into the sward or can be knocked apart with a light rake in dry conditions. Avoid mowing wet casts — that's when they smear and create muddy patches.

On fine-cut turf (golf greens, bowling greens, premium lawns at 15 mm or below), casts are a genuine surface management problem. Smeared casts damage cut quality and create uneven wet patches that don't drain properly.

## NZLA products for cast management

**NZLA Tea Saponin** (liquid, 200 to 400 mL per 100m²) — camellia tea seed extract (saponins) irritates worms and drives them deeper in the profile or off the surface. Apply at label rate in autumn when casting is at its worst.

**NZLA Worm Pellets** (granular, 2 to 4 kg per 100m²) — same active chemistry in granular form, slower release.

These are organic products with low environmental impact. The effect is reversible — worms return over weeks once the saponin breaks down. They don't kill worms, they relocate surface-active worms deeper.

For normal home lawns, using these products to manage worm casts isn't worth it. Save Tea Saponin and Worm Pellets for seeding situations (where they also reduce slug and snail pressure on new seedlings) or fine turf situations where cast management is genuinely needed.

## Don't try to eliminate worms

A healthy worm population is an asset. There's no acceptable broad-spectrum worm control for home lawns, and treating for worms just because a few casts appear is working against the lawn's long-term health.

## Encouraging worms

Mulch clippings rather than catching them — that organic matter on the surface is food for the soil biology. Top dress every couple of years with compost or quality screened topsoil. NZLA Humic+ feeds soil microbial activity which worms are part of. NZLA Amino+ provides amino acids that the whole soil food web uses.

Keep the soil moist through dry spells. Let the lawn go bone-dry and worms retreat deep and their benefit at the surface stops.

Use Grub+ only when needed for actual pest problems. Routine preventative insecticide applications have a cost to the wider soil biology, including worms.

## Telling worms from grass grubs

Earthworms are long, slim, pink to reddish-brown, segmented along the full length, no legs, no head capsule. They stretch and wriggle when handled.

Grass grub larvae are C-shaped, white-bodied, with a hard tan head capsule and three pairs of small legs at the front. They curl tightly when picked up.

If you dig into a yellowing patch and find long pink worms but no C-shaped grubs, the worms aren't causing the damage. Look for another cause — drought stress, dog urine, or core-aeration — before treating for anything.
