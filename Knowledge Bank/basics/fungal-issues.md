# Fungal Issues in Lawns

In lawn care, the two terms are interchangeable in practice. Most lawn diseases are fungal — Fusarium, dollar spot, brown patch, leaf spot, rust, red thread, powdery mildew are all caused by fungi. When someone says "I've got a fungus" they usually mean a fungal .

## Preventing fungal problems

Fungal pressure on lawns is almost entirely about conditions. Get these right and most  doesn't appear.

**Leaf moisture duration — the top priority.**

The single biggest driver of fungal  pressure is how long the leaf stays wet. Canopy moisture is where most fungal germination and infection begins.

Knock morning dew off each day. Drag a hose, rope, or brush across the lawn. The dew is gone in minutes and the canopy dries hours earlier than it would otherwise. Dew removal is the cheapest and most effective  prevention tool there is.

Water in the morning, not evening. Evening watering extends the overnight wet window — exactly the wrong thing.

Improve  where possible. Trim hedges or branches that restrict air movement across the lawn. Air movement dries the canopy and disperses fungal spores.

Sort drainage-and-soil-moisture. NZLA Penetrate moves surface moisture down through the soil profile, drying the canopy faster after rain.

**Balanced fertility — no nitrogen spikes.**

High nitrogen drives  pressure, particularly Fusarium and brown patch. Fast-release nitrogen pushes soft vulnerable growth. Use balanced slow-release feeds (All Seasons, All Seasons) rather than fast-release nitrogen hits. Through autumn into winter, ease off nitrogen further.

Iron and potassium harden the plant. NZLA Iron+ as a foliar through the cool months. The 16% potassium in All Seasons does the same job through the granular base.

## When to use fungicide

**Preventative** makes sense when:

- Heading into known high-pressure periods (autumn-winter for Fusarium, summer for brown patch on warm-season turf).
- Structural issues (shade, drainage-and-soil-moisture, core-aeration) can't be fully resolved so cultural fix alone isn't enough.

The lawns that get hit hardest are usually the ones doing the opposite of everything above: fast-release nitrogen, evening watering, no dew removal, blunt blades, scalped mowing, poor drainage-and-soil-moisture. Fix the conditions and chemistry becomes a backup rather than a crutch.
