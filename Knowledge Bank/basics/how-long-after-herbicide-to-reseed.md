# How Long After Herbicide Before Reseeding

Reseeding into soil or turf that still has active herbicide residue is one of the most common and frustrating causes of seeding failure. The germinating seedling has almost no tolerance for herbicide contact — even product concentrations that would barely affect established grass will kill a seedling.

These intervals apply to both new lawn seeding and oversowing into existing turf.

---

## Broadleaf herbicides (Gold, BWC)

**Gold (picloram + triclopyr):** Wait a minimum of 6 weeks before reseeding. Picloram has significant soil persistence — in some soils, 8 to 10 weeks is safer, especially if applied at higher rates or on heavy, wet, or cold soils where breakdown slows.

**BWC (clopyralid + triclopyr):** 6 weeks minimum. Same reasoning as Gold regarding picloram-related chemistry.

**The test:** If you're not certain whether the interval has been long enough, sow a small patch of fast-germinating ryegrass in the treated area. If it establishes cleanly, the residual is gone. If it fails or grows abnormally, wait another 2 weeks and retest.

---

## Selective grass herbicides (Meso, Etho, Blockade, Halo)

These products target specific grass species. All have reseeding implications.

**NZLA Meso (mesotrione):** Can be applied at sowing for ryegrass and tall fescue (it suppresses  without killing these species at the seedling stage). Do NOT apply to fine fescue seedings — mesotrione damages fine fescue seedlings. Post-emergence: standard programme continues without reseeding interval needed for ryegrass/fescue. If sowing fine fescue into a previously Meso-treated area, wait 6 weeks.

**NZLA Etho (ethofumesate):** Can be applied at or just after sowing for ryegrass. Effective as a pre-emergent protectant at sowing. No significant reseeding interval for ryegrass when used correctly at label rates.

**NZLA Blockade (propyzamide):** For warm-season lawns only. Blockade has residual activity of 3 to 4 months. Do not reseed warm-season grass into a Blockade-treated area for at least 12 to 16 weeks. Reseeding cool-season grasses into Blockade-treated areas is not recommended (off-label situation).

**NZLA Halo (clethodim):** Residual activity is low. 2 to 3 weeks before reseeding with fine fescue is typically sufficient. Halo is registered for fine fescue only — the reseeding would normally be fine fescue back into fine fescue.

---

## Glyphosate (non-selective — Roundup or similar)

Glyphosate breaks down relatively quickly in soil. Standard waiting period is 7 to 14 days after application before reseeding. In cool, wet conditions (slow microbial activity), 14 days is the safer end. Glyphosate has no soil persistence issue — the concern is ensuring the sprayed material has had time to work before cultivation.

Don't cultivate earlier than 7 days after a glyphosate kill — if there's still green growth present, cultivation may move viable growing points around rather than removing them.

---

## Pre-emergent herbicides (applied to established turf)

If your lawn programme includes pre-emergents (Etho, Meso) already in the soil at the time you want to oversow:

**Etho applied to existing turf:** Safe to oversow ryegrass at the time of or shortly after application. Not safe for fine fescue oversowing.

**Meso applied to existing turf:** Safe to oversow ryegrass 3 to 4 weeks after application (once some breakdown has occurred). Not safe for fine fescue until 6-week minimum.

---

## General principles

Soil temperature and moisture affect herbicide breakdown. Warm, moist, biologically active soil breaks down most herbicides faster. Cold, dry, or waterlogged soil slows breakdown — extend all intervals by at least 2 weeks in winter or extreme dry conditions.

When in doubt, test-sow before committing a full bag of seed to a treated area.
