# Thatch

Thatch is the layer of organic material that accumulates between the soil surface and the green leaf canopy. It consists of dead and partially decomposed stems, crowns, stolons, and roots — the structural parts of the grass plant that break down slowly.

Full article on the NZLA blog: newzealandlawnaddicts.com/lawn-tips/everything-you-need-to-know-about-thatch/

## Some thatch is fine

A thin thatch layer (under 12 to 13 mm) is actually beneficial. It insulates the root zone from temperature extremes, reduces moisture evaporation, and buffers the surface against foot traffic impact.

The problem starts when it builds beyond that. Thatch over 20 to 25 mm becomes a barrier. Water sits on top rather than penetrating to the root zone. Fertiliser can't reach the soil. Roots grow up into the thatch layer rather than down into soil — creating a shallow, stress-vulnerable root system. The humid, organic environment inside dense thatch is ideal for  and pest development.

## How to measure it

Cut a small plug of turf with a spade — about 100 mm deep. Look at the cross section. The thatch layer is the brown spongy zone between the bottom of the green leaf and the top of the soil. Measure it. Under 13 mm: fine. 13 to 20 mm: monitor and manage. Over 20 mm: action needed.

## What causes it to build up

**Grass species.** Rhizomatous and stoloniferous species (creeping bentgrass, some fescues) produce significantly more organic matter at the base than bunch-type grasses. NZLA's ryegrass and tall fescue range are bunch-type — they build thatch much more slowly than traditional bentgrass or some fine fescue species.

**Fast-release fertilisers.** A surge of rapid growth deposits more organic material than the soil biology can decompose in time. This is one reason NZLA doesn't sell fast-release nitrogen — the growth pattern it creates feeds thatch accumulation. Slow-release N produces steady growth that's easier for decomposers to keep up with.

**Leaving grass clippings is not the main cause.** Clippings decompose quickly and research consistently shows they don't meaningfully increase thatch. Thatch comes from stems, crowns, and roots — not leaf blades.

**Low soil pH.** Below about 5.5, the microbial activity responsible for decomposing organic matter drops off significantly. Thatch builds because decomposition slows.

**Compaction.** Poor air and water movement through the soil reduces biological activity at the interface where decomposition happens.

## Preventing it

The biology approach is underused. NZLA Nurture (containing N-fixing bacteria, P-solubilising bacteria, and beneficial fungi) and NZLA Restore (beneficial microbes, organic acids, plant stimulants) both support the soil microbial environment that decomposes organic matter at the soil surface. Applied regularly through the growing season, they maintain the biological activity that keeps thatch from building.

Core aeration annually improves air and water movement through the soil profile — both of which support decomposition. The cores break up on the surface and provide a soil mix that introduces decomposing microbes into the thatch layer.

Appropriate pH management. If the soil is acidic, periodic lime applications bring pH to the 6.0 to 6.5 range where biology is most active.

## Removing established thatch

Once thatch exceeds 20 to 25 mm, physical removal is needed. Biological management alone won't shift a thick established layer in a reasonable timeframe.

Use a scarifier (vertical mower / dethatching machine) available from hire shops. Set the blades to penetrate the thatch layer. Run across the lawn in two passes at 45 degrees to each other. The machine pulls up the thatch material and the lawn looks terrible afterward — bare, rough, thin. That's expected. Push through it.

Only scarify in cool weather with adequate soil moisture. Scarifying in heat or drought stress compounds the damage. Autumn (March to May) is the best window — warm soil for recovery, cooling air, reduced  pressure. Spring is the second option.

Remove all the pulled-up material — don't leave it to mat back down.

After scarifiers, core aerate, top dress lightly with sandy loam, and overseed with NZLA Starter fertiliser and appropriate grass seed. The lawn needs 4 to 6 weeks to recover to a reasonable appearance.

## How often to dethatch

On standard home lawns with ryegrass or tall fescue: rarely. These species build thatch slowly and annual coring is usually enough to keep it in check. If the plug test shows 13 mm or less, no scarifiers needed.

Fine turf, older browntop lawns, and lawns with high-input programmes may need dethatching every 2 to 3 years.

The coring and biology programme is the maintenance approach. Dethatching is the rescue operation when it's been neglected.
