# What happens if I miss the pre-emergent window?

The weeds germinate and establish before your chemical barrier is in place. Pre-em can't catch them at that point — once a weed has emerged with a root system in place, prodiamine won't touch it.

You're now into post-emergent territory, which is harder for grass weeds specifically.

For broadleaf weeds (dandelion, plantain, catsear, hawkbit, clover, oxalis, etc.) you've got reasonable post-em options:

NZLA BWC for the standard broadleaf list.

NZLA Gold for tougher weeds (storksbill, twin cress, mallow, etc.).

Apply when the weeds are actively growing at early to mid stages. The smaller and younger they are, the better the kill rate.

For grass weeds (summer grass, crabgrass, paspalum, poa annua), post-em options are much more limited:

NZLA Etho for poa annua specifically in perennial ryegrass and tall fescue lawns. Two applications 14-21 days apart.

NZLA Meso for some grass weeds (browntop, creeping bentgrass) in cool-season turf, with the 2-applications-per-12-months EPA cap.

NZLA Halo for selective grass kill in fine fescue lawns only.

For summer grass and crabgrass in cool-season turf, there's no fully selective post-em option in the NZLA range. Practical options are spot glyphosate (kills surrounding lawn too — overseed the patches afterward), or accepting the season's damage and getting the pre-em right next year.

For the missed-window season:

Accept some weed pressure for this year. Mow regularly to prevent the existing weeds setting seed (reduces next year's pressure). Plan the pre-em programme for next year tightly — Auckland late August at the latest, Canterbury late September. Watch soil temperatures rather than the calendar.

For poa annua specifically: missed autumn pre-em often leaves poa establishing through winter. NZLA Etho through autumn-winter can clean a lot of it up on ryegrass and tall fescue lawns. Then get the pre-em right next autumn.

A missed window isn't a disaster — it just makes the next pre-em window more important.
