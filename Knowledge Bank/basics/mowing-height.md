# Mowing Height and Frequency

## The third rule

Never take more than a third of the leaf off in a single mow.

If the lawn is 60 mm and you want it at 40 mm, that's a 33% cut — borderline acceptable. If you want to go from 60 mm to 25 mm in one pass, the lawn gets scalped badly. The plant loses more than half its photosynthetic area and goes into severe energy debt — yellowing, growth stall susceptibility.

The third you remove is the expendable third. The remaining leaf area is enough to keep the plant fed while it recovers. Lose more than that and recovery takes weeks.

The third rule also drives mowing frequency. If you're maintaining at 40 mm, mow when the lawn reaches 60 mm. At 25 mm target, mow at 37 mm. For a fast-growing lawn in spring, that's every 3 to 5 days for a premium cut, every 5 to 7 days for a standard home lawn.

When the lawn gets ahead of you (holiday, wet spell), bring it back in stages. 80 mm lawn targeting 30 mm: cut to 55 mm, wait 3 to 5 days, cut to 40 mm, wait, then 30 mm. The step-down HOC reset pattern avoids the scalping stress.

## Seasonal height adjustment

**Ryegrass** suits different heights at different times of year.

Summer: 25 to 50 mm for standard perennial rye. Lift to 40 to 50 mm in the worst heat to shade the soil and hold moisture.

Winter: 40 to 60 mm. More photosynthetic area for low-light conditions, more carbohydrate storage for cold tolerance, and a denser canopy that shades out moss. A lawn mown short heading into winter has less stored energy and less tolerance for cold stress.

Premium dark cultivars (MDR, Dark Perennial Rye) can be held closer — 20 to 40 mm in summer, 35 to 50 mm in winter. Their finer leaf and higher tiller density tolerate closer mowing without thinning.

RSR Ryegrass sits well at 20 to 50 mm. The stoloniferous habit means the canopy fills in laterally, so it tolerates closer mowing without gapping.

Fine Fescue: 25 to 60 mm range, lift higher in shaded winter conditions.

Tall Fescue: 50 to 70 mm generally, can go shorter in summer under active management.

Kikuyu: 25 to 50 mm during the summer growing season. Don't mow dormant kikuyu in winter.

Common mistake: keeping the same height year-round. Either too low for winter cold tolerance or too high for summer presentation. Adjust seasonally.

Transition gradually. Don't drop from 50 mm winter height to 25 mm summer height in one mow. Bring it down across 3 to 4 cuts in early spring as growth fires up.

## Mowing frequency

Driven by how fast the lawn is growing, not the calendar. Mow when the lawn has grown a third above the target height.

**Summer (October to April):** cool-season grasses grow 25 to 50 mm per week in peak growth windows. Standard home lawn at 30 to 50 mm target needs mowing every 7 to 10 days. Premium short-cut lawns every 5 to 7 days. In deep January-February heat, growth slows and mowing can extend to every 10 to 14 days.

**Winter (June to August):** growth slows dramatically below 10°C. Most lawns need mowing every 2 to 4 weeks. Auckland and upper NI closer to every 2 weeks; Canterbury and southern South Island every 3 to 4 weeks. Some lawns barely need a cut in July — don't force it.

Warm-season lawns (kikuyu, couch): every 5 to 10 days in summer during active growth. Don't mow dormant warm-season turf in winter.

## Clippings — leave or catch?

**Leave them as a rule.** Grass clippings are roughly 4% nitrogen. Returning them to the soil through mulch mowing feeds the lawn the same nitrogen it used to produce the growth — a 20 to 30% reduction in fertiliser need over a season. The clippings break down within days in active growing conditions.

**Catch when:**
- Disease is active in the lawn. Collecting infected clippings removes the inoculum from the surface rather than spreading it back through the canopy.
- The grass has got away and the clipping volume would smother the lawn rather than mulch back in.
- The lawn has gone to seed (poa, browntop seed heads) — don't spread seed around.
- Mowing in wet conditions where clippings clump.

Otherwise mulch and save on fertiliser cost.
