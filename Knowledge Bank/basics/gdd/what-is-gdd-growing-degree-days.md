# What is GDD (growing degree days)?

A measure of accumulated heat over time. Above a base temperature, every day's warmth contributes to the running total.

The base temperature differs by application. For cool-season grass growth and PGR timing, base 0°C and 10°C are both used. For pre-em timing on summer annual weeds, base 13°C (around the germination threshold).

The calculation is straightforward: for each day, take (daily mean temperature) minus (base temperature). If positive, add to the running total. If below zero or below the base, count as zero. Sum across days to get GDD accumulated.

Why use it. Plant development and pest activity track temperature accumulation, not the calendar. A cold spring delays everything. A warm one fires it earlier. GDD captures that better than dates.

The standard uses in turf:

**PGR re-application timing.** Trinexapac-ethyl (NZLA PGR) typically needs re-application every 150 to 200 GDD base 0°C, depending on rate. Track GDD and reapply when you hit the target rather than guessing on calendar days.

**Pre-em timing for summer grass.** Apply before GDD base 13°C reaches the germination threshold (varies by species).

**Disease prediction models.** Some Fusarium and dollar spot models use GDD plus humidity to predict outbreak windows.

For most home users, GDD is overkill — calendar plus soil temperature works fine. For fine turf management, sports turf, or anyone running a tight PGR programme, it's the tool.

Online tools and weather apps for your region will give GDD totals if you want to track them.
