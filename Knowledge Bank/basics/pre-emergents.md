# Pre-emergent Herbicides

## How pre-em works

Pre-emergent stops weed seeds germinating. The active ingredient (in NZLA Blockade: prodiamine) sits in the top centimetre of soil as a chemical barrier. When a weed seed tries to push its first root through that zone, the chemistry kills it at the developing root tip before it ever reaches the surface.

Pre-em only works on seeds in the germination phase. It has no effect on established  — they're past the vulnerable stage. Once a weed has emerged and rooted, pre-em won't touch it.

## When to apply — spring pre-em for summer grass

Timing is the whole game. The barrier needs to be in place before target  germinate, not after.

Summer grass (crabgrass) germinates when soil temperatures at 50 mm depth hit 12°C and stay there. Apply 2 to 3 weeks ahead of that window to give the chemical barrier time to form.

Regional timing: Auckland and upper NI — late August. Central North Island — mid-September. Canterbury and southern South Island — late September into early October. These are guides; watch actual soil temperatures rather than the calendar. Some years spring arrives early, some years late.

Activate with 6 mm of rain or irrigation-general within 7 days of application. Without activation, the prodiamine doesn't bind into the soil matrix and the barrier never forms. Do not apply immediately before heavy rain forecast (over 25 mm) as the chemical can be washed off-target.

A second application 90 to 120 days later extends the barrier through the full summer grass germination window.

**NZLA Blockade rate:** 10 to 30 mL per 100m² for summer grass control.

## Autumn pre-em for poa annua

Poa annua germinates as soil temperatures fall into the 18 to 22°C range in autumn. Apply Blockade 2 to 3 weeks before that window.

Regional timing: Auckland and upper NI — mid to late March. Central North Island — late March. Canterbury and South Island — late March into early April. Use NIWA's soil temperature data for your region or a soil thermometer at 50 mm depth.

Same activation requirement — 6 mm of rain or irrigation-general within 7 days.

A second application 90 to 120 days after the first carries the barrier through the peak poa germination window into early winter.

**NZLA Blockade rate:** 10 to 40 mL per 100m² for poa annua.

## Label restriction — important

NZLA Blockade is registered for warm-season turf only in NZ (kikuyu, couch), despite the chemistry being safe on cool-season grass and widely used on cool-season internationally. Off-label use on cool-season turf cannot be officially recommended.

## Overseeding and pre-em — they don't mix

Pre-em doesn't distinguish between weed seed and grass seed. Both hit the same chemical barrier and die at the root tip.

Residual period for Blockade: 4 to 6 months under typical conditions (full strength for the first 3 months, declining through months 4 to 6).

Plan seeding-and-over-seeding to land at least 4 months after the most recent Blockade application, ideally 6 months.

**Spring Blockade application:** residual carries through summer. Normal autumn seeding-and-over-seeding (March onwards) clears the residual window fine.

**Autumn Blockade application:** residual carries through winter. Earliest seeding-and-over-seeding is mid to late spring — affects any winter or early spring seeding-and-over-seeding work.

Don't try to overseed into recently-applied Blockade at "half residual." The results are patchy at best. For urgent seeding-and-over-seeding on small patches, replacing the top 50 mm of soil bypasses the residual layer.

## What to do if you miss the window

Once the target  have germinated and established, pre-em won't touch them. You're into post-emergent territory.

**Broadleaf :** reasonable post-em options exist. NZLA BWC for standard broadleaf; Gold for tougher species. Apply when actively growing at early to mid growth stages.

**Grass  (summer grass, crabgrass):** post-em options are limited on cool-season turf. NZLA Etho targets poa annua specifically in ryegrass and tall fescue. For summer grass in cool-season lawns, the main practical option is spot glyphosate with seeding-and-over-seeding of the dead patches, or accepting the season's pressure and getting pre-em right next year.

A missed window isn't a disaster. Mow regularly to prevent  setting seed and plan the next pre-em timing carefully.
