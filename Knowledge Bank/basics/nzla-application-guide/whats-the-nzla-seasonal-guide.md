# What is the NZLA seasonal application guide?

A year-round framework that matches NZLA products to the lawn's seasonal needs. Not a rigid prescription — a starting point you adapt to your specific lawn, region and goals.

The seasonal logic:

**Spring (September to November).** Growth firing up. Lawn coming out of winter dormancy and putting on rapid new growth. The push window. Apply NZLA All Seasons N+ (or Mini Prill on dense lawns) once growth is consistent for a fast green-up. Pre-em Blockade for summer grass and crabgrass (note NZ label warm-season turf only). NZLA Starter for any overseeding or patch repair.

**Summer (December to February).** Active growing season but heat stress builds in dry inland areas. Slow-release base (All Seasons or All Seasons N+) every 6 to 8 weeks at standard rate. Watch for disease pressure (brown patch in heat-humidity). Wetter or Wetter 3W if dry patch develops. Iron+ for colour without forcing growth. Don't push high-N in mid-summer heat.

**Autumn (March to May).** Hardening window. Lawn building reserves for winter. All Seasons (21-1-16 with iron) is the autumn pick — moderate N, good K, iron for hardening. Pre-em Blockade for poa annua before soil cools into germination range. Start preventative Fungus Pro through autumn as Fusarium pressure climbs.

**Winter (June to August).** Granular response slows as soil temperatures drop below 10°C. Switch to foliar programme. Charger every 2 weeks for maintenance feeding and lateral density. Iron+ for colour without forcing growth. Liquid N for steady N delivery when needed. Fungus Pro protectant continues; Azoxy if disease shows up.

The standard year-round NZLA programme for a typical home lawn:

All Seasons or All Seasons N+ every 6 to 8 weeks October through May. Foliars (Charger, Iron+) every 2 to 3 weeks during winter. Blockade pre-em twice (spring and autumn) where label-appropriate. Fungus Pro protectant through the wet months. Spot treatments as needed.

Full year programme guide on the NZLA website covers regional adjustments and lawn-specific variations.
