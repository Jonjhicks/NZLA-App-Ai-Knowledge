# What is the difference between slow-release and fast-release fertiliser?

How fast the nitrogen becomes available to the plant after application.

**Fast-release fertilisers** are typically urea-based with no coating or treatment to delay release. Once the granule hits moist soil it dissolves within hours, the nitrogen is fully available, and the plant takes up what it can in a 2 to 3 week window. After that the nitrogen is gone — leached through the soil, lost to the atmosphere as ammonia, or taken up and used.

Result: a quick visible green-up within days, peak response at 7 to 10 days, then the lawn drops back into a hungry state until you reapply. Spike and trough.

**Slow-release fertilisers** use coating technology (polymer-coated urea, sulphur-coated urea, PCSCU, methylene urea polymers) or chemistry (ammonium stabilisation) to meter the nitrogen out gradually over weeks. The lawn gets steady continuous feeding instead of a hit.

Result: less dramatic initial green-up, sustained colour and growth for 6 to 10 weeks, no trough at the end.

Practical implications:

Fast-release is cheaper per kg. Useful when you want a fast push for a specific reason (recovery from wear, spring kick-start). Bad choice as the year-round base feed because the spike-and-trough pattern feeds disease and creates uneven growth.

Slow-release costs more per kg but you apply less often and the total nitrogen used is lower. Better growth quality, lower disease pressure, less environmental loss.
