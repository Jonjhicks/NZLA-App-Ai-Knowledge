# What is the difference between fungus and disease in a lawn?

Same thing in practical use. Most home lawn diseases are caused by fungi (Fusarium, dollar spot, brown patch, leaf spot, rust, red thread, powdery mildew — all fungal). When someone says "I've got a fungus in my lawn" or "I've got a disease in my lawn," they usually mean the same thing.

The technical distinction:

Fungus is the organism. A type of life form distinct from plants, animals or bacteria — mushrooms, moulds, yeasts, plant pathogens, beneficial soil mycorrhizae, brewer's yeast — all fungi.

Disease is what the fungus does to the lawn. The collection of symptoms the plant shows when the fungus is feeding on or infecting it. Brown patches, mycelium webbing, leaf spots, bleached areas, dead tissue.

Most turf diseases are fungal, but a few have other causes:

Bacterial diseases exist in turf (bacterial wilt for example) but are rare in home lawns.

Viral diseases are rare in lawns.

Nematode damage (microscopic root-feeding worms) looks like disease but isn't fungal.

"Disease" as a turf term covers all of these, while "fungus" specifically means a fungal cause. For practical home lawn purposes, the two words are interchangeable in most contexts.

Treatment uses fungicides specifically when the cause is fungal. NZLA Azoxy and NZLA Fungus Pro are the two NZLA fungicide options.

If you describe a problem as "fungus" or "disease" in a forum or to a lawn care professional, both terms work fine. The diagnostic question is which specific fungus (or other cause) and how to treat it.
