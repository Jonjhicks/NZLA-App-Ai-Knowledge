# Should I mow shorter in winter or longer?

Longer in winter. Lift the height when the lawn slows down for cold.

Why longer suits winter:

**More photosynthetic area at low light.** Winter light is weaker and the sun angle lower. Longer leaf gives the plant more surface to gather what light there is. Critical for cool-season grasses ticking over through winter.

**More carbohydrate reserves.** Plants store energy as carbohydrates in leaf, crown and root tissue. More leaf = more storage = better cold tolerance and faster spring recovery.

**Shades out moss and weed seedlings.** A tighter, taller canopy keeps light off the soil surface where moss spores and weed seedlings would establish.

**Insulates the crown.** The growing point of grass is at the base of the plant (the crown). Longer leaf protects the crown from frost damage and temperature swings.

Practical heights:

Standard perennial ryegrass: 25 to 50 mm in summer, lift to 40 to 60 mm in winter.

Premium dark ryegrass cultivars (MDR, Dark Perennial Rye): 20 to 40 mm in summer, 35 to 50 mm in winter.

Tall fescue: 50 to 70 mm year-round generally, can go shorter in summer if active management.

Fine fescue: 25 to 60 mm range, lift higher in winter shade.

Kikuyu: 25 to 50 mm summer (the kikuyu growing season). Winter not relevant for kikuyu in most of NZ as it stops growing below 8°C.

Couch: 10 to 30 mm summer. Like kikuyu, dormant or near-dormant in cooler months.

The transition. Don't drop from a winter height of 50 mm straight to a summer height of 25 mm in one mow. Bring it down gradually across 3 to 4 mows in early spring as growth fires up. The third rule applies to seasonal transitions too.

Common mistake: keeping the same height year-round. Either too low for winter cold tolerance or too high for summer presentation. Adjust seasonally.
