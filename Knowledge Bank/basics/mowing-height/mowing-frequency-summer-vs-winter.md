# How often should I mow in summer vs winter?

Driven by how fast the lawn is growing.

The principle: mow when the lawn has grown a third above your target height. Don't mow on a calendar — mow when the lawn says it's ready.

Summer growth rates (mid-spring through early autumn) for cool-season grasses in NZ:

Active growth period October through April for most of the country. Grass grows 25 to 50 mm per week during the peak growth windows (October-December and again March-April).

Mowing frequency that gives third-rule cuts: every 5 to 7 days for premium short-cut lawns (25 to 30 mm target), every 7 to 10 days for standard home lawns (30 to 50 mm target).

In the deepest heat of January-February, growth often slows because of stress — mowing frequency can extend to every 10 to 14 days while the lawn is hot.

Winter growth rates (June through August) for cool-season grasses:

Growth slows dramatically below 10°C soil temperature. Most cool-season lawns grow 5 to 15 mm per week in winter, depending on region.

Mowing frequency: every 2 to 4 weeks. Auckland and upper North Island may need every 2 weeks; Canterbury and southern South Island typically every 3 to 4 weeks.

Some lawns barely need mowing in deep winter (July). That's fine — don't force a cut on a lawn that hasn't grown enough to justify it.

For warm-season lawns (kikuyu, couch):

Active growth from October through April. Mow every 5 to 10 days in summer at typical lawn heights.

Dormant or near-dormant from June through August. Don't mow dormant warm-season turf — it's not growing, and mowing dormant tissue creates damage that won't recover until growth fires up.

Practical pattern across the year for a standard ryegrass lawn:

October to April: weekly mowing roughly.

May-June: every 2 weeks.

July-August: every 3 to 4 weeks.

September: back to weekly as spring growth resumes.

Watch the lawn, not the calendar. The third rule is what matters.
