# Ready Lawn

## What it is

A pre-grown lawn delivered as rolls or slabs and laid like carpet over prepared soil. Also called instant lawn or turf rolls. The grass has been grown for 12 to 24 months at a turf farm, then cut as slabs about 30 mm thick — grass, root mat, and a layer of soil.

The visual result is immediate. The establishment isn't. Ready lawn needs the same attention to watering and early care as a seeded lawn for the first 4 to 6 weeks.

## Ready lawn vs seeded

**Ready lawn pros:** instant result the same day, no rough-looking establishment window, can be laid year-round (weather permitting), works on slopes where seed would wash away.

**Ready lawn cons:** 5 to 10 times the cost of seed, species limited to what the turf farm grew, joins between slabs can stay visible for months.

**Seeded pros:** cost-effective for larger areas, full choice of NZLA species and cultivars, no slab lines.

**Seeded cons:** 6 to 12 week establishment window where the lawn looks rough, weather-dependent timing, more weed pressure during establishment.

Ready lawn suits fast results on small areas — entrance lawns, small backyards, repairs. Seed suits cost-effective coverage of larger areas where the establishment window is acceptable.

## First weeks care

**Watering — the main job.** The lawn arrived with a shallow root mat. It needs to grow roots into the soil beneath before it's properly established. Until then, the mat dries out fast.

First 2 weeks: twice a day in warm conditions, once a day in cool. Keep the root mat consistently moist — 5 to 10 mm each application. Enough to soak the root mat and the top 20 mm of soil below it.

Weeks 2 to 4: as roots anchor into the soil, back off to once daily or every other day with deeper watering.

Weeks 4 to 6: transition to twice-weekly deep watering as establishment completes.

Test: try to lift a section of turf by hand. If it lifts as a slab (root mat comes up), roots haven't established into the soil yet. Keep watering. If the grass tears off but the lawn stays in place, the roots are anchored.

**Traffic.** Keep off the new lawn for the first 4 weeks where possible. Foot traffic on un-rooted turf shifts the slabs and damages the root mat. Keep dogs off for 4 to 6 weeks — dog urine on establishing grass kills patches.

**Feeding.** Don't overdo it early.

NZLA Starter granular at laying (or applied by your contractor): 1.5 kg per 100m², watered in. Feeds the lawn through establishment.

At 4 to 5 weeks: NZLA Liquid Starter as a foliar (100 to 200 mL per 100m² in 5 L water) supports root development.

Transition to NZLA All Seasons or All Seasons N+ maintenance programme at 8 to 12 weeks once fully established.

## First mow

When the grass has grown to 60 to 80 mm and the lift test shows roots have anchored. Typically 2 to 4 weeks after laying, depending on season.

Set the mower high — take only the top third off. If the grass is 80 mm, cut to about 55 mm. Don't jump straight to your target maintenance height.

Sharp blade. Tearing leaf on a new lawn does extended damage.

Catch the clippings on the first mow — returning clippings to a brand-new lawn can block light to the grass beneath.

Mow when the lawn is dry. Wet ground plus a heavy mower creates tracks and root mat damage.

Mow slowly — rushing the mower can shift slabs that haven't fully rooted.

After the first mow, treat the lawn as established within a week or two. Continue mowing by the third rule and bring the height down gradually to your maintenance target.

## Species-specific aftercare

Different grass species have different requirements after laying. The basics above apply to all; these are the additions.

### Ryegrass

Initial mowing height 40 to 50 mm. Once established, bring to 30 mm maintenance height. Use the catcher during establishment — ryegrass grows fast and clippings can be heavy. Broadleaf weed control: NZLA Gold once fully established (after 4 to 6 weeks and at least 2 mows).

### Tall fescue

First mow 7 to 10 days after install at 40 to 50 mm. Once established, minimum mowing height 40 mm for most cultivars — check with your supplier, some require 50 mm minimum. Tall fescue is more drought-tolerant once established but needs consistent watering during the first 4 weeks. Weed control: NZLA Gold.

### Fine fescue

Fine fescue is the most temperature-sensitive during establishment. In summer: irrigate lightly during the 2pm to 4pm hottest period for the first 7 to 10 days to cool the surface. Minimum evapotranspiration replacement is 4 to 6 mm/day in hot conditions — don't let it dry out for even half a day. Initial mow at 35 mm, then reduce to 25 to 35 mm maintenance height once established. Weed control: NZLA Gold only — do not use BWC or Meso on fine fescue.

### Kikuyu

Warm-season species — grows fast in summer, goes dormant or very slow in winter. Initial mow at 40 to 50 mm, maintenance height 20 to 30 mm. The lower maintenance height distinguishes kikuyu from cool-season — that dense, fine surface requires more frequent mowing in summer. Broadleaf weed control is more limited on kikuyu — speak to Jon in the forum about appropriate options given the situation.

Spring scalp: once established, a close scalp in early spring followed by All Seasons top dressing gives kikuyu a cleaner, leafier start to the growing season and reduces the need for vertical cutting. More in the warm-season annual programme article.

### Couch

Also warm-season — may discolour or go dormant during winter; this is normal, not a problem. Once conditions warm, colour returns. During establishment, same watering regime as other species. Maintenance height 25 mm. Mow every 5 to 6 days at 30 mm minimum once established. Couch is sensitive to some herbicides — check the product label before applying anything other than NZLA Gold.
