# Lawn Pests — Basics

## When  are most active

NZ lawn pest activity follows predictable seasonal patterns.

**Grass grub (Costelytra zealandica):** larvae feed on roots October to December as soil warms. Adults emerge as beetles December-January, fly, mate and lay eggs for the next generation.

**African Black Beetle (Heteronychus arator):** similar pattern to grass grub. Root-feeding larvae spring through summer; adults active flying and mating through summer.

**Argentine Stem Weevil (Listronotus bonariensis):** larvae feed inside grass stems and at the base through spring and early summer. Damage tends to be subtle until cumulative.

**Porina (Wiseana spp.):** caterpillars build vertical burrows from late summer, feeding at night through autumn into winter. Heaviest damage September to November coming out of winter when grass growth is slow.

**Sod webworms:** small caterpillars in silk-lined surface tunnels. Damage accumulates through dry summer periods on stressed lawns.

**Armyworm:** larger caterpillars feeding in groups. Fast damage when populations spike in summer through early autumn.

**Billbugs:** adults visible walking on lawn in spring; larvae feed at the crown and root through summer.

For treatment timing: NZLA Grub+ (chlorantraniliprole) covers all of these but can only be applied once per year — so timing matters. Spring (October to December) catches grass grub, black beetle larvae, stem weevil and early caterpillar pressure. That's the standard window for most NZ home lawns. For porina specifically, autumn application targets the active larval stage more precisely.

Monitor before treating. Many lawns have low pest pressure that never reaches a damage threshold. The square spade test in spring is the way to confirm whether grub counts justify intervention.

## Telling  apart from 

**The lift test.** Grip a handful of grass and pull firmly. If the lawn lifts away like carpet — roots have been chewed — you have a soil-dwelling pest. Disease patches keep their roots intact; the grass won't lift.

**The cobweb test.** Early morning in damp conditions, walk the lawn. Fungal mycelium shows as persistent white cottony webbing on the leaf surface. Dew drops evaporate as the sun rises. Webbing that persists means fungal .

**The pattern.** Disease patches are usually round with defined edges, sometimes with a darker outer ring. Pest damage is more irregular, with bird activity (pulling grubs) extending the visible area.

**The season.** Soil-dwelling  peak October through December. Caterpillars peak late summer through autumn. Fusarium peaks autumn into winter. Brown patch peaks in summer humidity.

**The soapy water flush.** For surface caterpillars (sod webworm, armyworm, porina), pour soapy water over the suspect area. Caterpillars surface within minutes. Fungal  doesn't respond.

Lift test positive: NZLA Grub+. Mycelium present: Fungus Pro and Azoxy. Sometimes both are present simultaneously —  stress the lawn moves in on the weakened plants. Treat both if diagnostics indicate both.

## Organic and cultural options

Cultural control is the foundation. A healthy, thick, well-fed lawn handles minor pest pressure without intervention. Most NZ home lawns have some grub and caterpillar presence that never reaches a damage threshold because the grass outgrows it. Sound fertility, correct mowing height, proper irrigation-general, and density are real deterrents.

Specific organic options:

**Beneficial nematodes** are available in NZ for grass grub control. The nematodes infect and kill grub larvae. Effectiveness is real but conditions-dependent — soil temperature above 12°C and consistent moisture are needed. Available through specialist suppliers. Worth considering on organic-certified lawns where chemical insecticide isn't an option.

**NZLA Tea Saponin and Worm Pellets** (camellia tea seed extract) are the organic chemistry in the NZLA range. These are aimed specifically at worm casting control on fine cut turf — not at the grub class . Useful on bowling greens and putting greens where surface casting is a problem. Note: worms are beneficial overall and should only be controlled where surface casting is a genuine issue.

For the main NZ home lawn  at threshold densities, NZLA Grub+ is more effective than organic options. Organic approaches work best as part of an integrated strategy — cultural prevention as the base, biological if available, chemical when damage threshold is confirmed.
