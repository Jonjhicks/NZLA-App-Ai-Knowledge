# Slow-Release vs Fast-Release Fertiliser

The release mechanism of a granular fertiliser is as important as its NPK ratio. Two products with identical NPK numbers but different release technologies behave very differently on a lawn.

---

## How slow-release works

Slow-release fertilisers use a coating or chemical process to control the rate at which nutrients become available to the plant. NZLA uses PCSCU (polymer-coated sulphur-coated urea) — each granule has a wax-based outer coating that water and microbial activity slowly break down over 8 to 12 weeks, releasing nitrogen gradually throughout.

The result: consistent, sustained feeding rather than a surge. The lawn greens up steadily and holds that colour for 6 to 8 weeks from a single application.

---

## How fast-release works

Fast-release products (including agricultural urea, many garden centre blends, and any fertiliser without a coating) dissolve quickly in moisture and become immediately plant-available. The nitrogen spike is rapid — the lawn greens up fast — but the available nutrient is gone from the soil quickly through leaching and volatilisation.

Two to three weeks after a fast-release application, the lawn typically returns to its pre-application colour. Another application is needed. The more frequently you apply, the higher the cumulative burn risk and cost.

---

## The burn risk difference

Fast-release nitrogen is at high concentration in the soil immediately after application. On wet foliage, in heat, or if over-applied, the osmotic pressure differential between the concentrated salt solution and the plant root draws water out of the root (reverse osmosis). The result: brown tips, scorch, and in severe cases, dead patches.

Slow-release fertilisers have a much lower burn risk because the concentration of available nutrient at any given moment is low. The coating prevents the salt spike.

Agricultural urea applied to a lawn in summer is a common and avoidable mistake. It burns reliably. The cost saving over a slow-release product disappears in the repair work.

---

## Why NZLA uses PCSCU

PCSCU is made primarily in the US and is more expensive than the cheaper Chinese-manufactured PCSCU or alternative slow-release technologies. NZLA uses US-sourced PCSCU because of its consistency of coating quality and release profile.

A thin or poorly applied coating on a PCSCU granule means inconsistent release — some granules dump their nutrient load early. A quality PCSCU product has consistent coating thickness, which produces a predictable release curve.

The quality difference matters most in summer when burn risk is highest and when a poorly timed nutrient dump from an inconsistent product creates patches.

---

## When to choose which

**Slow-release (NZLA All Seasons, All Seasons N+):** Standard lawn maintenance programme. Apply every 6 to 8 weeks through the growing season. Reduced burn risk, consistent colour, lower application frequency.

**Fast-release (not in the NZLA range for lawn granulars):** NZLA doesn't include fast-release granular products in its lawn range precisely because of burn risk and inconsistency.

**Liquid fertilisers** (Charger, Liquid Boost, Iron+): Foliar-applied liquids work differently from granulars. They're absorbed through the leaf surface rather than through the root system. Charger and Iron+ are low-salt formulations with low burn risk when applied correctly. Liquid Boost (high N) has more burn potential — apply in cool conditions, avoid hot days.

---

## Practical summary

If you're buying fertiliser for a NZ home lawn:
- Look for polymer-coated or sulphur-coated urea as the nitrogen source.
- Avoid plain urea, ammonium nitrate, or ammonium sulphate as the primary nitrogen source on established turf.
- Slow-release products cost more per kg but last longer and reduce risk — total programme cost is typically similar or lower than frequent fast-release applications.
