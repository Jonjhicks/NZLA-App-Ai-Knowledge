# Blunt Mower Blades

## How to tell if your blades are blunt

Look at the grass tips a day after mowing.

A sharp blade cuts cleanly. The tip stays green, the cut edge is straight, and the leaf carries on growing normally.

A blunt blade tears the leaf. The tip looks ragged or shredded, and within a couple of days the damaged tissue dies back — turning white or brown. From a distance, the whole lawn takes on a faint grey or white haze when viewed against the light.

A few other indicators:

The mower struggles more than usual. A blunt blade takes more engine power to push through the grass. You feel the difference.

Grass doesn't collect cleanly in the catcher. A clean cut launches the clipping into the . A torn cut can leave the clipping partially attached.

The blade itself tells you. Pull the spark plug, tilt the mower carefully (oil side up to avoid leaking into the air filter), and inspect the edge. A sharp blade has a clean, smooth edge. A blunt blade has a rounded edge with chips, dents, or a slightly rolled lip from contact with stones.

Sharpen twice a season for most home lawns — once heading into spring, once mid-summer. Or every 20 to 25 hours of mowing.

## What blunt blades cost you

The visible damage is the easy part to explain. The less obvious costs are real.

**Disease entry.** A clean cut seals quickly. A torn leaf tip has damaged tissue with a far larger surface area exposed to the environment. Fusarium, dollar spot, red thread, brown patch — fungal pathogens use those torn wound sites to enter the plant. Lawns mowed consistently with blunt blades carry measurably higher  pressure. It's one of the cheapest  prevention steps available.

**Water loss and heat stress.** Damaged leaf tips lose water faster than clean-cut ones. The plant diverts more resources to maintaining hydration, which leaves less for growth and stress tolerance. On hot summer days, the difference between sharp and blunt cuts shows up as faster wilting.

**Aesthetic damage.** The most visible effect. Torn tips die back within days, turning the lawn to a washed-out pale haze. Premium dark cultivars like Midnight Dark Rye show this particularly hard because the contrast between dark green and bleached tips is stark.

Sharp blades plus correct mowing frequency are the two easiest and cheapest upgrades you can make to lawn quality. Neither requires products.

Balance the blade after sharpening so the mower runs smoothly. An unbalanced blade vibrates, wears out the mower deck bearings, and doesn't cut evenly across the width.
