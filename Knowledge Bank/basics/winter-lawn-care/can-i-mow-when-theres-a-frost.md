# Can I mow when there is a frost on the lawn?

No. Wait until the frost has melted off and the leaves are no longer brittle.

Frost-bound grass cells are full of ice. The leaves are stiff and snap rather than bend. Walking on them, and especially mowing them, breaks the cells and bruises the leaf. The damage shows up over the following days as yellow or brown footprints and tracks that take weeks to recover.

Mid-morning is usually safe in most parts of NZ. The frost lifts as the sun gets on the lawn, the cells thaw, the leaves go back to flexible. Touch the grass. If it is still crunchy or icy, wait longer.

Same logic applies to very wet lawns — wait until the surface has dried out enough that the mower is not making tracks. The damage from mowing wet ground (compaction, ruts, tearing) is similar to mowing on frost, just for different reasons.

If you are stuck in a long cold patch where the lawn does not dry out for days, you can leave the mowing for another week. Lawns in winter grow slowly and a missed cut will not hurt. A scalped or bruised lawn in winter takes far longer to fix than a slightly long one.
