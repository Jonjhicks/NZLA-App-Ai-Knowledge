# Should I aerate my lawn in winter?

Generally no. Save core aeration for autumn or spring when growth is active enough to recover from the damage.

The issue with winter aeration:

Aeration creates open holes in the lawn and exposes the crown to cold and wet conditions. In active growing weather (spring or autumn), the holes fill in within 4 to 6 weeks as the surrounding grass grows over and the cores break down on the surface. In winter, growth is slow or stopped, so the holes stay open for months.

That creates two problems. The exposed crowns are vulnerable to frost damage and fungal infection. And the lawn looks rough through the entire winter rather than recovering fast.

When winter aeration might make sense:

Severely waterlogged ground that needs immediate drainage relief. Aerating creates direct channels for water to drain through compacted soil. Better than letting the lawn drown for weeks. Recovery in spring fills the holes.

Sports fields, golf courses or commercial properties where ongoing use creates compaction faster than recovery. Some winter aeration is part of the maintenance pattern there — but those properties are managing professionally with full recovery support.

For most home lawns, save aeration for late September through November or March through April. Soil temperatures above 10°C support recovery.

What you can do in winter instead:

**NZLA Penetrate** — moves water down through the profile, reduces surface waterlogging without physically aerating. Suits winter conditions.

**NZLA Aerate** — soil-amending polymer for clay and silt soils. Improves structure gradually without creating physical holes. Monthly application through winter is fine.

**Fork through severely waterlogged patches.** A garden fork pushed in 100 mm and rocked back and forth creates drainage channels without the open hole damage of core aeration.

Plan the proper core aeration for early spring once soil temperatures are above 10°C and growth is firing.
