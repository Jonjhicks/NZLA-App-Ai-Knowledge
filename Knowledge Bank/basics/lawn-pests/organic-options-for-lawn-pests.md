# Are there organic options for controlling lawn pests?

Some, with mixed effectiveness depending on the pest.

**Cultural control is the foundation.** A healthy thick lawn handles minor pest pressure without intervention. Most NZ home lawns have some grass grub and caterpillar presence that never reaches damage threshold because the grass outgrows it. The standard cultural levers — sound fertility programme, correct mowing height, proper irrigation, density that crowds out weak spots — reduce pest susceptibility more than most users realise.

**Biological control.** Some commercial nematode products are available in NZ for grass grub. Beneficial nematodes infect and kill grub larvae. Effectiveness is real but conditions-dependent (soil temperature and moisture matter; dry conditions reduce success). Available through specialist suppliers rather than mainstream retail. Worth considering for organic-certified lawns where chemical insecticide isn't an option.

**Camellia tea seed extract (saponins).** NZLA Tea Saponin and Worm Pellets use this organic active for worm cast control on fine cut turf. Not aimed at grub-class pests but useful as part of an organic toolkit for the specific problem of casts on bowling greens or putting greens. Note: worms are generally beneficial — only control where casting is a genuine surface problem.

**Bt (Bacillus thuringiensis).** Biological caterpillar control used in some commercial agriculture. Specific Bt strains target specific caterpillar species. Available but not widely promoted for turf in NZ.

**Diatomaceous earth, neem and similar.** Sometimes promoted as organic lawn pest control. Effectiveness on the main NZ turf pests (grass grub, black beetle, porina) is limited. Better suited to garden pest control than turf.

**Encouraging predators.** Birds (blackbirds, magpies, starlings) feed on grubs and caterpillars. Hedgehogs eat porina. Predator presence reduces pest pressure but doesn't fully control infestations once established.

For most NZ home lawn pest problems, chemical control (NZLA Grub+) is more effective than organic options at threshold pest densities. Organic options work best as preventative culture combined with monitoring, with chemical intervention reserved for confirmed threshold pressure.
