# NZLA Application Guide

## Building a year-round programme

Five components form the framework. Adapt each to your lawn's situation.

**1. Granular base feed.** The foundation. NZLA All Seasons (21-1-16 + 5% Fe) or All Seasons N+ for spring push. Apply every 6 to 8 weeks October through May. All Seasons N+ for the spring growth push and post-stress recovery; All Seasons for steady-state maintenance and the autumn hardening window.

**2. Foliar supplements.** Between granular applications and through winter. NZLA Charger every 2 to 3 weeks for maintenance feeding and lateral density. Iron+ for colour pickup. Liquid Boost when the lawn needs a push after stress, recovery, or pre-event. Foliar takes over completely from granular from May through August when soil temperature drops.

**3. Pre-emergent.** NZLA Blockade twice a year at the germination windows — spring for summer grass and crabgrass, autumn for poa annua. Note the NZ label restriction: Blockade is registered for warm-season turf (kikuyu, couch) only in NZ.

**4. Disease management.** NZLA Fungus Pro on a routine 4-week pattern through the high-pressure months (autumn into winter, every 2 weeks in high humidity). NZLA Azoxy as backup when active  appears — maximum 2 consecutive applications then rotate to Fungus Pro.

**5. Establishment and recovery.** NZLA Starter granular and Liquid Starter for new lawns, seeding-and-over-seeding, and post-renovation recovery. Applied as needed, not on a calendar.

## Seasonal guide

**Spring (September to November).** Growth firing up. Apply All Seasons N+ once growth is consistent for spring kick-start. Pre-em Blockade for summer grass (late August Auckland, late September Canterbury — before soil temperatures hit 12°C). NZLA Starter for any seeding-and-over-seeding or patch repair. Spot-treat broadleaf  with BWC before they set seed.

**Early summer (December to January).** Active growing season. Slow-release All Seasons or All Seasons N+ every 6 to 8 weeks. Watch for dry patch — Wetter or Wetter 3W if hydrophobic areas develop. Iron+ between granulars for colour without forcing growth. Don't push high-N on heat-stressed lawns.

**Late summer (February).** Often the recovery window after summer stress. Light All Seasons or All Seasons N+ to push back into autumn condition. Watch for brown patch in humid inland areas — Azoxy if it appears.

**Autumn (March to May).** Hardening window. All Seasons (21-1-16 with iron) is the autumn pick — moderate N, good K, iron for hardening. Pre-em Blockade for poa annua (March to April depending on region — before soil temperatures fall into the 18 to 22°C poa germination range). Start Fungus Pro protectant as Fusarium pressure builds.

**Winter (June to August).** Switch from granular to foliar. Charger every 2 weeks, Iron+ every 2 to 3 weeks, Liquid N if more N is needed. Fungus Pro protectant continues; Azoxy if active  shows. Mow less, at lifted height. Watch for Fusarium.

## What to use right now — quick monthly guide

**September:** All Seasons N+ for spring kick-start + Blockade pre-em (Auckland and upper NI).

**October:** Charger foliar between granulars. Blockade for central NI and Canterbury at end of month.

**November:** All Seasons mid-spring base feed. Iron+ for colour.

**December:** Iron+ and Charger between granular applications. Wetter if dry patch starting.

**January:** All Seasons summer base feed. Wetter monthly if needed. Watch for brown patch.

**February:** Light All Seasons or All Seasons N+. Iron+ for colour. Start thinking about autumn prep.

**March:** All Seasons autumn hardening feed + Blockade pre-em for poa. Start Fungus Pro preventative.

**April:** Fungus Pro continues. Transition toward foliar. Iron+ for colour through autumn.

**May:** Full switch to foliar — Charger + Iron+ every 2 weeks. Fungus Pro on routine.

**June to August:** Charger + Iron+ fortnightly. Fungus Pro 4-week pattern. Azoxy if Fusarium appears. Mow every 2 to 4 weeks at raised height.

Regional adjustment: upper NI (Auckland, Northland) is 2 to 4 weeks ahead of Canterbury on most timing decisions. Use soil temperature as the trigger rather than the calendar where possible.
