# Herbicide Basics

## Selective vs non-selective

**Selective herbicide** kills specific plant types while leaving others unharmed. The chemistry exploits differences in how plant species metabolise the active ingredient. A broadleaf selective kills dandelions and plantain but leaves grass species alone — grass breaks down the active quickly before it causes damage.

The NZLA selective range:
- **BWC** — broadleaf selective for cool-season turf
- **Gold** — heavier broadleaf control, wider weed spectrum
- **Halo** — grass-in-grass selective for fine fescue only
- **Etho** — poa annua and grass  in ryegrass and tall fescue
- **Meso** — broad spectrum, cool-season turf
- **Sedge Pro** — sedge control, broad turf safety

**Non-selective herbicide** kills everything it contacts. Glyphosate is the most common. It inhibits an enzyme present in essentially all plants — grass, broadleaf, weed, ornamental. Use it for total kill before renovation, hard surfaces, or severe weed patches that will be reseeded.

Glyphosate isn't in the NZLA range but is commonly used alongside NZLA products. The right tool when you're accepting that everything in the spray area will die.

## Pre-emergent vs post-emergent

**Pre-emergent** stops weed seeds germinating. The active sits in the top centimetre of soil. When a weed seed tries to push its first root through that layer, the chemistry kills it at the root tip before the weed ever reaches the surface.

Pre-em only works on seeds — it has no effect on established  already past germination. It's a preventative barrier, not a rescue treatment. NZLA Blockade (prodiamine) is the main pre-em. Apply spring for summer grass and crabgrass, autumn for poa annua.

**Post-emergent** kills established  after they've come up. The herbicide is applied to the plant directly — absorbed through leaves or roots. BWC, Gold, and Etho are all post-emergent.

The two work together. Pre-em handles the next generation; post-em handles what's already there. A sensible annual weed programme uses both.

## Is this herbicide safe for my grass?

Read the label specifically for which grass species are listed as safe. Selective herbicides are safe on specific species and damaging or lethal on others.

Key restrictions in the NZLA range that catch people out:

**NZLA Halo (haloxyfop)** — registered for fine fescue with a hard 15 mL/100m² rate cap. It WILL kill perennial ryegrass, tall fescue, browntop, kikuyu and couch. People see "selective" and assume it's safe for all turf. It isn't.

**NZLA Etho (ethofumesate)** — registered for perennial ryegrass and tall fescue. DO NOT use on fine fescue. May also damage tall fescue if not actively growing.

**NZLA Blockade (prodiamine)** — NZ label is registered for warm-season turf only (kikuyu, couch). The chemistry is safe on cool-season grass and widely used internationally on it, but off-label use here can't be recommended.

**NZLA Meso (mesotrione)** — cool-season turf only. Damages warm-season grasses (kikuyu, couch).

**NZLA BWC and Gold** — safe on established cool-season turf (perennial ryegrass, tall fescue, browntop, fine fescue). Check the label for kikuyu and couch.

The protocol before any new herbicide use: identify what grass species you actually have, then check the herbicide label specifically for that species. If unsure, spot-test on a small inconspicuous area and wait 2 to 3 weeks for any damage to show.

Killing a lawn with the wrong herbicide is recoverable but expensive and time-consuming. Check the label first.
