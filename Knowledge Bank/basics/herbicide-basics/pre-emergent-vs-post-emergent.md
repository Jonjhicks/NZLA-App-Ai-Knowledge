# What is the difference between pre-emergent and post-emergent herbicide?

When the herbicide acts — before or after the weed has come up.

**Pre-emergent** stops weed seeds germinating. The herbicide sits in the top centimetre of soil as a chemical barrier. When weed seeds try to push roots down through that layer, the chemistry kills them at the developing root tip and they never reach the surface.

Pre-em only works on seeds. It doesn't touch existing established weeds — they're already past the vulnerable germination stage. So pre-em is a preventative tool: get the barrier in place before the target weeds germinate, not after.

NZLA Blockade (prodiamine) is the main pre-em. Apply spring before summer grass and crabgrass germinate (soil temperatures hitting 12°C and rising). Apply autumn before poa annua germinates (soil temperatures falling into 18-22°C).

**Post-emergent** kills established weeds after they've emerged. The herbicide is applied to the weed plant itself — absorbed through leaves or roots — and disrupts the plant's growth, photosynthesis or hormone systems until it dies.

Post-em works on weeds you can see. NZLA BWC (MCPA + clopyralid + diflufenican) and Gold (picloram + triclopyr) are the broadleaf post-emergents. Etho (ethofumesate) covers poa annua and various broadleaf weeds at early growth stages. Meso (mesotrione) has both pre and post activity on cool-season turf.

**The two work together.** Pre-em handles the next generation; post-em handles what's already there. A typical year-round weed programme uses both — pre-em twice a year at the germination windows, post-em as needed for escapees.

For a heavily-weeded lawn, post-em first to deal with what's visible, then pre-em at the next appropriate window to stop the seed bank.
