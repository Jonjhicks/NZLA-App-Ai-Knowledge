# What is the difference between selective and non-selective herbicide?

What plants the herbicide kills.

**Selective herbicide** kills specific plant types and leaves others alone. The chemistry exploits differences in how plants metabolise or respond to the active ingredient. For example, broadleaf selectives kill dandelions, plantain and clover but leave grass species alone — the grass species rapidly break down the active before it does damage.

The NZLA selective range:

**BWC (Bow & Arrow)** — broadleaf selective. Kills broadleaf weeds in cool-season turf, leaves the grass alone.

**Gold (Triumph Gold)** — heavier broadleaf selective. Wider weed coverage than BWC, particularly on tougher weeds.

**Halo (haloxyfop)** — grass-in-grass selective. Kills most grass weeds in a fine fescue lawn while leaving the fine fescue intact (with strict rate limits).

**Etho (ethofumesate)** — selective for ryegrass and tall fescue. Kills poa and various weeds in those species.

**Meso (mesotrione)** — selective for cool-season turf, with the ability to be used during seeding.

**Sedge Pro (halosulfuron)** — selective for sedges, leaves turf grasses alone.

**Non-selective herbicide** kills everything it touches. Glyphosate is the most common non-selective. It works by inhibiting an enzyme present in essentially all plants — broadleaf, grass, weed, lawn, ornamental. Spray it on a mixed area and everything dies.

Glyphosate isn't in the NZLA range but commonly used alongside NZLA products. Useful for total kill before lawn renovation, spot treatment of severe weed patches (with overseeding to follow), edges and hard surfaces.

Practical implication:

Always check what species a selective herbicide is registered on before applying. Etho will kill fine fescue. Halo will kill ryegrass. Sedge Pro won't touch a sedge if the broadleaf herbicide you used killed the sedge's neighbours.

Non-selective is the right tool when you accept everything in the spray area will die.
