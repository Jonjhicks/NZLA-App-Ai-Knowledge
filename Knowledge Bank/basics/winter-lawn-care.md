# Winter Lawn Care

## What to do in deep winter

Less than you'd think, and different to other seasons.

**Foliar programme replaces granular.** Soil temperatures below 10°C mean granular response drops off. The soil biology and root uptake that process granular nitrogen have slowed down. Switch to foliars.

NZLA Charger every 2 weeks — maintenance feeding without forcing growth. Holds colour and lateral density through the cool months.

NZLA Iron+ every 2 to 3 weeks — colour without nitrogen. Iron drives chlorophyll directly and hardens the leaf against cold and . The lawn stays presentable without the soft growth that  feeds on.

NZLA Liquid N if the lawn needs more steady nitrogen beyond what Charger delivers. Temperature ceiling 26°C — fine in NZ winter conditions.

**Disease management is the main active task.** Fusarium pressure peaks in winter. NZLA Fungus Pro on a routine 4-week pattern through the wet cold months, dropping to every 2 weeks in high-humidity periods. Multi-site contact chemistry with essentially no resistance risk — this is the year-round protectant.

Watch for active  despite the preventative. If Fusarium shows up, NZLA Azoxy as systemic knockdown. Maximum 2 consecutive Azoxy applications, then rotate back to Fungus Pro.

**What NOT to do in winter:**

Don't apply high-nitrogen granular. Slow soil uptake plus soft vulnerable growth equals exactly the conditions Fusarium loves.

Don't core aerate. Save that for spring or autumn when growth is active enough to fill the holes.

Don't push aggressive renovation or heavy seeding-and-over-seeding. Wait for spring.

Don't expect  patches to fill in. They'll regrow as the lawn comes out of winter.

## Mowing in winter

Yes — just less often and at a higher cut.

Lawns still grow through winter, especially in the upper North Island. Skipping mowing entirely means the lawn gets away, then you take too much off in one hit come spring.

Frequency: South Island and cooler regions every 3 to 4 weeks. Auckland and upper NI every 2 to 3 weeks. Watch the lawn, not the calendar. If it hasn't grown enough to need cutting, leave it.

Height: lift to 40 to 60 mm for ryegrass. Longer grass photosynthesises better in low winter light, holds more carbohydrate reserves for cold tolerance, and shades out moss.

Don't mow when frost is on the grass. Frost-bound grass cells are full of ice and snap rather than bend. Walking on them — let alone mowing — breaks cells and leaves bruised footprints and tracks that take weeks to recover. Wait until the frost has melted and the leaves are no longer brittle.

Don't mow when the ground is wet enough that the mower leaves tracks. Same class of damage for different reasons.

Sharp blades matter more in winter. Torn leaf tips in cold damp conditions are direct  entry points.

## Aeration in winter

Generally no. Core aeration creates open holes and exposes crowns to cold and wet. In spring or autumn, growth fills holes within 4 to 6 weeks. In winter, growth is slow or stopped, so holes stay open for months — exposed crowns sitting in cold wet conditions, vulnerable to frost and fungal infection.

Winter aeration might make sense for severely waterlogged ground that needs immediate drainage-and-soil-moisture relief. The direct channels help water move through. Recovery fills the holes in spring.

What works instead of coring in winter:

NZLA Penetrate moves water down through the soil profile without creating holes. Useful for waterlogged areas through winter.

NZLA Aerate (soil-conditioning polymer) improves structure gradually. Monthly application through winter is fine.

A garden fork in severely waterlogged patches — push in 100 mm, rock back and forth, creates a drainage-and-soil-moisture channel without the open-hole damage of coring.

Plan the proper core aeration for early spring once soil temperatures are above 10°C and growth is firing.

## Pre-emergent in winter

Possible, but not where most of the value is.

By winter, the autumn poa annua germination window (soil temps 18 to 22°C falling) is largely past. A winter Blockade application catches some late poa germination and provides residual into early spring, but it doesn't fix the autumn miss — the poa that's already up is established and pre-em won't touch it.

For already-established poa in ryegrass and tall fescue lawns: NZLA Etho targets poa post-emergence through autumn and winter. Two applications 14 to 21 days apart.

For summer grass pre-em: winter is too early. The germination trigger (soil temps hitting 12°C in spring) is months away and Blockade residual wouldn't last to the target window.

Get the timing right at the proper windows — spring pre-em in late August (Auckland) to late September (Canterbury), autumn poa pre-em in March to early April. That's where most of the value is. Winter applications are a second-best option for a missed window, not a substitute for correct timing.
