# My lawn is too thick or matted - what's going on?

Two different problems that look similar from above.

Thatch build-up. Dead and living plant material accumulating between the soil and the leaf canopy. You can see it by parting the grass with your fingers and looking at the layer above the soil. More than 10 to 15 mm of thatch is too much. Symptoms: lawn feels spongy underfoot, water beads off rather than soaking in, lawn dries out fast, disease pressure goes up. Fix is dethatching or scarifying in autumn.

Matted growth from over-fertilising. Too much nitrogen pushes soft fast growth that mats together at the surface. Lawn looks lush but the matting traps moisture and invites disease. Fix is dial back nitrogen, raise mowing height, sometimes a light scarify to open up the canopy.

Stoloniferous species (kikuyu, couch, NZLA RSR Ryegrass) naturally produce more above-ground growth than tillering-only species. The thick "matted" look is normal for these grasses and isn't a problem unless the mowing height is too high.

If the lawn is just dense and you're mowing more often than you'd like, that's a sign of a healthy lawn, not a problem. NZLA PGR (trinexapac) reduces vertical growth and mowing frequency by a third to a half through the regulated period.

Mowing too tall over a long period also produces a matted-looking lawn because leaf material accumulates. Bring the cutting height down gradually over several mows (don't more than a third in any one cut), then mow more frequently to maintain the lower height.

Old lawns that haven't been renovated in years often build up thatch and matted growth together. A full renovation (scarify, core aerate, top dress, overseed) clears it out and gives the lawn a fresh start.
