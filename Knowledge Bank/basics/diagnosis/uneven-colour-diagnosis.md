# Why does my lawn have uneven colour?

Several possibilities depending on the pattern.

Stripes from spreader application. Fertiliser or wetting agent applied unevenly. The pattern is regular, in lines that match how you walked. Fix is cross-pattern application going forward (half north-south, half east-west) and waiting out the current unevenness, which usually corrects within a few weeks.

Patches where the irrigation pattern hits and doesn't hit. Pop-up sprinklers can leave dry spots between heads, those areas dry out and yellow first. Check coverage with the can test (straight-sided containers on the lawn during a run) and adjust head spacing or pattern.

Different grass species mixed through the lawn. Older lawns often have multiple species (ryegrass, fescue, browntop, poa) that show different colours at different times of year. Browntop in particular looks paler than ryegrass through summer. Overseed with NZLA Dark Perennial Rye or RSR Ryegrass to gradually unify the lawn.

Soil variation. Different soil types across the lawn (from past landscaping, fill material, drainage installation) hold different amounts of nutrients and water. Patches with sandier or thinner soil look paler. Top dress with consistent material over a few seasons.

Shade gradient. Areas under partial shade are typically darker than full sun areas. Normal and usually not a problem.

Hydrophobic dry patch in one section but not another. NZLA Wetter on the affected area corrects it.

After fungicide, herbicide or PGR application: temporary uneven colour for a week or two while the chemistry is working. Normal, resolves on its own.

Dog runs and walkways from the back door: high-use thinning and concentrated urine over time. Overseeding plus addressing the underlying use pattern.

For overall pale lawn, NZLA Iron+ gives an even green response within a few days.
