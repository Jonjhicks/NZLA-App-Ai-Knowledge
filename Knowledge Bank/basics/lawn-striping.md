# Lawn Striping

Lawn stripes are a light reflection effect, not a colour difference. Grass bent towards you reflects less light (appears darker), grass bent away reflects more (appears lighter). The alternating light/dark bands are created by mowing in opposite directions on parallel passes.

That's the whole mechanism. Understanding it tells you everything you need to know about how to get better stripes.

---

## What you need

**A roller.** The more the grass bends, the stronger the stripe effect. A standard rear-wheel mower creates a mild stripe through the weight of the wheels. A dedicated striping roller attached behind the cutter deck bends the grass much more and creates the sharp, stadium-style stripe you're after.

Most rear-roller cylinder mowers (typical in the fine turf and cricket pitch world) create strong stripes automatically. For rotary mowers: aftermarket striping kits are available, or a simple roller filled with water can be made cheaply and pulled behind the mower.

**Healthy, dense turf.** Sparse, thin grass doesn't bend consistently. A dense, healthy sward with good tiller density gives you the consistent surface you need. This is the most overlooked variable — people invest in a roller and wonder why the stripes look patchy on a thin lawn.

**The right mowing height.** Stripes show best at 20 to 40 mm. Very tall grass bends randomly and doesn't hold the direction. Very short grass (below 15 mm) has less leaf length to bend.

---

## Mowing technique

Mow in straight passes. Start along the longest straight edge (a fence, driveway, or string line). Lift the mower slightly at the end of each pass to turn without dragging. Come back in the parallel pass in the opposite direction.

Keep passes parallel — use a fixed reference point (a fence post, a mark on the lawn edge) to stay straight. Wandering lines are immediately visible in stripe patterns.

**Classic straight stripes:** parallel passes across the lawn, alternating direction.

**Checkerboard/diamond:** mow two sets of parallel stripes at 90° to each other. Each square receives two passes — one in each direction — which is why the squares alternate in a 4-way light pattern.

---

## Holding the stripe

Stripes fade as the grass grows upright and the bent stems recover. In actively growing turf, stripes last 3 to 7 days before a re-mow is needed to refresh them.

PGR (NZLA Primo) doesn't directly create stripes, but a lawn on a PGR programme tends to hold stripes longer — slower vertical growth and increased density both help. It also has finer texture and less leaf height variation, which produces a more uniform stripe.

Iron (Iron+) darkens overall colour which increases the contrast between bent/unbent grass. This is why a deep dark green lawn stripes more dramatically than a pale one.

---

## What makes stripes look bad

**Curved mowing passes.** Stripes reveal every wander in your mowing line. Stay straight.

**Inconsistent overlap.** Leaving gaps or overlapping excessively creates uneven bands.

**Scalping.** A scalped lawn looks stressed and thin — no roller fixes that.

**Worn or thin patches.** Bare areas don't reflect light the same way. Sort the underlying lawn first.

**Low-quality mower blade.** A torn, frayed leaf surface scatters light rather than reflecting it. Sharp blade, clean cut = cleaner stripe.
