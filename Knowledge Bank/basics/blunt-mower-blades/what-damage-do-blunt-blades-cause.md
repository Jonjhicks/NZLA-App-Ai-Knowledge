# What damage do blunt mower blades do to my lawn?

Three real costs, all of them avoidable.

**Open wounds for fungal disease.** A clean cut from a sharp blade seals quickly. A torn cut from a blunt blade leaves jagged, damaged tissue with a much larger surface area exposed. Fungal pathogens — Fusarium, dollar spot, red thread, brown patch — use those wounds as entry points into the plant. Lawns mowed with blunt blades have measurably higher disease pressure than lawns with sharp blades, all other factors equal.

**Water loss and stress.** Damaged leaf tips lose water faster than clean-cut tips. The plant has to use more of its resources just to maintain hydration, which leaves less for growth and stress tolerance. On a hot summer day, the difference between sharp and blunt blade cuts shows up as faster wilting and earlier heat stress.

**Aesthetic damage.** The most visible effect. Torn tips die back within days, turning white or brown. The whole lawn takes on a faint grey or pale haze when viewed against the light. Premium dark cultivars (MDR, Dark Perennial Rye) show this far more obviously than standard ryegrass because the dark green contrast against bleached tips is stark.

The blade itself isn't the only factor. Mowing wet grass with a sharp blade still does some damage because the blade pushes through soft tissue rather than cutting cleanly. Same principle.

Practical fix: sharpen blades twice a season (spring, mid-summer), or every 20 to 25 hours of mowing. Balance the blade after sharpening so the mower runs smoothly. Cheap insurance against disease pressure and the easiest meaningful lift you can make in lawn quality.
