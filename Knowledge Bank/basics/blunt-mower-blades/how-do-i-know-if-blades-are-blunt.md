# How do I know if my mower blades are blunt?

Look at the grass tip a day after mowing.

Sharp blade cuts cleanly. The tip stays green, the cut edge is straight and tidy, and the leaf carries on growing normally.

Blunt blade tears the leaf. The cut tip looks ragged or shredded, often turning white or brown within a couple of days as the damaged tissue dies back. From a distance the whole lawn takes on a faint white or grey haze when viewed against the light.

A few other signs:

Mower works harder. A blunt blade requires more engine power to push through the grass. You'll feel the mower struggling more than usual.

Grass doesn't pick up well in the catcher. A clean cut launches the clipping into the airflow that carries it to the catcher. A torn cut sometimes leaves the clipping attached or only partially severed.

Visible damage on the blade. Pull the spark plug for safety, tilt the mower carefully (oil side up to avoid leaking into the air filter), and look at the blade edge. A sharp blade has a clean edge, no major nicks or rolled edges. A blunt blade has a rounded edge, often with visible chips, dents or a slightly rolled lip from contact with stones.

Frequency check: most home mowers need sharpening twice a season — once heading into spring, once before peak summer growth. Or every 20 to 25 hours of mowing if you're tracking that.

Blunt blades aren't just cosmetic — torn leaf tips lose water faster and create open wounds where fungal disease enters. Sharp blades are real disease prevention.
