# Hose-End Sprayer Calibration

Hose-end sprayers (like the Ortho Dial'N'Spray) work on a dilution principle, not a fixed delivery rate. This makes them fundamentally different from knapsack sprayers and requires a different calibration approach.

---

## How hose-end sprayers work

A hose-end sprayer has a reservoir bottle (typically 1 to 2 litres) that connects to your garden hose. The dial on the head sets a dilution ratio — how much concentrate is drawn from the bottle per litre of water passing through. Common dial settings include 1:10, 1:20, 1:50, etc. (or labelled as tablespoons per gallon in some imported models).

The key difference from a knapsack: the water volume isn't controlled — your hose pressure determines how fast water flows. The dial controls the ratio of concentrate to water, not the absolute volume of either.

---

## The challenge for NZLA product rates

NZLA products are labelled in mL per 100m² (mL of product per 100 square metres of lawn). A hose-end sprayer doesn't naturally tell you how much product you're delivering per area.

To calibrate correctly you need two pieces of information:

1. **How fast does your hose-end sprayer cover your lawn?** (Coverage rate in m² per minute or per litre)
2. **What dilution ratio delivers the right product per 100m²?**

---

## Step 1 — Measure your output

Fill the reservoir with plain water. Set the dial to a mid-range dilution (e.g. 1:20 or equivalent). Walk at normal spraying pace over a marked 10m x 10m area (100m²). Time how long it takes, and check how much water was drawn from the reservoir.

Alternatively: use a measuring container to catch spray output for 30 seconds at a consistent walking pace. Calculate litres per minute of total spray output.

Most standard hose-end sprayers at normal NZ water pressure output 4 to 8 litres per minute of total spray (water + concentrate combined).

---

## Step 2 — Calculate concentrate per 100m²

If your sprayer outputs 5 litres/minute and takes 2 minutes to cover 100m²: total spray volume over 100m² = 10 litres.

At a 1:20 dilution setting: for every 20 parts water, 1 part concentrate is drawn. In 10 litres of total spray, approximately 0.5 litres (500 mL) of concentrate is drawn from the bottle.

Compare to label rate: if the label says 100 mL/100m², you'd be massively over-applying at this setting. Drop the dial to a lower ratio to reduce concentrate draw.

---

## Practical approach

The easiest method: pre-measure your concentrate.

Fill the reservoir with exactly the right amount of product for your lawn area first, then top up with water to the fill line. Set the dial to its lowest/most dilute setting (ensures even distribution across the full lawn area). Walk the full lawn area. The reservoir empties as you cover the full area, which delivers the right product dose.

This "pre-measure and deplete" method bypasses the dilution ratio complication entirely. You know the correct product volume (from the label rate × your lawn area), you put that volume in the bottle, and you use it all over the correct area.

---

## Important limitations

**Variable water pressure.** Hose pressure changes as other taps are opened or flow demand changes. This affects the dilution ratio mid-spray. Pre-measuring and depleting the bottle reduces this risk.

**Coverage uniformity.** Hose-end sprayers have wide spray patterns and don't give the precise even coverage of a calibrated knapsack. For herbicide applications where even coverage is critical (selective weed killers need to hit the leaf), a knapsack is more reliable.

**Hose-end sprayers work best for:** fertilisers (Charger, Iron+, Liquid Boost), wetting agents, and fungicides. For herbicide application, use a knapsack if you want reliable even coverage.
