# Granular Fertiliser Basics

## Reading the bag — NPK

Three numbers tell you most of what you need to know: N-P-K. Nitrogen, phosphorus, potassium, listed as percentages.

**Nitrogen (N) — first number.** Drives leaf growth and colour. High first number (25%+) means a push feed. Too much nitrogen with no potassium balance creates soft growth susceptible to .

**Phosphorus (P) — middle number.** Root development. High middle (10%+) usually means a starter or establishment fertiliser. Low middle (under 3%) is typical for maintenance.

**Potassium (K) — third number.** Plant strength, cold tolerance resistance. A K rate that matches N (for example 21-1-16) means balanced maintenance. Lawns with good K handle stress better.

Common NZLA examples:
- All Seasons 21-1-16 + 5% Fe — balanced maintenance, year-round base
- All Seasons N+ 32-1.3-8.3 + 2% Fe — high N push for spring and recovery
- NZLA Starter 19-10-4 + trace — high P for new lawns and establishment

**Slow-release vs fast-release.** Look for "polymer coated," "PCSCU," or "controlled release" on the bag. These meter nitrogen out gradually over 6 to 10 weeks. Fast-release urea dissolves within days of wetting, giving a quick green-up that fades fast — a spike-and-trough cycle that stresses the lawn and feeds  over time.

Slow-release costs more per kg but you apply less often, nitrogen use is lower, and the lawn quality is better. All Seasons uses PCSCU technology (40% available, 60% slow-release). All Seasons N+ is 70% fast + 30% slow — a push product, not a base feed.

Rate is on the bag as kg/100m² or kg/ha. Divide kg/ha by 100 to get kg/100m². All Seasons at 2 kg/100m²: a 10 kg bag covers 500m².

## How often to fertilise

Slow-release granular every 6 to 8 weeks through the growing season — roughly October through April for most of NZ. That's 4 to 5 applications across the warmer months.

In winter, soil temperatures drop below 10°C and granular response slows dramatically. The lawn can't metabolise the nutrients fast enough to make granular worthwhile. Switch to foliar feeding (Charger, Iron+) for the cool months.

Summer doesn't need a higher frequency — every 6 to 8 weeks on a slow-release base is consistent through the season.

## Watering in

Within 24 hours of application, ideally the same day. 5 to 10 mm of irrigation-general.

Slow-release coatings only begin releasing nitrogen when the granule gets wet. Dry granular on dry soil does nothing until moisture arrives. Applying just before forecast rain is ideal.

High-nitrogen products (All Seasons N+) can burn if granules sit on damp foliage in hot conditions — irrigation-general moves the nitrogen off the leaf and down to the soil surface where it belongs.

Always apply onto dry foliage (granules bounce off cleanly rather than sticking), then water in.

Don't over-water — 5 to 10 mm is enough. Saturating the lawn doesn't help the fertiliser.

## Summer application

Yes, with the right product and care.

Slow-release products (All Seasons, All Seasons) work well through summer. The metered release avoids nitrogen spikes on heat-stressed plants.

Avoid high-nitrogen push products (All Seasons N+, Liquid Boost) on visibly heat-stressed lawns. Forcing growth in 30°C heat with no irrigation-general is counterproductive and increases brown patch risk. Save the push products for spring and early autumn.

Never apply onto wet foliage in hot weather — granules sticking to wet leaves in 28°C+ heat can cause burn marks where the granule sat.

Don't apply in the leadup to extreme heat (35°C+ forecast). Wait for a milder window.

## Autumn timing

Late February through April depending on region. Soil temperatures are still warm enough for the lawn to use the feed, and you're setting it up for winter resilience.

All Seasons (21-1-16 + 5% Fe) is the autumn pick. The 16% potassium and 5% iron harden the plant against cold and . Avoid high-nitrogen push products heading into the cool damp months — soft growth into autumn is exactly what Fusarium feeds on.

Regional timing: Auckland upper NI late February into March; central NI late March; Canterbury and South Island March into early April.

The autumn granular pairs well with the pre-em Blockade application for poa annua — both go down in roughly the same window.
