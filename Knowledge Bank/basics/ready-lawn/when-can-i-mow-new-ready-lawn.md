# When can I mow a new ready lawn?

When the grass has grown to 60 to 80 mm and the roots have anchored into the soil below the turf cap. Typically 2 to 4 weeks after laying, depending on season and weather.

The test for whether to mow:

Grip a handful of grass and pull up firmly. If the lawn lifts as a turf cap (the root mat comes up as a slab), the roots haven't established into the soil yet. Wait longer. If the grass tears off in your hand but the lawn stays in place, the roots are anchored — mow time.

First mow specifics:

Set the mower to the highest cut setting that still takes meaningful material off. Aim for the third rule — if the grass is 80 mm, cut to about 55 mm. Don't drop straight to your target maintenance height.

Sharp blade. Always. Tearing leaf during the first mow on un-established turf does extended damage.

Catch the clippings on the first mow. The clippings on a brand-new lawn can sit on the surface and prevent light reaching the grass underneath.

Mow when the lawn is dry. Wet ground plus a heavy mower equals tracking marks and root mat damage.

Mow slowly. Don't rush the mower across the new lawn — the wheels can shift turf cap slabs that aren't fully rooted yet.

Second and subsequent mows:

After the first mow, the lawn behaves like an established lawn within a week or two. Continue mowing as growth dictates — third rule each time. Bring the height down gradually if you want a lower target than where you started.

What if growth is uneven across the lawn?

Sections that establish faster than others is normal. Mow at the height that suits the slowest section initially. As the lawn evens up over a few weeks, you can bring the target down.

If parts of the lawn lift the turf cap when tested, mow around those sections and give them another 1 to 2 weeks before mowing them with the rest.
