# Fertiliser Burn

Fertiliser burn happens when nitrogen or salt concentration at the root zone or on the leaf surface exceeds what the plant can handle. The result is yellowing, browning, and in severe cases dead patches. It looks similar to  or drought stress, which is why it often gets misdiagnosed.

## How it happens

**Too much product.** Applying above the label rate concentrates nitrogen and salts in the soil faster than the grass can use them. Osmotic stress draws water out of plant cells — the same mechanism as pouring salt on a snail.

**Granules on wet foliage.** Granular fertiliser sitting on wet grass blades releases nitrogen directly onto the leaf surface. The concentrated salt contact scorches the tissue. A dry lawn with rain coming in 12 to 24 hours is fine — the granules are washed off the leaf and into the soil. Wet foliage at application is the problem.

**Uneven spreading.** A spreader overlap, stuck spreader opening, or hand-applying unevenly puts too much product in a band or patch. The classic stripy scorch from a spreader run is caused by overlap between passes.

**Application in heat.** Liquid nitrogen products on a hot lawn increase scorch risk — the grass is already stressed and concentrated N compounds the damage.

**Spilled granules.** A bag tipped over, a spreader jam, or granules concentrated in a spot from windy conditions. Any concentration of granular fertiliser on one spot will burn.

## What it looks like

Stripy yellow or brown patches following mowing/spreading lines: spreader overlap or uneven application.

Circular or irregular brown patches after a granular application: granules concentrated in spots, often from a spreader jam or uneven spread.

General yellowing or browning across the whole lawn 24 to 48 hours after application: applied to wet foliage, or too heavy an overall rate.

Burn pattern with green outer ring: similar to dog urine — localised over-concentration.

## Immediate response

**Water heavily and immediately.** If you notice it quickly (within hours), flush the area with as much water as the soil can absorb. The goal is to dilute the nitrogen concentration and push salts down below the root zone. Multiple heavy waterings over the next 24 hours.

For granular burn from wet foliage: water immediately and continuously. You're trying to dissolve the granules off the leaf surface before the damage compounds.

## Recovery

Minor burn — grass yellowed but not dead: the grass will often recover over 2 to 3 weeks with good watering and no further stress. Keep the lawn well-irrigated while it recovers. A light foliar application of NZLA Liquid Boost once active green growth is visible helps recovery, but don't push it while the plant is stressed.

Moderate burn — patches of dead grass mixed with recoverable areas: the dead patches won't recover. Reseed once the soil nitrogen has dropped back (2 to 3 weeks of regular watering). Water the affected area daily through the recovery period to flush salts down.

Severe burn — large areas dead: treat as a renovation. Water the area heavily to flush salts, wait 2 to 3 weeks, then overseed with NZLA Starter and appropriate grass seed.

## Prevention

**Calibrate the spreader.** A few minutes checking the application rate before spreading a full lawn saves potential hours of recovery. See the sprayer-calibration article for the process.

**Never apply to wet foliage.** The single most preventable cause of granular fertiliser burn. If the lawn is wet, wait.

**Use slow-release products.** NZLA All Seasons uses PCSCU slow-release technology — the nitrogen releases gradually through the coating rather than all at once. This significantly reduces burn risk compared to fast-release or urea-based fertilisers, particularly in warm conditions.

**Apply in mild conditions.** Not in heat, not in drought stress.

**Follow label rates.** Label rates are set for normal lawn conditions. More product doesn't produce proportionally better results — it produces burn risk and waste.

## Slow-release vs fast-release

Slow-release fertilisers (NZLA All Seasons, All Seasons N+, Starter — all using PCSCU technology) release nitrogen over weeks to months as the coating breaks down. The nitrogen enters the soil gradually at a rate the grass can use. Burn risk is low.

Fast-release and urea-based products (not sold by NZLA) release all their nitrogen immediately. High-nutrient burst with high burn risk, particularly in warm conditions or on already-fertilised lawns. This is one reason NZLA doesn't sell fast-release granular products.
