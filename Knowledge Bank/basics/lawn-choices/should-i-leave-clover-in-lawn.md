# Should I leave clover in my lawn?

Better to get on top of it. Clover competes with the grass and gives an uneven look that no amount of mowing fixes.

Why customers sometimes ask about leaving it:
- The argument is that clover fixes nitrogen and reduces fertiliser needs.
- In practice on a home lawn it doesn't work cleanly. Clover patches grow at different rates to the surrounding grass, the lawn looks patchy, and the fertiliser saving is small compared to the visual cost.

What clover actually does to a home lawn:
- Forms distinct patches that look uneven against the grass.
- Flowers attract bees, which is a problem for bare feet (stings) and for kids' play.
- Goes slippery underfoot when wet.
- Recovers from wear differently to grass, so traffic patterns show up worse.
- Competes with grass for space, slowly reducing turf density over years.

The control:
- NZLA BWC at label rate handles white clover well. Standard chemistry, one application usually does the job.
- Spring or autumn timing when clover is actively growing.
- Follow up with a granular feed (NZLA All Seasons or All Seasons N+) to thicken the lawn into the gaps.

The case for a pure grass lawn:
- Predictable controlled nitrogen from NZLA's slow-release granular range gives consistent steady response across the whole lawn, not patchy unpredictable fixation in isolated spots.
- Even colour and texture.
- Better wear recovery.
- No bee stings, no slipperiness, no patchy growth.

For lawns with a few isolated clover patches, spot-treat with BWC mixed in a knapsack sprayer at label rate. For lawns with widespread clover, blanket application followed by a strong fertility programme rebuilds density over a season.

The fertility programme matters either way - a thin under-fed lawn gets re-invaded by clover whether you sprayed or not. Density is the long-term defence.
