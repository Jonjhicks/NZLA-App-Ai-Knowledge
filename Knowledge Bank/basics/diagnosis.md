# Lawn Diagnosis

## Where to start

Before reaching for a product, gather three pieces of information.

**What does it look like and where.** Patches in shade tell a different story to patches in full sun. Pet pathways tell a different story to garden edges. Take photos — a wide shot of the whole lawn and a close-up of the worst affected area. Both are useful.

**When did it start.** A problem that appeared after a hot dry week is likely heat or . A problem creeping in over months points to fertility or core-aeration. A dramatic overnight appearance in a cool wet spell is almost always Fusarium.

**What's changed recently.** New chemistry, new pet, tree removed or planted, sprinkler installed or broken, mowing height changed, fertiliser changed. These modify what the lawn is doing and often point directly at the cause.

Once you have these three, most problems narrow down fast.

## Yellow patches

Work through in order:

**Round or irregular patches after a humid spell:** fungal . Look for a defined patch edge, sometimes with a darker ring around it. NZLA Azoxy or Fungus Pro for treatment.

**General pale yellow across the whole lawn:** nitrogen deficiency. Apply NZLA Liquid N, Liquid Boost, or a granular feed. If the lawn is growing well but just pale, iron deficiency is more likely and NZLA Iron+ gives a visible response within a few days.

**Circular yellow patches with dead spot in the middle in a pet-used area:** dog urine. Concentrated nitrogen and salt burns the centre, the perimeter gets fertilised.

**Yellow in stripes or bands:** uneven fertiliser distribution. Spreader missed in some lines. Corrects within weeks.

**Yellow in shoulder seasons (cool damp autumn or spring):** yellow patch  (Rhizoctonia cerealis). Often self-limiting as conditions change. Azoxy if persistent.

**Yellow in heavy shade:** insufficient light. Chemistry won't fix it. NZLA Fine Fescue is the most shade-tolerant cool-season option.

## Brown patches

Brown is past yellow — the grass has stopped functioning.

**Round well-defined brown patches in summer humidity:** brown patch . NZLA Azoxy or Fungus Pro for treatment.

**Patches that lift like loose carpet when you pull the grass:** grass grub or black beetle larvae beneath. Pull up a section and look for white curl-shaped grubs in the soil. NZLA Grub+ for treatment.

**Patches that won't take water regardless of irrigation-general amount:** dry patch. Water beads off the surface — hydrophobic soil. NZLA Wetter breaks the surface tension and rehydrates the area.

**Brown patches in high-use areas (pathways, dog runs, play zones):** wear damage and core-aeration. Core aeration plus seeding-and-over-seeding with NZLA RSR Ryegrass fixes it.

**Brown patches in low spots after heavy rain:** waterlogging — roots have drowned. Improve drainage-and-soil-moisture with NZLA Aerate and Penetrate.

**Whole sections brown but not patchy in warm-season lawns:** normal winter dormancy. Kikuyu and couch go dormant in cold weather and green up in spring.

## Dead spots

Dead is past brown — the grass is gone.

**Round dead spots with a green ring outside in a pet-used area:** female dog urine. Flush with water to dilute the salt, reseed with NZLA Starter.

**Dead patches in lines or stripes:** chemical spillage. Petrol, oil, or leaking sprayer/spreader.

**Patches that pull up like carpet:** grub damage. Treat new grubs with NZLA Grub+ and reseed the existing dead patches.

**Dead patches with no clear cause and irregular shape:**  that progressed untreated. Reseed once the  pressure has cleared.

**Dead patches where a trampoline or pool sat for weeks:** suffocation. Remove the object, reseed.

**Dead patches over buried debris (old concrete, pipe trench, gravel layer):** shallow soil over an obstruction — roots can't get deep enough.

For most dead spot repairs the approach is the same: rake out dead material, apply NZLA Starter, sow seed, water lightly and frequently. Then address the cause.

## Lawn not growing

**Time of year:** cool-season lawns slow at soil temperatures below 8°C. Warm-season lawns (kikuyu, couch) are dormant under 14°C. This is normal, not a problem.

**Drought stress:** lawn shuts down to survive. Water deeply (15 to 25 mm) and growth resumes within days.

**Heat stress:** cool-season lawns stop growing above 28°C without irrigation-general. Deep watering revives them.

**Nitrogen deficiency:** NZLA Liquid Boost or Liquid N gives a growth response within a week if N is the issue.

**Compaction:** roots can't function in dense soil, top growth slows. Core aeration plus NZLA Aerate through summer.

**PGR carry-over:** if a growth regulator was applied recently, the lawn is doing exactly what it's supposed to do.

**Recently applied herbicide:** selective herbicides slow the lawn for 2 to 3 weeks. Normal — resume feeding once the lawn is releasing.

## Thinning lawn

**Under-fed:** the most common cause. A steady fertility programme (All Seasons every 6 to 8 weeks, foliar between) builds density.

**Mowing too short:** cutting under 25 mm on standard ryegrass stresses the lawn and opens gaps. Lift to 40 to 50 mm.

**Compaction:** roots can't develop in dense soil. Core aeration annually.

**Wear and traffic:** seeding-and-over-seeding with NZLA RSR Ryegrass for self-repairing capability.

**Shade:** the thinner the canopy, the less light penetrates to support density. NZLA Fine Fescue for shadier spots.

**Repeated mild :** long-term cumulative damage. Treat  when it appears, then push recovery with Liquid Boost.

## Thick, matted lawn

Two different problems that look similar.

**Thatch build-up:** dead and living material accumulating between soil and canopy. Part the grass and check the layer above the soil — more than 10 to 15 mm is too much. Symptoms: spongy underfoot, water beads rather than soaks in pressure up. Fix is scarifiers in autumn.

**Over-fertilised matting:** too much nitrogen pushing soft growth that mats together. Dial back nitrogen, raise mowing height, light scarify to open the canopy.

Stoloniferous species (kikuyu, couch, RSR Ryegrass) naturally look thicker and denser at the surface than bunch-type grasses. That's normal growth habit, not a problem.

## Scalped patches

Scalping is where the mower has hit a high spot and cut to soil. Shows as brown or bare patches in the same places after each mow.

The immediate damage: light scalp recovers in 2 to 4 weeks if you raise the cutting height and apply NZLA Liquid Boost. Heavy scalp with exposed soil needs reseeding with NZLA Starter.

The underlying cause: uneven ground. Options to fix the high spot — raise the mowing height (avoids most minor bumps), top dress over a couple of seasons to build up the area around the bump, or physical levelling for severe cases.

## Uneven colour

**Regular stripes from spreader application:** fertiliser applied unevenly. Cross-pattern application (half north-south, half east-west) prevents this. Current unevenness corrects over a few weeks.

**Dry patches where irrigation-general misses:** coverage gaps between sprinkler heads. Check coverage and adjust spacing.

**Mixed species:** older lawns with multiple species (ryegrass, fescue, browntop, poa) showing different colours. Overseed with NZLA Dark Perennial Rye or RSR Ryegrass to gradually unify.

**Soil variation:** different soil types across the lawn from past landscaping or fill material. Consistent top dressing over years improves it.

**Hydrophobic dry patch:** NZLA Wetter on the affected area.

**General paleness across the whole lawn:** NZLA Iron+ gives an even green response within a few days.

## Pests vs  — the key diagnostic tests

**The lift test.** Grab a handful of grass and pull firmly. If the lawn lifts like a carpet, the roots have been chewed through by a soil pest (grass grub, black beetle larvae, porina, billbugs). Disease patches keep their roots, so the grass won't lift.

**The cobweb test.** Walk the lawn early morning while dew is present. Fungal mycelium shows up as white cottony webbing that persists as the dew dries. Bare dew droplets lift as the sun gets on them. Persistent webbing means fungus.

**The soapy water flush.** For surface-feeding caterpillars (sod webworm, armyworm, porina), pour a watering can of soapy water over the suspect area. Caterpillars come up within minutes. Diseases don't.

Lift test positive: treat with NZLA Grub+. Mycelium present: treat with Fungus Pro and Azoxy. Sometimes both are present simultaneously —  stress the lawn moves in on the stressed plants. Treat both if the diagnostics indicate both.

If the problem still doesn't fit any of these, post a photo on the NZLA app forum with what you observed and when. Most problems get called within a day.
