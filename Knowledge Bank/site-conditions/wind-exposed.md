# Wind-Exposed Lawns

Constant wind changes the water and nutrient equation significantly. An exposed lawn on a ridge or open paddock or coastal site is losing moisture from the leaf surface faster than the same lawn in a sheltered spot would, even at the same temperature. It's not a soil problem or a product problem — it's an evapotranspiration problem.

## What Wind Does to a Lawn

Leaf surfaces lose water through transpiration. The rate at which this happens is partly determined by the vapour gradient between the leaf surface and the surrounding air. Wind strips the humid air layer from around the leaf and replaces it with drier air, accelerating moisture loss. The plant responds by closing its stomata to conserve water — which also slows photosynthesis. The result is slower growth, more stress, and faster wilting during dry periods.

On sandy or shallow soils, this compounds quickly. A coastal lawn on sandy soil in an onshore wind can show drought stress within a couple of days without irrigation-general, even in spring.

Salt spray from coastal wind adds another layer. Airborne salt deposits on the leaf surface create osmotic stress and physical leaf damage. In extreme coastal exposure, the windward face of grass leaves shows browning from salt accumulation during dry periods.

## Species Choice

Drought tolerance and wind resistance go together — a deeper root system handles both stresses better than a shallow-rooted species.

**NZLA Elfin Tall Fescue** or **Ultimate blend** is the right cool-season choice for exposed sites. Deeper roots than ryegrass, better drought tolerance, handles wind-drying stress more effectively. For any exposed site in the cool-season turf regions of NZ, this is the species to use over pure ryegrass.

**NZLA Kikuyu** in upper North Island warm-climate areas handles wind and drought well once established. Aggressive root and stolon system accesses water from a wide soil volume.

Avoid species with high water requirements on exposed sites. Fine-leaved premium ryegrass cultivars bred for density and colour perform well in sheltered, well-watered conditions and suffer on exposed sites.

## Management Adjustments

**Wetting agent programme.** NZLA Wetter or Wetter 3W should be a standard feature of the programme, not an option. Pre-summer application (October/November) and monthly through the dry season. On sandy exposed soils, consider applying every 3 to 4 weeks rather than monthly.

**Irrigation timing and rate.** Water in the early morning before the wind picks up rather than mid-afternoon when evaporation is highest. Deep watering (15 to 25 mm) twice a week beats daily light application because more of the water makes it into the rootzone where it's accessible.

**Mowing height.** 50 to 60 mm on exposed sites. Taller leaf provides some self-shading of the soil surface, reduces evaporation from the soil, and buffers the plant against wind stress better than a tightly mown lawn.

**Fertiliser approach.** More frequent foliar applications (NZLA Charger, Liquid Boost, Iron+ monthly) supplement the granular base fertiliser on exposed sites. On sandy exposed soils, granular fertiliser leaches faster due to higher evapotranspiration driving more irrigation-general events. Foliar feeds are efficient because they bypass the leaching problem.

**Spray timing.** Never apply contact herbicides on a windy day. Drift, evaporation of product before uptake, and plant stress from the wind all reduce efficacy. Pick a calm morning window. For regions like Wellington, this might mean watching the forecast and planning around the calm days rather than a set schedule.

## Physical Solutions

Windbreaks are worth far more than any product in genuinely exposed sites. Even a 1.5 to 2 metre hedge reduces wind speed significantly in the protected zone downwind. The protected distance is roughly 5 to 10 times the hedge height. A 2-metre macrocarpa or native hedge can improve the lawn microclimate for 10 to 20 metres behind it.

Native NZ windbreak species — ngaio, pittosporum, coprosma, kowhai — establish well and provide year-round shelter. Planning a windbreak is a 3 to 5 year investment but transforms what's achievable on the lawn behind it.

For urban sections where a large hedge isn't practical, fences and structures create smaller sheltered zones. Even partial shelter on the windward side of the property makes a measurable difference to the protected portions.

## Coastal Wind Plus Salt

Exposed coastal sections combine wind desiccation with salt spray. Species selection is even more important here — couch and tall fescue handle this combination markedly better than ryegrass. See the coastal and sandy soil section for the full management approach on combined wind and salt sites.

## Realistic Expectations

An exposed ridge-top lawn at the same level of management effort will look different from a sheltered valley lawn. That's not a failure of products or technique — it's the physics of the site. With drought-tolerant species, a consistent wetting agent programme, and regular foliar feeding, an exposed lawn can look good. It won't match a sheltered lawn without a windbreak to change the microclimate.
