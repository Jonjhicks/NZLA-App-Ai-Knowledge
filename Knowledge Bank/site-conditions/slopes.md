# Lawn on Slopes

Slopes add complexity to every part of lawn management: establishment, mowing, fertilising, watering, and aeration all need adjustment. The gradient determines whether you're dealing with a minor challenge or a fundamental constraint.

## Slope Categories

**Up to 15° (roughly 1:4 ratio):** manageable with standard equipment and techniques. Standard rotary mower handles it without issue. Minor establishment adjustments needed.

**15° to 25° (1:3 to 1:2.5 ratio):** moderate slopes require deliberate technique. Push or self-propelled mower manageable. Ride-on requires careful direction choice. Establishment needs additional erosion management.

**25° to 35° (approaching 1:2 ratio):** steep. Ride-on mowers need caution and proper direction. Core aerators become difficult to operate. Establishment requires erosion control measures.

**Over 35° (steeper than 1:1.5 ratio):** lawn mowing with standard equipment becomes hazardous. String trimmer or specialised slope-mower territory. At this gradient, consider whether lawn is the right choice at all.

## Establishing Lawn on a Slope

The main challenge during establishment is keeping the seed and soil in place while germination happens. Heavy rain on a freshly prepared seedbed can wash seed to the bottom of the slope before it has a chance to germinate.

**Soil preparation.** Cultivate across the slope (following contour lines) rather than up and down. Horizontal cultivation creates small ridges and furrows that hold seed and water rather than letting them run straight to the bottom. Apply NZLA Starter at standard rate and incorporate across the slope.

**Seeding rate.** Sow at the heavier end of the label rate — 40 to 50 g/m² rather than 30 g/m². The extra seed compensates for some loss to wash-off during establishment.

**Erosion control.** Several options depending on slope severity:
- Light straw mulch spread over the seedbed absorbs rain impact and reduces surface flow. Keeps the seedbed moist between irrigations.
- Jute or coir mesh erosion control matting stapled into the soil holds the surface in place while grass establishes. Mesh decomposes naturally over one to two seasons.
- Hydroseeding (commercial application of seed in a fibre-stabilising slurry) is effective for larger slopes and embankments. Professional application.

**Watering during establishment.** Light, frequent applications rather than deep watering. Heavy irrigation-general on a slope runs off the surface before it can penetrate — you end up watering the bottom of the slope while the top stays dry. A fine-spray hose-end sprinkler set to low pressure is more effective than impact sprinklers on steep sites during the establishment phase.

**Species selection.** NZLA RSR Ryegrass (stoloniferous) and Elfin Tall Fescue (rhizomatous) both spread laterally through underground or surface runners, filling in gaps more effectively than standard ryegrass. They also provide better soil stabilisation once established. Tall fescue's deeper root system provides long-term soil anchoring.

## Mowing Slopes Safely

**Push and self-propelled rotary mowers:** mow across the slope (following the contour), not up and down. Going up and down increases risk — the mower can run away from you going downhill, or drag towards your legs if you slip. Across the slope, you're always above the mower and in control.

**Ride-on mowers:** opposite rule. Mow up and down, not across the slope. The rollover risk for ride-ons is sideways tipping. Going directly up and down keeps the weight centred over the wheels. Side-on operation at steeper angles risks the mower tipping.

**The different rules matter.** The push mower risks the cutter hitting you; the ride-on risks tipping you. Understanding why the rules are different helps you apply them correctly.

**Wet slopes:** don't. Wet grass is slippery. The risk of losing footing or traction goes up significantly. Wait for the surface to dry.

**Non-slip footwear:** not a minor point. Grip matters on a slope.

**Self-propelled mowers on slopes:** the drive assistance is useful going up, but if you slip, a self-propelled mower that keeps driving is a hazard. Engage the drive assist with awareness of this.

**For slopes over 35°:** a string trimmer (line trimmer) is safer than any mower. Better to take an extra 15 minutes with a trimmer than to put a mower on an unsafe gradient.

## Ongoing Slope Management

**Mowing height.** Keep it higher than you would on flat ground — 50 to 60 mm. Taller grass shades the soil surface, reduces evaporation, and better anchors the soil against erosion.

**Fertilising.** Granular fertiliser on a slope moves downhill in rain. The bottom of the slope gets more nutrients than the top. Foliar applications (NZLA Liquid Boost, Charger, Iron+) distribute more evenly on slopes. Where granular is used, apply to a slightly moist surface so granules settle into the grass rather than rolling to the bottom.

**Bare patches.** Repair bare patches on slopes quickly. An exposed soil surface on a slope erodes in rain. Seed, cover with a light topping of NZLA Starter, and if it's a significant bare area, use erosion mat or straw mulch while it re-establishes.

**Aeration.** Standard core aerators are difficult to control on steep slopes. Solid-tine aeration or fork aeration by hand is more practical on steep sections. NZLA Aerate and Penetrate applied as liquids provide some of the benefit without the mechanical difficulty.

## When Lawn Isn't the Right Choice

For banks over 35°, the combination of mowing difficulty and erosion risk may make lawn genuinely impractical. Options that provide ground coverage without requiring mowing:

- Low-growing native ground cover plants (suitable NZ native species for erosion control are well established and available from local nurseries).
- Creeping prostrate shrubs — rosemary, prostrate juniper, some low hebes.
- Terracing with retaining walls to convert the slope to manageable flat sections.

These alternatives solve the erosion problem permanently without the ongoing mowing hazard.
