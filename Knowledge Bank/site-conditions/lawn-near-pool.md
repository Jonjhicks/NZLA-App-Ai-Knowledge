# Lawn Near a Swimming Pool

Pools and lawns create a few specific management challenges. None of them are unsolvable, but they're worth knowing about before you start spending money on products.

---

## Chlorine splash and runoff

Pool water splashing onto lawn is the most common issue. Chlorine (sodium hypochlorite in traditional pools) is essentially bleach — direct contact with turf at high concentration causes bleaching and tip burn.

**Splash damage** from normal pool use is usually minor and localised to the pool edge. Grass within 300 to 500 mm of the pool edge may show periodic yellowing. This is cosmetic and recovers between swim seasons.

**Backwash runoff** is more serious. Pool filter backwash contains concentrated chlorine and is high-volume. Directing backwash repeatedly onto the same turf area will damage or kill grass over time. Redirect backwash to a drain or area away from lawn.

**What helps:** keep pool water chemistry balanced (avoid over-chlorinating), maintain grass density near the pool edge with regular Charger + Iron+ foliar applications to keep it resilient and recovering quickly.

---

## Salt water pools

Salt water pools use a chlorinator to convert dissolved salt (NaCl) to chlorine. The pool water itself has a similar chlorine level to a traditional pool when correctly maintained.

The concern with salt-water splash isn't the chlorine — it's the salt accumulation. Repeated small amounts of salt-water splash onto the same area will cause salt build-up in the soil, increasing osmotic pressure on root uptake.

**Management:** Penetrate at 200 mL/100m² applied around the pool edge in spring and autumn helps move salt through the soil profile. On very impacted areas, a deep drench irrigation-general to leach salt is useful.

---

## pH and algaecide drift

Lawn care products applied near a pool need care:

**Spray drift into pool water:** Herbicides (broadleaf or grass-selective) drifting into pool water create a contamination issue. Don't spray herbicides on a windy day near a pool. Maintain the minimum 15 km/h wind rule strictly and spray away from the pool. A light shield (cardboard, corflute) held between the spray and the pool edge provides good protection during spot treatments.

**Algaecides and pool shock:** Pool algaecides (typically quaternary ammonium compounds) splashed onto grass in concentration can burn turf. Similar mechanism to fertiliser burn. If pool shock or algaecide lands on the lawn, flush immediately with water.

---

## Edge management

The lawn-to-pool interface accumulates grass clippings, fertiliser granules, and organic debris in the pool. Regular edging and sweeping prevents this. A wider gap (200 to 300 mm pavers or concrete edging) between turf and pool is the most effective long-term solution.

Don't apply granular fertiliser directly to the pool edge. If granules enter the pool, they contribute nitrogen to pool chemistry and can cause algae issues. Apply foliar fertilisers to the area immediately adjacent to the pool rather than granulars — no granule scatter risk.

---

## Herbicide use near pools

Apply broadleaf herbicides (Gold, BWC) on calm days only. A small amount of spray drift into pool water isn't catastrophic but the practice of spraying near pools in windy conditions should be avoided.

For selective grass herbicides like Halo or Blockade: same precaution. Keep these away from pool water.

If you need to treat  at the very pool edge and can't avoid spray drift risk: spot treat with a small handheld sprayer on a calm day, directing spray downward and shielding the pool edge.

---

## Salt-tolerant species around pools

In very high-splash zones, species selection makes a difference. Fine fescue and tall fescue handle periodic chlorine and salt splash better than perennial ryegrass. For a pool-adjacent lawn that takes significant regular splash, NZLA Ultimate Tall Fescue or an appropriate fescue blend is worth considering over straight ryegrass.
