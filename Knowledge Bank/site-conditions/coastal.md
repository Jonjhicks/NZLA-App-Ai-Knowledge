# Coastal and Sandy Soil Lawns

Coastal and sandy soil lawns share the same fundamental challenge: low water and nutrient retention. Add salt spray on exposed coastal sites and you've got three stresses running simultaneously.

## The Challenge Stack

**Sandy soil drains too fast.** Sandy soil has low cation exchange capacity (CEC) — it can't hold onto water or nutrients the way clay or loam can. Fertiliser applied in a heavy rain event leaches through the profile before the plant can use much of it. The lawn dries out between irrigations faster than a clay-based lawn would. The programme needs to account for this throughout the year.

**Coastal salt spray accumulates on leaves and in soil.** Salt spray carried by onshore winds lands on grass leaves. A single event isn't a problem — the grass handles it. Persistent salt accumulation over dry periods is where damage happens. Salt interferes with the plant's ability to take up water (osmotic stress), leading to browning on the windward leaf surface. Heavy rain helps flush salt off leaves and dilute it in the soil. During dry windy spells it accumulates.

**Wind compounds both problems.** Wind speeds up water loss from the leaf surface beyond what passive evaporation would cause. Even on a day that isn't hot, a consistent coastal wind dries a sandy-soil lawn faster than an inland sheltered clay lawn would in comparable conditions.

## Species Choice

**Tall fescue** — NZLA Elfin or Ultimate blend — is the best all-round cool-season choice for coastal and sandy soil sites. Deeper roots than ryegrass reach further down the soil profile for water. Moderate salt tolerance. Handles wind desiccation better than ryegrass. Worth prioritising over pure ryegrass mixes on sandy soil.

**NZLA Couch** in the upper North Island handles salt and sandy soils well and is a natural fit for coastal properties where warm-season turf is appropriate. Once established, it's drought-tolerant and aggressively self-repairing.

**Kikuyu** is similarly tolerant of coastal conditions in the upper NI — its deep stoloniferous growth handles sandy soils and recovers well from salt events.

**Ryegrass** is less salt-tolerant and has shallower roots than tall fescue. Still usable on coastal sites with adequate management, but not the first choice.

**Fine fescue** is less suited to exposed coastal conditions — it suits shade and cool damp inland sites more than sandy exposed ones.

## Soil Moisture Management

NZLA Wetter or Wetter 3W is an important part of the coastal lawn programme, not just an option. Sandy soil becomes hydrophobic when dry — water hits the surface and runs off rather than penetrating. A pre-summer Wetter 3W application (October/November) sets up the soil for summer. Follow with Wetter or Wetter 3W monthly through the dry season.

Watering strategy: 15 to 25 mm twice a week rather than light daily watering. Sandy soil won't hold what you apply if you water lightly — the moisture evaporates before the plant can use it. Deep watering gets moisture down to the rootzone where it's accessible.

After heavy rain, hold off irrigation-general for several days. Sandy soils drain fast but the profile does hold a useful amount of water after a significant rainfall event.

## Fertiliser Approach

Apply fertiliser at moderate rates more frequently rather than heavy rates twice a year. The leaching that happens through sandy soil makes a 6 kg/100m² application less efficient than three applications of 3 kg spread through the growing season. NZLA All Seasons granular at lighter rates every 6 to 8 weeks during the growing season is the right approach.

Foliar feeding between granular applications is particularly useful on sandy soil because it bypasses the soil retention problem — NZLA Charger or Liquid Boost applied monthly keeps visible nutrients in the plant rather than relying solely on what the soil is holding. Iron+ on a 4 to 6 week cycle maintains colour.

## Salt Management

After significant onshore wind events in dry conditions, a light irrigation-general of the lawn flushes salt off the leaf surface and dilutes what's in the top 50 mm of soil. This is simple and effective. You don't need to do anything more complex than run the sprinkler for 15 to 20 minutes after a salt event.

Regular mowing removes salt-damaged leaf tips before the damage accumulates.

Species selection is the long-term salt management tool. The right species choice reduces how much salt spray matters.

## Long-Term Soil Improvement

Sandy soil CEC improves with organic matter addition over time. Top dressing with sandy loam plus compost rather than pure sand gradually builds the water and nutrient holding capacity. Mulch mowing returns clippings to the soil. NZLA Humic+ supports organic matter development and microbial activity.

This is a years-to-decades process, not a single-season fix. Start it and be consistent. Each year's top dressing is a marginal improvement that compounds over time.

## Pumice and Volcanic Soils

Pumice soils in the Bay of Plenty, central North Island, and parts of Rotorua and Taupo share the same fundamental drainage-and-soil-moisture characteristics as sandy coastal soils but without the salt exposure. They have very low CEC and leach water and nutrients fast. The same programme applies: frequent moderate fertiliser applications, wetting agent programme, deep infrequent watering, organic matter building over time.

## Realistic Expectations

A coastal lawn on exposed sandy ground will not look the same as a sheltered inland lawn on loam. It doesn't mean the lawn can't look good — it means the inputs are higher, some browning on the windward face is normal, and the programme needs to be consistent. With the right species, a wetter agent programme, and adapted fertiliser approach, a well-maintained coastal lawn looks good most of the year.
