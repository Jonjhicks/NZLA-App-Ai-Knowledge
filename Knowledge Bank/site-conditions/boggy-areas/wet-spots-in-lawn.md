# Why are some areas of my lawn always wet?

Several possible causes. Identifying which one is the issue points to the right fix.

Low spot collecting water:
- Surface water drains downhill to a low point and pools.
- Lawn in that spot is constantly damp.
- Fix is either to raise the low spot (top dress over multiple seasons) or improve drainage out of it.

High water table:
- Ground water sits close to the surface in that area.
- Common in low-lying suburbs, near water bodies, on river plains.
- Subsoil drainage required for serious cases.
- Long-term issue, not something soil amendments fix.

Compacted soil layer below the surface:
- Surface water can't move down through compacted layer.
- Common where lawns sit over old building rubble, gravel access tracks, or driveway over-runs.
- Aeration plus NZLA Aerate may help if compaction is shallow.
- Deep mechanical aeration for deeper compaction.

Spring or seep from underground:
- Water emerges from underground in that spot.
- Often appears in winter and dries in summer.
- Common at the base of slopes where water moves laterally through soil.
- Drainage installation needed if the area is important.

Clay layer over subsoil:
- Surface lawn on permeable soil, but clay layer below traps water.
- Water builds up at the clay layer until it overflows at the surface.
- Drainage installation or deep aeration may help.

Soil naturally wet:
- Some sites are just wet ground. Drained swampy area, low coastal land, river floodplain.
- Lawn species selection matters more than drainage installation.
- Some lawn species tolerate wet conditions better than others.

Identification approach:
- Dig a 300 mm hole in the wet area in dry weather.
- Watch how water enters the hole.
- Water seeping in from the sides at depth: water table issue.
- Water sitting on top after rain but soil dry below: surface compaction or hydrophobic layer.
- Soil saturated throughout the profile: high water table.

Quick wins for typical home lawn wet spots:
- Aeration (core or solid tine) to admit air to the soil.
- NZLA Aerate to improve water movement.
- NZLA Penetrate to push surface water down through the profile.
- Cultural management: don't walk on wet patches, keep traffic off, raise mowing height.

For chronic wet patches that don't respond to surface treatments, drainage installation is the durable fix. See "how do I improve drainage" Q&A for the layered approach.
