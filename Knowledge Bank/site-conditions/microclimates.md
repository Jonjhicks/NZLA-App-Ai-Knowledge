# Microclimates in Suburban Lawns

A single property can have three or four genuinely different growing environments within a few metres of each other. The part of the lawn that keeps dying might not have a  problem or a nutrient deficiency — it might simply be sitting in conditions that are fundamentally different from the rest of the lawn.

## What Creates Microclimates

**Sun exposure.** In the southern hemisphere, north-facing areas receive significantly more sun than south-facing areas. A north-facing slope or the south side of a house can be the difference between 8 hours of direct sun and 2 hours. More sun means warmer temperatures, faster evaporation, higher water demand, and more  stress from heat. South-facing areas stay cooler, damper, and are more susceptible to fusarium and moss.

**Shade from structures.** A fence, garage, or neighbouring house doesn't just reduce light — it changes the whole microenvironment. Shaded areas stay wetter longer, have slower evaporation, and accumulate moisture that favours cool-season . The edge between full shade and full sun often shows a clear line in the lawn's condition.

**Wind exposure.** Prevailing wind hits one side of the property harder. An exposed corner dries out faster, needs more water, and shows salt stress on coastal properties. A sheltered corner holds moisture and can develop moss or algae. The mowing strip along a sheltered fence line behaves completely differently from the open middle of the lawn.

**Heat from hard surfaces.** Paving, concrete paths, driveways, and brick walls absorb and re-radiate heat. Lawn adjacent to a concrete driveway on a north-facing aspect can be 5°C warmer than the same lawn 3 metres away. This accelerates growth in spring, increases stress in summer, and changes the season timing for that zone.

**Surface water flow.** Water moves across and through the property. Low spots collect it, high spots shed it. Roof runoff concentrates in specific spots. Driveway runoff tracks across the lawn edge. One side of the lawn may be getting more water than the other simply from how rainfall moves across the section.

**Soil variation.** Different parts of the same lawn often have different soil. Building sites add fill. Previous owners install drainage-and-soil-moisture in one corner. A tree was removed and the hole filled with different material. An old concrete path was broken up and buried. The soil under the back lawn might be half a metre of quality topsoil while the front lawn sits on 100 mm of topsoil over gravel. These differences produce real variation in how the lawn responds to the same programme.

## Recognising and Responding to Microclimates

**Diagnose before treating.** If a section of lawn consistently underperforms — won't green up with fertiliser, keeps getting stays dry or stays wet — look at the environment before assuming it's a product or management issue. What's different about that spot? Is it shadier? Wetter? More exposed? Does it have different soil?

**Zone-based management.** Treating a varied lawn as a single uniform area leads to either over-applying in tolerant zones or under-applying in stressed zones. Once you've identified the main zones, adjust accordingly:
- Shaded zones: less fertiliser, shade-tolerant species on reseed, less watering, fungicide monitoring
- Exposed/dry zones: wetting agent, more frequent watering, drought-tolerant species, Iron+ for colour support
- Wet zones: aeration, top dressing, species suitable for intermittent wet conditions
- Hot paved-adjacent zones: more water monitoring, higher mowing height

**Species zoning.** On a major overseed or renovation, you can sow different species into different zones. NZLA Fine Fescue for the shaded south-facing side, NZLA RSR Ryegrass or Ultimate blend for the open sunny lawn. The lawn comes up looking similar in colour and texture but is better suited to each area's actual conditions.

## Common Suburban Patterns

The front lawn tends to get more attention and more sun (often a north-facing street aspect in NZ). Back lawns carry more wear from kids and pets and often have more shade from the house in the afternoon.

Side lawns between the house and fence are frequently the worst performers on a property. Narrow strips in shade with restricted air movement, often used as a thoroughfare creating a worn track. These areas benefit from shade-tolerant species, higher mowing height, and reduced expectations.

The washing line zone gets concentrated foot traffic from the same path every wash day. Over a season that creates a worn track. The same goes for the path between the back door and the shed, or the route the dog takes along the fence.

Recognising these patterns means you can either protect those zones with stepping stones or accept the wear and build in annual reseeding as part of the routine.
