# Boggy Areas and Wet Lawn Spots

Wet ground is trying to tell you something. Before throwing products or seed at it, work out what's causing the wetness — because the fix depends entirely on the cause.

## Why Parts of a Lawn Stay Wet

**Low spot collecting surface water.** The most common cause. Water drains downhill to the lowest point and pools there. The lawn never fully dries out because every rain event refills the low spot. Fix is either to raise the spot with top dressing over multiple seasons, re-grade the area to redirect water flow, or install drainage-and-soil-moisture out of it.

**High water table.** Ground water sits close to the surface in that location. Common in low-lying suburbs, near streams or water bodies, on river plains and coastal flats. This is a structural issue — soil amendments and aeration don't fix a water table. Subsoil drainage-and-soil-moisture is needed for serious cases.

**Compacted layer below the surface.** Water percolates down through the topsoil then hits a compacted layer (clay pan, old gravel track, building rubble, construction core-aeration) and can't move further. It backs up and sits in the root zone. Core aeration and NZLA Aerate can help where the core-aeration is shallow. Deeper core-aeration may need mechanical deep tine aeration.

**Spring or seep from underground.** Water emerges from underground — often from a lateral flow through the soil at the base of a slope. These spots are typically dry in summer and wet in winter. Installing a drainage-and-soil-moisture cut-off above the seep redirects the flow before it reaches the lawn.

**Clay layer trapping water.** Surface soil is permeable but a clay layer below holds water. Fills up from below and the saturation works up through the profile. Drainage installation or deep aeration may help.

**Identifying which it is:** dig a 300 mm hole in the wet area during dry weather. Watch where water enters. Seeping in from the sides at depth suggests a water table issue. Sitting on top after rain but dry below suggests surface core-aeration or a hydrophobic layer. Saturated throughout the profile points to high water table.

## Quick Wins for Modest Wet Patches

Not all wet spots need drainage-and-soil-moisture installation. For mild to moderate cases:

- Core aerate or solid tine aerate to break core-aeration and admit air to the soil profile. NZLA Aerate liquid applied into the aeration holes improves water movement through the soil matrix.
- NZLA Penetrate applied after rain events helps push surface water down through the profile rather than letting it sit.
- Raise the mowing height in wet areas (50 to 60 mm). More leaf, better photosynthesis, more transpiration pulling water out of the soil.
- Keep traffic off wet patches. Walking on saturated soil compacts it further and makes the problem worse.
- Top dress low spots repeatedly over multiple seasons to raise the surface and improve drainage-and-soil-moisture away from the area.

For most home lawn wet patches, a combination of aeration and top dressing over two to three seasons makes a meaningful improvement.

## Growing Lawn in Genuinely Wet Conditions

If the site is wet because it's just wet ground — drained swamp, low coastal land, floodplain — drainage-and-soil-moisture installation is the durable fix. Surface management makes a marginal improvement at best.

Where drainage-and-soil-moisture isn't practical, species selection matters:

Tall fescue handles intermittent waterlogging better than ryegrass. The deeper root system tolerates periods of wet conditions more than shallow-rooted ryegrass cultivars. NZLA Ultimate or Elfin Tall Fescue blends are the right choice for consistently damp sites.

Ryegrass and fine fescue both struggle under persistent waterlogging. Fine turf species (browntop, bentgrass) are the least tolerant.

Cultural adjustments for wet lawn areas: mow at 50 to 60 mm, reduce fertiliser inputs (anaerobic wet soil handles nitrogen poorly — excess drives  rather than growth), and keep machinery and foot traffic off when wet.

## When Lawn Isn't the Right Answer

Some sites genuinely aren't lawn ground. If you've addressed drainage-and-soil-moisture, tried appropriate species, and the lawn still fails every wet season, the site is telling you something.

Alternatives that work with wet conditions rather than fighting them:
- Bog garden with wet-tolerant plantings (native sedges, irises, flaxes, cardinal flower).
- Rain garden designed to handle intermittent inundation.
- Raised garden bed or terraced planting over a gravel base.
- Native wetland plantings for rural and lifestyle block sites.

Repeatedly replanting grass in fundamentally unsuitable conditions is expensive and demoralising. Recognising the site limitation and changing the design is often the better investment.

## Soil Chemistry in Chronic Wet Areas

Persistently wet soil goes anaerobic. The smell of hydrogen sulphide (rotten eggs) indicates anaerobic conditions. Soil ecology in anaerobic conditions is impaired: nutrient cycling slows, beneficial microbes are replaced by anaerobic bacteria, black layer forms. pH often drops due to acid accumulation.

Before establishing lawn in a previously boggy area that's been drained or improved, test the soil. pH, nutrient levels, and organic matter content may all need addressing before grass can establish successfully.
