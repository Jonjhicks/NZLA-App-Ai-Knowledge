# How do I manage a coastal lawn?

Three combined challenges: salt spray, sandy soil, and constant wind.

Salt management:
- Salt spray from ocean lands on grass leaves and accumulates over time.
- Heavy rain helps flush salt off leaves and out of soil.
- During dry windy spells, salt accumulates faster.
- Periodic freshwater irrigation flushes both leaf and soil salt.

Salt-tolerant species:
- NZLA Couch (cold-tolerant bermuda) handles salt spray reasonably well.
- Tall fescue has moderate salt tolerance.
- Kikuyu handles salt better than ryegrass.
- Ryegrass and fine fescue are less salt-tolerant.
- For severe coastal exposure, look at warm-season species (in upper NI) or seashore paspalum (specialty species).

Sandy soil management:
- Most coastal NZ soils are sandy.
- Low CEC, water and nutrients drain through fast.
- Wetting agents help retain moisture (NZLA Wetter or Wetter 3W).
- More frequent fertiliser applications at lower rates.
- Build organic matter over time with top dressing and humic acids.

Wind management:
- Constant wind dries leaves faster than passive evaporation.
- Windbreaks (hedges, fences, shrubs) reduce wind speed at lawn level.
- Higher cutting height (50 to 60 mm) provides some leaf protection.
- Drought-tolerant species handle wind stress better.

Fertility programme:
- Granular base at standard rate (NZLA All Seasons or All Seasons N+).
- More frequent foliar applications (Liquid Boost, Iron+, Charger) to keep nutrients available between rainfall events that flush sandy soil.
- Higher iron application than typical because coastal sandy soils often have iron locked up.

Watering:
- Sandy soils need more frequent watering than clay.
- Deep watering 2 to 3 times a week rather than daily light applications.
- After heavy rain, no irrigation for several days while soil drains.

Hydrophobic dry patch:
- Sandy soils are prone to becoming hydrophobic in dry weather.
- NZLA Wetter or Wetter 3W as standard summer practice.
- Pre-summer Wetter 3W application carries through the worst of the dry season.

Long-term improvements:
- Top dressing with sandy loam plus compost over years gradually improves soil.
- Native windbreak planting protects the lawn.
- Realistic expectations - coastal lawns won't look like inland sheltered lawns.

For typical NZ coastal property lawns, the combination of right species choice, regular wetting agent use, and accepting some limitations from the exposure gets a serviceable lawn. Premium lawn quality is harder to achieve at the coast than inland.
