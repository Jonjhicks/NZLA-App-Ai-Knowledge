# How do I manage a wind-exposed lawn?

Wind exposed lawns sit on ridges, hilltops, open paddocks or exposed coastal sites where constant wind affects how the lawn grows.

What wind does:
- Dries leaves faster than passive evaporation.
- Reduces soil moisture availability through increased evapotranspiration.
- Can physically damage soft new growth.
- Carries salt spray inland on coastal sites.

Symptoms:
- Lawn browns on the windward side (the side facing prevailing wind).
- Patchy growth where some areas catch more wind than others.
- Higher fertiliser and water requirement than sheltered lawns.
- Difficulty maintaining colour through summer.

Species selection:
- Drought-tolerant species handle wind stress better (tall fescue, kikuyu in upper NI).
- NZLA Ultimate Tall Fescue or Elfin Tall Fescue for cool-season exposed sites.
- NZLA Kikuyu for warm-region exposed sites.
- Avoid species that need consistent moisture (premium ryegrass cultivars struggle).

Management adjustments:
- Higher mowing height (50 to 60 mm) for leaf protection.
- More frequent watering during dry windy spells.
- Wetting agents (NZLA Wetter or Wetter 3W) to retain soil moisture.
- More frequent foliar feeding (NZLA Charger monthly) because nutrients wash through faster.

Physical mitigation:
- Windbreaks (hedge, fence, shrub planting) significantly reduce wind speed at lawn level.
- Even a 1.5 m hedge cuts wind speed substantially over the lawn it protects.
- Strategic positioning of structures (sheds, fences) creates sheltered zones.

Soil considerations:
- Wind-exposed sites often have shallow soil that's been blown away historically.
- Top dressing with quality topsoil over years builds depth and water-holding capacity.
- Organic matter additions help.

Cultural management:
- Don't apply fertiliser before a forecast windy spell - will dry off the lawn faster.
- Mow in cool calm conditions where possible.
- Reseed promptly into any bare patches before erosion takes the soil.

Coastal wind plus salt:
- Compounded challenge of two stresses.
- See coastal-lawn Q&A for combined approach.

For lifestyle blocks on exposed sites:
- Strategic windbreak planting transforms the lawn microclimate.
- Native NZ shelter species (kowhai, ngaio, pittosporum) work well.
- 5-year plan for windbreak establishment changes what's possible for the lawn.

For urban exposed lots:
- Existing structures (neighbour's house, garage, fences) often provide some shelter.
- Identify the actual exposure pattern before assuming wind is uniform.
- Species and management for the exposed parts, standard programme for sheltered parts.

Realistic expectations:
- Wind-exposed lawns won't look the same as sheltered lawns at the same level of effort.
- The right species and a wetter focus get a good functional lawn.
- Premium quality requires shelter.
