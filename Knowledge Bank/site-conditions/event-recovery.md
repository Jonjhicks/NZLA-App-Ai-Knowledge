# Lawn Recovery After Events

Marquees, weddings, large gatherings, and other events can do significant damage in a short time. The damage is real and recovery takes work, but a well-established lawn recovers faster than most people expect.

---

## What an event does to a lawn

**Compaction.** The primary issue. Multiple people walking on the same ground compresses the soil profile, drives out air, and restricts root growth. A saturated lawn gets far worse core-aeration than a dry one — soil particles can be moved permanently with wet-ground foot traffic.

**Physical turf damage.** Direct traffic tears leaf tissue, breaks stems, and in severe cases physically removes turf. High heels are disproportionately damaging — the contact pressure from a stiletto heel is similar to that of a small bulldozer.

**Marquee poles and pegs.** Concentrated puncture damage, usually not a significant issue on its own.

**Light deprivation.** A marquee or floor covering over turf for more than 72 hours in warm conditions will bleach the grass beneath (etiolation). The longer the event structure sits, the worse this is. In summer, 5 to 7 days under a solid cover can kill the grass.

**Soil and thatch disruption.** Heavy foot traffic on an established sward pushes the surface layer down unevenly. This shows as an irregular, bumpy surface after recovery.

---

## Assessment after the event

Walk the area and assess four things:

1. **Thatch status.** Is the sward compressed and lying flat? Push it upright and check if it springs back. Compressed but alive = recovery is straightforward. Not springing = more work needed.

2. **Soil core-aeration.** Push a screwdriver or pencil into the soil. If it penetrates easily to 50 mm, core-aeration is minimal. If it's hard to push in at all, significant core-aeration has occurred.

3. **Physical turf survival.** How much live grass remains? If more than 60% of the area has live grass — even if it looks terrible — it will recover without reseeding. Below 40% cover, plan to reseed.

4. **Bleaching vs death.** Light-deprived grass looks yellow and weak but is usually alive. Pinch a leaf — if there's green at the base of the leaf blade, the plant is alive. Dead grass has no green anywhere and the leaf crumbles or tears without moisture.

## Recovery programme

### Immediately after the event

Remove structures and any temporary flooring as soon as possible. Light deprivation time matters.

Lightly irrigate if the area is dry. Don't irrigate if the soil is already saturated — core-aeration from foot traffic on wet ground is worse.

### Week 1

**Aerate.** Solid-tine or hollow-tine aeration. Get air back into the compacted profile. A hired walk-behind aerator covers ground quickly. If you have a small area, a fork can work but is slow.

**NZLA Penetrate** at 200 mL/100m² applied as a drench. Water in well. Penetrate breaks down hydrophobic layers and restores water and air movement through the soil profile. Particularly helpful on clay and fine-texture soils.

**Wetting agent** (Wetter 3W at 100 mL/100m²) helps redistribute moisture and supports recovery.

### Week 1 to 2

**Nutrition.** NZLA Liquid Boost 200 mL/100m² as a foliar if the turf is showing active but stressed growth. High nitrogen (15-0-0) gives the plant what it needs to push new growth. Don't apply if the grass is severely bleached and still recovering colour.

### Week 2 to 4

**All Seasons granular** 2 kg/100m² once the turf is actively growing. Supports sustained recovery.

**Azoxy** at 50 mL/100m² if the area is showing any signs of  pressure (soft, discoloured, water-soaked patches). Stressed turf after core-aeration is highly susceptible to dollar spot and fusarium.

**Topdress** if the surface is significantly uneven. Light layer of quality topdressing mix worked in with a drag mat or brush.

### Where reseeding is needed

Wait until the surviving grass is actively growing and the soil structure has been improved (post-aeration, post-penetrant). Resowing into compacted, hydrophobic soil wastes seed.

Oversow at 15 to 20 g/m² for ryegrass/fescue blends in bare or thin areas. Add NZLA Liquid Starter at sowing at 100 mL/100m² to support seedling establishment.

## Preventing the worst of it

For future events:

**Boards or walkway matting** distribute foot traffic load dramatically. Temporary event matting spread across high-traffic paths prevents concentrated core-aeration and reduces turf death significantly.

**Avoid events on a saturated lawn.** If the ground is wet from recent rain, even a small gathering does more damage than a large event on dry ground.

**Timing.** An event in April or May (cool-season regions) gives the lawn all of spring to recover. A December event means the grass is recovering through summer heat.

**Give your lawn a good feed 4 to 6 weeks before a major event.** A well-fed, dense turf handles foot traffic significantly better than a thin, hungry lawn.
