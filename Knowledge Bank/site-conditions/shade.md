# Lawn in Shade and Under Trees

Shade is the most commonly mismanaged condition in home lawn care. People apply more fertiliser and more chemistry trying to fix a lawn that's actually struggling because of insufficient light. No product compensates for photosynthesis that can't happen.

That said, shade-tolerant species and thoughtful management can produce a functional, reasonable-looking lawn in partial shade. Understanding where the line is makes the difference between a manageable situation and a frustrating ongoing failure.

## How Much Shade Is Too Much

**Full sun (6+ hours direct sun):** any NZ lawn species will perform here. This isn't the problem zone.

**Partial shade (4 to 6 hours direct sun):** NZLA Fine Fescue or tall fescue blend handles this well. With appropriate species and management adjustments, the lawn can look good.

**Heavy shade (2 to 4 hours direct sun):** this is the difficult zone. Fine fescue is the right species but the lawn will be thinner and less vibrant than a full-sun lawn. Management needs to be adjusted significantly. Results depend on the quality of the light that does reach the area.

**Deep shade (under 2 hours direct sun or dense canopy all day):** grass can't grow here long-term at any acceptable quality. This includes the zone under dense evergreen trees, the north-facing side of a solid 2-metre fence, and narrow slots between buildings. The answer is non-grass groundcover, mulch, or paving — not better grass.

## Species Selection

**NZLA Fine Fescue** (creeping red fescue plus chewings fescue blend) is the most shade-tolerant cool-season species available in the NZLA range. It's evolved in forest-edge conditions in cool temperate Europe and handles filtered light and moisture better than any other common lawn species. It has lower nitrogen requirements than ryegrass, slower growth, and better  resistance under low-light conditions.

Trade-offs: Fine fescue has lower wear tolerance than ryegrass. It's slow to establish and slow to recover from damage. It's also sensitive to a number of herbicides — check species restrictions before applying NZLA Etho, Gold, or others to a fine fescue lawn.

**NZLA Elfin Tall Fescue** or Ultimate blend handles partial shade (4 to 6 hours) well and is more wear-tolerant than fine fescue. For lawns that have both shaded and open areas, a blend of fine fescue and tall fescue is a practical choice — the fine fescue handles the shade, the tall fescue handles the sunnier sections.

**Ryegrass** (including RSR) needs 6+ hours of direct sun. It thins progressively in shade and eventually dies out. Don't establish or overseed pure ryegrass into areas getting less than 5 hours of direct sun.

**Warm-season species** (kikuyu, couch) need full sun. They won't perform in shade.

## Managing Lawn Under Trees

Trees add three layers of challenge on top of shade: root competition for water and nutrients, leaf litter, and often acidic soil from leaf decomposition.

**Root competition.** Tree roots — especially shallow surface-feeder species — compete directly with the lawn for water and nutrients. The lawn is often outcompeted during dry spells. Management response: more frequent irrigation-general during dry periods, more frequent foliar feeding (NZLA Liquid Boost, Iron+ bypass some of the root competition by feeding through the leaf rather than the soil), and moderate granular fertiliser applications rather than expecting one heavy application to feed both the tree and the lawn.

**Lifting the canopy.** Removing lower branches up to 2.5 to 3 metres height is often the most practical improvement for lawn under trees. More light reaches the ground, air movement improves, and the overall microenvironment shifts. This is more effective than any product application for improving lawn under trees.

**Leaf litter.** Leaves left on the lawn block light and create damp, anaerobic conditions that favour  and moss. Remove leaves regularly during autumn — don't let them sit for more than a week or two. A leaf blower through the area every 7 to 10 days through autumn keeps on top of it.

**Pine and conifer acid soils.** Pine needles add acidity to soil over years. Lawn under conifers often struggles from both shade and low pH simultaneously. Test soil pH if the lawn under conifers is persistently poor. Lime application may help. This is particularly relevant for older established properties with large pine trees.

**Deciduous vs evergreen trees.** Deciduous trees shed their canopy in winter and admit full winter sun. This is actually a reasonable environment for lawn — some deciduous tree areas grow reasonable lawns because summer shading is offset by winter light and the root competition is less severe when the tree is dormant. Dense evergreen trees (pine, macrocarpa, totara, kauri) create year-round deep shade and often year-round root competition — the most difficult conditions for any lawn species.

## Cultural Adjustments for Shade

**Raise the mowing height.** This is the most important management change. In shade, longer leaves capture more of the available light. Mow shaded areas at 60 to 80 mm rather than the 40 to 50 mm you might use for full sun.

**Reduce fertiliser.** Shaded turf can't process as much nitrogen as full-sun turf. Excess nitrogen in low-light conditions drives soft-susceptible growth. Use NZLA All Seasons at half the label rate in persistently shaded zones, and favour foliar applications (Liquid Boost, Iron+) over heavy granular applications.

**Reduce traffic.** Shaded turf has less recovery capacity than full-sun turf. It takes longer to repair from wear damage. Stepping stones, defined paths, or simply redirecting foot traffic away from shaded areas extends the time between repairs.

**Improve air movement.** Shade and low air movement together create ideal  conditions. Trimming lower branches, removing understory shrubs, and occasionally raking or blowing out leaf litter all improve air movement.

## When to Stop Trying

If you have an area that gets under 2 hours of direct sun, where fine fescue establishment has failed multiple times, and where you're spending money on products that don't produce results — the site is telling you something.

Alternatives that work with the conditions rather than fighting them:
- Dark bark mulch under trees looks better than thin patchy grass and requires no ongoing input.
- Shade-tolerant native groundcovers (many NZ native species perform well in shade).
- Stepping stones through the mulched area for access without bare soil.
- Garden bed with shade-tolerant plants — ferns, hebes, hostas, shade-loving perennials.

The cost of these alternatives is generally less than another year of repeatedly reseeding an area that can't support lawn.
