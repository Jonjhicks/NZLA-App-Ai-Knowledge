# Can I grow a lawn in heavy shade?

Difficult but possible with the right species and realistic expectations.

What heavy shade means:
- Less than 4 hours of direct sunlight per day.
- Filtered light through dense tree canopy all day.
- North-facing sides of buildings and walls (south-facing in southern hemisphere).
- Under mature trees with full canopy.

What grass struggles with in shade:
- Photosynthesis. No matter what chemistry you apply, grass needs light to convert into energy.
- Air movement. Shaded areas often have poor air flow, increasing disease pressure.
- Tree root competition. Trees take water and nutrients from soil that the lawn needs.
- Cool wet conditions. Shaded soil stays damp longer, favouring moss and disease.

Species selection:
- NZLA Fine Fescue (creeping red fescue and chewings fescue blend) is the most shade-tolerant cool-season option in NZ.
- Tall fescue handles moderate shade.
- Ryegrass struggles in shade - thins out quickly under low light.
- Warm-season grasses (kikuyu, couch) don't tolerate shade well at all.

Management adjustments:
- Lift cutting height (60 to 80 mm). Taller leaves capture more light.
- Reduce nitrogen. Shaded turf can't process as much nitrogen, excess invites disease.
- Improve air movement. Trim back lower tree branches if possible.
- Reduce traffic on shaded turf. Less recovery capacity than full sun turf.

Realistic expectations:
- A shaded lawn will never look like a full-sun lawn.
- Density will be lower.
- Recovery from damage will be slower.
- Some patches may be permanently thin.

Alternatives to lawn in heavy shade:
- Native ground covers (NZ-suitable ground cover plants).
- Mulch areas under trees instead of trying to grow grass.
- Shade-tolerant plants designed for the conditions.

For modest shade (4 to 6 hours direct sun): NZLA Fine Fescue or a fescue-heavy blend establishes and maintains reasonably well.

For deep shade (under 4 hours direct sun): be realistic. Either accept a thin lawn that won't be premium, or convert the area to a non-grass alternative.

Chemistry won't fix a light problem. Don't waste money on fertiliser, fungicide, or seed in deep shade where the limiting factor is sunlight itself.
