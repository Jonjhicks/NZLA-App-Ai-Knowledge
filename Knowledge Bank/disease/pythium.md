Pythium is a group of oomycetes — water moulds, not true fungi — that cause root and foliar diseases on stressed turf, primarily in warm, humid conditions. The distinction from fungi matters because some fungicide chemistries that work on true fungi have limited activity on oomycetes. NZLA Azoxy is on the label for pythium, so it remains the main tool in the range.

Three distinct forms show up on NZ lawns, each requiring a slightly different response.

## Pythium foliar blight

This is the dangerous one. Act fast or the lawn is gone.

Pythium foliar blight appears suddenly after a warm humid spell. Patches of greasy, slimy-looking turf develop and spread with alarming speed — in ideal conditions the  can destroy a significant area of lawn within 24 to 48 hours. That rate of spread is much faster than fusarium or brown patch and is the primary reason pythium demands immediate response.

**What it looks like:** Irregular greasy or water-soaked patches that appear almost overnight. In early morning before the dew lifts, a cottony grey-white mycelium may be visible draped across the affected area. The patches look flattened and matted. Individual blades are dark, slimy, and can be pulled apart without resistance.

**Conditions:** Air temperatures above 30°C with night temperatures above 20°C and high relative humidity. Wet turf from overnight irrigation-general, rain followed by persistent cloud cover, or poor drainage-and-soil-moisture — combined with that heat — is the trigger. Soft, lush growth from recent high-nitrogen application significantly increases susceptibility.

**NZ occurrence:** Primarily upper North Island — Auckland, Northland, Bay of Plenty — January through March in hot, humid summers. Rare but possible in Marlborough and Nelson under exceptional conditions. Uncommon on most NZ home lawns outside the upper North Island.

**Treatment:** Speed matters above everything else. NZLA Azoxy at the label rate applied immediately at the first sign of foliar blight. Don't mow 24 hours either side of application. Reduce irrigation-general immediately — drying the canopy is the first cultural response. Cut back nitrogen; soft growth from recent fertiliser feeds the .

NZLA Azoxy is the main tool in the NZLA range for pythium. For bowling greens, golf greens, or putting surfaces where pythium is a recurring pressure, professional-grade pythium chemistry (mefenoxam, propamocarb) through commercial channels is the complete answer.

**After the outbreak:** Pythium foliar blight kills the turf in affected patches. Once conditions dry out and the  is stopped, reseed the dead areas in autumn when temperatures come down.

## Pythium root rot

Root rot is slower and more insidious. The lawn shows gradual decline — yellowing, thinning, general loss of vigour — that's difficult to pin to a single cause without looking at the roots. Infected roots become discoloured (tan to brown), soft, and rotted.

**Conditions:** Poor drainage-and-soil-moisture, overwatered or waterlogged soils. Can occur year-round on poorly drained lawns — not purely a summer .

**Treatment:** Fix the drainage-and-soil-moisture first. NZLA Penetrate to move surface water through the soil profile. Reduce irrigation-general frequency. NZLA Azoxy for the chemical side. Address core-aeration through core aeration. Root rot recovery is slow — weeks to months depending on severity.

## Pythium damping-off

Damping-off affects newly germinated seedlings, killing them shortly after emergence. Classic scenario: spring or early summer seeding, warm soil, regular irrigation-general to keep the seedbed moist. The combination of warm wet conditions and tender young seedlings is ideal for pythium damping-off.

**Treatment:** Reduce irrigation-general frequency. The instinct to keep new seedings constantly moist creates damping-off conditions. Water deeply and less frequently — moisture in the root zone without permanent surface saturation. NZLA Azoxy at sowing can reduce pressure. If damping-off keeps recurring, reseed in autumn when cooler conditions suppress pythium.

## Reducing pythium risk

Early morning irrigation-general. Trim back hedges and plantings that create still, humid air. Moderate nitrogen through summer rather than high-N pushes. Adequate mowing height (40-50mm on home lawns). NZLA Penetrate through wet seasons to improve drainage-and-soil-moisture.

For most NZ home lawns outside the upper North Island, pythium is an occasional issue rather than a routine concern.
