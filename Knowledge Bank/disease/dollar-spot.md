# Dollar Spot

Dollar spot is the most widely distributed turfgrass  in the world. It shows up in New Zealand lawns every year — spring and autumn are the peak windows, but it appears in summer too when warm days give way to cool, humid nights and morning dew sits on the grass for hours.

## Identifying dollar spot

Patches are small, round, and bleached. Early on they're 30 to 50mm across — roughly the diameter of a coin, which is exactly where the name comes from. As the  progresses without treatment, patches merge into larger irregular damaged areas.

The best diagnostic tool is the individual leaf blade, not the patch. Look for an hourglass-shaped lesion: bleached white in the centre, with a reddish-brown to tan border crossing the blade. That pattern is specific to dollar spot. If it's there, you've got your answer.

In the morning, before the dew burns off, you'll often see a fine white cottony mycelium draped across the patches. It disappears as soon as the sun hits it or the dew evaporates. Catching it early morning is one of the clearest confirmation signs.

**Telling it apart from other diseases:**

Red thread produces pink to coral-coloured thread-like structures on the leaf tips — very different from the hourglass lesion of dollar spot. Both appear under low-fertility conditions, so you can have both at once, but the visual is distinct.

Fusarium patch prefers cooler, wetter conditions and produces orange-brown patches that can develop a darker outer ring. It's primarily a late autumn and winter . Dollar spot peaks in warmer months.

Brown patch appears in summer heat as larger irregular tan areas, often with a dark smoke ring around the margin. Dollar spot patches stay tighter and more defined.

## What causes it

The pathogen responsible is Clarireedia jacksonii (older resources use the name Sclerotinia homoeocarpa — same updated taxonomy). It spreads through infected clippings, mowing equipment, footwear, and water moving across the lawn surface.

The conditions it needs: warm days in the 20–30°C range, cool nights, high humidity, and prolonged leaf wetness from morning dew. The soil under an active dollar spot outbreak is often dry. The  thrives in a dry soil, wet canopy combination — the plant is stressed from below through lack of soil moisture and wet from above through dew and humidity. That combination makes the grass vulnerable.

Low nitrogen is the other key trigger. Dollar spot is a deficiency . Lawns that are underfed — growing slowly, light in colour, with long gaps between fertiliser applications — are far more susceptible than well-fed, actively growing turf.

Fine-leaf grasses (fescues, browntop bent) are more susceptible than perennial ryegrass. On a ryegrass lawn, dollar spot tends to be less aggressive and easier to manage culturally. On a fine fescue or bent lawn, it requires more consistent attention.

## Prevention

The single most useful prevention is keeping the lawn properly fed. Dollar spot is a low-fertility  — underfed lawns are the primary target. An actively growing, well-nourished lawn outpaces the ; an underfed lawn in slow growth can't.

**Steady fertility.** NZLA All Seasons (21-1-16 with 5% iron) every 6 to 8 weeks through the growing season. The potassium and iron in that formula harden cell walls and reduce susceptibility to fungal entry. Going longer than 8 weeks between applications creates the low-fertility window that dollar spot targets.

**Foliar top-ups.** NZLA Charger (7-0-7 with iron and manganese) every 3 to 4 weeks between granular applications keeps nitrogen in the critical range. Low salt index makes it safe in warm conditions.

**Iron supplementation.** NZLA Iron+ through the cooler months (roughly May to August) maintains plant hardiness without driving soft growth. Softer tissue is more vulnerable to infection.

**What NOT to do with fertility:** Don't hold back fertiliser to avoid . That logic applies to some diseases. Dollar spot is the opposite — underfeeding makes it worse. Don't use boom-and-bust timing either. Long low-fertility windows are exactly what the pathogen exploits.

**Leaf wetness management.** After nitrogen, how long the leaf stays wet is the other main driver of dollar spot pressure. Morning dew is the primary target. Disrupting the morning dew period — mowing early, dragging a hose across the surface, improving  — consistently shortens outbreaks. Dew removal in the morning has shown dramatic reductions in dollar spot severity in fine turf research.

Switch irrigation-general to early morning — midnight to 6am. Evening irrigation-general extends leaf wetness through the night, which is exactly the conditions dollar spot thrives in. Deep, infrequent irrigation-general is better than light, frequent applications.

**Mowing discipline.** Mow regularly with sharp blades. Blunt blades tear rather than cut, creating more entry points for infection. Regular cuts remove older leaf tissue and keep the sward in active growth.

## Seasonal risk in New Zealand

**September to November:** The spring window. Fertility programme resumes after winter, but if it's late getting started, the lawn is exposed. Dollar spot can establish fast in spring if the lawn is underfed heading into warm weather.

**March to May:** Often the worst period. Days are still warm enough for the pathogen, nights cool down quickly, and dew periods get longer. April and May are when the worst outbreaks typically build.

**December to February:** Less common but possible during warm, humid spells in northern New Zealand, particularly in areas with poor  or a history of the .

## Treating an active outbreak

**Step 1: Feed the lawn.**

Dollar spot is signalling that the lawn is underfed. A foliar nitrogen application is the fastest response. NZLA Charger every two weeks during the outbreak delivers nitrogen quickly and gets the lawn growing. An actively growing lawn outpaces the .

Don't hold back fertiliser because you're worried about making  worse. Holding back fertiliser extends a dollar spot outbreak.

**Step 2: Fix the moisture environment.**

Switch irrigation-general to early morning. Add dew removal to the morning routine. If leaf wetness is the reason the  is running, fixing that is as important as the chemistry.

**Step 3: Mow properly.**

During an active outbreak, catch the clippings rather than leaving them on the lawn — this removes infected leaf tissue and reduces spread. Mow regularly, keep height consistent, don't let the lawn get long between cuts.

**Step 4: Chemistry if needed.**

NZLA Azoxy (azoxystrobin) is NOT registered for dollar spot in New Zealand. This catches people out because azoxystrobin is widely used against dollar spot overseas, but the NZ label doesn't include it. Don't use Azoxy expecting dollar spot control.

NZLA Posterity is on label for dollar spot. It's a Group 7 SDHI fungicide with both preventative and curative activity. Apply at 5ml per 100m² as a preventative before conditions build, or 10ml per 100m² when  is active. Up to four weeks of protection per application. Apply in calm, dry conditions, avoid mowing the day before or after, keep foot traffic off until dry.

For most home lawns, the cultural steps above — nitrogen, moisture management, mowing — will clear dollar spot within two to three weeks. Use Posterity when conditions are persistently wet pressure is recurring year on year, or cultural management alone isn't holding it back.

Two foundations: get the fertility right so the lawn isn't vulnerable, and have Posterity available for when cultural control alone isn't enough.

**Active outbreak response:**

Charger every two weeks until patches stop appearing and the lawn is visibly growing through them. If the outbreak is spreading or conditions are persistently wet, apply Posterity at 10ml per 100m². Strict morning irrigation-general. Dew removal.

**Post-outbreak:**

Continue All Seasons on schedule. Charger every 3 to 4 weeks. If the area has recurring history, apply Posterity at the preventative rate (5ml per 100m²) four weeks after the outbreak treatment, ahead of the next high-pressure window.

## Recovery

Dollar spot kills the leaf tissue in the affected patches. Recovery isn't the dead blades coming back — it's new growth from the plant filling in over the damage. Expect two to three weeks of visible recovery time after the  is under control.

Keep the fertility programme running through recovery. If patches haven't filled back in after three to four weeks, the area may need seeding-and-over-seeding. NZLA RSR Ryegrass is fast to establish and tolerant of wear once it's up.

## Fine turf note

Home lawns on perennial ryegrass respond well to the cultural and fertility approach. Fine turf — browntop bent, fine fescue, couch at tight HOC — is more susceptible and can experience severe, recurring outbreaks. For bowling greens, putting greens, cricket outfields, or high-spec ornamental lawns, Posterity becomes a regular part of the programme rather than an occasional tool. Apply preventatively ahead of the known risk windows.

For severe or recurring dollar spot on fine turf that the NZLA range isn't fully controlling, additional chemistry from other suppliers may be needed. That's worth a conversation with a turf professional familiar with the specific grass type and local  history.

## If the treatment isn't working

If patches aren't responding after two to three weeks of consistent management, revisit the diagnosis. Dollar spot shares visual characteristics with other issues.
