# Can brown patch kill my lawn?

Yes, in severe cases. Brown patch can kill the grass plant in the affected area, not just damage the leaves. Recovery from severe brown patch often requires overseeding the dead patches.

Whether your lawn recovers depends on:

**Severity at the time of intervention.** Brown patch caught early (visible smoke ring, defined patches under 200 mm) usually responds well to fungicide and recovers cleanly. Brown patch left untreated for weeks until large dead areas have formed kills the plants in those areas and the only path back is overseeding.

**Species.** Ryegrass and tall fescue can be killed outright by severe brown patch. Kikuyu and couch (warm-season species in heat conditions) are more resistant — usually they recover from leaf damage without losing the plant.

**Cultural support during recovery.** A heat-stressed lawn under nitrogen stress doesn't recover well. Get the conditions right alongside chemistry.

The realistic outcome:

For a mild outbreak (small patches, caught early), fungicide treatment plus cultural support clears the visible disease within 2 to 3 weeks. The lawn fills in over 4 to 6 weeks as grass grows back over thin patches.

For a moderate outbreak (multiple patches up to 500 mm, some plant death), fungicide stops the disease spreading but the killed areas don't regrow. Plan for overseeding the dead patches in autumn when conditions cool.

For a severe outbreak (large areas of dead grass, weeks of untreated progression), the whole patch may need to be replaced. Kill any surviving disease, prep the soil, overseed at autumn timing. Full recovery takes 8 to 12 weeks.

How to minimise damage:

Catch it early. Monitor through summer heat for early signs (smoke ring, small patches). Treat at the first sign rather than waiting to see how bad it gets.

NZLA Azoxy at the first sign. Systemic knockdown stops the spread. Maximum 2 consecutive applications then rotate to Fungus Pro.

Iron+ for colour without forcing N growth during recovery. All Seasons after the disease has been controlled for steady recovery feeding.

Address the underlying cause — excess nitrogen, humidity, poor airflow.

Brown patch is treatable but not always reversible at the plant level. Speed of response matters.
