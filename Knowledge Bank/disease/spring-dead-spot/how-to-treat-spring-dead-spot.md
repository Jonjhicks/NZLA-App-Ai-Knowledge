# How do I treat spring dead spot?

Chemistry plus cultural management. Timing of the chemistry is the most important detail.

Chemistry:
- NZLA Azoxy applied in autumn (March to May in upper NI) before soil temps drop below 15 °C.
- Water in lightly after application so the active reaches the root zone.
- Two applications across autumn at label interval.
- Don't apply once the lawn has gone dormant for winter - the chemistry needs the lawn active to work effectively.

Cultural management is critical for long-term improvement:
- Stop nitrogen applications by late February. Late autumn nitrogen pushes growth into the disease window.
- Improve drainage on poorly drained lawns. Spring dead spot loves saturated soil.
- Soil test for pH. If above 7.0, sulphur applications over time bring it down to a less favourable range.
- Manage potassium - adequate potassium improves disease resistance.
- Maintain mowing height - don't drop too low going into winter.

Recovery in spring:
- The lawn fills back in from the edges through stoloniferous growth.
- This is slow on kikuyu and couch - 2 to 4 months for a 300 mm patch to fully close.
- Accelerate with NZLA Liquid Boost foliar feed once the lawn is actively growing.
- Iron+ for colour while the recovery is happening.
- For faster repair, replug the dead patches with healthy turf from elsewhere in the lawn (plugs from inconspicuous areas).

Honest framing:
- Spring dead spot is one of the harder warm-season diseases to manage.
- Established outbreaks recur year after year unless cultural management is genuinely addressed.
- The chemistry suppresses but doesn't eradicate.
- Some couch and kikuyu lawns with persistent spring dead spot eventually need partial conversion to less susceptible varieties or species.

For NZ home lawns with kikuyu or couch where spring dead spot has appeared, a programme over 2 to 3 seasons of autumn Azoxy plus cultural correction (drainage, fertility timing, pH) usually brings it under management. One season alone won't fix it.
