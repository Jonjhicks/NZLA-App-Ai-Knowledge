# What is spring dead spot?

A root and crown disease caused by Ophiosphaerella species, primarily affecting warm-season turf (couch, kikuyu). Patches of dead turf appear in spring as the lawn comes out of winter dormancy.

What it looks like:
- Circular dead patches, 100 mm up to 1 m across.
- Patches appear in spring as the warm-season lawn greens up - the dead patches stay brown while the surrounding lawn turns green.
- Patches often recur in the same spots year after year and expand outward over time.
- Roots and stolons of dead patches are blackened and rotted.
- Affected patches can be invaded by weeds during the slow recovery.

What drives it:
- Cool soil temperatures over winter (the pathogen is active when soil is below 15 °C).
- Wet, poorly drained soil.
- High pH (above 7.0).
- High nitrogen applied late in autumn.
- Lawn going into winter stressed.
- Recurring outbreaks year after year as the pathogen builds in the soil.

Where it shows up in NZ:
- Upper North Island couch and kikuyu lawns (Auckland, Northland, Bay of Plenty, Waikato).
- Sports field surfaces (cricket grounds, rugby fields with couch base).
- Increasing as kikuyu and couch use grows in NZ home lawns.

NZLA Azoxy applied in autumn (March to May) on susceptible lawns provides suppression. The chemistry needs to reach the root zone, water in lightly after application. Two applications across autumn at label interval covers the at-risk window.

Cultural management:
- Avoid late autumn nitrogen on warm-season lawns. Stop nitrogen by late February.
- Improve drainage on susceptible lawns.
- Soil test for pH - acidify if above 7.0.
- Encourage recovery in spring with adequate fertility once the lawn is actively growing.

Recovery from existing patches:
- The lawn fills back in from the edges through stoloniferous growth (slow process over a season).
- Reseeding or replugging the patches accelerates recovery.
- Don't expect overnight fixes. Spring dead spot is a chronic issue once established.
