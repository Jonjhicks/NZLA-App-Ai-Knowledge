# Anthracnose

## Questions this article answers
- Why is my lawn dying in patches in summer?
- My lawn has irregular dead patches with tiny black dots on the blades
- What is anthracnose?
- Why does my lawn look yellow-orange in January and February?
- My lawn has poa annua and it is dying in summer — what is wrong?
- Why does my lawn always get sick in the heat?
- How do I treat anthracnose on my lawn?

Anthracnose is a stress-driven fungal  caused by Colletotrichum cereale. It shows up on turf that's already under pressure — summer heat, drought, low mowing height, marginal fertility, core-aeration — and it hits poa annua (annual bluegrass) hardest. On well-managed home lawns it's less of a regular occurrence than on sports turf or golf courses, but it turns up on any lawn that's being pushed hard through summer.

## What it looks like

Patches of yellow to orange-yellow turf that fade through tan to brown and die. The patches are usually irregular rather than the circular shapes you see with brown patch or fusarium — they follow stress patterns in the lawn rather than forming neat rings.

On individual leaves, look for small dark spots or lesions, often with a light centre. At a more advanced stage, tiny black acervuli (the fungal fruiting bodies) are visible on dead leaf tissue. They look like minute black dots on the bleached blades — you need a hand lens or phone magnification to see them clearly, but they confirm the diagnosis.

Two forms occur:

**Foliar blight.** The  progresses through the leaf tissue, causing patches of yellow-orange that die from the tip downward. Damage is at leaf level.

**Basal rot.** The more serious form. The pathogen attacks the crown of the grass plant at soil level. Whole plants die rather than just losing leaves. Basal rot is harder to stop once established and requires seeding-and-over-seeding to recover.

## What drives it

Anthracnose is a stress  first and a pathogen second. The fungus is present in most soils — it takes advantage of turf that can't fight it off. The main stress triggers:

**Low mowing height.** The single biggest predictor. Turf mowed short (under 30mm) has less leaf area to photosynthesise, reduced root depth, and less plant tissue to fight back. Research consistently identifies low HOC as the most significant anthracnose risk factor.

**Summer heat.** Active above 25°C. The combination of heat stress and  pressure creates a pincer that weakens the lawn faster than either alone.

**Low or imbalanced fertility.** Nitrogen deficiency is a contributor, but the potassium-to-nitrogen balance matters specifically for anthracnose — low potassium weakens cell walls and makes the plant more susceptible.

**Compaction.** Restricted root growth means the plant can't support itself adequately through heat and drought stress.

**Drought stress.** Inconsistent irrigation-general that lets the lawn dry out between waterings, particularly on sandy soils.

**Poa annua contamination.** Annual poa (winter grass) is highly susceptible. Mixed-species home lawns with poa contamination are more vulnerable than clean ryegrass or fescue lawns. On golf greens with significant poa, anthracnose is a major summer management challenge.

**Acidic soil.** Below pH 5.5 pressure increases across multiple pathogens. Anthracnose responds to pH correction toward 6.0 to 6.5.

## NZ occurrence

More common in the upper North Island (Auckland, Northland, Bay of Plenty) through January to March. Shows up further south in particularly hot, dry summers. More of a sports turf and golf problem than a typical home lawn issue, but any home lawn with poa contamination and summer stress can develop it.

## Treatment

**Lift the mowing height immediately.** This is the fastest impactful action. If the lawn is being cut at 25mm, raise to 40mm minimum. The  pressure drops when the plant has more leaf area to work with.

**Consistent irrigation-general.** Not overwatering, but no drought stress either. Deep watering two to three times a week rather than daily light applications. Gets moisture to the root zone without keeping the surface constantly wet.

## Prevention

The prevention programme is: maintain adequate mowing height year-round (especially through summer stress periods), keep a consistent fertility programme with adequate potassium, manage irrigation-general for root-zone moisture without surface saturation, address core-aeration through aeration, and correct soil pH if it's running below 6.0. A healthy unstressed lawn deals with anthracnose pressure naturally. The  primarily gets hold on lawns already in trouble.

## Telling anthracnose from other summer diseases

**vs Brown patch.** Brown patch has the distinctive smoke ring at the active margin and hits in hot humid conditions. Anthracnose doesn't produce a smoke ring and is driven more by stress than by humidity alone.

**vs Pythium blight.** Pythium spreads extremely fast (hours) with a greasy, slimy look and sometimes white mycelium. Anthracnose spreads more slowly and doesn't have that greasy appearance.

**vs Drought damage.** Drought follows topography — high spots, thin soils, areas over hard substrate. Anthracnose can appear in any area that's under stress, regardless of topographic position. The black acervuli on dead blades confirm anthracnose; drought doesn't produce them.

**vs Heat stress.** General heat stress produces even browning rather than irregular patches. Anthracnose produces discrete damaged areas that progress over days rather than the sudden whole-lawn browning of a heat event.

If patches appeared through a summer stress period with irregular shape, the lawn is contaminated with poa, and you can see tiny black dots on dead blades under magnification — that's anthracnose.
