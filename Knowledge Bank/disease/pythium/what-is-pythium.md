# What is pythium and how do I recognise it?

Pythium is a group of water moulds (technically oomycetes, related to but not true fungi) that cause root and foliar disease on stressed turf, particularly in warm humid conditions. Two main forms on lawns.

Pythium foliar blight:
- Patches of greasy, slimy-looking turf appear suddenly after a warm humid spell.
- Patches expand rapidly, sometimes in a few hours.
- Wet early morning shows a cottony grey-white mycelium on affected leaves.
- Dies off by late morning as conditions dry.
- Most devastating on putting greens but can hit home lawns under the wrong conditions.

Pythium root rot:
- Roots become discoloured, soft, rotted.
- Above-ground symptoms are general decline, yellowing, thinning.
- Less dramatic visual than foliar blight but harder to identify and slower to recover.
- Tends to be chronic rather than acute.

Pythium damping-off:
- Affects newly germinated seedlings, killing them just after emergence.
- Patches of bare ground in a new seeding where seedlings emerged then died.
- Often associated with overwatering during establishment.

What drives pythium:
- Warm humid conditions, particularly nights above 20 °C with high humidity.
- Saturated or waterlogged soil.
- Overwatered turf, especially overnight irrigation.
- High nitrogen levels pushing soft growth.
- Sandy soils with poor drainage when over-irrigated.

NZ relevance:
- Foliar blight: occasional on putting greens and high-input turf in upper NI summers.
- Root rot: chronic issue on stressed turf.
- Damping-off: common cause of new seeding failure, particularly spring sowings on warm soil with overwatering.

NZLA Azoxy is listed for pythium leaf blight and pythium root rot on the label. Specific pythium chemistry (mefenoxam, propamocarb) isn't currently in the NZLA range but Azoxy provides useful protection in most home lawn situations.
