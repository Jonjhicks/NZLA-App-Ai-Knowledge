# Take-All Patch

## Questions this article answers
- Why does my browntop lawn keep dying in circles?
- What is take-all patch?
- Why do my lawn patches keep coming back in the same spots every year?
- My lawn has expanding circular dead patches — what is causing it?
- Why does my lawn  come back after I lime it?
- What  affects browntop grass?
- My bowling green has circular dead patches — what is it?

## What it is

Take-all patch is a root  caused by Gaeumannomyces graminis var. avenae. It primarily affects browntop (Agrostis species), creeping bentgrass, and to a lesser extent ryegrass. Mostly relevant on older browntop-dominant lawns, fine turf (bowling greens), and newly established lawns on disturbed soils.

## Identifying it

Circular patches of dead or dying turf, 100 mm up to 2 m across. Patches start small and expand outward each season. The roots underneath are blackened and rotted — the most reliable diagnostic indicator. Pull up affected turf and the roots come away with obvious decay, dark right to the crown.

As the centre of each patch dies and other grass species (poa, broadleaves) move in to fill the space. The original grass species is gone from that spot.

Typically appears in autumn or spring when soil temperatures are 12 to 18°C. Recurrent year after year in the same areas, slowly growing outward.

## What drives it

Sandy, low organic matter soils are the most common host environment. The pathogen thrives where the soil biology that would normally suppress it is low.

Recently limed soil — or soil with pH above 7.0 — is a high-risk environment. Gaeumannomyces favours alkaline conditions, which is why lime applications frequently trigger or worsen outbreaks. Newly established lawns on disturbed soil are vulnerable for the same reason: the beneficial soil biology that suppresses the pathogen hasn't established yet.

Poor drainage-and-soil-moisture and core-aeration are additional contributors.

## NZ relevance

Older browntop-dominant home lawns where the species mix has been established for years. Bowling greens and croquet lawns with browntop or cotula base. Occasionally on ryegrass lawns after significant soil disturbance or heavy recent lime applications.

## Management

Take-all patch is one of the harder lawn diseases to handle. The pathogen persists in the soil indefinitely, and there's no quick chemical fix. Cultural management is where most real progress comes from.

**Soil pH.** The single most actionable lever. If pH is above 6.5, bringing it down toward 6.0 to 6.2 creates conditions significantly less favourable for the pathogen. Apply sulphur to acidify gradually — this is a multi-season process, not a rapid change. Test the soil first to know where you're starting from, then track annually. Avoid lime applications on lawns with take-all history.

**Soil biology.** Gaeumannomyces is suppressed by a healthy, diverse soil microbiome. Depleted biology (from heavy tillage, soil disturbance, or chemical overuse) removes the competition and gives the pathogen a free run. NZLA Humic+ and NZLA Amino+ support beneficial soil microbial activity and help restore a competitive biological environment in the root zone.

**Drainage and core-aeration.** Waterlogged, compacted soil favours root diseases in general. Improve where possible.

**Thatch management.** Don't let thatch build significantly — it harbours the fungal inoculum and reduces  to the soil surface. But don't aggressively scarify into an active  window. Scarification creates the soil disturbance the pathogen exploits.

**Species shift.** For browntop lawns where take-all is a recurring, expanding problem, gradually shifting the species mix toward less susceptible options (tall fescue, ryegrass) in affected areas is the practical long-term approach. Browntop is particularly susceptible; replacing it in the worst spots removes the most vulnerable host.

## Recovery and realistic expectations

Reseeding bare patches with tall fescue or ryegrass (less susceptible than browntop) is the best way to address the areas the  has already killed. Reseed after improving soil conditions — seeding-and-over-seeding into unchanged soil with elevated pH gives the new grass little advantage.

Some improvement in year one is achievable, but take-all patch rarely resolves cleanly in a single season. A programme over two to three years of pH correction, biology support, and targeted reseeding produces genuine results.

For seriously affected lawns, have realistic conversations about the level of care needed. Take-all on a browntop lawn that's also alkaline, compacted, and poorly drained needs several things fixed simultaneously before it responds. If only one factor improves, progress will be slow.
