# Snow Mould

## The short version

For most New Zealand lawn owners, snow mould is fusarium patch behaving in cold conditions. The pathogen is the same (Microdochium nivale), the chemistry is the same, and the management is the same. If you're in the lower South Island or on an alpine site, read on. If you're in Auckland, skip straight to the fusarium patch article.

## Two types, one relevant

**Pink snow mould** (Microdochium nivale) is the one that matters in New Zealand. Same pathogen as fusarium patch — historically called Fusarium nivale before the taxonomy changed. In mild NZ autumn and spring conditions, this shows as fusarium patch. In extended cold wet conditions, particularly lower South Island and inland frost-prone sites, it presents with a more pronounced pink to red colouration and often slower recovery because the lawn isn't actively growing.

**Grey snow mould** (Typhula species) requires sustained snow cover for extended periods. Largely irrelevant for most of NZ. Possibly relevant at alpine village lawns and ski field accommodation properties at altitude, but not a mainstream New Zealand lawn problem.

## What pink snow mould looks like in cold conditions

Round patches 100 to 300 mm across. Pink to red-tinged edges, most visible in morning light when conditions are damp. Cottony white mycelium on wet patches. Centre becomes pale tan as the grass dies. Slower spread than classic fusarium patch — the low temperatures slow the pathogen down somewhat, but the lawn also isn't actively defending itself.

## Conditions

- Soil and air temperatures 3 to 10°C
- Prolonged wet, surface moisture
- High nitrogen applied late in autumn pushing soft growth into cold weather
- Poorly drained lawn
- Extended cold periods without a dry break

## NZ regions affected

Lower South Island — inland Canterbury, Otago, Southland — where extended cold wet winters are normal. High-altitude lawns anywhere. North Island lawns with poor drainage-and-soil-moisture in cold snaps can show it but it's more likely to just be standard fusarium patch.

## Treatment and prevention

Same approach as fusarium patch:

NZLA Azoxy and NZLA Fungus Pro are the chemistry options. Apply preventatively in late autumn (March to April) on susceptible lower South Island lawns before conditions deteriorate. For a lawn with a history of cold-weather  outbreaks, two applications across late March and April — Azoxy first, rotate to Fungus Pro on the second application.

Cultural management:
- Stop nitrogen by mid-autumn (late February for South Island lawns). Late N pushes soft vulnerable growth into the  window.
- Improve drainage-and-soil-moisture. Wet profiles through winter are the primary driver.
- Maintain mowing height going into winter. Don't drop too low.
- Keep mowing until the lawn actually stops growing. Uncut long growth matting down over winter creates ideal  conditions.

For NZ's lower South Island and alpine lawns, the preventative programme is worth taking seriously — the  window there is longer and the lawn has less ability to recover quickly.

For everything else in New Zealand: standard fusarium patch management covers it. Don't let the different name complicate the approach.
