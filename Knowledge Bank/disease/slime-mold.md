Whatever you're looking at — slimy, crusty, yellow, grey, white, or that particularly alarming bright orange variety that looks like something went badly wrong overnight — it's almost certainly slime mould, and your lawn is fine.

Slime mould is the most dramatic-looking lawn problem that requires the least intervention of anything you'll encounter.

## What it actually is

Slime mould is not a fungus. It's a separate class of organism entirely, somewhere between a fungus and a single-celled protozoan. It lives in soil and thatch, feeding on bacteria, fungal spores, and decomposing organic matter. It does not infect or feed on living grass plants.

The species you'll see on NZ lawns include Mucilago, Physarum, and Fuligo — the latter being the yellow-orange one that's earned the informal name "dog vomit slime mould." The name is accurate.

## What you're seeing

**Active plasmodial stage:** a slimy, viscous mass on the lawn surface. Can be yellow, orange, white, grey, or purple depending on species. It moves slowly but visibly, which is disconcerting the first time you see it.

**Fruiting stage:** crusty or powdery patches on grass blades and at the soil surface, often grey, black, or white. Looks like the lawn has been dusted with ash or fine powder.

Both stages are temporary. The whole episode typically runs two to three days before the slime mould disperses naturally. Your lawn comes through with no lasting damage.

The one minor effect is temporary shading. Where slime mould sits on grass blades for several days, it blocks some light. You might see slight yellowing in the covered area. It recovers within days.

## Why it appears

Warm damp conditions plus available organic matter. It comes out of the soil or thatch after warm summer rain, particularly in shaded spots that stay moist, in areas with heavy thatch, or near garden mulch. Most common through summer and early autumn. Some lawns get it in the same spots year after year because the conditions are consistently right there.

## What to do

**Hose it off.** A strong jet from a garden hose knocks slime mould off the grass in minutes. That's the standard approach and it works immediately.

If you'd rather wait, it disperses on its own within a few days. For the dried fruiting stage, a light rake lifts the crusty residue. Bag the rakings rather than composting near the lawn.

**Do not apply fungicide.** Slime mould is not a fungus and fungicides have no effect on it. Wasted product and unnecessary chemistry on the lawn.

## If it keeps coming back

Same spots each summer: persistently damp conditions combined with heavy thatch or nearby organic matter. Reducing thatch through periodic scarifiers, improving drainage-and-soil-moisture, and being careful with garden mulch near lawn edges all reduce recurrence. But for most home lawns, slime mould appears occasionally, gets hosed off, and that's the end of it.
