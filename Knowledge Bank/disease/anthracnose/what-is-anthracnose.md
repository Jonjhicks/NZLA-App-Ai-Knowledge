# What is anthracnose on a lawn?

A fungal disease (Colletotrichum cereale) that affects stressed turf, particularly in summer and into early autumn. Mostly an issue on poa annua and stressed cool-season turf, but can show on ryegrass, fescue and others when conditions are right.

What it looks like:
- Patches of yellow or orange-yellow turf that fade to tan and die.
- Patches are often irregular, not the round circles you see with fusarium or brown patch.
- Black fruiting structures (acervuli) visible on dead leaf tissue with a hand lens.
- Foliar blight phase shows on leaf blades. Basal rot phase attacks the crown of the plant and is more serious.

What drives it:
- Heat and humidity in summer.
- Stressed turf (drought stress, low fertility, low mowing height, compaction).
- Poa annua under stress is the classic host. Anthracnose is one of the main reasons golf greens with poa contamination decline through summer.
- Acidic soil (below 5.5 pH).
- Low potassium contributing to weak cell walls.

NZ relevance:
- Moderate. Shows up in upper North Island and stressed home lawns through summer.
- More commonly seen on golf and sports turf than home lawns.
- Mixed-species home lawns (ryegrass with poa contamination) are vulnerable.

NZLA Azoxy (azoxystrobin) is listed for anthracnose control on its label. Apply at first sign or as a preventative on susceptible lawns going into summer. Standard FRAC Group 11 resistance management applies, rotate to Fungus Pro after two applications.

Cultural side is half the battle. Strong fertility programme, sensible mowing height, adequate irrigation, dense competitive turf. Anthracnose on a healthy unstressed lawn is much less of a problem than on a struggling one.
