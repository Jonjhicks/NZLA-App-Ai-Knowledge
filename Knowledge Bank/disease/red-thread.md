# Red Thread

- Why does my lawn turn pink in autumn?

Red thread is a cool-season foliar  caused by Laetisaria fuciformis. It's primarily a cosmetic problem — it looks unpleasant but rarely kills the lawn. The underlying message is consistent: the lawn is underfed. Feed it properly and red thread clears up on its own within a few weeks.

## Identifying red thread

The name describes it exactly. Look for pink to coral-red thread-like structures projecting from the tips of grass blades — these are fungal "stromata," 1 to 5mm long, easy to see in cool damp morning conditions before the dew has cleared. They look like tiny coloured whiskers at the leaf tips. When you rub them between your fingers they feel slightly waxy.

Patches are 50 to 200mm across, straw-coloured to bleached underneath the fungal growth. In the early stages the patches look slightly different in colour and texture from the surrounding lawn; the pink-red threads on the leaf tips are the confirmation.

The threads are the diagnostic feature. If you see pink-red wisps at the grass tips, particularly in cool damp conditions, that's red thread. Nothing else looks quite the same.

## Telling red thread from rust

These two diseases share the same underlying cause — low nitrogen — and appear in similar conditions, which is why people mix them up. They look very different up close.

**Red thread** has the distinctive pink-red thread-like structures at the leaf tips. The threads project from the blade. Patches are roughly circular with a bleached straw colour.

**Rust** produces orange-yellow pustules (raised spots containing powdery spores) along the length of the blade, not just at the tip. Walk through a rust-affected lawn and orange dust rubs off on your shoes and trouser legs. That doesn't happen with red thread.

The practical test: walk through the affected area. Orange powder on your feet = rust. No powder but visible red whiskers on blade tips = red thread.

Both diseases can appear on the same lawn at the same time in a chronically underfed situation. The cultural fix is identical for both — feed the lawn — though the chemistry differs slightly.

## What causes red thread

The pathogen needs cool temperatures (15–24°C), extended leaf wetness, and a nitrogen-deficient lawn. Those conditions describe most NZ lawns in late summer through autumn and into early winter.

Low nitrogen is the core driver. Slow-growing, underfed grass can't outgrow the  — the damaged tips build up faster than the lawn recovers. An actively growing, well-fed lawn outpaces Laetisaria fuciformis. The  is essentially a symptom of inadequate nutrition.

Perennial ryegrass and fine fescues are the most susceptible species. Kentucky bluegrass and tall fescue, while present in some NZ mixes, are also susceptible.

Red thread is more common on lawns that are:
- Running a light or infrequent fertiliser programme
- In shade, where growth is already slower than open areas
- On compacted or poorly draining soil (restricted root growth limits nutrient uptake)
- Being watered in the evening rather than morning (extended leaf wetness)

## Is red thread serious?

Mostly not. The pathogen attacks leaf tips primarily — it doesn't penetrate the crown or kill the plant. The grass stays alive; only the leaf tissue is damaged. Once growth resumes properly (with feeding), new leaves push through that aren't infected and the damaged tips get mowed off.

Red thread rarely causes plant death. Even a heavy outbreak leaves the plants alive, and the lawn recovers fully once fertility is addressed.

When red thread warrants more concern:
- On showcase or premium lawns where appearance matters — it looks rough for the weeks it's active
- On chronically underfed lawns where it recurs every year — this indicates an ongoing fertility problem worth properly fixing
- Combined with other stresses (drought, shade, core-aeration) where the compounding effect creates more noticeable decline
- On fine fescue or browntop bent, which are more sensitive and take longer to recover

For most home lawns on ryegrass, red thread is a cosmetic annoyance that the lawn outgrows within two to three weeks of a feed. It doesn't need dramatic intervention.

## How long it takes to clear

**With cultural fix only:** five to seven days for growth to noticeably pick up after feeding, then two to three weeks for visible recovery as new growth replaces damaged leaf. Full clearing within four to six weeks.

**With cultural fix plus Azoxy:** spread stops within days, visible recovery within two to three weeks. Faster, but rarely necessary.

**Without intervention:** red thread is self-limiting. It runs for four to eight weeks during favourable conditions then fades when temperatures shift or the fertility situation changes. The lawn looks rough during this period but generally recovers fully. Not ideal, but it won't kill the lawn.

What affects recovery speed: severity at the point of intervention (worse at the start = longer recovery), growing conditions during recovery (warmer weather and active growth = faster), species (ryegrass clears quickly; fine fescue is slower), and underlying fertility (chronically underfed lawns recover more slowly even after a single feed — the programme needs to be consistently better, not just a one-off application).

## When red thread keeps coming back

Recurring red thread every autumn and winter is a clear signal that the fertiliser programme isn't right. The  is showing up because the underlying fertility is consistently marginal. Get onto a proper programme — All Seasons every six to eight weeks through the growing season, Charger between applications — and most recurring red thread resolves within two seasons.

Check for compounding factors: shade that limits growth, core-aeration that restricts root uptake, or heavy thatch that reduces the available soil volume for roots. Fixing those alongside the nutrition programme gives the best long-term result.
