# Is slime mold dangerous to my lawn?

No. Slime mold is one of the most harmless things that shows up on a lawn.

Why it's harmless:

**It doesn't feed on grass.** Slime molds feed on bacteria, fungal spores, dead organic matter and microscopic prey in the soil and on plant surfaces. They don't infect or damage living grass plants.

**The grass underneath stays alive.** Even where slime mold covers grass blades temporarily, the grass continues growing underneath. Once the slime mold disperses (usually within a week), the grass returns to normal.

**No long-term damage.** Slime mold doesn't damage soil structure, doesn't kill plants, doesn't spread to nearby healthy areas. After it's gone, you wouldn't know it had been there.

The only minor issues:

**Temporary shading.** Where the slime mold sits on grass blades for several days, it can temporarily block light to the grass. The grass underneath may show slight yellowing during the active period. Recovers within days.

**Aesthetic concern.** Slime mold looks unusual — slimy, weird-coloured, sometimes alarming-looking (especially the bright yellow "dog vomit" variety). Some people find the appearance disturbing on their lawn.

**Concern for sensitive individuals.** Some slime molds release spores at the fruiting stage. People with severe respiratory sensitivities or fungal allergies might react. But for most people, slime mold poses no health concern.

Is it dangerous to pets?

No. Slime mold isn't toxic to dogs, cats or other animals. Pets occasionally investigate or even eat slime mold without consequence (although it's not recommended).

Is it dangerous to children?

No. Slime mold can be safely touched. Standard hygiene (wash hands after) applies but no specific concern.

Why slime mold appears:

Warm damp conditions plus available organic matter in the soil or thatch. Common after warm summer rain, particularly on lawns with heavy thatch or near garden mulch areas.

When it disappears:

Naturally within days to a week. Slime mold completes its life cycle, releases spores, and disperses. The visible mass disappears, the lawn returns to normal.

No action required:

The most common professional advice for slime mold is "leave it alone." It runs its course quickly and leaves no lasting damage.

If you want to hurry it along:

Hose it off with water. Knocks the visible slime mold off the lawn surface. The lawn underneath comes through clean.

Rake out dried fruiting structures.

That's it. No fungicide, no special treatment, no soil amendment. Slime mold is the easiest "lawn problem" you'll ever have.
