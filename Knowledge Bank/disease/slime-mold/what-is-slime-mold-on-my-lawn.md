# What is slime mold on my lawn?

A bizarre but harmless organism that occasionally appears on lawns in damp warm conditions. Not actually a mould or a fungus — slime molds are their own group of organisms with characteristics of both fungi and protozoa.

What it looks like:

**The plasmodial stage** is a slimy, viscous mass that creeps across the lawn surface. Yellow, orange, white, or grey depending on the species. Slimy and amoeba-like in texture. Can move slowly — visibly different position from one hour to the next.

**The fruiting stage** appears as crusty or powdery patches on grass blades and the soil surface. Often grey, black, white or yellow. Looks like the grass has been dusted with strange powder or coated in a chalky residue.

**Sometimes called "dog vomit" slime mold** — a particularly memorable yellow-orange variety that looks exactly like its informal name suggests. Bright yellow when fresh, fades to tan or brown as it matures.

Where you find it:

Damp organic-matter-rich areas. Often around the base of trees, in heavy mulch, on lawns over composted areas, in shaded spots with persistent moisture. Appears after warm wet weather, typically summer through autumn.

What it actually does:

Almost nothing harmful. Slime molds feed on decomposing organic matter and bacteria — not on living grass. The grass underneath is unaffected. Sometimes the slime mold temporarily blocks light to the grass, causing slight yellowing in the affected area, but the lawn recovers immediately once the slime mold disperses.

How long it lasts:

Active slime mold typically lasts a few days to a week in any one spot. Once the moist conditions change or the slime mold runs out of food in that location, it disperses naturally. The lawn returns to normal within days.

Why people often worry about it:

The appearance is dramatic — slimy, weird-coloured, sometimes large patches. Looks like a serious problem at first glance. The names ("slime mold," "dog vomit") sound alarming.

In practice, slime mold is harmless and self-limiting. Most lawns get slime mold once or twice in a decade and never have a serious issue with it.

When to treat:

Almost never. Slime mold doesn't need treatment.

If the appearance bothers you, hose it off with water (knocks the visible slime mold off the lawn) or rake out the dried fruiting structures.

No fungicide is required or recommended. The organism isn't a fungus and fungicides aren't designed for it.
