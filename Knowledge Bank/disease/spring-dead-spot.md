# Spring Dead Spot

## Questions this article answers
- Why does my kikuyu or couch lawn have dead circles in spring?
- My warm-season lawn has patches that did not green up in spring
- What is spring dead spot?
- Why do the same circles die every year on my couch lawn?
- My lawn has dead rings after winter — what is it?
- How do I treat spring dead spot on kikuyu?

## What it is

Spring dead spot is a root and crown  caused by Ophiosphaerella species. It affects warm-season grasses — mainly couch and kikuyu. The patches don't appear until spring, when the lawn starts greening up. The dead areas stay brown while the rest of the lawn comes alive. That's the defining feature.

## Identifying it

Circular dead patches, 100 mm up to a metre or more across. Appear in spring as the warm-season lawn greens up, contrasting sharply with the recovering surrounding lawn. Roots and stolons in the affected area are blackened and rotted — pull up a handful of the dead grass and the base comes away with obvious decay.

One of the most reliable indicators: the patches recur in the same spots year after year and slowly expand outward from those sites. Weeds move in quickly while the grass is recovering.

## Why it happens

The pathogen is active in cool soil — below 15°C. NZ autumn and winter soil temperatures in the upper North Island, which is where couch and kikuyu are most common, regularly create this window. The  develops underground through winter, and the damage only becomes visible in spring.

Conditions that make it worse:

High nitrogen applied late in autumn pushes soft vulnerable growth into the cool-soil window. Wet, poorly drained soil is the other major driver. High soil pH (above 7.0) favours the pathogen. Lawns going into winter stressed from heat, drought, or heavy wear are more susceptible.

## NZ relevance

Upper North Island — Auckland, Northland, Bay of Plenty, Waikato — wherever couch and kikuyu are maintained as home lawns or sports surfaces. Sports fields (cricket grounds, rugby fields on couch) are particularly affected. Growing as kikuyu and couch use increases in NZ residential lawns.

## Treating and preventing

NZLA Posterity is registered for spring dead spot. Apply in autumn (March to May) before soil temperatures drop below 15°C. Two applications across the autumn window at label interval. Water in lightly after application — the active needs to move into the root zone, not just sit on the leaf surface.

Timing is everything. Wait until the lawn is dormant and you've missed the window. The chemistry needs an actively growing lawn to work through.

Cultural management is where long-term improvement comes from:

Stop nitrogen by late February on warm-season lawns. Late N is one of the most consistent drivers of spring dead spot.

Improve drainage-and-soil-moisture. Saturated soil through winter gives the pathogen ideal conditions.

Soil test for pH. If pH is above 7.0, work to reduce it over time. Sulphur applications bring pH down gradually — this is a multi-season process, not a quick fix. Getting the pH below 7.0 makes the conditions significantly less favourable for the pathogen.

Maintain adequate potassium levels. K improves overall  resistance in warm-season grasses.

Don't drop mowing height going into winter. Scalped turf goes into the cold stressed and more vulnerable.

## Recovery in spring

The patches fill back in from the edges through stoloniferous growth. On kikuyu this is vigorous once warm weather returns. On couch it's slower and more methodical.

Accelerate recovery with NZLA Liquid Boost as a foliar feed once the lawn is actively growing — iron-rich foliar stimulates leaf and stolon production. NZLA Iron+ hardens the recovery growth and improves colour while the patches close.

For faster repair on larger patches: replug with healthy turf taken from an inconspicuous area of the same lawn. A plug every 150 to 200 mm across the dead area speeds closure dramatically.

## Honest expectations

Spring dead spot is one of the more frustrating warm-season diseases. Established outbreaks recur year after year while the pathogen builds in the soil. The chemistry suppresses it, cultural management reduces it, but neither delivers a one-season fix.

Realistically: a programme of autumn Posterity plus genuine cultural correction (fertility timing, drainage-and-soil-moisture, pH management) over two to three seasons brings spring dead spot under meaningful control. The first season you'll likely still see patches — smaller and recovering faster, but still there. By season two or three on a properly managed programme, the patches should be minor or absent.

Some couch and kikuyu lawns with persistent severe spring dead spot eventually get partial renovation — reseeding affected areas with less susceptible species or converting to a different grass type in the worst spots. That's the honest long end of the road when cultural management hasn't been possible.
