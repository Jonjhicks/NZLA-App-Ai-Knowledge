# What is snow mould?

A cool-season fungal disease that affects turf under prolonged cold wet conditions. Two main types in NZ:

Pink snow mould (Microdochium nivale, formerly Fusarium nivale). The same pathogen that causes fusarium patch in milder NZ conditions, but with a distinct cold-weather presentation. Pink-tinged patches appear after extended cold wet weather. NZ relevance: lower South Island and inland frost-prone sites.

Grey snow mould (Typhula species). Requires actual snow cover for extended periods. Largely irrelevant for most of NZ - we don't get sustained snow on lawns. Possible in alpine villages and ski field accommodation lawns at altitude.

For most NZ lawn owners, "snow mould" effectively means pink snow mould / fusarium patch in cold conditions. The fusarium patch coverage in the bank applies. The cold-weather presentation differs slightly but the chemistry and management are the same.

What pink snow mould looks like:
- Round patches 100 to 300 mm across.
- Patches pink or red-tinged at the edges, particularly visible in morning light when conditions are damp.
- Cottony white mycelium visible on wet patches.
- Centre often becomes pale tan as the grass dies.

Conditions:
- Cold (3 to 10 °C).
- Wet, prolonged surface moisture.
- Lawn stressed by cold.
- High nitrogen recently applied (pushing soft growth into cold weather).

NZ regions where it shows:
- Inland Canterbury, Otago, Southland.
- Higher altitude lawns anywhere.
- Lawns with poor drainage in winter.

Treatment:
- NZLA Azoxy or Fungus Pro - same chemistry as for fusarium patch in milder NZ conditions.
- Apply preventatively in late autumn going into winter on susceptible lawns.
- Cultural management: stop nitrogen by mid-autumn, improve drainage, mow at higher height going into winter.

For most NZ home lawns north of mid-Canterbury, snow mould as a distinct entity isn't worth worrying about. The same chemistry covers fusarium patch which is the same pathogen in milder conditions.
