# Is snow mould the same as fusarium patch?

Pink snow mould and fusarium patch are caused by the same pathogen (Microdochium nivale). Different names for the same disease showing in different conditions.

In milder NZ conditions (autumn, mild winter, early spring), the disease shows as fusarium patch - round patches, cottony mycelium in wet morning conditions, pink-tinged edges sometimes visible.

In colder NZ conditions (extended cold wet weather, particularly lower South Island and high-altitude sites), the same pathogen shows what's traditionally called pink snow mould - similar patches but with more pronounced pink colouration and slower recovery because the lawn isn't actively growing.

For practical management on NZ lawns, treat them as the same disease:
- Same chemistry: NZLA Azoxy or Fungus Pro.
- Same timing: preventative late autumn into winter, curative when symptoms show.
- Same cultural management: stop nitrogen mid-autumn, improve drainage, mow at sensible height going into winter.

Grey snow mould (Typhula species) is a different disease that requires sustained snow cover. Not relevant for most NZ home lawns. Possible in alpine accommodation lawns at ski field altitudes.

For lower South Island and inland Otago/Southland lawns where extended cold wet conditions persist through winter:
- More aggressive preventative spray programme in autumn makes sense.
- Two Azoxy applications across late March and April, rotating with Fungus Pro.
- Heavier focus on draining the lawn well and stopping nitrogen by late February.

For most of NZ where "snow mould" is really fusarium patch in cold weather:
- Standard fusarium management applies.
- Don't get distracted by the naming, the chemistry and approach are the same.

The terminology comes from northern hemisphere turf literature where actual snow cover is common. NZ adaptation of the terms can confuse customers, hence the clarification.
