# What's the difference between brown patch and yellow patch?

Two different Rhizoctonia species causing similar-looking patches at different times of year.

Brown patch (Rhizoctonia solani):
- Warm season disease. Air temps over 25 °C plus humidity.
- Patches turn brown and die in the centre.
- Active January through March in NZ peak summer.
- Can be devastating on cool-season turf during heatwaves.
- More common on home lawns than yellow patch.

Yellow patch (Rhizoctonia cerealis):
- Cool season disease. Air temps 10 to 20 °C plus humidity.
- Patches turn yellow but centre often stays green.
- Active in spring and autumn shoulder seasons.
- Less damaging than brown patch, often resolves on its own.
- More common on fine turf (bowls, golf) than home lawns.

Same chemistry (NZLA Azoxy) works against both. Same cultural management (reduce irrigation, improve air movement, moderate nitrogen).

How to tell which one you've got:
- Time of year: summer = brown patch; spring/autumn = yellow patch.
- Centre of patch: dead and brown = brown patch; green or yellow = yellow patch.
- Patch size: brown patch often larger (up to 1 m+), yellow patch usually 100 to 300 mm.

Treatment is essentially the same for both. The distinction matters more for understanding when each is likely to appear than for choosing chemistry.

For lawn owners worried about either: Azoxy preventative in early summer covers brown patch, in autumn covers yellow patch. Most home lawns don't need that level of preventative use. Curative response when symptoms appear is sufficient for the majority of situations.

If you've had recurring problems with both, the underlying issue is usually the lawn environment (heavy shade, restricted air flow, overhead irrigation timing) rather than just bad luck. Cultural correction addresses both diseases at once.
