# What is yellow patch disease?

A cool-season fungal disease caused by Rhizoctonia cerealis. The cool-weather cousin of brown patch (which is Rhizoctonia solani). Shows up in autumn and early spring when conditions favour it.

What it looks like:
- Yellow rings or arcs of turf, 100 to 300 mm across.
- The yellow ring surrounds healthier-looking grass in the centre (different to brown patch where the centre dies).
- Patches can merge as the disease spreads.
- Often shows on overcast wet days, fades back as conditions dry.

Conditions:
- Cool temperatures (10 to 20 °C).
- High humidity, wet leaf surfaces.
- Spring or autumn shoulder seasons.
- Heavy nitrogen recently applied.

Hosts:
- Ryegrass, fescue, browntop.
- Particularly noticeable on bowling greens and high-input fine turf.
- Home lawns can show it but less commonly than brown patch.

NZ relevance:
- Moderate. More commonly seen on golf and bowls turf than home lawns.
- Cooler regions (Wellington, lower South Island) more prone than warmer regions in shoulder seasons.

NZLA Azoxy is effective against yellow patch (same Rhizoctonia genus as brown patch, similar chemistry). Apply at first sign or as a preventative on susceptible lawns going into the shoulder season window.

Yellow patch is one of those diseases that often clears on its own as conditions change. Mild infections in a cool wet autumn week can resolve naturally if the lawn dries out and warms up. The persistent infections that need chemistry are usually on susceptible lawns under continued favourable conditions.

Different to brown patch (warm season Rhizoctonia solani) in timing and symptoms but managed similarly. Same general approach to cultural management: don't overdo nitrogen, reduce canopy moisture, maintain decent air movement.
