Rust is a cool-season foliar  caused by Puccinia species. It's one of several low-nitrogen diseases — dollar spot and red thread are in the same family — and like those diseases, the primary message is that the lawn isn't getting enough nutrition to grow through the pressure. Feed the lawn properly and most rust clears up within a few weeks.

## Identifying rust

The visual is unmistakable once you know it. Orange to yellow-orange pustules along the length of grass blades — small raised dots filled with powdery spores that rub off onto anything in contact with the grass. Walk through a rust-affected lawn and you come out with orange on your shoes, socks, or trouser legs. That orange dust is the spores.

The whole affected area takes on an orange or yellow-orange cast. Patches can be scattered or widespread across larger areas, depending on severity and how long it's been running.

**Severity stages:**

Mild: scattered patches look orange-yellow, orange dust transfers on contact, the lawn looks unwell but not dead.

Moderate: larger areas affected, leaves visibly carrying multiple pustules along most of their length, strong orange contact transfer.

Severe: whole sections of the lawn orange-yellow, heavily spore-covered blades, walking through the lawn coats everything in orange.

**Timing.** Rust is most active late summer through autumn — typically February through May in most of NZ. Some pressure persists into early winter. Less common in spring and early summer.

**Conditions that favour it.** Mild temperatures (15–25°C), cool damp mornings with dew, slow-growing underfed turf. The pathogen thrives when the grass is growing slowly — the same growth-limiting conditions (low nitrogen, drought stress, core-aeration) that drive red thread and dollar spot.

## Telling rust from other diseases

**vs Red thread.** Red thread has distinctive pink-red thread-like structures at the leaf tips. Rust has orange pustules distributed along the blade, not just at the tip. Both are low-nitrogen diseases appearing in similar conditions but the visual is distinct.

**vs Nitrogen deficiency yellowing.** An underfed yellow lawn doesn't transfer orange dust on contact. Rust does. That's the quick test.

**vs Leaf spot.** Leaf spot produces defined dark spots with tan or pale centres on individual blades — not orange powdery pustules.

**vs Autumn fading.** Cool-season grass naturally slows and can yellow in autumn. No orange dust on contact.

## Is rust harmful?

Mostly cosmetic. The Puccinia fungus stays on the leaf surface and doesn't penetrate the crown. Plants stay alive — only the leaf tissue is damaged. Feed the lawn and it grows out of the ; the infected leaves get mowed off and the new growth comes through clean.

Plant death from rust is rare. Severe, prolonged rust on a chronically underfed and stressed lawn can weaken plants over time, contributing to thinning, but it doesn't cause the acute patch death you see with Fusarium or brown patch.

When rust matters more:

On showcase lawns where the orange-yellow appearance is unacceptable during the weeks it's active.

When it recurs every year — chronic annual rust indicates a fertility programme that's never been right. The lawn is spending months each year in a state of slow growth that invites the .

For people sensitive to fungal spores — walking through a heavily rusted lawn releases orange spore clouds. Worth noting for high-traffic family or pet areas.

## Treatment

**Cultural fix first.**

Feed the lawn. NZLA All Seasons granular gets growth moving. In active growing conditions (soil temps above 10°C, spring through autumn), the lawn outgrows rust within two to four weeks of consistent feeding starting. Charger foliar for a fast response between granular applications.

Mow regularly and catch the clippings. This is important. The clippings carry orange spores — leave them on the lawn and they redistribute. Bag or dispose of clippings well away from the lawn during an active outbreak.

Reduce leaf wetness. Morning irrigation-general rather than evening. Dew removal. Improved  through the canopy.

**Chemistry when needed.**

NZLA Fungus Pro (thiram) is on label for rust. Rates per the label: 200ml per 100m² in 20 litres of water for early  signs; 800ml per 100m² in 20 litres for more widespread or advanced . Reapply every four weeks.

NZLA Azoxy is NOT on label for rust in New Zealand. This is a common misconception — azoxystrobin covers rust in other countries but the NZ Azoxy registration doesn't include it. Don't reach for Azoxy expecting rust control.

For most home lawns, the cultural fix — feed, mow and catch, manage leaf wetness — is enough. Fungus Pro is the tool for showcase lawns where the cosmetic appearance matters more, or when the cultural approach isn't clearing it fast enough.

## Winter rust: a different approach

Rust in winter requires a different response to autumn or spring rust, because the standard "just feed it" approach has a complication: pushing high nitrogen in cool conditions invites Fusarium patch, which causes far worse damage than rust.

**What works in winter:**

Foliar feeding rather than granular. Cool soil temperatures (below 10°C) reduce granular nitrogen uptake efficiency. Switch to NZLA Charger foliar every two weeks — the moderate nitrogen with potassium and iron supports growth without the excess N that invites Fusarium.

NZLA Iron+ every two to three weeks. Iron drives chlorophyll and visible green pickup without any nitrogen. It hardens the plant against multiple  pressures and keeps the lawn looking reasonable through winter without the Fusarium risk of heavy N application.

Mow regularly (every two to three weeks at minimum, lifted height of 40 to 60mm for ryegrass) and catch the clippings to manage spore load.

Dew removal and morning irrigation-general as standard.

Fungus Pro if chemical support is needed — same label rates as above.

**Accept some limitation.** Rust in winter often persists at low levels until spring brings warmer growing conditions. The lawn won't fully outgrow it until October-November when growth fires up. If appearance through winter is acceptable, consistent cultural management (foliar feeding, mowing with catching) plus patience handles it. Once spring growth returns, the lawn clears the rust within a few weeks naturally.

Don't push aggressive nitrogen in winter chasing a quick fix. Fusarium is a worse outcome.

## Why rust keeps coming back

Recurring annual rust comes down to a few consistent causes:

**Chronic underfeeding.** The most common cause. The lawn has never been on a proper programme and is growing slowly all the time — the state that favours rust. Fix: get onto a consistent NZLA All Seasons programme every six to eight weeks through the growing season.

**Late-season fertility drop.** Even on lawns that are fed through summer, the autumn timing is often missed. Rust peaks in February through May — exactly when fertility is running out after summer. The fix is the autumn All Seasons application in late February to March, ahead of the risk window rather than in response to .

**Drought stress.** Underfed lawns that also go through drought stress emerge into autumn weakened and rust-prone. Consistent summer irrigation-general, and NZLA Wetter for hydrophobic soil areas, reduces this compounding factor.

**Spore load accumulation.** Years of annual rust build up the spore population in the soil and leaf debris. Catching clippings during active  reduces the spore load year on year.

**Susceptible species or cultivars.** Some ryegrass cultivars are more rust-prone than others. If the lawn is predominantly a susceptible variety and sits in conditions that favour slow growth (shade, core-aeration), rust will be a recurring challenge regardless of feeding. In those situations, Fungus Pro as a preventative in late summer alongside the fertility programme is the realistic management approach.

Two to three years of a consistent fertility programme, with the autumn feed not being skipped, typically turns chronic annual rust into an occasional and manageable one.
