# Brown Patch

## Questions this article answers
- Why does my lawn have large brown patches in summer?
- My lawn has circles with a darker smoky ring around the edge — what is it?
- What is brown patch ?
- My lawn is dying in big patches during hot weather
- How do I treat brown patch on ryegrass?
- Why does my lawn get big brown patches in January and February?
- What is the smoke ring on a lawn patch?

Brown patch is a warm-weather  caused by Rhizoctonia solani. It hits in summer heat — when daytime temperatures sit above 25°C with high humidity and the leaf stays wet overnight. In New Zealand that window is primarily January through March, though warm humid summers in the north can push it earlier and later.

It's less common in NZ than in subtropical climates, but when it shows up it can do real damage fast. A large outbreak on a ryegrass or tall fescue lawn left untreated for two or three weeks can kill significant areas that then need seeding-and-over-seeding to recover.

## Identifying brown patch

**Patch size and shape.** Patches range from 100mm to several metres across. Roughly circular in early stages, becoming irregular as patches grow and join together. The damage area can be very large in severe cases.

**The smoke ring.** This is the diagnostic feature. In early morning when the  is actively spreading, watch for a darker grey-purple ring around the outer edge of the patch — the zone where the fungus is advancing into healthy tissue. The smoke ring is brown patch's signature and distinguishes it clearly from Fusarium (which has pink-white cottony mycelium at the edges), dollar spot (which has smaller defined patches with hourglass leaf lesions), and drought damage (which has no ring at all).

The smoke ring is most visible in cool, humid early morning conditions. It can be faint or absent in dry conditions.

**Blade appearance.** Individual blades in the affected area show tan-to-brown dieback from the tip down. The pattern is more general than the specific hourglass lesion of dollar spot.

**Season.** Brown patch is a summer . If you're seeing large irregular brown patches with a smoke ring in December to March during a warm humid stretch, brown patch is the first consideration. Fusarium (cool damp winter), dollar spot (late summer-autumn, smaller patches), and drought damage (uniform, follows topography) all look different.

## Telling brown patch from other diseases

**vs Fusarium patch.** Fusarium is a cool-season  (autumn to winter). Brown patch is a warm-season  (summer). Fusarium patches have a more orange-brown colouring and pink cottony mycelium in the morning. Brown patch is grey-brown with the smoke ring. If it's July and cold, it's not brown patch.

**vs Dollar spot.** Dollar spot patches are much smaller — 30 to 50mm, coin-sized. Brown patch is 100mm to several metres. Dollar spot has the diagnostic hourglass leaf lesion pattern. Brown patch doesn't.

**vs Leaf spot/Helminthosporium.** Leaf spot shows dark spots on individual blades before any patches develop — it progresses blade by blade. Brown patch produces rapid whole-area dieback.

**vs Drought damage.** Drought follows topography — high spots where the soil dries fastest go brown first. Brown patch ignores topography. Drought produces uniform browning; brown patch has the moving smoke ring edge.

**vs Pythium.** Pythium also appears in summer heat with greasy-looking patches and sometimes white fluffy mycelium. Pythium tends to be more irregular and "greasy" looking, spreads very rapidly, and often follows irrigation-general or drainage-and-soil-moisture patterns. Both are summer diseases requiring prompt treatment — if in doubt, treat for both.

## What drives brown patch

**Temperature.** Rhizoctonia solani needs soil temperatures above 15°C and air temperatures consistently above 25°C. It requires at least 10 consecutive hours of high humidity or leaf wetness to initiate infection. Hot, humid evenings followed by overnight wetness are the ideal setup.

**Excess nitrogen.** High nitrogen produces soft, lush leaf tissue that Rhizoctonia invades easily. The same mistake as autumn nitrogen with Fusarium, but in the opposite season. Pushing All Seasons N+ or high fast-release nitrogen through summer when temperatures are high significantly increases brown patch susceptibility. Slow-release nitrogen sources are less risky than fast-release in summer.

**Evening watering.** Irrigation that runs in the evening keeps the leaf wet through the warmest, most humid hours of the night — exactly what the pathogen needs. Switch to early morning.

**Poor .** Still, humid air trapped by fences, hedges, or overhanging branches keeps the canopy wet longer after rain or irrigation-general.

**Species susceptibility.** Perennial ryegrass and tall fescue are more susceptible than warm-season grasses. Kikuyu and couch, when growing actively in summer heat, tolerate brown patch much better — the  may cause leaf damage but rarely kills the plant.

## Can brown patch kill the lawn?

Yes. This is the main difference between brown patch and the low-fertility cosmetic diseases (red thread, rust, dollar spot) — brown patch can and does kill grass plants in the affected area under severe conditions.

The outcome depends on how quickly treatment is applied:

**Caught early** (small patches, smoke ring visible, no large dead areas): fungicide stops the spread, the lawn fills in over four to six weeks as surrounding grass grows over thin patches. No seeding-and-over-seeding needed.

**Moderate outbreak** (multiple patches up to 500mm, some plant death): fungicide stops the  but killed plants don't regrow. Plan for seeding-and-over-seeding the worst patches once conditions cool in late summer or autumn.

**Severe outbreak** (large areas of dead grass, weeks of untreated spread): significant plant death. Overseeding required. Full recovery takes eight to twelve weeks of good autumn growing conditions.

Recovery timing: if brown patch runs through January-February and is stopped in late February, the remaining dead patches can be overseeded in March-April when conditions cool. NZLA RSR Ryegrass germinates well in autumn temperatures. NZLA Liquid Starter and Starter granular support establishment.

## The management summary

Monitor from late November. First sign of the smoke ring, get Azoxy on. Don't push high-N through the hottest months. Switch to morning irrigation-general through summer. Maintain  through the canopy. Fungus Pro in rotation with Azoxy for high-risk lawns or persistent history.

Most NZ home lawns don't see brown patch every year — the climate isn't hot and humid enough consistently. When it does show up, fast response keeps it manageable.
