# Powdery Mildew

## Questions this article answers
- My lawn has a white powdery coating on the grass blades
- Why does my lawn look like it has been dusted with flour?
- What is the white stuff on my grass in the shade?
- My fine fescue lawn has a white coating — what is it?
- Why does the shaded part of my lawn get white powder on it?
- How do I treat powdery mildew on lawn grass?

## What it looks like

A white to grey powdery coating on grass blades. Looks like the leaves have been dusted with flour. Shows up in patches rather than uniformly, most commonly in shaded areas or spots with restricted .

The fungus is Erysiphe graminis. Unlike most lawn diseases it grows on the leaf surface rather than penetrating the tissue, which is part of why it's rarely fatal but hard to eliminate while conditions stay the same.

## What drives it

Shade is the dominant factor. Powdery mildew thrives where direct sun is limited — under trees, against north or south-facing fences, in the shelter of buildings and structures. The fungal spores spread on still air and germinate in the surface moisture that builds up where  is poor.

Cool to mild temperatures suit it best. The  is most active at 15 to 22°C with high humidity. Autumn and spring shoulder seasons are the typical outbreak windows, though persistent shade can keep it present through summer.

Grass species matters. Fine fescue in heavy shade is the classic situation — the species tolerates shade, but the conditions fine fescue is asked to grow in are exactly what powdery mildew wants. Overhead irrigation-general in shaded areas extends leaf moisture duration and amplifies the problem.

## How serious is it?

Rarely fatal. Powdery mildew is mostly a cosmetic issue. The grass looks unwell but keeps growing. The fungus blocks some photosynthesis and weakens plants slightly, but it doesn't cause the rapid dieback you see with fusarium or brown patch.

Where it does cause meaningful damage: heavily shaded lawns where the grass is already at its limits, or when combined with other stresses (poor drainage-and-soil-moisture, low fertility, drought). Chronic long-term mildew in the same spots can gradually thin the lawn — not dramatic death, but a slow decline that accumulates over years.

Year-after-year recurrence in the same spots is often a sign the location isn't ideal for grass rather than a  management failure.

## Fix the conditions first

Chemistry works on the symptoms. Fix the conditions and the  stops establishing.

**Reduce shade where possible.** Trim overhanging branches. Cut back vegetation blocking sunlight. An extra hour of direct sun daily on the affected spot can shift the conditions enough to stop mildew establishing. For permanent structural shade (buildings, fences), accept the limitation and manage around it.

**Improve .** Trim hedges or shrubs that channel still air across the lawn. Air movement disperses spores and dries the leaf surface — both reduce mildew pressure significantly.

**Reconsider overhead irrigation-general.** Watering in the evening extends the leaf wetness period overnight. Switch to morning irrigation-general, particularly in shaded areas, so the lawn has the day to dry.

**Consider the grass species.** If the spot is permanently shaded and fine fescue is failing, the answer may be changing what's there rather than fighting the environment. Some locations work better as ground cover or garden bed than as lawn.

## When to use chemistry

NZLA Fungus Pro (thiram) is on label for powdery mildew.

**Preventative rate:** 200 mL per 100m² in 20 L water, every 4 weeks during high-pressure periods.

**Curative rate:** 800 mL per 100m² for active outbreaks.

When chemistry makes sense: showcase lawns where the visual is the priority and shade genuinely can't be reduced. Sports turf and fine turf where the species choice is fixed and the spot can't be changed. High-value patches where cultural intervention isn't practical.

The honest limitation of chemistry here: the conditions driving the  are unchanged, so mildew returns as soon as the fungicide wears off. Ongoing chemistry is a management band-aid, not a solution. If you're applying Fungus Pro to the same spots two or three times a year every year, the effort is better invested in addressing what's actually causing it.

For most home lawns with mild powdery mildew in a shaded corner, treatment isn't necessary. The lawn lives with it. Address what you can culturally, keep fertility up with All Seasons, and don't worry about chemistry unless it's visually unacceptable or the lawn is a showcase.
