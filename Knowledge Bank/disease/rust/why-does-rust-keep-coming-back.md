# Why does rust keep coming back every year?

Same answer as for any chronic low-fertility disease — the lawn is chronically underfed and the conditions that favour rust haven't been fixed.

The reasons rust recurs:

**Chronic underfeeding.** Rust is a low-nitrogen disease. If the lawn has never been on a proper fertility programme, it's growing slowly all the time. Slow growth + appropriate conditions = rust every year.

The fix: a consistent fertility programme. NZLA All Seasons (21-1-16 + 5% Fe) every 6 to 8 weeks through the growing season, or All Seasons as the value alternative. NZLA Charger foliar between granular applications.

**Late-season hunger.** Even on lawns that are well-fed through summer, fertility drops off through late summer and autumn — the time when rust is most active. The lawn becomes vulnerable specifically at the season when rust pressure peaks.

The fix: don't skip the autumn feed. NZLA All Seasons in late February through March hardens the lawn going into autumn-winter and reduces rust susceptibility.

**Drought stress.** Underfed lawns that also suffer drought stress through summer come into autumn weakened and rust-vulnerable.

The fix: consistent irrigation through summer dry periods. NZLA Wetter for hydrophobic soils where water won't penetrate.

**Slow-growing species in suboptimal conditions.** Fine fescue and some other slower-growing grasses are more rust-susceptible than fast-growing perennial ryegrass. If the species is part of the issue, species change or supplementary management is needed.

**Spore load building up.** Once rust has been on a lawn for a season, the spore load in the soil and leaf debris carries over. Years of chronic rust build the spore population to levels that re-infect easily each season.

The fix: catching the clippings during active rust periods removes the spore-bearing leaf from the lawn. Compost it well away from the lawn or dispose of it.

The year-on-year fix:

**Get the steady fertility programme right.** Most chronic rust clears within one season of consistent feeding.

**Don't skip the autumn feed.** Late February through March is the timing that protects the lawn through the rust season.

**Address other stresses** (drought, compaction, shade, mowing height) that compound the underlying issue.

**Manage the spore load** by catching clippings during active disease.

**Be realistic about species.** Some shade-tolerant fine fescue spots will get rust regardless of feeding because the species in the spot is at its environmental limit.

Long-term:

After 2 to 3 years of consistent fertility programme, chronic rust typically becomes rare. The lawn is in a state where it can outgrow disease pressure rather than succumb to it. That's the real fix — make the lawn so healthy it doesn't get rust in the first place.
