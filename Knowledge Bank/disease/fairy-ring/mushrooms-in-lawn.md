# Why are mushrooms growing in my lawn?

Mushrooms are the fruiting bodies of soil-dwelling fungi. The visible mushroom is a small part of a much larger underground fungal network. Most lawn mushrooms aren't damaging the lawn directly.

What's happening underground:
- Various fungi live in the soil, breaking down organic matter (old tree roots, buried wood, leaf litter, thatch).
- When conditions are right (warm wet, after rain), the fungus produces mushrooms above ground to release spores.
- The underground fungus continues whether you remove the mushrooms or not.

Common situations:
- After heavy autumn rain on a mature lawn: standard mushroom flush. Cosmetic only.
- In a ring or arc pattern: fairy ring, Type 3. The underground fungus is moving outward and producing mushrooms at the leading edge.
- Single mushrooms scattered across the lawn: random fruiting, usually from buried organic matter.
- Repeated mushrooms in the same spot year after year: a buried object (old tree stump, wood debris) is the food source.

What to do:
- For aesthetic reasons, knock them off with a rake or kick them over before they release spores. Doesn't kill the fungus but removes the visible mushrooms.
- Don't compost lawn mushrooms - some are toxic to humans and pets.
- Don't try to dig the fungus out. Underground fungal networks are large and digging just creates damage without solving the problem.
- For repeated mushrooms in a defined spot, check for buried organic matter and remove it if practical.

Pet and child safety:
- Some lawn mushrooms are toxic. Without expert identification, treat all unknown mushrooms as potentially dangerous.
- Keep kids and dogs away from mushroom flushes until you've cleared them.
- If your dog has eaten a mushroom and is unwell, contact your vet immediately.

Long-term:
- Mushrooms reduce naturally over time as the underground food source is consumed.
- A lawn with healthy soil biology (worm activity, regular organic matter cycling) tends to produce fewer dramatic mushroom flushes because organic matter is processed steadily rather than building up.
