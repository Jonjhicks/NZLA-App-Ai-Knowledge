# What is the difference between algae and moss in a lawn?

Two different organisms that often appear in similar conditions but look different and behave differently.

**Moss** is a non-flowering plant with tiny leaves arranged on a short stem. Texture is fluffy, velvety or carpet-like. Colour ranges from bright green to dark brown depending on species and conditions. Stands up off the soil surface as discrete plants that you can pick up between fingers. Can grow tall enough to compete with grass for light. Spreads by spores and fragments.

**Algae** is a primitive plant-like organism with no real leaves or stems. Appears as a slimy or scummy film on the soil surface — green, black or grey depending on the species. Doesn't stand up off the surface — it spreads as a layer. Slippery when wet. Often described as "scum" or "film" rather than as a plant.

What they have in common.

Both thrive in the same conditions — shade, damp, compaction, poor drainage, thin grass canopy, low fertility. Both are symptoms of underlying issues rather than primary problems. Both can be killed with NZLA Moss (benzalkonium chloride) at 200 mL per 10 L water per 100m².

When you see moss and algae together, treat the conditions, not just the symptoms.

Why the distinction matters.

For chemistry, NZLA Moss covers both effectively. The cultural fix is largely the same too — open up shade, improve drainage, reduce compaction, lift mowing height, get fertility right.

The differences come in the diagnostic story. Moss appearing in shaded patches suggests light is the primary issue. Algae appearing as a surface film suggests drainage and waterlogging are the primary issue. Both can coexist but usually one is more prominent than the other.

If you've got both in the same area, both diagnostics apply — the underlying conditions are favouring both forms of life over grass, which is a strong signal the lawn needs fundamental work rather than just chemistry.
