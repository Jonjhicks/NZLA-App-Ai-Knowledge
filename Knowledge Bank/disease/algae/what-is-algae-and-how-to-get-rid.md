# What is algae and how do I get rid of it?

Algae shows up as a slimy black, green or grey film on the soil surface, between and around grass plants. Different from moss, which has a fluffy or velvety texture and is a more developed plant. Algae is a primitive plant-like organism that grows where it has constant moisture and light at the soil surface.

Where you find it.

Shaded thin lawns where the grass canopy isn't covering the soil. Damp poorly-drained patches. Compacted soil where water sits on the surface. Areas under overhanging trees or eaves where moisture lingers. Often appears alongside moss in similar conditions.

Why it matters.

Algae itself doesn't kill grass, but it creates a slippery surface that's hard to walk on, blocks light reaching any grass trying to establish, and creates conditions that further encourage moss and disease. Persistent algae is a signal that the lawn surface is staying too wet for too long.

How to get rid of it.

Cultural fix is the foundation. Open up shade where possible. Sort out drainage and compaction (core aeration if soil is compacted). NZLA Penetrate to move surface moisture down through the profile and dry the surface faster. Lift mowing height to develop a denser canopy that shades the soil and prevents algae establishing.

NZLA Moss (benzalkonium chloride) at 200 mL per 10 L water per 100m² also kills algae on contact along with moss. Same DANGER label restrictions apply — fine turf and cotula only, PPE non-negotiable. Wet the algae first, spray, let it dry. Black scummy film dies off within hours.

After kill, rake the dead material out and address the underlying conditions. Without fixing the conditions, the algae comes back within weeks.

Spot test the affected area with the Wetter or Penetrate to see if surface drainage is the underlying issue.
