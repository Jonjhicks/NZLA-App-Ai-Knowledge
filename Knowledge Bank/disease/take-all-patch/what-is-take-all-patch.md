# What is take-all patch?

A root disease caused by Gaeumannomyces graminis var avenae. Primarily affects browntop (Agrostis), creeping bentgrass and ryegrass to a lesser extent. Particularly relevant on browntop lawns and on lawns transitioning from one species mix to another.

What it looks like:
- Circular patches of dead or dying turf, 100 mm up to 2 m across.
- Patches start small and expand outward each season.
- Centre of the patch often colonised by weeds or other grass species (poa, broadleaves) while the original grass dies.
- Roots underneath are blackened and rotted.
- Often appears in autumn or spring when soil temps are 12 to 18 °C.

Where it shows up in NZ:
- Older lawns with browntop dominance or contamination.
- Sandy soils more than clay.
- Lawns with high pH (above 7.0) or recently limed.
- Bowling greens with browntop and cotula.
- Newly established lawns on disturbed soil (the disease takes advantage of biological disruption).

What drives it:
- Sandy, low organic matter soils.
- Recently limed soil (alkaline conditions favour the pathogen).
- Poor drainage.
- Compacted soil.
- Soils with low microbial activity that would otherwise suppress the pathogen.

Diagnosis confirmation:
- Roots are blackened when you pull up affected turf.
- Patch pattern is expanding outward year over year.
- Often shows on browntop lawns specifically.

NZLA Azoxy (azoxystrobin) is listed for take-all patch control internationally and the chemistry transfers to NZ. Apply preventatively in autumn on susceptible lawns. Once established, take-all is hard to eradicate, the chemistry slows it but doesn't eliminate it overnight.

Cultural management:
- Soil acidification (sulphur over time) to bring pH below 6.5.
- Improve drainage.
- Avoid heavy lime applications.
- Encourage soil biology with NZLA Humic+ and Amino+.
- Don't dethatch in spring on susceptible lawns (creates the disturbance the pathogen exploits).
