# Yellow Patch

## Questions this article answers
- Why does my lawn have yellow rings in autumn?
- What are the pale yellow circles on my lawn in spring?
- My lawn has rings with a green centre and yellow outer band — what is it?
- Is yellow patch the same as brown patch?
- Why does my lawn get ring shapes in cool weather?
- My lawn has yellow arcs not full circles — what is that?

## What it is

Yellow patch is caused by Rhizoctonia cerealis, the cool-weather cousin of brown patch (Rhizoctonia solani). Where brown patch comes in summer heat, yellow patch shows up in the shoulder seasons — autumn and spring — when conditions are cool and humid.

## Identifying it

Yellow to pale rings or arcs of turf, 100 to 300 mm across. The key identifying feature: the centre of the ring often stays green or relatively healthy-looking while the ring itself turns yellow. This is different to brown patch, where the centre dies.

Patches can merge as the  spreads across a section of lawn. Most noticeable on overcast wet days. Can fade back as conditions dry and warm.

## Conditions

Air temperatures of 10 to 20°C combined with high humidity and wet leaf surfaces. Autumn as temperatures drop, and spring as the lawn comes out of winter. High nitrogen recently applied is a consistent driver — soft pushed growth is more susceptible.

## Is it serious?

For most home lawns, yellow patch is a self-limiting nuisance rather than a serious threat. The grass underneath the yellow ring is usually alive, and the  often resolves naturally as conditions change — a warmer drier week can clear it without any intervention.

Where it becomes more problematic: bowling greens and fine turf with persistently cool humid conditions, or lawns where heavy nitrogen and overhead irrigation-general combine to maintain ideal  conditions season after season.

## Treatment

NZLA Azoxy at label rate, applied to dry leaf as a foliar spray. Two applications maximum before rotating to NZLA Fungus Pro for resistance management.

For lawns that have had yellow patch in previous years, a preventative Azoxy application going into the shoulder season (March to April for autumn, September for spring) is a reasonable approach. Most home lawns don't need this level of management — curative response when symptoms appear is enough.

Once active infection is controlled, push recovery with NZLA Liquid Boost foliar feed and NZLA Iron+ for colour and hardening.

## Cultural fixes

These matter more than chemistry for most lawns:

**Irrigation timing.** Switch to morning irrigation-general if you're watering in the evening. Evening watering keeps the canopy wet overnight — the perfect  environment. Morning irrigation-general gives the lawn the day to dry.

**Nitrogen management.** If yellow patch appears regularly, review the fertility programme. Moderate nitrogen and good timing reduces soft vulnerable growth.

**Drainage.** NZLA Penetrate reduces surface moisture duration and moves water through the soil profile. Useful for lawns that stay wet for extended periods after rain.

**Airflow.** Trim back shrubs or hedges that restrict  over the lawn. Air movement dries the canopy and disperses fungal spores.

**Mowing height.** Don't drop into a  window. A decent canopy height maintains better air movement through the grass.

## Yellow patch vs brown patch

Both are Rhizoctonia diseases and the chemistry approach is similar, but they're different pathogens operating in different conditions.

Brown patch (Rhizoctonia solani): warm season, over 25°C, active December through March. Centre of the patch dies. Can be severe on cool-season lawns in a hot NZ summer.

Yellow patch (Rhizoctonia cerealis): cool season, 10 to 20°C, active in autumn and spring. Centre of the ring often stays green. Milder in impact, often self-limiting.

If you're seeing ring patches in summer heat, it's brown patch. Ring patches in a cool wet autumn or spring, yellow patch.

The cultural management overlaps for both: don't overdo nitrogen, reduce surface moisture, maintain watch irrigation-general timing. Get these right and both diseases become less common.

For lawns where either  keeps returning year after year in the same spots, the underlying cause is the lawn environment, not bad luck. Irrigation timing and excessive nitrogen are the most common drivers. Fix those and the chemistry becomes a backup rather than a crutch.
