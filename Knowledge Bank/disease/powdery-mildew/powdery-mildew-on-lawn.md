# Powdery mildew on lawn — what causes it?

White powdery dusting on the leaves, especially in shaded areas. Looks like the leaves have been dusted with flour. Caused by the fungus Erysiphe graminis.

The conditions:

Shade is the biggest single factor. Powdery mildew thrives where direct sun is limited — under trees, against north or south-facing fences, in the lee of buildings.

Poor airflow makes it worse. The fungal spores spread on still air and germinate in the leaf moisture that builds up where airflow is restricted.

High humidity with cool temperatures (15 to 22°C) is the sweet spot for the disease.

Some grass varieties are more susceptible. Fine fescue in heavy shade is the classic situation — the species itself is shade-tolerant but powdery mildew loves the conditions fine fescue is being asked to grow in.

Cultural fix:

Open up shade where possible. Trim back overhanging branches, remove vegetation that's blocking sun. Even an extra hour of direct sun a day makes a measurable difference.

Improve airflow. Trim hedges that channel still air across the lawn.

Reduce overhead watering — overhead irrigation in shaded areas extends leaf moisture duration and amplifies the disease.

Consider whether the grass species suits the spot. If powdery mildew is recurring on fine fescue in deep shade, the answer may be accepting that grass can't fully solve the spot and using shade-tolerant ground cover instead.

Chemistry:

NZLA Fungus Pro (thiram) is on label for powdery mildew. 200 mL per 100m² in 20 L water for prevention, every 4 weeks. 800 mL per 100m² for curative treatment on active outbreaks.

Powdery mildew is mostly cosmetic and rarely kills the lawn outright. Often it's a symptom that the species mix or shade level needs addressing rather than a problem to keep treating chemically.
