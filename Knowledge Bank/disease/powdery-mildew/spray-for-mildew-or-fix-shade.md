# Do I need to spray for powdery mildew or just fix the shade?

Fix the shade first. Powdery mildew is largely a shade-driven disease — addressing the cause is more effective than ongoing chemistry on the symptoms.

The order of intervention:

**Step 1: Open up shade where possible.** Trim overhanging branches. Remove low hedges that block air movement across the lawn. Cut back vegetation that's casting shade. Even an extra hour of direct sun a day on the affected spot can shift the conditions enough that powdery mildew stops establishing.

**Step 2: Improve airflow.** Trim hedges or fences that channel still air across the lawn. Air movement disperses the fungal spores and dries the leaf surface — both reduce mildew pressure.

**Step 3: Reconsider the grass species.** If the spot is permanently shaded and you can't change that, fine fescue tolerates shade better than ryegrass or tall fescue. Sometimes the right answer is changing the grass species in the spot rather than fighting the conditions on the wrong grass.

**Step 4: Reduce overhead watering.** Especially in shaded areas. The combination of shade plus damp leaf is the perfect environment for mildew. Water less frequently and direct watering away from chronically shaded spots if possible.

**Step 5: Last resort — fungicide.** NZLA Fungus Pro on label for powdery mildew. 200 mL per 100m² in 20 L water for prevention, every 4 weeks during high-pressure periods. 800 mL per 100m² for curative treatment.

When chemistry is the right answer:

Showcase or premium lawns where the visual matters and shade can't be removed.

Sports turf and golf turf where the species choice is fixed for performance reasons and shade can't be addressed.

Specific high-value patches where cultural intervention isn't possible.

Why cultural-first works better long-term:

Mildew chemistry treats the symptom but the conditions that drive the disease are unchanged. So mildew comes back as soon as the fungicide wears off — typically within 4 weeks of application. The result is ongoing chemistry on the same problem year after year.

Cultural fixes (shade reduction, airflow, species change) address the underlying condition. Once the conditions change enough, mildew stops establishing and you don't need to keep spraying.

For lawns where some shade is permanent:

A combination of accepting the spot (lower expectations) plus chemistry as needed (Fungus Pro 2 to 3 times a year) plus species choice (fine fescue if it suits the rest of the lawn) is the practical middle ground.

Don't accept ongoing fungicide as the only intervention. Always look for what's driving the conditions first.
