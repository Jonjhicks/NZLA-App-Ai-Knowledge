## What moss is telling you

Moss doesn't invade healthy lawns. It fills a niche where grass is struggling. Find moss on your lawn and it's telling you one or more of five things: too much shade, too much moisture, compacted soil, low fertility, or you're mowing too short.

The fix is two-part. Kill the moss that's there, then change the conditions. Do only the first half and you'll be back doing it again in three months.

## Identifying moss

Moss forms a low dense mat of tiny plants over the soil surface. It doesn't have roots — it draws moisture directly through its leaves. Green and springy when wet, compressed and brown when dry. Patches can be small (under a tree, against a fence) or cover large areas where conditions are uniformly moss-friendly.

Don't confuse moss with algae. Algae is a dark green to black slimy film that sits on the soil surface, usually in poorly drained spots. Moss is a distinct plant with a visible structure. They can occur together but need different approaches.

## What drives moss

**Shade.** The biggest single driver. Moss thrives where grass struggles — shaded spots don't have enough light for a dense competitive grass canopy, so moss gets a foothold. North-facing fences, under trees, in the lee of buildings.

**Poor drainage-and-soil-moisture.** Wet conditions favour moss. Water sitting at the soil surface is ideal moss habitat.

**Compaction.** Compacted soil keeps moisture at the surface and creates weak thin grass. Combined with shade or damp, core-aeration creates the perfect moss environment.

**Low fertility.** An underfed lawn thins out and opens up. Moss fills the gaps.

**Mowing too short.** Short turf exposes the soil to light at the surface level — exactly the conditions moss needs to establish. Scalping is particularly bad.

## Treating active moss

NZLA Moss (benzalkonium chloride 500 g/L) kills moss quickly. Brown-black kill begins within 5 to 15 minutes of application.

**Label note:** NZLA Moss is registered for fine turf and cotula. It's a DANGER-classified product — full PPE is non-negotiable: gloves, eye protection, coveralls.

**Rate:** 200 mL per 10 L water per 100m² standard. Up to 500 mL per 10 L for severe, heavy infestations.

**Application:** Apply on a still, mild day with no rain forecast for 24 hours. If the moss is dry, wet it first — moss takes up the chemistry better when hydrated. Don't water for 24 hours after application.

**After treatment:** Rake out the dead blackened moss within 5 to 7 days. The bare areas left behind are ideal for seeding-and-over-seeding — rake in seed and apply NZLA Lawn Starter granular to the area.

## When to treat

Autumn (March to May) is the primary treatment window. Moss is actively growing through autumn as conditions cool and moisture builds. Active moss takes up the chemistry properly and dies cleanly. This pre-empts the worst of the winter moss season.

Spring (September to October) is the second window. Clean up moss that came through winter before the main growing season begins.

Avoid deep winter (June to August) — the moss is metabolically slow and results are slower. If you have to treat in winter, pick the warmest available day with good drying conditions and expect slower kill. Avoid mid-summer — hot dry conditions stress both moss and grass, and the chemistry is less effective on stressed plants.

For persistent moss problems: spring AND autumn treatments combined with year-round cultural improvement is the professional approach.

## Mowing height for mossy lawns

Lift the mowing height. Short mowing exposes soil to light at the surface level — the exact condition moss needs to establish. A denser canopy shades the soil and makes the environment less hospitable to moss.

For ryegrass lawns with moss problems, 50 to 60 mm is the target. Above the standard maintenance height of 25 to 50 mm, but the denser canopy is worth it. In heavily shaded areas, push to 60 to 70 mm — the extra leaf area also helps the grass photosynthesise in low light.

Don't mow short thinking you'll expose or stress the moss. That just helps it. Don't scalp — even a single scalp in a moss-prone area creates a bare patch that takes weeks to recover and is immediately colonised by moss.

On warm-season lawns (kikuyu, couch), moss is less of an issue — the dense lateral spread of these species naturally crowds moss out. Standard mowing heights are generally fine if the lawn is healthy.

## The long-term fix

Killing the moss is the easy part. Making it stay gone requires addressing the conditions:

**Shade.** Open it up where you can. Trim overhanging trees and branches. Even an extra hour of direct sun a day on the affected spot makes a measurable difference. Where shade genuinely can't be removed (north fences, fixed structures), accept that some level of moss management is ongoing rather than solvable. Fine fescue tolerates shade better than ryegrass.

**Drainage.** NZLA Penetrate reduces surface moisture duration and moves water down through the soil profile. Use it routinely on moss-prone spots. Address structural drainage-and-soil-moisture where possible.

**Compaction.** Core aerate on compacted sites. NZLA Aerate applied after coring improves soil structure and helps keep the profile open longer.

**Fertility.** Get the lawn on a steady programme. All Seasons every 6 to 8 weeks through the growing season builds a dense, competitive canopy. NZLA Iron+ hardens the leaf, which helps resist  and moss establishment in susceptible spots.

**Mowing height.** Already covered above — lift it and hold it there.

## Will moss come back?

If the conditions stay the same, yes. Moss spores travel constantly on wind, water, animals and shoes. Every lawn gets new spores landing on it regularly. Whether they establish is entirely about the conditions they land in.

For lawns where two or three of the key conditions can be substantially improved, moss can become rare or absent. Get shade, drainage-and-soil-moisture and fertility right, lift the mowing height, and moss stops getting a foothold.

For lawns where the conditions can't be meaningfully changed — deep permanent shade, drainage-and-soil-moisture problems you can't fix, soil you can't improve — moss management becomes ongoing rather than solved. Twice-yearly NZLA Moss applications keep it back. That's not a failure, it's a realistic programme for that site.

Specific persistent spots (under one tree, against one fence) are often better accepted for what they are. Some spots don't suit grass and no amount of chemistry changes that. Garden bed, ground cover, or paving sometimes makes more sense than an ongoing battle to grow grass in conditions that don't support it.
