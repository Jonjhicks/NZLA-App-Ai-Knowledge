# Fusarium Patch

## Questions this article answers
- Why does my lawn have orange or salmon-coloured patches in winter?
- My lawn has white cottony mould with orange patches in autumn
- What is fusarium patch?
- Why does my lawn die in patches in winter and spring?
- My lawn has circular patches going orange-brown — what is it?
- How do I treat fusarium on my lawn?
- Why does my lawn always get sick in winter?

Fusarium patch — the common name used in New Zealand for what researchers call Microdochium patch, caused by the fungus Microdochium nivale — is the most damaging cool-season turf  in NZ. It's active all winter, it doesn't need snow cover, and it spreads fast under the right conditions. Left untreated it can kill large sections of a lawn.

## What causes it

Microdochium nivale infects cool-season grasses most aggressively when air temperatures are between 0 and 10°C and the leaf surface is persistently wet. It's specifically a cold-weather pathogen — warm summer temperatures suppress it, but from April through to September in most of NZ the conditions are right.

Perennial ryegrass, fine fescues, and browntop bent are all susceptible. Fine-leaf species are more vulnerable than ryegrass, but under persistent wet and cold conditions any cool-season grass will develop the .

The pathogen spreads through infected clippings, mowing equipment, and footwear. Once it's in a lawn, the spores persist in the soil for years. Reinfection from the resident spore bank happens whenever conditions are right, which is why lawns with a history of Fusarium keep getting it — the spores are there, and whenever the conditions align, they germinate.

## Identifying Fusarium patch

Knowing the three stages helps with both diagnosis and treatment timing.

**Early stage.** Small roughly circular patches, 50 to 100mm across, that look slightly different from the surrounding lawn — tan, yellow-orange, or rust-brown, with a slightly waterlogged or oily texture. The most diagnostic feature is the mycelium visible in early morning: a white to pale pink cottony growth at the patch edges, sitting between the leaf blades rather than on top as discrete droplets. When you see that fluffy pink-white growth at a patch edge in the morning, Fusarium is your call. Treat immediately at this stage — it's the best outcome.

**Mid stage.** Patches enlarge and join into larger irregular areas. Colour deepens to definite orange-brown or tan-brown. The mycelium at the active edges may still be visible in early morning but becomes harder to spot as the  establishes into the patch interior. New patches appear alongside existing ones. This is when most people notice the  for the first time. Treatment is still effective but visible damage is present.

**Late stage.** Large irregular dead areas across the lawn, multiple patches joined into sections. Grass plants die through the crown rather than just the leaf — the plants are gone, not just damaged. Recovery at this stage requires seeding-and-over-seeding in spring, not just treatment.

## How to tell Fusarium from morning dew

This is a common source of confusion in early winter.

Dew sits as individual water droplets on the leaf surface. It's even across the whole lawn, and it's gone by mid-morning once the sun is on the grass.

Fusarium mycelium is fluffy and cotton-wool-like, sitting between the blades rather than beading on them. It doesn't break up like dew. Check the same spot at 6am and again at 11am — if the growth is still there two hours after the dew has cleared, it's mycelium.

The other check is what's underneath. Dew sits on healthy green grass. Fusarium mycelium sits on a tan, orange-brown, or off-colour patch. If there's a damaged area under the suspicious growth, that's your answer.

Other things that can look similar: spiderwebs (gone when dew clears, geometric structure rather than fluffy, no damaged patch underneath), slime mould (more even and powdery than mycelium, doesn't kill grass), Pythium (also white and fluffy but appears in summer heat, not winter).

## What drives Fusarium — and why it keeps coming back

Recurring Fusarium has underlying causes that haven't been fixed. The fungicide treats the ; the conditions underneath are what made the lawn susceptible.

**Autumn nitrogen.** Applying high-nitrogen fertiliser in late summer and autumn produces lush, soft growth heading into cool wet conditions. That soft tissue is highly susceptible to Microdochium infection. This is the single most common cause of recurring winter Fusarium. The fix isn't to stop fertilising — it's to use the right product. NZLA All Seasons (21-1-16 with 5% iron) provides moderate nitrogen with high potassium and iron rather than a straight high-N formula. Potassium hardens cell walls and has been shown to suppress Microdochium infection; iron drives chlorophyll without pushing soft growth.

**Persistent leaf wetness.** Evening irrigation-general that runs until midnight, poor drainage-and-soil-moisture, shade that keeps the canopy wet into midday, hedges blocking  — all of these maintain the wet canopy Microdochium needs. Fix: move irrigation-general to early morning (midnight to 6am), trim back obstacles to address drainage-and-soil-moisture issues with NZLA Penetrate and physical drainage-and-soil-moisture improvements where needed.

**Compaction and drainage-and-soil-moisture.** Compacted soil holds water at the surface and restricts root growth. Core aeration in late summer (February-March) opens the profile before the  season.

**Soil pH.** Microdochium patch is more severe in acidic conditions. Maintaining soil pH in the 6.5 to 6.7 range reduces  pressure. A soil test reveals where you sit.

**Heavy thatch.** Thatch holds moisture against crowns and provides habitat for fungal inoculum. Scarify in late summer if thatch is significant.

**Mowing height.** Lawns cut too short heading into winter are more susceptible. Lift to 40 to 60mm for ryegrass home lawns and keep it there through winter.

**Soil spore load.** Once Fusarium has established, spores persist in the soil for years. Every time conditions are right, reinfection happens from the resident population. Reducing conditions that favour germination is the long-term fix.

## Prevention programme

**Autumn fertility (most important).** NZLA All Seasons in March-April is the right autumn feed. Moderate nitrogen, high potassium (16%), 5% iron. This profile hardens the plant rather than pushing soft growth. Don't follow with a high-N liquid foliar in May. NZLA Iron+ foliar through winter (May to September) keeps the lawn green and maintains plant hardiness without adding nitrogen load.

**Morning dew removal.** Run a drag rope or hose across the lawn early morning to break up dew. This shortens the daily leaf wetness window by several hours. Done consistently through winter it makes a measurable difference to both the frequency and severity of outbreaks.

**Mowing.** Lift height to 40-60mm for winter. Keep mowing regularly — a lawn that goes uncut for weeks develops matted growth that holds moisture against the crowns. Mow in the morning when the lawn is as dry as possible.

**Aeration and drainage-and-soil-moisture.** Core aerate in late summer (February-March). Address structural drainage-and-soil-moisture issues before the  season arrives. NZLA Penetrate through the wet season helps surface water move through the profile.

No resistance risk with Fungus Pro, so it's the rotation backbone across the whole winter. Add Azoxy or Posterity for systemic cover when conditions are severe or the lawn has a history of bad outbreaks.

## Treating active Fusarium

**Get fungicide on immediately.** Don't wait to see how bad it gets.

NZLA Fungus Pro at 50ml per 25m² in 5 litres of water is the first line. Contact protectant that knocks back surface mycelium within 24 to 48 hours and protects healthy tissue around the patch. Reapply every two weeks under high pressure, every four weeks in moderate conditions.

Back it up with NZLA Azoxy (23ml per 100m²) for systemic activity — it moves into the leaf tissue and disrupts the infection already inside the plant. Active infections are pulled back within three to five days. Maximum two consecutive Azoxy applications before rotating to a different mode of action. Microdochium nivale is documented to develop resistance to Group 11 strobilurin chemistry with repeated use.

NZLA Posterity (10ml per 100m² for active ) is the other systemic option. Group 7 SDHI, different mode of action from Azoxy. Using Posterity in rotation — alternating with Azoxy, or as a standalone — adds another chemistry type and reduces resistance pressure. Up to four weeks protection per application.

A practical winter rotation: Fungus Pro in April → Azoxy in May → Fungus Pro in June → Posterity in July → Fungus Pro in August. Full-season coverage with multiple modes of action.

**Cultural steps alongside chemistry.** Fungicide without cultural management is short-term. Switch to morning irrigation-general if not already done. Add dew removal to the daily routine. Avoid high-N applications during active . Iron+ for colour without growth stimulus.

**Don't push nitrogen in winter.** Cool soils mean the lawn can't use it efficiently, and lush growth in cool wet conditions is what Microdochium exploits. Iron+ is the right winter colour tool.

## How long treatment takes to work

Two separate timelines: the  stops quickly, the lawn recovers slowly.

Fungus Pro knocks back surface mycelium within 24 to 48 hours. Azoxy or Posterity pull back active internal infections within three to five days. New patches stop appearing within a week of a good fungicide application. Existing patches stabilise in size.

Visible recovery — patches filling in — depends on whether the grass is growing. In active growing conditions (soil temps above 10°C) recovery starts in week 3 to 4 and most patches are gone within 6 to 8 weeks. In winter cold conditions, there's no visible recovery until spring. The patches sit there looking dead from June or July until October or November — not because the treatment failed, but because the grass isn't growing.

Signs treatment is working: new patches stop appearing, existing patch margins stop advancing, morning mycelium disappears after Fungus Pro application.

Signs it's not working: new patches keep appearing two weeks after application (consider resistance, poor coverage, or rain washing product off before it dries), patches keep spreading despite stopped mycelium (systemic rate or coverage issue), same areas recur despite treatment (underlying cultural conditions unchanged).

## Recovery

**Mild Fusarium (caught early, small patches, no crown death).** The plants are still alive. Four to eight weeks of recovery in active growing conditions. No seeding-and-over-seeding needed.

**Moderate Fusarium (multiple patches, some plant death).** Dead plants don't regrow. Surrounding grass fills in over thinner areas but clearly bare patches may need seeding-and-over-seeding. Eight to twelve weeks in good growing conditions.

**Severe Fusarium (large dead areas, weeks of untreated progression).** Overseeding required for full recovery. Plan for spring seeding-and-over-seeding as a deliberate renovation step. Lightly scarify dead patches for good seed-to-soil contact. NZLA RSR Ryegrass at 2 to 3kg per 100m² for most home lawns. NZLA Liquid Starter foliar and NZLA Starter granular for establishment support. Full recovery from severe winter damage typically takes until late November or December.

**The winter timing.** Fusarium recovery doesn't happen in winter. A lawn hit in May or June looks rough from June through to October even after successful treatment. That's the season, not the treatment. Once spring growth returns, the patches fill in. Southern regions (Canterbury, Otago, Southland) have later recovery — October to November is more realistic than September.

**When to renovate.** If Fusarium has been recurring severely for two or three years and the lawn is chronically thin and patchy, a full renovation may be a better call than continued reactive management. But only if the underlying causes are fixed at the same time — drainage-and-soil-moisture, soil pH, core-aeration, autumn nutrition. Starting fresh on the same broken conditions produces the same outcome.

## Regional timing across New Zealand

Auckland and Northland:  onset April-May, milder winters, earlier spring recovery from September. Canterbury, Otago, Southland: peak pressure July-August, later spring recovery October-November, more likely to need seeding-and-over-seeding. Wellington and Hawke's Bay sit between those extremes. Start preventative fungicide applications earlier in the north (late March) and adjust timing south accordingly.
