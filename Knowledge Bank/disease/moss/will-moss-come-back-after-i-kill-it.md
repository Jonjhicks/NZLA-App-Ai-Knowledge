# Will moss come back after I kill it?

Yes, if the conditions that favoured it are still present. Killing the visible moss without changing the conditions buys you weeks to a few months before it returns.

Why moss returns:

Moss spores travel constantly on wind, water, animals and shoes. Every lawn gets new moss spores landing on it regularly. Whether those spores establish depends entirely on the conditions where they land. A lawn with the conditions that favour moss (shade, damp, compaction, low fertility, short mowing) will keep getting moss regardless of how thoroughly the previous moss was killed.

So the question isn't really "will moss come back" — it's "will the conditions on my lawn keep favouring moss establishment?"

If the conditions stay the same, moss returns. Typical timing for moss recurrence after kill-only treatment:

In moderately moss-friendly conditions, 3 to 6 months.

In strongly moss-friendly conditions (deep shade, persistent damp), 2 to 3 months.

In ideal moss-friendly conditions (north-facing structural shade with poor drainage and short mowing), within weeks.

How to make moss NOT come back:

The five conditions to change, in priority order:

**Shade reduction.** Open up canopy where possible. The biggest single lever.

**Drainage improvement.** NZLA Penetrate for surface moisture management. Address structural drainage where possible.

**Compaction relief.** Core aerate, NZLA Aerate on heavy soils.

**Fertility programme.** All Seasons or All Seasons N+ every 6 to 8 weeks. A dense well-fed lawn crowds moss out.

**Mowing height.** Lift to 50 to 60 mm minimum on moss-prone lawns.

If you can change 2 or 3 of these substantially, moss recurrence becomes rare or absent.

If you can't change any of them significantly:

Manage rather than eliminate. NZLA Moss application twice a year (autumn and spring) keeps moss back. Accept that some patches will always have a tendency toward moss. Choose grass species suited to the spot (fine fescue for shaded areas) rather than fighting the conditions.

For lawns with moss in specific localised patches (under a tree, against a fence) and a healthy moss-free majority of the lawn:

Treat the specific patches. Accept that those patches are different to the rest of the lawn. Garden bed extension or ground cover alternatives may suit some persistently mossy spots better than ongoing chemistry on grass that refuses to thrive there.

The honest answer: moss is a symptom. Treating the symptom alone never permanently solves it.
