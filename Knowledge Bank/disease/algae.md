# Algae

## Questions this article answers
- There is a slimy black film on my lawn — what is it?
- My lawn has a green scummy layer on the soil
- Why is there slippery green or black slime on my lawn?
- What is the difference between algae and moss on a lawn?
- My lawn is slippery after rain — why?
- How do I get rid of black slime on my lawn?
- Why does algae keep coming back in the same spots?

Algae shows up as a slimy black, green, or grey film on the soil surface, between and around grass plants. It's a primitive organism — not a true plant — that colonises bare or thin lawn areas where the soil surface stays consistently wet and light can reach the ground. It looks unpleasant, creates a slippery hazard, and signals that the underlying conditions are favouring organisms other than grass.

## Algae vs moss: what's the difference

Both appear in similar conditions and are often found together, but they look different and behave differently.

**Moss** is a non-flowering plant with tiny leaves arranged on a short stem. Texture is fluffy, velvety, or carpet-like. It stands up off the soil surface as discrete plants you can pick up between your fingers. Colour ranges from bright green to dark brown depending on species and conditions.

**Algae** is a slimy or scummy film on the soil surface — green, black, or grey. It doesn't stand up off the surface; it spreads as a flat layer. Slippery when wet. You'd describe it as scum or film rather than as a plant.

When you see both together, both diagnostics apply — the underlying conditions are strongly favouring anything other than grass.

## Why algae forms

Four conditions create the environment for algae:

**Constant surface moisture.** Algae needs water at the soil surface to live and reproduce. Heavy clay soils, compacted areas, spots with poor drainage-and-soil-moisture, and low areas where water sits after rain all create this. Over-irrigation-general — daily light watering that keeps the surface constantly damp — is another common cause.

**Light reaching the soil.** Algae photosynthesises and needs light. Bare patches, thin lawn from wear or  damage, scalped turf, and areas where the grass canopy has thinned all expose the soil surface to light.

**Low grass density.** A healthy, dense lawn shades the soil and outcompetes algae for light and moisture. A thin or patchy lawn leaves the door open.

**Poor drainage-and-soil-moisture.** Surfaces that stay wet between rain events let algae establish and spread. Heavy clay, compacted soil, and hydrophobic patches where water beads off rather than soaking in all create persistent surface moisture.

Other contributing factors: shade from fences, overhanging trees, or eaves extends the surface moisture window by reducing evaporation. Mowing too short shrinks the grass canopy and exposes more soil. High-nitrogen feeding that produces soft growth followed by  thinning creates the thin patches algae fills.

## Getting rid of algae

**Cultural fix is the foundation.** Chemistry alone buys weeks before algae returns — the conditions that created it haven't changed. Address the cause alongside the kill treatment.

NZLA Penetrate helps move surface moisture down through the profile and dry the surface faster after rain or irrigation-general. Apply through wet seasons to reduce how long the surface stays damp. For core-aeration-driven drainage-and-soil-moisture problems, NZLA Aerate breaks the surface tension in compacted soil and improves water movement.

Improve  where possible — trim back hedges, branches, or fences that trap still damp air and shade the area. Core aerate if core-aeration is contributing. Lift mowing height to develop a denser canopy that shades the soil surface.

**NZLA Moss** (active: benzalkonium chloride) kills algae on contact along with moss. Apply at the label rate, wet the target area thoroughly, allow to dry. The algae film dies within hours and can be raked out. No residual activity — it kills what it touches rather than providing ongoing prevention.

After killing the algae, address the underlying conditions or it returns within weeks. Reseed thin or bare patches (NZLA RSR Ryegrass for most situations) to re-establish a covering grass canopy that prevents reinvasion.

## Prevention

A dense competitive grass canopy is the best long-term defence against algae. That means: adequate fertility for active growth, correct mowing height (not scalped), good drainage-and-soil-moisture, and addressing core-aeration regularly. Lawns maintained in good health don't give algae the bare soil and persistent moisture it needs.

In persistently shaded areas where growing a competitive grass canopy is difficult, NZLA Moss as a regular treatment alongside drainage-and-soil-moisture management is the realistic ongoing approach.

## Algae in specific situations

**Under trees and eaves.** Shaded areas with restricted evaporation are classic algae spots. The soil stays damp longer than open areas, light still filters through enough for algae, and the grass canopy is often thin from shade stress. Improve drainage-and-soil-moisture with Penetrate, trim canopy where possible, use a shade-tolerant grass species (coarse fescue, shade ryegrass mixes), and maintain with NZLA Moss treatment through wet months.

**On new lawn areas.** Algae appears on bare or thinly-seeded areas during establishment, particularly if irrigation-general keeps the surface constantly wet. Reduce irrigation-general frequency once the seed is germinating — allow the surface to partially dry between watering cycles. Surface algae on a new seeding competes with seedlings for light and can suppress establishment. NZLA Moss at label rates can treat it but check the label for seedling safety before applying on very young grass.

**On hard surfaces adjacent to lawn.** Algae often bridges from paving, paths, and concrete onto the lawn edge. The hard surface stays wetter longer and provides a reservoir. Regular NZLA Moss treatment on the adjacent hard surfaces reduces the source. Improving drainage-and-soil-moisture at the lawn-to-paving edge reduces the moisture bridge.

**Slippery safety hazard.** Wet algae is genuinely slippery, particularly on paths and sloped areas. If algae extends onto hard surfaces that people walk on, treat it promptly with NZLA Moss. The safety aspect is worth taking seriously beyond just the lawn aesthetics.
