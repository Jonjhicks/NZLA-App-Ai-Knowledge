# How do I read a Safety Data Sheet (SDS)?

Safety Data Sheets (also called MSDS - Material Safety Data Sheets) are detailed safety documents for every chemical product. The label on the bottle is a summary; the SDS is the full reference.

What's in an SDS:

Section 1 - Identification:
- Product name and trade name.
- Manufacturer details.
- Recommended use.

Section 2 - Hazards Identification:
- The most useful section for home users.
- GHS hazard pictograms (skull, flame, exclamation, etc.).
- Signal words (Danger, Warning).
- Hazard statements (H-statements like "Toxic if swallowed").
- Precautionary statements (P-statements like "Wear protective gloves").

Section 3 - Composition:
- Active ingredients and concentrations.
- Other ingredients of safety concern.

Section 4 - First Aid:
- What to do if eye contact, skin contact, ingestion, inhalation.
- When to seek medical attention.
- Specific to that product's chemistry.

Section 5 - Fire Fighting:
- What to use to extinguish.
- Hazards from burning.

Section 6 - Spill and Leakage:
- Personal precautions.
- Containment and cleanup.

Section 7 - Handling and Storage:
- Safe handling guidance.
- Storage requirements.

Section 8 - Exposure Controls and PPE:
- Required PPE for safe use.
- Engineering controls (ventilation, etc.).

Sections 9 to 16: technical data, regulatory information, transport, ecotoxicity, etc.

How to use an SDS:
- Read sections 2, 4, 7, 8 before first use of a product.
- Keep a copy accessible (paper or digital).
- Refer to it if there's an exposure incident.

Where to find SDS:
- NZLA website should have SDS available for download for each product.
- Manufacturer websites for other products.
- Suppliers should provide on request.

For home lawn users:
- Most NZLA products at home lawn rates are low-to-moderate hazard.
- Standard precautions (gloves, eye protection, sensible handling) cover most situations.
- The SDS is the safety reference if anything goes wrong.

When to consult the SDS:
- Before first use of a product.
- After an exposure incident (eye splash, skin contact, ingestion).
- Before storing in proximity to other chemicals.
- For mixing decisions if combining products.
- For spill cleanup.

For commercial operators:
- SDS must be available at the workplace.
- Workers must be trained in SDS reading.
- WorkSafe NZ requirements apply.

Don't:
- Use a product without knowing the basic hazards.
- Ignore changes to SDS for products you've used for years (formulations and regulations change).
- Assume product safety based on packaging or marketing - the SDS is the actual safety reference.

Common SDS hazard symbols:

Skull and crossbones - acute toxicity (poisonous if swallowed, inhaled, or absorbed).
Exclamation mark - health hazard (skin irritation, allergic reaction, less severe toxicity).
Health hazard (silhouette with star burst) - serious long-term health effects.
Environment (dead tree/fish) - aquatic toxicity.
Corrosion - skin/eye corrosion or metal corrosion.
Flame - flammable.
Flame over circle - oxidiser.

Match the symbols to the product hazards and adjust your handling accordingly. Most home lawn products show one or two symbols, indicating moderate hazards manageable with standard precautions.

For NZLA products specifically, the SDS for each product is the definitive safety reference. Standard precautions handle most products well, but knowing the specific hazards lets you respond appropriately to any unusual situation.
