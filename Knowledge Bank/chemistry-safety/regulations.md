# NZ Lawn Chemical Regulations

## ACVM registration

All lawn chemicals sold in NZ — herbicides, fungicides, insecticides, and some fertilisers — must be registered under the Agricultural Compounds and Veterinary Medicines (ACVM) Act, administered by MPI.

Registration means the product has been assessed for safety to people, animals, and the environment. Each product is registered for specific uses: particular turf species, target application rates. The approved label is part of the registration.

Applying a product to a use not on the NZ label is "off-label" use. It may be legal in limited circumstances but is the operator's risk — for turf damage, for personal safety, and for any regulatory consequences. For home users the main risk is turf damage and wasted money. For commercial operators, off-label use can carry regulatory consequences.

NZ registration is separate to other countries. A product registered in Australia or the US isn't automatically registered in NZ, and the approved uses may differ. NZLA Blockade is a clear example: registered in NZ for warm-season turf (kikuyu, couch) only, even though in other countries it's used on cool-season turf.

To check whether a product is currently registered and for what uses: search the ACVM register on the MPI website (mpi.govt.nz). Enter the product name or active ingredient and review the current registration status and label.

## HSNO

The Hazardous Substances and New Organisms Act (HSNO), administered by the EPA, sets classification and handling requirements for hazardous chemicals. This is where the DANGER / WARNING / CAUTION signal words on product labels come from, and where transport and disposal rules derive. NZLA Moss (benzalkonium chloride 500 g/L) is a DANGER-classified product under HSNO.

## Label compliance

The label is a legal document. Following it protects you legally and practically. Key label sections to read: registered uses, application rates, timing restrictions, withholding periods if livestock present, buffer zones near waterways, re-entry intervals.

The "read the label" advice isn't bureaucratic caution. Product damage, resistance development, and poor results are mostly label failures. The label reflects what was tested and what works.

## Regional council rules

Some regional councils in NZ have restrictions on chemical use near waterways, in residential areas, or on certain land types. If you're near a stream, wetland, or other sensitive area, check your regional council rules before applying herbicides or insecticides.
