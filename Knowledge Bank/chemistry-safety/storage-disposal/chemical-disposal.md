# How do I dispose of leftover lawn chemicals?

Important to do this right - chemical waste in drains, gardens, or general rubbish causes real environmental damage.

Don't:
- Pour chemicals down sinks, toilets, or drains.
- Pour chemicals onto garden, lawn, or paving.
- Put chemical containers in regular rubbish (even rinsed).
- Burn chemical containers.
- Bury chemicals.

Do:

For unused chemical (still in original container):
- Check if a neighbour, friend, or community gardening group can use it before deciding it's waste.
- Council hazardous waste collection days for genuine disposal.
- Hazardous waste contractors for larger quantities.

For partial unused product:
- Try to use it for its intended purpose at label rate.
- If genuinely surplus, hazardous waste disposal.

For empty containers:
- Triple-rinse with water before disposal.
- The rinse water can be used in your next spray batch (don't pour it elsewhere).
- After triple rinsing, container can usually go in general recycling.
- Some councils want chemical containers in hazardous waste even after rinsing - check.

NZ council options:
- Most councils run hazardous waste collection days 1 to 4 times a year.
- Some have permanent hazardous waste drop-off locations.
- Check your council website for dates and locations.
- Auckland Council, Wellington, Christchurch all have established programmes.

What counts as hazardous waste for lawn chemicals:
- Expired or unidentified chemical products.
- Containers that can't be triple-rinsed (e.g. with residue stuck inside).
- Mixed chemical residues.
- Any chemical you wouldn't want a child to handle.

Triple-rinsing process:
1. Fill empty container 10 to 25% with clean water.
2. Replace cap, shake well.
3. Empty rinse water into sprayer for use, or onto target area.
4. Repeat twice more.
5. Container should now be safe for recycling.

Spray solution surplus at end of application:
- Most NZLA products: spray any surplus over treated area at lower rate (just spread thinner).
- Don't leave concentrated product in sprayer overnight.
- Clean sprayer after use.

Spray rinsate (from cleaning sprayer):
- Should be sprayed on treated area at low rate.
- Don't pour down drains or onto driveways.

For commercial users:
- Different disposal requirements often apply.
- Some councils require commercial chemical disposal documentation.
- Talk to council or industry body for current requirements.

Specific NZLA product considerations:
- NZLA Gold (picloram-containing): the rinse water can contain residual picloram that damages garden plants. Use rinse water on the same area being treated, not on driveway or elsewhere.
- NZLA Sticker (organosilicone): higher-risk concentrate, handle carefully during disposal.
- NZLA Moss (benzalkonium chloride): toxic to aquatic life. Don't put rinse water near waterways or drains.

The general principle: chemicals you put into your lawn should stay in your lawn ecosystem or be processed through proper hazardous waste streams. Don't move chemical residues to other environments (drains, paving, streets, gardens away from the lawn).
