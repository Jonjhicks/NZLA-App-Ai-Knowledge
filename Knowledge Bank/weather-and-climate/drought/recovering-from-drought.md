# How do I recover my lawn after drought?

Steady rewetting, then push recovery. Don't try to fast-track the lawn back to green in a week.

Rewetting:
- Don't dump a huge amount of water immediately. Drought-stressed soil often becomes hydrophobic and water runs off rather than soaking in.
- Apply NZLA Wetter or Wetter 3W as the first response. Breaks the hydrophobic layer and gets water into the soil.
- Then water deeply over several days. 5 to 10 mm per day for 3 to 5 days to rewet the soil profile.
- Switch to normal deep watering programme (15 to 25 mm per week) once the profile is rewetted.

Don't push fertility immediately:
- Wait until the lawn has rehydrated and you see growth resuming (typically 1 to 2 weeks after rewetting).
- Pushing nitrogen into a still-stressed lawn causes more damage than help.
- Once growth resumes, NZLA Liquid Boost or Liquid N applied at a moderate rate accelerates recovery.

Push recovery:
- Iron+ for colour as the lawn greens back up.
- Restore for stressed tissue recovery.
- Amino+ for biostimulation supporting overall recovery.
- Avoid PGR (you want growth, not regulation).

Address what died:
- Patches that don't recover within 4 to 6 weeks are dead. Reseed with NZLA Starter and Liquid Starter.
- Autumn reseeding (March to May) usually works better than reseeding immediately after summer drought.
- For badly damaged lawns, plan a proper autumn overseeding to fill in across the whole lawn.

Don't:
- Cut the lawn very short in the first 2 weeks of recovery. The crowns are still vulnerable.
- Spray weeds or apply heavy chemistry. Wait until the lawn is fully recovered.
- Apply granular fertiliser at high rates. Risk of burn on stressed turf.

Realistic timeline:
- Visible green-up: 1 to 3 weeks after rewetting (lawn that survived).
- Full canopy density: 4 to 8 weeks.
- Bare patches reseeded: 6 to 12 weeks (autumn timing).
- Lawn fully recovered: 2 to 4 months.

Lessons for next year:
- If recovery is slow or significant areas died, the lawn was probably set up poorly going into drought. Improve preparation next spring (deeper rooting, wetting agent programme, growth regulation).
- Soil test if recovery is unusually poor - underlying soil issues might be limiting recovery.
