# Drought Management and Recovery

Drought preparation starts in spring, not when the lawn goes brown. The work done in October and November determines how the lawn performs in February.

## Preparing for Summer Drought

**Root depth.** Deep roots reach deeper soil moisture. Build root depth from early spring by watering deeply and infrequently — 25 mm in two sessions per week rather than light daily applications. Shallow watering produces shallow roots. A lawn going into summer with a root system at 100 to 150 mm depth handles 4 to 6 weeks of drought. One with roots at 30 to 50 mm struggles after 10 days.

**Wetting agent programme.** Apply NZLA Wetter 3W in October to November, before the dry season arrives. This gets water moving efficiently into the soil profile throughout summer rather than beading off as the soil becomes hydrophobic. Wetter 3W has a 12-week residual from a single application. Supplement with monthly NZLA Wetter or Penetrate through the hot period.

**Growth regulation.** NZLA PGR (trinexapac) applied in late spring slows vertical growth and redirects energy into root and stress-tolerance systems. A regulated lawn handles heat and drought better because it's not pushing soft, high-maintenance growth through summer. Apply 4 to 6 weeks before the hottest period of summer.

**Fertility and potassium.** Don't go heavy on nitrogen heading into summer — pushed soft growth doesn't handle drought well. Adequate potassium matters more than high nitrogen for stress tolerance. NZLA All Seasons N+ (16% potassium) through spring builds the mineral balance the lawn needs to cope with summer.

**Species and soil.** Tall fescue (NZLA Elfin, Ultimate) has significantly deeper roots than ryegrass and handles drought markedly better. If the lawn is being renovated or overseeded in autumn, including tall fescue in the blend is the single best structural change for drought performance. NZLA Humic+ and Amino+ through spring support soil biology and organic matter that contribute to water retention.

## During a Watering Ban or Extended Drought

Most established cool-season lawns in NZ can survive 6 to 8 weeks of complete drought without dying. The leaves brown but the crown — the growing point at soil level — stays alive. This is protective dormancy, not death.

**What to do:**
- Raise cutting height to 50 to 60 mm. More leaf means less soil exposure, less evaporation, and less stress on the crown.
- Mow less frequently. A stressed lawn growing slowly doesn't need regular mowing, and mowing in drought conditions causes additional stress.
- Stop fertilising. Pushing growth into drought conditions accelerates decline.
- Reduce traffic. Don't repeatedly walk across drought-stressed turf — compressed crowns are more vulnerable to permanent damage.
- Stop herbicide and fungicide applications. Stressed lawn responds badly to chemistry.

If the restriction allows any limited watering (bucket or hand-held hose on certain days): water the highest-value areas of the lawn rather than spreading limited water thinly across everything. One deep application (15 to 20 mm) once a week is more effective than daily light applications.

**New lawns (under 12 months old)** and recently reseeded areas are more vulnerable than established lawns. Prioritise these if you have any water allocation.

## Recovery After Drought

**Rewetting first, pushing growth second.** Don't dump a large amount of water immediately — drought-stressed soil is often hydrophobic and water runs off rather than soaking in.

1. Apply NZLA Wetter or Wetter 3W first. This breaks the hydrophobic layer and allows water to re-enter the soil.
2. Water moderately over 3 to 5 days: 5 to 10 mm per day while the soil profile rewets.
3. Once the soil is rewetted and growth begins resuming (1 to 2 weeks), switch to standard deep watering programme.

**Don't rush fertility.** Wait until visible growth has resumed before applying nitrogen. Pushing nitrogen into still-stressed grass causes more damage than help. Once growth is clearly happening: NZLA Liquid Boost or Liquid N at a moderate rate accelerates recovery. NZLA Iron+ for colour. NZLA Restore for stressed tissue recovery.

**Don't mow short** in the first 2 weeks of recovery. Keep height at 50 to 60 mm.

**Address dead patches.** Areas that haven't shown any green at the crown within 4 to 6 weeks of rewetting are dead. Autumn is the best time to reseed them — conditions are better for establishment than the tail end of summer. Plan a proper overseed of heavily affected lawns in March to May.

**Realistic recovery timeline:**
- Visible green-up: 1 to 3 weeks after rewetting.
- Reasonable canopy density: 4 to 8 weeks.
- Dead patches reseeded and recovering: 6 to 12 weeks (autumn timing).
- Lawn fully back: 2 to 4 months.

A lawn that browns badly from drought was probably set up poorly for summer. Use the recovery period to plan the following spring's preparation better — deeper watering, earlier wetting agent application, drought-tolerant species in any autumn renovation.
