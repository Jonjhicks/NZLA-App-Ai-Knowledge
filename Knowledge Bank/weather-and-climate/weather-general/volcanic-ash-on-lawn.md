# How do I deal with volcanic ash on my lawn?

NZ-specific event. Volcanic ash from Ruapehu, Taupō, or Taranaki eruptions can deposit on lawns at various distances depending on wind direction and eruption size. Last significant ash event affecting populated areas was Ruapehu's 1996 series, but the risk is real for any future event.

What volcanic ash is:
- Fine ground rock particles, mostly silica and minerals.
- Mildly acidic if recently erupted.
- Heavy weight on leaves can smother grass.
- Long-term, the minerals contribute to soil fertility once weathered in.

Immediate response (light ash, less than 10 mm):
- Don't walk on the ash-covered lawn until it's wet.
- Light rainfall washes most of it down to soil level.
- Avoid rubbing the ash into the grass (sharp particles can damage leaves).
- Sprinklers or gentle hosing settles dust and reduces breathing hazard.

Heavier ash (10 to 50 mm):
- Remove most of it physically before the lawn suffocates.
- Use a leaf blower on low setting once the ash is settled.
- Then water in what's left to wash it down to soil level.
- The lawn underneath may take weeks to recover from the smothering.

Very heavy ash (over 50 mm):
- Strip the ash physically.
- Underlying lawn likely dead from suffocation.
- Reseeding required in autumn.

Soil chemistry:
- Volcanic ash is initially acidic but mineral-rich.
- Long-term contribution to soil fertility is positive once weathered.
- Soil test if you've had significant ash deposition to confirm pH and nutrient status.

Health and safety:
- Volcanic ash contains fine particles harmful to breathe. Wear a P2/P3 mask when working with it.
- Avoid contact with eyes.
- Hose down ash-affected outdoor surfaces to reduce airborne dust.
- Follow Civil Defence guidance during and after eruption events.

Lawn recovery:
- NZLA Aerate helps water and air move through the ash-affected soil layer.
- Humic+ supports soil biology recovery.
- Liquid Boost and Iron+ push recovery once growth resumes.
- For reseeded areas, NZLA Starter granular and Liquid Starter as standard establishment.

NZ context:
- Ash events are infrequent but real. Central North Island and Bay of Plenty are at higher risk.
- Council and GeoNet provide ash advisory information during eruption events.
- Insurance for ash-related damage varies - check policies.

For most NZ home lawn owners, volcanic ash isn't a routine consideration. But for those near active volcanic systems, the chance of dealing with ash at some point is real, and knowing the recovery process is useful.
