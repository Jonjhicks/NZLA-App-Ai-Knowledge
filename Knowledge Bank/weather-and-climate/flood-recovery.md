# Flood Recovery

Lawn flooding ranges from brief surface ponding that clears within hours to days-long inundation under silt-laden water. How much damage results depends primarily on two things: how long the water was sitting, and whether silt was deposited.

## What flooding does to a lawn

**Oxygen deprivation.** Roots need oxygen. Waterlogged soil drives out the air and roots begin to suffocate within 24 to 48 hours in warm conditions. Cool temperatures slow this — a lawn submerged for 24 hours in winter does far less root damage than the same inundation in January.

**Disease pressure.** Saturated soil and stressed grass are prime conditions for Pythium, Phytophthora, and Fusarium. Disease can follow flooding within days in warm conditions.

**Silt smothering.** If the floodwater carried sediment, the lawn may emerge under a layer of silt. This physically smothers the grass and blocks light and gas exchange. The thicker the silt layer, the more critical the response time.

**Soil structure damage.** Heavy water movement over the surface dislodges soil particles and compacts the surface. Existing drainage-and-soil-moisture pathways can be blocked.

## First steps — when the water clears

**Don't walk on it wet.** If the soil is saturated, foot traffic compacts it and causes further damage. Wait until water has drained and the surface is firm enough to walk on.

**Hose off silt quickly.** Silt that has settled on the grass and is still wet can be removed with a garden hose. Once it dries and sets, removal is harder. Use a gentle flow across the canopy, not a pressure wash into it. For large areas, a light drag with the back of a rake moves silt off the grass before it dries.

**Mow cautiously.** Once the soil can be walked on, mow at a higher than normal height to remove dead and damaged leaf material. One notch above the usual mowing height. Don't scalp a stressed lawn.

## Assessment

Once the immediate response is done, assess what you're dealing with.

**Surface silt layer.** Up to 10 mm — the grass should push through with care and time. 10 to 25 mm — the grass underneath may survive but recovery is slower and more intervention is needed. Above 25 mm of settled silt — the grass underneath is unlikely to recover. Renovation is needed.

**Days of inundation.** 1 to 2 days in cool conditions: likely to recover. 3 to 7 days in warm conditions: significant root damage likely, recovery variable. More than 7 days: extensive root loss, renovation probable.

**What the grass looks like.** Green grass poking through is a good sign. Yellowed, flattened, slimy grass is struggling. Areas that look completely white or brown and don't show any green within a week after the water has gone are likely dead.

## Recovery programme

**Penetrant wetting agent.** NZLA Penetrate applied once the surface has dried enough to walk on. Reopens surface water repellency and core-aeration from the flood. Helps the next rainfall move through the profile rather than sitting on the surface.

**Disease prevention.** Warm-season flooding is a Pythium setup. Apply NZLA Azoxy within the first week after recovery on warm-season turf, or on any lawn where the flood occurred in summer. NZLA Fungus Pro for preventative Fusarium cover in cooler conditions.

**Nutrition, carefully.** Don't push nitrogen into a stressed root system. Wait until you can see active new growth starting — usually 2 to 4 weeks after water recedes — then apply a moderate foliar feed: NZLA Charger at 200 to 300 mL per 100m² plus Iron+ at 200 mL per 100m². This provides nutrients the plant can absorb from the leaf while the root system rebuilds. Hold off granular until the root system is functional again.

NZLA Restore at 200 to 400 mL per 100m² soil drench supports the biological recovery of the root zone — beneficial microbes and organic acids that help rebuild the root environment after stress.

**Aerate once firm.** Core aeration relieves any surface core-aeration from water movement and water weight. Do this once the surface is firm enough for the aerator — at least 1 to 2 weeks after water clears, more if the soil is still wet.

**Overseed thin and bare areas.** Once the lawn has been through its initial recovery (4 to 6 weeks), oversow thin or bare patches. Use NZLA Perennial Rye at 25 to 30 g per m² on bare spots, or NZLA Starter granular at 1.5 kg per 100m² to feed the incoming seed.

## Significant silt deposits — renovation approach

For lawns under 25 mm or more of settled silt where the grass is dead or unrecoverable:

The silt itself is often fine for growing in once it's been worked. Rather than removing it all, work it in with the existing soil. Core aerate, then overseed into the mixed layer with NZLA Starter and appropriate grass seed.

If the silt layer is very thick (50 mm or more) and uneven, levelling first makes the job easier. Spread it more evenly across the surface using a flat shovel or drag, then aerate and seed.

The exception: contaminated floodwater silt from industrial or sewage sources. Don't work this material into the profile. Get proper advice on remediation.

## What not to do

Don't fertilise heavily into a root system under water stress — the roots can't process it and you risk burn or toxic salt levels at the root zone.

Don't apply pre-emergent herbicides to a flood-damaged lawn. The stressed root system is vulnerable and the lawn needs everything it can get to recover.

Don't delay. Lawns that get immediate attention (silt removed, penetrant applied, early  management) recover significantly better than those left for a week.

## Realistic timeline

Minor flooding (1 to 2 days, no silt): 2 to 4 weeks to full recovery with correct management.

Moderate flooding (3 to 5 days, light silt): 6 to 10 weeks to presentable. Full recovery by the following season.

Severe flooding (more than a week, heavy silt, significant plant death): partial or full renovation required. New establishment timeline applies — 8 to 12 weeks to usable, one full season to quality.
