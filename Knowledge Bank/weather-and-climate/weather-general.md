# Weather Events — Hail, Wind, Volcanic Ash

Beyond the main seasonal challenges, a few specific weather events cause distinctive lawn damage in NZ. None of them are routine — but knowing how to respond when they do happen saves a lot of guesswork.

## Hail Damage

Hailstones impact leaves and bruise the tissue. Most lawns look terrible for a week or two after a significant hail event and recover well without intervention.

**What hail does:** physical impact crushes leaf cells, causing brown bruising patterns across the lawn. Large hailstones can damage crowns in concentrated impact zones. The cool conditions following a hail event (often associated with cold fronts) add cold stress on top of the physical damage.

**Signs:** brown or grey bruising across the surface, most visible on the side of the lawn facing the wind direction the hail came from. Not confused with  — the bruising is uniform across large areas and follows the hail direction.

**Immediate response:** don't walk on the lawn until ice has cleared and the soil has firmed. Don't mow for at least a week. The temptation is to tidy up quickly — resist it.

**Recovery:** NZLA Liquid Boost or moderate Liquid N once growth resumes accelerates the response. NZLA Iron+ for colour during the recovery period. NZLA Restore for tissue recovery support. Most hail damage resolves with 4 to 6 weeks of active growth.

**Persistent dead patches:** impact zones with crown damage may not recover on their own. Assess at 6 weeks — anything without green crown tissue at that point needs reseeding. Autumn is the best window for this.

**Where hail is more likely in NZ:** inland Canterbury, Otago, and Hawke's Bay see occasional damaging hail. North Island summer thunderstorms sometimes produce large hail. Coastal areas are less exposed. Document damage with photos for insurance purposes if significant.

## Wind Damage

Wind damages lawns in two ways: physical damage from storms, and chronic desiccation from persistent exposure.

**Physical wind damage:** major gusts bring down branches and debris, flatten lawns, and occasionally lift turf in extreme events. Clear debris promptly — anything sitting on the lawn blocks light and accelerates damage underneath. Flattened lawn from strong winds typically recovers fully within a few weeks once conditions normalise.

**Desiccation from hot dry wind:** this is the more significant ongoing issue for many NZ lawns. Hot north-westerly winds in Canterbury, Otago, Marlborough, and Hawke's Bay strip moisture from leaves faster than root systems can replace it. The lawn browns on the windward face even when soil moisture is present — it's a leaf-level water deficit, not a soil moisture deficit.

Signs of wind desiccation: browning on one side of the lawn following the wind direction; browning that reverses within a few days of the wind dropping and irrigation-general resuming; soil that's still moist when you check it even though the grass looks stressed.

Response: deep watering before forecast wind events gives the soil profile moisture reserves. NZLA Wetter or Wetter 3W applied through the dry windy season maintains soil receptivity to whatever water is applied. Higher mowing height (50 to 60 mm) provides more leaf surface and some canopy protection.

For chronic wind exposure (lifestyle blocks on ridges, coastal properties, exposed sections): species selection matters. Tall fescue handles wind desiccation better than ryegrass because of its deeper root system. NZLA Elfin or Ultimate blend for exposed cool-season sites. Windbreak establishment — native species, macrocarpa shelter, or structural fencing — transforms the lawn microclimate over 3 to 5 years.

**Salt spray from coastal wind** compounds the desiccation problem. See the coastal and sandy soil article for the combined approach.

## Volcanic Ash

A NZ-specific risk for properties near active volcanic systems — Ruapehu, Taupō, Taranaki — and for central North Island properties downwind of eruption events. The last significant ash fall affecting populated areas was Ruapehu's 1996 series, but the risk is ongoing.

**What volcanic ash is:** fine ground rock particles, mostly silica-based, mildly acidic when recently erupted. Heavy accumulation smothers grass by blocking light. The mineral content is ultimately beneficial for soil once weathered, but the immediate effect is physical damage.

**Health and safety first.** Volcanic ash contains fine particles harmful to breathe. Always wear a P2 or P3 respirator when working with it. Follow Civil Defence and GeoNet guidance during and after eruption events before going outside to deal with lawn damage.

**Light ash (under 10 mm):** don't walk on it until it's wet. Light rainfall or gentle hosing settles dust and begins washing the ash into the soil. Most fine ash at this depth is incorporated by rainfall, irrigation-general, and worm activity without significant intervention.

**Moderate ash (10 to 50 mm):** physical removal before the lawn suffocates. A leaf blower on low setting (once ash is settled and moist) shifts bulk material. Then water in what's left to wash it into the soil. The lawn underneath may take weeks to recover from the smothering.

**Heavy ash (over 50 mm):** strip physically. The lawn underneath is likely dead. Plan for new lawn establishment once the site is cleared and stable.

**Soil chemistry after ash:** volcanic ash is initially acidic. A soil test a few months after significant ash deposition is worth doing to check pH and nutrient status before making fertiliser programme decisions. Long-term, volcanic ash minerals weather into plant-available nutrients — NZ's fertile volcanic soils in the North Island are partly the result of thousands of years of this process.

**Recovery programme after ash clearance:** NZLA Aerate and Wetter if the surface has become compacted or hydrophobic under the ash. NZLA Humic+ to support soil biology recovery. Liquid Boost and Iron+ once growth resumes. Reseeded areas with NZLA Starter and appropriate seed.
