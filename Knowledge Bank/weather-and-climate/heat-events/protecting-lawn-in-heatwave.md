# How do I protect my lawn during a heatwave?

Several things help, mostly about water management and reducing stress on the lawn.

Water timing:
- Early morning (4 to 7 am) is the best window. Cool, low evaporation, the water has the day to soak in.
- Avoid midday watering - much of it evaporates before reaching roots, and water droplets on leaves in full sun can cause scorch.
- Evening watering in heatwaves invites disease (wet canopy through warm humid night).

Water depth:
- Deep watering (15 to 25 mm) once or twice during the heatwave is better than light daily applications.
- Light frequent watering keeps the lawn surface wet but doesn't reach roots, which then stay shallow and vulnerable.
- The goal is wetting the soil profile down to 100 to 150 mm.

NZLA support products:
- NZLA Wetter or Wetter 3W applied before or during the heatwave gets water moving into the soil rather than beading off heat-baked surface.
- NZLA Aerate on compacted soil helps water move through dense soil.

Mowing changes:
- Raise cutting height to 50 to 60 mm. Tall grass shades soil, reduces evaporation, keeps lawn cooler.
- Skip mowing during the heatwave. The lawn isn't growing much, and the stress of mowing on a stressed lawn isn't worth it.
- If you must mow, do it early morning with a sharp blade.

Avoid:
- Fertiliser applications. Pushing growth into a stressed lawn makes things worse.
- Herbicide or fungicide. Stressed lawn responds badly to chemistry.
- Heavy foot traffic, kids' games, pet runs on the lawn during the heatwave.
- Mowing with a dull blade (tears the leaves, dehydrates faster).

Direct heat reduction:
- Light irrigation (5 mm) at mid-afternoon can cool the lawn briefly through evaporative cooling. Counterintuitive but works on extreme heat days. Mostly relevant for sports turf.
- Garden umbrellas or shade cloth for small high-value areas in extreme conditions.

After the heatwave:
- Resume normal watering programme.
- Assess damage and reseed any dead patches in autumn.
- Don't rush to fertilise immediately - wait until the lawn is fully recovered.

Most well-managed NZ cool-season home lawns survive normal NZ heatwaves with some browning but full recovery. The investment in soil preparation through spring is what makes the difference.
