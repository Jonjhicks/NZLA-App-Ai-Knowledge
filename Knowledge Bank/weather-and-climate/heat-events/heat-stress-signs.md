# How do I know if my lawn is heat-stressed?

Several signs that appear in order as the stress builds.

Early signs:
- Lawn takes on a blue-grey cast across the surface. The chlorophyll is still there but the leaves are wilting slightly.
- Footprints stay compressed for several seconds after you walk across instead of springing back.
- The lawn looks slightly dull rather than its usual vibrant green.

Moderate stress:
- Distinct grey-green colour, particularly in afternoon heat.
- Leaves curling or folding lengthwise to reduce surface area exposed to sun.
- Lawn feels crunchy underfoot.
- Patches in the hottest spots (full sun, north-facing) starting to brown.

Heavy stress:
- Brown patches expanding across the lawn.
- Crispy dead-looking grass in the worst spots.
- The crown (growing point at soil level) still has green tissue if you part the grass with your fingers - if so, the lawn is recoverable.

Severe stress (recovery uncertain):
- Whole sections of lawn brown and crispy with no green at the crown.
- Soil below is bone dry.
- Recovery depends on how long it stays stressed before water arrives.

Causes:
- Air temps over 28 °C combined with no irrigation.
- Drought conditions building up over weeks.
- Hot dry wind drying the lawn out faster than the roots can replace water.
- Cool-season lawns (ryegrass, fescue) in particularly hot regions (inland NI, Canterbury, Hawke's Bay).

Different from disease:
- Heat stress affects whole lawn or large sections, not defined round patches.
- Heat stress responds quickly to deep watering (improvement within days).
- Disease patches have defined edges and don't respond to water alone.

What to do:
- Water deeply (15 to 25 mm). NZLA Wetter helps water reach the root zone.
- Raise cutting height.
- Reduce traffic on the stressed lawn.
- Don't fertilise or spray chemistry while stressed.

Most cool-season home lawns in NZ show heat stress for a few weeks each summer and recover when rain returns. It's not the disaster it looks like.
