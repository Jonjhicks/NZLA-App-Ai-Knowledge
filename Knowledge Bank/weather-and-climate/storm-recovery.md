# Storm, Flood and Cyclone Recovery

Cyclone Gabrielle (2023) caused widespread lawn damage across the North Island from silt deposition, flooding, saltwater inundation, and debris. Recovery approach depends entirely on what type of damage the lawn received — they're different problems with different fixes.

## Assess Before You Act

Before spending money on anything, work out what you've got:

- Silt or sediment deposited over the lawn from river or stormwater flooding.
- Standing freshwater that sat on the lawn for an extended period.
- Saltwater inundation from storm surge, coastal flooding, or tide surge.
- Physical debris (branches, building material, contaminated material).
- Pure wind damage or flattening with no flooding.

The response for each is different.

## Silt and Sediment

**Light silt (under 25 mm):** the lawn may survive underneath. Once the material is dry enough to work with, rake and work it into the surface. Natural rainfall and worm activity continue the mixing process. The lawn may look rough but often recovers.

**Moderate silt (25 to 50 mm):** physically remove most of it, particularly if the material is poor quality (heavy clay, contaminated). Even fine river silt at this depth blocks light and air from the grass below for long enough to kill it. What's left after physical removal can be worked in.

**Heavy silt (over 50 mm):** the lawn underneath is likely dead from suffocation. Strip the silt, assess the original topsoil condition, and plan for new lawn establishment. This is not a repair job — it's a new lawn project.

**Contamination:** silt from urban stormwater flooding or rivers passing industrial areas may contain contaminants (sewage, heavy metals, petrochemicals). If the source is uncertain, get the material tested before working it into the soil.

## Freshwater Flooding

**Under 24 hours:** grass is more flood-tolerant than most people expect. Once the water drains, remove debris, assess for silt, and resume normal management within a week or two.

**24 to 72 hours:** some root suffocation from oxygen deprivation is likely. The lawn may yellow and thin in affected areas. Wait until the soil firms up enough to walk on without core-aeration, then core aerate to restore air movement. Don't fertilise immediately — stressed roots don't process nitrogen efficiently and you risk burning. Apply NZLA Aerate and Humic+ to support soil biology recovery. Light recovery foliar (NZLA Liquid Boost) once growth visibly resumes.

**Over 72 hours:** significant die-off likely. Anaerobic conditions develop — look for black soil layer and sulphur smell. Aeration is urgent once the soil is workable. The soil biology will take months to recover. Reseed bare areas in autumn.

**Process for typical freshwater flood recovery:**
1. Wait for water to drain and soil to firm.
2. Clear silt and debris.
3. Core aerate as soon as the surface will take it.
4. Apply NZLA Aerate and Wetter if surface has become hydrophobic.
5. Apply NZLA Humic+ to support soil biology recovery.
6. Wait 2 to 3 weeks, then assess green-up.
7. NZLA Liquid Boost foliar once growth resumes.
8. Reseed dead areas with NZLA Starter and appropriate seed in autumn.

## Saltwater Inundation

Saltwater is significantly more damaging than freshwater. Salt accumulates in the soil and creates osmotic stress on grass roots — high salt concentration outside the roots draws water out of the plant by osmosis. Sodium also displaces calcium and magnesium from soil exchange sites, damaging soil structure.

**Immediate response (first 24 to 48 hours):** heavy freshwater flush as soon as practical. This is the single most important step — the sooner you dilute and leach the salt, the better the outcome. Apply 25 to 40 mm of freshwater over the affected area. If rain is forecast, let it help.

**Ongoing:** continue regular heavy flushing for the first few weeks. The salt in the soil profile needs to move down and out. NZLA Aerate helps water movement through the soil.

**Gypsum:** calcium sulphate (gypsum) helps displace sodium from soil exchange sites — worth applying at 20 to 30 kg per 100 m² on confirmed sodic post-salt areas and watering in. Soil test 3 to 4 weeks after the event can confirm sodium levels and guide further treatment.

**NZLA Humic+:** supports soil biology and helps buffer soil chemistry during recovery.

**Species note:** warm-season grasses (couch) are generally more salt-tolerant than cool-season ryegrass and fescue. For coastal properties with ongoing salt risk, species selection matters for long-term resilience.

**Realistic timeline:** light salt exposure — 4 to 8 weeks recovery. Moderate exposure — 3 to 6 months. Heavy or prolonged exposure may require full re-establishment.

## Wind and Physical Storm Damage

Wind damage without flooding usually resolves faster. A flattened lawn from cyclone-force winds comes back within weeks once conditions normalise.

Clear debris promptly — anything sitting on the lawn blocks light and accelerates damage. Branches, leaves, and material from buildings should be removed as soon as safely accessible.

For debris compression (heavy objects sitting on the lawn for days): the grass underneath is likely yellow or dead. Core aerate once the object is removed, then assess. Often the grass recovers from the crowns; in worse cases, overseed in autumn.

## Insurance, Documentation, and Contamination

Photograph everything before you start any clearance work. Insurance claims and EQC assessments need documentation.

For contaminated material (sewage, chemical residue, industrial runoff): don't work it into the soil without testing. Professional remediation may be required for serious contamination.

EQC and council resources during declared natural hazard events may cover some remediation costs — check with local council and EQC for current guidelines.

## Recovery Timeline Summary

- Light damage (short freshwater flood, silt under 25 mm, wind only): 4 to 8 weeks active management.
- Moderate damage (extended flood, 25 to 50 mm silt, minor salt): autumn renovation, full recovery 3 to 6 months.
- Severe damage (heavy silt, prolonged inundation, saltwater saturation): full re-establishment, 6 to 12 months to good presentation.
