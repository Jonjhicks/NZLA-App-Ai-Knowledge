# How do I tell cold stress from winter disease?

The visual symptoms can overlap, but the patterns differ.

Cold stress / frost damage:
- Affects whole lawn or large open areas (anywhere the cold hit).
- Brown or grey patches without defined edges.
- Worst in low-lying areas where cold air settles.
- Worst in shaded areas that don't warm up during the day.
- No fungal mycelium visible.
- Doesn't expand or worsen day to day - it's a single event.

Fusarium patch (winter disease):
- Defined round patches 100 to 300 mm across.
- Patches expand over days and weeks if untreated.
- Pink or red tinge at the edges visible in damp morning conditions.
- Cottony white mycelium on wet patches in early morning.
- Often appears in patches that aren't the coldest spots - any area with extended canopy moisture is vulnerable.

Red thread:
- Distinct pink-red threads of mycelium visible on infected leaves.
- Patches irregular, not perfectly round.
- Cool moist conditions, low nitrogen lawn.
- Different from frost damage which doesn't show any visible fungal growth.

Combined situations:
- Cold-stressed lawn can become disease-stressed too.
- Frost damage in autumn can be followed by fusarium patch in the same area through winter.
- Push recovery and address fertility - a vigorous lawn handles both stresses better.

Action by diagnosis:
- Cold stress confirmed: wait for warmer conditions and spring growth. No chemistry helps.
- Disease confirmed: NZLA Azoxy or Fungus Pro at label rate.
- Mixed or unclear: treat the disease side with chemistry, address cold stress with cultural management and patience.

When to spray:
- Confirmed disease patches, conditions favourable to spread (cool wet, humid mornings): spray.
- Cold stress only, no fungal signs: don't spray. Chemistry doesn't help cold.
- Patches in winter, not sure which: if pink mycelium is visible at any point, it's likely fusarium. If no fungal signs over a week of observation, it's likely cold stress.

For ambiguous cases, post a photo on the NZLA app forum with the time of day, current weather, and a close-up. Disease pattern is usually easy to identify with a clear photo.
