# How do I help my lawn recover from frost damage?

Depends on what kind of damage you've got.

Light frost damage (no visible aftermath):
- No action needed. The lawn rebounds as soon as conditions warm.

Footprint frost damage (bruised tracks):
- Stop walking on the lawn during frosty mornings.
- Wait for spring growth - the bruised tissue won't recover during winter but new growth from the crown will fill in.
- Light overseeding in spring for badly bruised areas if the crown is damaged.

Heavy frost damage on warm-season turf:
- Don't panic, kikuyu and couch are usually dormant rather than dead.
- Wait until spring soil temps come back up (14 to 16 °C) and assess regrowth.
- Patches that don't regrow by mid-November are dead and need replugging or reseeding.

Spring recovery push:
- Once active growth resumes, NZLA Liquid Boost or Liquid N at moderate rate accelerates green-up.
- Iron+ for colour while the lawn fills back in.
- Patience - warm-season recovery from cold winters can take 6 to 8 weeks of spring growth to look normal.

For high-value lawn areas (event surface, premium home lawn):
- NZLA Frost Cloth applied overnight protects from severe frost.
- Particularly useful for new seedlings vulnerable to frost.
- Practical for small areas; impractical for large lawns.

Cultural protection going forward:
- Don't apply heavy nitrogen late in autumn (October/November). Soft growth is more vulnerable to early-season frost damage.
- Mow at the higher end of normal cutting heights through autumn (50 to 60 mm), providing more leaf protection for the crown.
- Improve drainage if waterlogged conditions worsen winter damage.

Species considerations:
- If frost damage is repeatedly affecting your warm-season lawn, you're at the cold limit of the species for your region.
- Cool-season overseed (ryegrass) in autumn provides winter colour and reduces visible damage.
- For South Island lawns, cool-season turf is the more reliable choice rather than fighting cold damage on warm-season.

For most NZ home lawns:
- Light frost damage requires no intervention.
- Spring growth handles most damage from a normal winter.
- Significant intervention is only needed for warm-season turf in colder regions or for fresh seedling protection.

NZLA range doesn't have a specific cold protection product because the chemistry doesn't really exist - frost protection is about timing, cultural management, and physical cover for vulnerable situations.
