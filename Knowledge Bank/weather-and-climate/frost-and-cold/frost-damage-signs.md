# How do I tell if my lawn has frost damage?

Frost damage shows in different ways depending on severity and what caused it.

Light frost damage (overnight frost, normal NZ winter):
- Lawn looks blue-grey for a few hours in the morning until the frost melts.
- Recovers fully as soon as temperatures rise.
- No real damage, just temporary appearance.

Footprint frost damage:
- Visible bruise marks where someone walked on frozen grass.
- Crushed leaf cells from foot pressure on frozen tissue.
- Brown bruise patterns last for weeks.
- Avoid walking on the lawn when frost is on the grass.

Heavy frost damage (severe cold event):
- Whole sections of lawn look black or dark brown after the frost melts.
- Affects warm-season grasses (kikuyu, couch) more than cool-season (ryegrass, fescue).
- Recovery slow as the lawn is in winter dormancy.

Frost burn from mowing:
- Brown patches in the shape of recent mowing passes.
- Happens when mowing frozen grass - the blade tears frozen tissue causing damage.
- Recovery in spring once growth resumes.

Long-term cold stress:
- Gradual thinning over winter on lawns at the cold limit of their species range.
- Particularly affects warm-season turf in southern parts of NZ.
- Lawn looks brown and dormant for extended periods.

Cool-season lawns vs warm-season:
- Cool-season (ryegrass, tall fescue, fine fescue): tolerate frost well, look fine through winter.
- Kikuyu: handles light frost but goes dormant for longer in cold winters, looks brown.
- Couch: more cold-tolerant variants (like NZLA Couch) handle NZ winters better than standard bermuda.

Recovery:
- Light frost: no action needed.
- Footprint damage: avoid further traffic, wait for spring growth.
- Heavy frost on warm-season turf: wait until spring for assessment.
- Spring recovery push with NZLA Liquid Boost and Iron+ once growth resumes.

Don't:
- Mow frosted lawn.
- Walk on frosted lawn.
- Apply nitrogen heading into cold conditions (the lush growth is more vulnerable).

For most NZ home lawns, frost is more visual than damaging. The lawn looks worse than it is on frosty mornings and recovers fully as temperatures warm.
