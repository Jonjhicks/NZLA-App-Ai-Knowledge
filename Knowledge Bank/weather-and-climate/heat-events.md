# Heat Stress and Heatwave Management

Heat stress in lawn grasses is a water deficit problem, not a temperature problem per se. Grass handles high temperatures fine when it has adequate water. What damages it is the combination of heat, high evapotranspiration, and insufficient soil moisture to keep up.

## Recognising Heat Stress

Stress develops in stages. Catching it early means a better response.

**Early signs:** the lawn takes on a blue-grey or grey-green cast — different from its normal green. Footprints stay compressed for several seconds after you walk across (the grass lacks the turgor pressure to spring back). The lawn looks slightly dull rather than crisp.

**Moderate stress:** leaves curling or folding lengthwise to reduce surface area exposed to sun. Crunchy underfoot. Patches starting to brown in the hottest spots — full sun exposures, areas adjacent to concrete paving, north-facing slopes.

**Heavy stress:** widespread browning. Grass feels brittle. Part the grass in a brown area and check the crown (the growing point right at soil level). Green tissue at the crown means the plant is alive and dormant. No green tissue means that section is dead.

**Distinguishing heat stress from :** heat stress affects large sections or the whole lawn relatively uniformly, especially in the most exposed areas. Disease patches are defined and often have a specific pattern (circular, with different edges). Heat stress responds to deep watering within days. Disease doesn't resolve with watering alone.

## Protecting the Lawn During a Heatwave

**Water timing.** Early morning — 4 am to 7 am. Cool, calm, minimal evaporation. The water reaches the rootzone efficiently and the leaf surface dries during the day. Evening watering during a heatwave keeps the canopy wet through a warm humid night, adding  risk on top of heat stress. Avoid it.

**Water depth.** One deep watering (15 to 25 mm) per session rather than light daily applications. Deep watering gets moisture to the full rootzone at 100 to 150 mm. Light daily watering wets only the top 30 to 40 mm, which evaporates between applications and trains roots to stay shallow.

**NZLA Wetter or Wetter 3W.** Applied before or during the heatwave, the wetting agent ensures water moves into the soil profile rather than beading off heat-dried surface. This is particularly important for any area that's been dry for a week or more — the hydrophobic layer develops fast in heat and water literally won't enter without a surfactant.

**Raise cutting height.** 50 to 60 mm. Taller grass shades the soil surface, reducing evaporation and keeping soil temperature lower. It also maintains more leaf area for photosynthesis when the plant is already under stress.

**Stop mowing if you can.** The lawn isn't growing much in peak heat. The stress of mowing on a dehydrated plant — especially with a dull blade that tears rather than cuts — isn't worth it. If you must mow, do it early morning with a sharp blade.

**What to avoid:** fertiliser, herbicide, and fungicide applications during heat stress. The lawn is in no condition to process them efficiently and the risk of damage is higher than normal.

## After the Heatwave

Resume normal watering programme. Assess what's survived and what's dead. Don't rush to fertilise — wait until the lawn has clearly resumed growth and the soil temperature has dropped back below 25°C.

NZLA Liquid Boost at moderate rates can accelerate green-up once growth resumes. Iron+ for colour. Avoid heavy nitrogen until the lawn is demonstrably past the stress period.

Dead sections that haven't shown any crown growth 2 to 3 weeks after the heat event and adequate rehydration need reseeding. Autumn is the best window for this in most of NZ — March to May, when conditions actively favour cool-season grass establishment.

## Preparing Before Summer Arrives

A lawn that's been set up well handles a normal NZ heatwave without serious damage. The preparation:
- Deep-rooted species (tall fescue as a component, particularly in Canterbury, Hawke's Bay, and exposed sites).
- Pre-summer wetting agent (NZLA Wetter 3W in October).
- Deep and infrequent watering habit established from spring so roots are already at depth before the hot weather arrives.
- NZLA PGR in late spring to regulate growth and harden the plant.
- Adequate potassium through spring (NZLA All Seasons N+) for cell membrane strength.

Most NZ cool-season home lawns show some heat stress browning through January and February every year. That's normal. A well-prepared lawn comes through it with recoverable dormancy rather than significant dead patches.
