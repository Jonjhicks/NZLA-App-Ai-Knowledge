# Frost, Cold Stress and Winter Recovery

Most NZ lawn frost damage looks worse than it is. The grass browns or greys, it looks dead, and then spring arrives and the lawn recovers. Understanding what's actually happening helps you decide when to intervene and when to wait.

## What Frost Does to Grass

**Light overnight frost (normal NZ winter):** the lawn looks blue-grey in the morning, recovers fully as temperatures rise. No damage, just temporary appearance.

**Footprint frost damage:** walking on frozen grass crushes the ice crystals inside the leaf cells. The cell walls rupture, leaving brown bruise marks that last for weeks. This is avoidable. Don't walk on a frosted lawn. Don't let anyone else either. The damage heals but it takes weeks of new growth to cover the bruised patches.

**Heavy frost on warm-season turf:** kikuyu and couch go into dormancy in winter and can survive significant cold, but extended periods of sub-zero temperatures cause cellular damage. The lawn looks black or dark brown after a severe frost, particularly on warm-season species. Recovery depends on whether the crown survived. Part the brown grass and check — green tissue at the base means the plant is alive and will recover.

**Frost burn from mowing:** mowing frozen grass tears and damages frozen leaf cells. Brown patches appear in the mowing pattern. Avoid mowing when frost is present on the grass.

## Telling Frost Damage from Winter Disease

They can look similar but the patterns are different.

**Frost / cold stress:**
- Affects large open areas where cold air settled overnight.
- Worst in low-lying spots, hollows, shaded areas that don't warm up during the day.
- No defined edges — it's wherever the cold hit.
- No fungal mycelium visible.
- Doesn't expand from day to day. It's a fixed event.
- Responds to warming temperatures and spring growth, not chemistry.

**Fusarium patch (winter ):**
- Defined round to oval patches, 100 to 300 mm across.
- Patches expand over days and weeks if untreated.
- Pink or reddish tinge at the patch edge in damp morning conditions.
- Cottony white mycelium visible on wet infected grass in early morning.
- Appears where canopy moisture is high, not just where it's coldest.
- Responds to NZLA Azoxy or Fungus Pro.

**Red thread:**
- Distinct pink-red threads of mycelium on infected leaves (visible with close inspection).
- Irregular patches, not cleanly round.
- Cool moist conditions, typically on low-nitrogen lawns.
- Does not respond to warming; responds to a nitrogen application and NZLA Fungus Pro.

The key test: look for fungal signs (mycelium, discolouration at the margins, spread over days). If you see them, it's . If you don't, and the damage is static and follows the cold air pattern, it's cold stress.

**When you're not sure:** take a close-up photo in the morning before temperatures rise. Post it to the NZLA app forum with the location, weather conditions, and a description of how long the patches have been there. Disease pattern is usually straightforward to identify with a good early-morning photo showing mycelium.

## Recovery from Frost Damage

**Light frost damage:** no action needed. The lawn recovers as conditions warm.

**Footprint damage:** keep traffic off the lawn during frosty mornings for the rest of the season. The bruised tissue won't repair itself during winter, but new spring growth will cover it within weeks.

**Heavy frost on warm-season turf:** wait until spring soil temperatures return to 14 to 16°C before assessing whether areas are dead or dormant. Kikuyu and couch that went dormant will start showing green growth from the crown and stolon nodes. Sections with no green growth by mid-November are dead and need replanting.

**Spring recovery push:** once growth is visibly resuming, NZLA Liquid Boost or moderate Liquid N accelerates green-up. NZLA Iron+ improves colour as the lawn fills back in. Don't push hard nitrogen too early — wait until the lawn is clearly growing.

## Cultural Protection

Heavy autumn nitrogen applications produce soft, frost-vulnerable growth. Back off nitrogen after March. Adequate potassium builds cell-membrane resilience against frost damage — NZLA All Seasons N+ through autumn maintains the right balance.

Higher mowing height through autumn (50 to 60 mm) provides more leaf mass to protect the crown from frost. Taller grass retains slightly more warmth at the base.

For small high-value areas — a newly seeded patch, an event lawn in a frost-prone area — NZLA Frost Cloth provides physical protection from overnight frost. Practical for limited areas, impractical for whole lawns.

For most NZ home lawns, frost is a visual inconvenience rather than a management crisis. Cool-season grasses (ryegrass, fescue) handle NZ winters well. It's the warm-season turf in southern regions that requires more watchfulness.
