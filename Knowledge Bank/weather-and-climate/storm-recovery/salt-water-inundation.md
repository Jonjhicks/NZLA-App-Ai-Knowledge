# My lawn was hit by saltwater - what now?

Saltwater inundation from storm surge, tsunami events, or king tide events with cyclone backing causes specific damage that needs specific recovery.

What saltwater does:
- Salt accumulates in soil, drawing water out of grass roots through reverse osmosis (the high salt concentration outside the roots pulls water from inside).
- Sodium displaces calcium and magnesium in the soil, damaging soil structure.
- Chloride is toxic to grass at high concentrations.
- Surface salt crust can form as water evaporates.

Immediate response (first 24 to 48 hours):
- Heavy freshwater flush as soon as practical. The more freshwater you can apply, the more salt leaches out.
- If natural rainfall is forecast in the days after, let it help.
- Don't worry about over-watering at this stage - getting salt out of the soil is the priority.

Soil management:
- NZLA Aerate physically helps water move through the soil to flush salt down through the profile.
- NZLA Humic+ supports soil biology recovery and helps with salt buffering.
- Gypsum (calcium sulphate) can help displace sodium from soil exchange sites and improve structure.

Assessing damage:
- Light salt exposure (brief contact, immediate flush): often minor damage, recovery within weeks.
- Moderate exposure (several hours of contact): significant browning and thinning, partial recovery over months.
- Heavy exposure (extended contact, no flush): severe damage, may need full reseeding.

Salt-tolerant grass species:
- Some grasses tolerate residual salt better than others.
- Couch and warm-season grasses tend to be more salt-tolerant than cool-season ryegrass and fescue.
- For coastal properties with chronic salt exposure, species selection matters - NZLA Couch handles salt spray reasonably well.

Long-term:
- Soil test for sodium and chloride 2 to 4 weeks after the event. Confirms what's still in the soil.
- Continued flushing during the recovery period (regular deep watering for 2 to 3 months).
- Gypsum application based on soil test results.

Realistic timeline:
- Light exposure: 4 to 8 weeks recovery.
- Moderate exposure: 3 to 6 months full recovery with active management.
- Heavy exposure: full re-establishment may be needed.

For NZ coastal properties where saltwater inundation is a real risk (low-lying coastal sites, harbour-fringe properties), having a recovery plan in place before an event matters. Storage of freshwater supply, basic chemistry on hand (Aerate, Humic+), and species selection that handles occasional salt all help.
