# How do I recover my lawn after a cyclone or major storm?

Cyclone Gabrielle in 2023 caused widespread lawn damage across the North Island. Recovery depends on what damage you've got - silt, flooding, debris, salt water, or just wind damage.

Assess the damage first:
- Silt or sediment deposits over the lawn (river flooding).
- Standing water that lasted more than 48 hours (drowning).
- Saltwater inundation (coastal).
- Debris (branches, building material, contaminated material).
- Pure wind damage with no flooding.

Silt and sediment recovery:
- Light silt (under 25 mm): work it into the lawn with a rake when dry, the soil will eventually mix in.
- Moderate silt (25 to 50 mm): physically remove most of it, especially if the material is poor quality.
- Heavy silt (over 50 mm): the lawn underneath is dead. Strip and start over.
- The silt may contain contaminants from upstream sources (sewage, industrial runoff) - get tested if unsure.

Standing water (drowning):
- Lawns sitting under water for over 48 hours often suffocate. Roots die from oxygen deprivation.
- Once water drains, assess the lawn condition.
- Black anaerobic soil layer underneath indicates the soil ecology is damaged - takes time to recover.
- NZLA Aerate helps water and air movement through the recovering soil.

Debris and contamination:
- Remove debris before it kills the grass underneath.
- Test any unknown deposits (oil, chemical residues) before working into the soil.
- Material from contaminated upstream sources may need professional assessment.

Wind damage:
- Whole lawn flattened with no flood: usually recovers fine within weeks.
- Trees fallen on the lawn: clear the tree, assess damage underneath, reseed bare areas.
- Branches and small debris: clear, then check for compaction or bruising.

Recovery programme:
- Start with assessment, don't rush to chemistry.
- Aerate (mechanical and NZLA Aerate) to restore soil function.
- Apply NZLA Starter to areas being reseeded.
- Liquid Boost for push as the lawn begins to recover.

Insurance and contamination:
- Document damage with photos before clearing.
- Major flood damage may be covered by EQC, council, or insurance depending on the event.
- For lawns contaminated by sewage or chemical spills, professional remediation may be needed.

Realistic timeline:
- Light damage: 4 to 8 weeks recovery with active programme.
- Moderate damage: full reseed in autumn after stabilising the site.
- Severe damage: full new lawn establishment, may take 6 to 12 months to look good.

For lawns in NZ flood-prone regions, the management approach assumes occasional major events. The investment in good drainage and resilient species pays off over years.
