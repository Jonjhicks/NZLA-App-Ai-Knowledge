# Otago and Southland Lawn Calendar

Otago and Southland is the coldest lawn management environment in New Zealand. Cool-season grasses only. The growing season is shorter, winters are longer, frost is a regular feature, and snow is possible across much of the region.

Disease pressure is dominated by fusarium patch and snow mould rather than the summer pathogens that cause issues in Auckland and BOP. Summer drought is less of a concern than elsewhere — the challenge is the other end of the year.

## Species Choice

Cool-season grasses suit the climate well. The question is which ones to prioritise.

NZLA RSR Ryegrass is a good all-round choice for family lawns — the self-repairing stolon system handles wear well and it germinates quickly during the autumn renovation window.

Tall fescue (NZLA Elfin, Ultimate) is worth including in seed mixes for its drought tolerance (Otago summers can be dry, particularly in Central Otago and inland) and its winter hardiness. For drier areas — Cromwell, Alexandra, Wanaka basin — tall fescue as the primary species makes good sense.

Fine fescue suits shaded areas which are common on Southland and Dunedin properties with established trees and shelterbelts.

Warm-season turf (kikuyu, couch) is not viable in Otago and Southland. Don't attempt it.

## The Annual Calendar

**January — mild summer.** Otago and Southland summers are cooler than the rest of New Zealand. Disease pressure from summer pathogens (brown patch, pythium) is low. Standard cool-season programme. Mow at 40 to 50 mm. Irrigation if dry — Central Otago and inland areas can have dry summers.

**February.** Late summer continuing. Watch for occasional dry spells particularly inland. Soil still warm — any seeding-and-over-seeding of worn summer areas can begin.

**March — best fertiliser timing.** Conditions cooling, autumn colours arriving. NZLA All Seasons or All Seasons N+. Begin renovation programme. Conditions often excellent for renovation with mild temperatures and adequate moisture.

**April — renovation month.** Soil temperatures 12 to 16°C — good germination conditions. Scarify, core aerate, top dress, overseed. Second autumn fertiliser. BWC for broadleaf . Fusarium pressure building —  watch begins in earnest.

**May — late autumn.** Last fertiliser before winter. Pre-winter fungicide: NZLA Azoxy or Fungus Pro on any lawn with fusarium history. This is more important in Otago/Southland than warmer regions because fusarium conditions persist longer here.

**June — winter.** Cool-season grasses slow significantly or stop. Frost becoming regular. Mowing frequency drops to every 3 to 4 weeks when growth continues. No nitrogen fertiliser from now until September.

**July — deep winter.** Cold. Snow possible across much of the region. Southland plains, Wanaka, Queenstown, and Te Anau all see meaningful snowfall in some winters. Don't walk on snow-covered lawn — compressing the crowns under snow increases  risk and causes physical damage. Pink snow mould (fusarium patch exacerbated by snow cover) is a real risk on susceptible lawns.

**August.** Cold continuing. Some years show signs of spring breaking in August; other years it remains cold through to September. No fertiliser. Equipment service and spring planning.

**September — spring building.** Soil temperatures still cool — wait for consistent warming before fertilising. Frost still possible, particularly inland and in Southland. First fertiliser when conditions are clearly improving, typically late September for coastal Otago and southern Southland, earlier for Dunedin.

**October — spring.** Growth resuming. First fertiliser application if not done in September. BWC herbicide window opens — spring is the best timing for broadleaf weed control. Weed seed germination happening.

**November.** Strong spring growth. Last big fertiliser push of spring. Wetter or Wetter 3W application for summer preparation — even Otago gets dry enough to benefit. PGR for high-input lawns.

**December — early summer.** Mild summer conditions. Continue maintenance programme. Disease pressure from summer pathogens is low, though rust can appear in shoulder seasons.

## Otago and Southland-Specific Notes

**Frost management** is important in the autumn programme. Don't apply high-nitrogen fertiliser late in autumn — it promotes soft, frost-vulnerable growth. Keep mowing height slightly higher through autumn to protect the crowns. For high-value small areas, frost cloth is a practical option.

**Snow mould** (which is effectively cold-adapted fusarium patch) is the most significant  risk in the region. The signs are circular pink-white patches appearing after snow melts or in persistently cold damp conditions. NZLA Fungus Pro is effective on snow mould. Preventative applications before winter on any lawn with history are worthwhile. Don't walk on snow-covered lawn — this creates core-aeration and disruption that increases susceptibility.

**Central Otago** (Cromwell, Alexandra, Clyde, Roxburgh) has its own character — cold winters with frost, warm and dry summers with high UV. It's the most extreme climate in New Zealand for lawn management. Tall fescue handles the summer dry; the frost management and fusarium programme handles the winter end. Watering infrastructure matters in Central Otago in summer.

**Southland** and the lower South Island generally gets adequate rainfall and doesn't have the same drought challenge as Central Otago. The cold is the main factor. Timing of spring start-up is later than Dunedin and coastal Otago — don't rush fertiliser and renovation timing into conditions that are still too cold.

**Spring timing.** The single biggest mistake for Otago and Southland lawns is applying spring fertiliser too early. Soil temperature matters more than calendar date. Wait until daytime temperatures are consistently above 10°C and frost risk is past before the first spring application.
