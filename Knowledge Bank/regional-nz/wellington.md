# Wellington Lawn Calendar

Wind defines Wellington lawn management more than any other factor. Even on a warm summer day, an exposed Wellington lawn dries out faster than a sheltered lawn would in the same conditions. Combined with cool damp winters, the programme has to account for both extremes.

Cool-season grasses dominate across the region. The climate doesn't suit warm-season turf — kikuyu and couch are marginal in Wellington and generally not recommended.

## Species Choice

NZLA RSR Ryegrass handles wear and self-repairs well. It's a solid base species for most Wellington lawns.

Tall fescue (NZLA Elfin, Ultimate) suits exposed sites where the wind-drying effect on summer lawns is pronounced. Its deeper root system also handles the dry spells that exposed Wellington sites experience.

Fine fescue works well in shaded areas under canopy, which are common on Wellington's hilly terrain and established neighbourhoods.

For exposed hillside or coastal sections, NZLA Ultimate blend (tall fescue dominant) is the better choice over pure ryegrass. The drought tolerance and wind-desiccation resistance make a real difference on those sites.

## The Annual Calendar

**January — summer.** Wellington summers are milder than Auckland and Canterbury. Less extreme drought stress, but wind drying is ongoing. NZLA Wetter or Wetter 3W for moisture retention on exposed sites. Continue regular watering. Mow at 45 to 50 mm. Disease pressure is moderate — less than Auckland but worth monitoring.

**February.** Late summer dry spells possible. Monitor for dry patch on exposed areas. Grub activity building on some lawns — check if bird activity (starlings probing the surface) is increasing.

**March — autumn fertiliser.** Best fertiliser application of the year: NZLA All Seasons or All Seasons N+. Begin renovation planning. Conditions typically good — warmth still in the ground, humidity increasing.

**April — renovation window.** Soil temperatures 13 to 17°C. Excellent germination conditions. Scarify, core aerate, top dress, overseed. Second autumn fertiliser. BWC for broadleaf . Fusarium patch watch begins as nights cool and dampness increases.

**May.** Conditions often damp and cool. Fusarium pressure building. Preventative NZLA Azoxy or Fungus Pro on any lawn with  history. Last fertiliser before winter.

**June — winter.** Cool and damp Wellington winter. Slow growth but lawns don't fully stop. Mowing every 3 to 4 weeks when growth continues. Fusarium vigilance ongoing. No nitrogen fertiliser.

**July–August — deep winter.** Hutt Valley and inland areas (Karori, hill suburbs) colder than waterfront and northern suburbs. Frost possible inland. Minimal intervention. Watch for fusarium in damp conditions.

**September — spring building.** Soil temperatures rising. First spring fertiliser when conditions are clearly improving, typically late September. BWC herbicide window opens.

**October — spring.** Strong growth. Weekly mowing or more. NZLA Wetter 3W application — getting the pre-summer wetter programme running before the wind-drying season is smart timing. Foliar feeding with Liquid Boost and Iron+.

**November.** Last big fertiliser push before summer. PGR option for high-input lawns.

**December — early summer.** Wind management becomes the dominant concern. Deep watering on exposed sites. Continue  monitoring.

## Wellington-Specific Notes

**Wind is the constant.** It changes the calculation for watering, spraying, and establishment. Never spray herbicides on a Wellington windy day — drift risk is high and herbicide efficacy is reduced when the plant is stressed. For new lawn establishment, check the forecast for a calm window and shelter new seed with a light straw cover or similar until germination.

**Exposed hillside lawns** dry out significantly faster than sheltered valley sites. A lawn on a north-facing Wellington hillside exposed to the nor'wester needs more water and a more drought-tolerant species than a sheltered Hutt Valley lawn. NZLA Ultimate blend is the right call for exposed sites.

**Coastal Wellington** (Lyall Bay, Miramar, Island Bay, Seatoun) deals with salt spray. Salt-tolerant species and periodic flushing after significant onshore winds. The wind and salt combination means lower achievable lawn quality on very exposed sections — set expectations accordingly.

**Hutt Valley** has its own microclimate — generally calmer, colder in winter (frost more likely than central Wellington), and warmer in summer. Lower rainfall relative to the western suburbs. Closer in character to inland cool-temperate management. Adjust timing slightly later for spring start-up and slightly earlier for autumn programme.

**Wairarapa and Kapiti** are administratively adjacent to Wellington but have quite different climates. Wairarapa is drier and more continental; Kapiti Coast is milder and more humid. Adjust the programme accordingly.

**Disease pressure.** Fusarium patch is the main  risk in Wellington — the cool damp winters and shoulder seasons create ideal conditions. Brown patch is less of a concern than in Auckland due to the milder summers. A preventative Azoxy or Fungus Pro application in April on susceptible lawns is the most efficient  management approach.
