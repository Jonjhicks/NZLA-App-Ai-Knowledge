# Bay of Plenty Lawn Calendar

Bay of Plenty runs warm and humid. Similar climate to Auckland but slightly warmer in summer, which means  pressure is comparable and fall armyworm was first detected here in New Zealand in 2022.

The region has two notable soil types that affect lawn management: the heavy clay soils of much of Tauranga and coastal BOP, and the pumice and volcanic soils of the inland areas (Rotorua, inland BOP). These behave very differently and need different approaches.

## Species Choice

The climate suits both cool-season and warm-season species well. Cool-season ryegrass and tall fescue blends perform through most of the year. Kikuyu and couch are viable on coastal lowland areas and often preferred for summer performance and lower-input management.

Tall fescue — NZLA Ultimate or Elfin — handles both the summer heat and the sandy pumice soils better than ryegrass. For anyone on pumice or volcanic soil, tall fescue is worth prioritising.

## The Annual Calendar

**January — peak summer.** Hot and humid. Disease pressure at maximum: brown patch, pythium, and dollar spot all active. NZLA Azoxy applied preventatively on any lawn with  history. Fall armyworm active — check for fast-appearing brown patches and caterpillar activity under the turf. NZLA Grub+ for fall armyworm, evening application, light irrigation-general after. NZLA Wetter or Wetter 3W programme especially important on pumice soils. Mow at higher heights.

**February.** Continued peak heat. Grub activity building. Black beetle larvae beginning in upper BOP. Deep watering twice weekly where possible. Iron+ for colour on tired-looking lawns.

**March — key fertiliser timing.** Heat easing, ground still warm. Best fertiliser application of the year: NZLA All Seasons or All Seasons N+. Begin renovation planning. On warm-season turf, watch for spring dead spot becoming visible as growth slows.

**April — renovation window.** Soil temperatures still 15 to 18°C. Scarify, core aerate, top dress, overseed. Second autumn fertiliser. NZLA Blockade autumn pre-emergent on warm-season turf (kikuyu, couch). BWC for broadleaf . Disease pressure shifting to fusarium as nights cool.

**May.** Last good fertiliser timing before winter. Final window for ryegrass seeding-and-over-seeding into warm soil. Fusarium patch emerging — preventative NZLA Azoxy or Fungus Pro on susceptible lawns.

**June — mild winter.** BOP winters are mild by NZ standards. Cool-season lawns grow slowly but don't stop entirely. Warm-season turf semi-dormant. Mowing frequency reduces but doesn't stop. Fusarium vigilance continues.

**July.** Slowest growth month. Minimal active intervention. Disease management continues if needed.

**August.** Spring pre-emergent window opens for Blockade on warm-season turf. First fertiliser as growth begins picking up later in the month.

**September — spring.** Growth resuming strongly. First spring fertiliser: NZLA All Seasons. BWC herbicide window. Pre-summer Wetter 3W application to set up water management.

**October.** Heavy spring growth. Weekly mowing and more. Liquid Boost and Iron+ for a foliar spring push. Last window for any structural lawn work before summer conditions close in.

**November.** Last big fertiliser before summer. Disease prevention begins: preventative Azoxy on susceptible lawns. PGR for high-input lawns. Kikuyu edging intensive.

**December — early summer.** Deep watering programme in place. Disease prevention active. High growth rate on warm-season turf. Mow at higher heights on cool-season lawns.

## Bay of Plenty-Specific Notes

**Pumice and volcanic soils** need different management to clay or loam. They have low cation exchange capacity — they don't hold nutrients well, so they leach fast. Apply fertiliser more frequently at lower rates rather than one or two heavy applications. NZLA All Seasons at slightly lighter rates every 6 to 8 weeks is more efficient than a single heavy spring hit. Wetting agents are particularly important on pumice soils because they dry out fast and become hydrophobic when dry.

**Fall armyworm** is established in BOP and should be factored into the summer programme for any well-managed lawn. Learn to identify it: fast-appearing brown patches (appearing over days, not weeks), caterpillars visible when you pull back the turf or flush the surface with soapy water, moth activity at dusk. NZLA Grub+ evening application. Early treatment is more effective than waiting until damage is widespread.

**Onehunga weed in Mount Maunganui.** There is documented clopyralid resistance in onehunga weed populations in the Mount Maunganui area. Standard BWC (which contains clopyralid) may give reduced or no control. If you're seeing poor BWC performance on onehunga in that area, consider timing (spring when actively growing) or speak to a turf professional about alternative approaches.

**Coastal areas** — Mount Maunganui, Papamoa, Whakatāne — deal with salt exposure. Species tolerance and occasional flushing with fresh water after exposure events helps.
