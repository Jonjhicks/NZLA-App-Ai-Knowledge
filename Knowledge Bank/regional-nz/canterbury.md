# Canterbury Lawn Calendar

Canterbury's main lawn challenges are heat, drought, and nor'westers in summer, and cold with occasional frost in winter. Disease pressure is lower than humid regions — the dry summers don't favour the same pathogens that cause problems in Auckland and BOP.

Getting through summer without the lawn browning off entirely requires either a reliable irrigation-general programme, a drought-tolerant species choice, or both.

## Species Choice

Tall fescue is the right call for Canterbury. NZLA Elfin Tall Fescue or Ultimate blend with its rhizomatous spreading handles summer drought and heat stress markedly better than ryegrass. A Canterbury lawn on pure ryegrass without reliable irrigation-general will look brown from January through to April and recover slowly in autumn. Tall fescue handles the same conditions much better.

RSR Ryegrass is a useful addition to the seed mix for its self-repairing properties — the combination of tall fescue's drought tolerance and RSR's wear recovery is a solid choice for a family lawn.

Cool-season only. Kikuyu and couch are marginal in Christchurch and not viable further inland.

## The Annual Calendar

**January — hot and dry.** The lawn often goes brown without irrigation-general and that's not necessarily a problem — dormancy is a survival strategy, not a failure. NZLA Wetter or Wetter 3W is important for maximising efficiency of whatever irrigation-general you're applying. Deep infrequent watering (25 mm twice a week) beats light daily sprinkling. Mow at 50 to 60 mm to protect the soil surface. Don't apply heavy fertiliser to heat-stressed turf. Nor'westers can dry the lawn down to nothing in 24 to 48 hours — be ready to irrigate after a significant nor'wester event.

**February.** Continued heat. Watch for grass grub damage starting to show as larvae begin feeding on roots. Pull back a section of turf and check for the classic C-shaped white grubs.

**March — key autumn timing.** Temperature easing. Best fertiliser timing of the year: NZLA All Seasons or All Seasons N+. Begin the renovation programme. NZLA Grub+ if grub damage present or grub numbers are above threshold (more than 5 to 6 per spade square). Hydrate the lawn before applying chemistry to heat-stressed turf.

**April — renovation month.** Soil temperatures 12 to 18°C — ideal for ryegrass germination (10 to 14 days). Scarify thatch, core aerate, top dress, overseed with NZLA RSR Ryegrass or tall fescue blend plus NZLA Starter. Second autumn fertiliser. BWC for broadleaf . This is the most important month in the Canterbury lawn calendar — the work you do in April sets the lawn up for the following year.

**May.** Last autumn opportunity for renovation. Final fertiliser before winter. Watch for fusarium patch as nights cool.

**June — winter starts.** Cool-season grasses slow significantly. Frost possible across Canterbury, more so inland. Mowing reduces to every 3 to 4 weeks. No nitrogen fertiliser.

**July–August — winter.** Cold. Inland Canterbury can see significant frost and some snow. Don't apply any fertiliser. Equipment maintenance time. Plan spring programme.

**September — early spring.** Soil temperatures rising slowly — frost still possible in September especially inland. First spring fertiliser later in the month when conditions are clearly warming.

**October — spring.** Growth resuming. BWC herbicide window opens. NZLA Wetter 3W application late October to set up summer water management — this is good timing in Canterbury as you're getting ahead of the dry season.

**November.** Heavy spring growth. Last big fertiliser before summer. PGR application for high-input lawns wanting density and controlled growth. Mowing frequency increases to weekly.

**December — early summer.** Heat building. Reduce nitrogen. Deep watering programme established before the summer dry hits. First nor'westers of the season.

## Canterbury-Specific Notes

**Drought is the defining challenge.** Infrastructure matters — a lawn without an irrigation-general system in Canterbury will go brown every summer. With an efficient system and tall fescue as the base species, you can keep a good lawn through summer. Without irrigation-general, autumn renovation and accepting summer dormancy is the management approach.

**Nor'westers** are a feature of Canterbury summers that affects management decisions. A north-west wind event can raise temperatures 10°C and strip moisture from the lawn very fast. Don't apply contact herbicides on a nor'wester day — the stress to the plant means unpredictable results. Water after a significant nor'wester event even if it's not your normal irrigation-general day.

**Inland Canterbury** (Methven, Rakaia, Geraldine, Mackenzie Basin) is colder than Christchurch — frost timing earlier in autumn and later in spring. Adjust calendar timing accordingly. The Mackenzie Basin in particular has conditions more akin to Otago.

**Soil variability.** Canterbury soils range from heavy clay to light shingle to alluvial loam. Clay soils benefit strongly from core aeration and top dressing with quality compost. Light shingle soils leach fertiliser quickly — more frequent applications at lower rates is more efficient than heavy infrequent feeding.
