# West Coast Lawn Calendar

The West Coast gets more rain than anywhere else in New Zealand. Some areas exceed 5000 mm annually. Waterlogging and  are the dominant lawn management challenges, not drought or heat.

Cool-season grasses only. Mild temperatures through the year mean the growing season is long, but the persistent moisture creates  pressure that has to be managed year-round rather than just in shoulder seasons.

## Species Choice

Tall fescue (NZLA Elfin, Ultimate) handles wet conditions better than ryegrass. It has deeper root development and is less prone to the fungal issues that affect ryegrass in persistently wet conditions.

Ryegrass (NZLA RSR, Perennial Rye) works but requires more  management in wet conditions. If ryegrass is your base species, having a fungicide programme planned is realistic rather than optional.

Fine fescue suits shaded West Coast sections under bush or established canopy, which are common on West Coast lifestyle properties.

Avoid species that are particularly -susceptible in your location's conditions. Annual bluegrass (poa annua) establishes easily in West Coast lawns but is short-lived and -prone — not worth encouraging.

## The Annual Calendar

**January — mild summer.** West Coast summers are pleasant — mild temperatures, still significant rainfall but often the driest period of the year. Disease pressure from summer pathogens is lower than humid warm regions. Standard cool-season programme. Mow at 45 to 50 mm. NZLA Penetrate helps move any surface water through the profile where drainage-and-soil-moisture is limited.

**February.** Continued mild conditions. Better weather than most of the year. Some lawns look their best now — growth controlled manageable, summer greenness.

**March — autumn fertiliser.** Best timing of the year: NZLA All Seasons or All Seasons N+. Begin renovation planning. The renovation window is narrow on the West Coast because wet autumn conditions can arrive early and make scarifiers and seeding-and-over-seeding difficult.

**April — renovation window, conditions permitting.** Aim to complete renovation work early in April before the autumn rains intensify. Scarify carefully — heavy scarifiers in wet conditions tears up the lawn and compacts the soil. Core aerate to improve drainage-and-soil-moisture (critical on West Coast heavy clay soils). Overseed with NZLA Starter. Second fertiliser application. BWC for broadleaf . Fusarium pressure building.

**May — late autumn.** Last fertiliser before winter. Pre-winter fungicide is essentially mandatory on the West Coast: NZLA Azoxy or Fungus Pro. The wet cold conditions from June through August are ideal fusarium conditions. Don't go into winter without preventative fungicide on any lawn with  history.

**June, July — wet winter.** High rainfall. Waterlogging risk. Disease pressure high year-round but highest in the cold wet winter. NZLA Azoxy or Fungus Pro for active fusarium. Don't walk on saturated lawn — core-aeration on wet clay destroys soil structure. Mowing continues slowly, but only when the surface is firm enough.

**August.** Still wet and cold. Disease vigilance continues. Late winter — start planning spring programme.

**September — spring.** Conditions improving. Soil temperatures rising. First spring fertiliser. BWC herbicide window opens.

**October.** Strong spring growth. Weekly mowing. Disease pressure from cool pathogens easing. Continued drainage-and-soil-moisture management.

**November.** Heavy spring growth. Last big fertiliser push. Where moss has established in sheltered damp areas, NZLA Moss application in spring when moss is actively growing gives the best results.

**December — early summer.** Relatively better conditions. Continue maintenance programme. The "summer window" on the West Coast is worth maximising for any renovation or improvement work.

## West Coast-Specific Notes

**Drainage is the foundation.** A West Coast lawn on poorly drained soil in a sheltered damp spot is fighting the climate. Before investing heavily in the lawn programme, address drainage-and-soil-moisture. Surface drainage-and-soil-moisture first (grading and channelling surface water away), then subsurface (agricultural drain, sand slit installation where justified). NZLA Aerate and Penetrate support drainage-and-soil-moisture management but can't replace physical drainage-and-soil-moisture infrastructure on a fundamentally wet site.

**Disease programme** is not optional here. Fusarium patch on the West Coast needs proactive management, not reactive. Two fungicide applications a year as a preventative (May and October) plus monitoring and curative application if needed is the minimum for susceptible lawns. NZLA Azoxy and Fungus Pro on rotation reduces the risk of resistance development.

**Fertiliser management.** The combination of high rainfall and leaching soils means fertiliser doesn't stay available as long. More frequent applications at lower rates is more efficient than heavy infrequent feeding. NZLA All Seasons at 3 to 4 kg/100m² every 8 to 10 weeks through the growing season is more effective than 6 kg twice a year.

**Moss** is common in sheltered, damp, shaded West Coast lawn areas. Address the conditions that favour moss (drainage-and-soil-moisture, shade, low pH, core-aeration) alongside NZLA Moss treatment. Treating moss without addressing the causes gets you a few months of improvement before it returns.

**Renovation window.** The usable renovation window on the West Coast is shorter than most regions — typically a 4 to 6 week window in late March and April before autumn rains make conditions unsuitable. Plan and execute renovation promptly when the window opens. Don't wait for the "perfect" day.

For some West Coast sections, particularly those under dense bush canopy in persistently wet low-light conditions, lawn isn't a realistic option. Native ground covers, mulch, or paths are more appropriate for sites where the conditions fundamentally oppose lawn establishment.
