# Northland Lawn Calendar

Northland is New Zealand's warmest region. Subtropical influence means mild winters, warm humid summers, and a climate where warm-season turf genuinely thrives rather than just surviving.

Disease pressure is high year-round. Fall armyworm is established in Northland. The management programme reflects a warm humid climate from January through to December.

## Species Choice

This is where the species decision matters most. Cool-season ryegrass struggles in Northland's peak summer heat and humidity — it can look good in autumn, winter, and spring but often looks tired and patchy through January and February. For homeowners who want a lawn that performs well year-round, warm-season turf is the better option.

**NZLA Kikuyu** is the practical choice for most Northland family lawns. Aggressive, tough, handles heat and wear, and recovers fast. The downsides are that it needs edging discipline (it invades beds and gardens relentlessly), it goes brown in winter, and it needs PGR management during the growing season to keep it looking tight. But for a lawn that takes heavy use and minimal input over summer, it's hard to match.

**NZLA Couch** gives a finer texture than kikuyu. Better-looking when well-maintained, requires more regular mowing. Also brown in winter.

For homeowners who prefer cool-season turf for its year-round colour, NZLA tall fescue blends (Ultimate, Elfin) are the most summer-tolerant cool-season option. Avoid pure ryegrass as the base species in Northland — it's not built for the summer conditions.

## The Annual Calendar

**January — peak summer.** Hot and humid. Disease pressure at maximum: brown patch, pythium active. NZLA Azoxy preventatively on susceptible cool-season lawns. Fall armyworm active — check for overnight or fast-appearing brown patches. NZLA Grub+ evening application for fall armyworm. Wetter or Wetter 3W for moisture management. Mow at 45 to 50 mm for cool-season; 30 to 40 mm for warm-season. PGR on kikuyu to manage summer growth surge.

**February.** Continued peak conditions. Fall armyworm risk continuing. Heavy  window. Grub activity building on cool-season lawns. Deep watering where needed.

**March — important transition.** Summer heat easing. Best fertiliser timing for cool-season lawns: NZLA All Seasons or All Seasons N+. Warm-season turf still growing actively. Begin renovation planning for cool-season areas.

**April — renovation window.** Soil still warm — excellent germination conditions. Scarify, core aerate, top dress, overseed cool-season areas. NZLA Blockade autumn pre-emergent on warm-season turf (kikuyu, couch) to suppress annual grass . Second autumn fertiliser. BWC for broadleaf . Disease pressure shifting toward fusarium on cool-season turf.

**May.** Last big fertiliser before winter on cool-season lawns. Fusarium watch. Preventative Azoxy or Fungus Pro on susceptible lawns. Warm-season turf growth slowing.

**June — mild winter.** Northland winters are mild. Cool-season lawns keep growing slowly. Warm-season turf semi-dormant but not fully brown like it would be in Christchurch. Some mowing still needed — expect every 3 to 4 weeks. NZLA Green pigment can be applied to warm-season turf for cosmetic colour through winter. No nitrogen fertiliser.

**July.** Slowest month. Growth minimal on all species. Limited active intervention. Watch for fusarium on cool-season areas in wet spells.

**August.** Spring pre-emergent window for Blockade on warm-season turf (late August, soil temps 10 to 12°C). First fertiliser as growth begins picking up.

**September — spring.** Warm-season turf breaking dormancy actively. First spring fertiliser: NZLA All Seasons. BWC window opens. Wetter 3W pre-summer application.

**October.** Heavy growth — kikuyu particularly vigorous. Mowing frequency up significantly. Last big spring fertiliser. PGR on kikuyu.

**November.** Heat building. Disease prevention begins on cool-season lawns: preventative Azoxy. Edge kikuyu weekly. Reduce nitrogen on warm-season turf.

**December — early summer.** Disease prevention active. Deep watering in place for cool-season lawns. PGR on kikuyu if growth rate needs managing. Fall armyworm season beginning.

## Northland-Specific Notes

**Warm-season vs cool-season decision** is more consequential in Northland than anywhere else. It's not just a species preference — it changes the whole management programme, the appearance through the year, and the input requirements. Kikuyu is lower-input in summer, higher-input in terms of edging and containment. Cool-season is more work in summer but better-looking in winter. Be clear about which matters more before establishing or renovating.

**Kikuyu management** in Northland requires edging discipline. Kikuyu stolons spread across paths, into garden beds, and under fences. An edge once a month in the growing season is the minimum. For garden beds adjacent to kikuyu, a solid physical edge (timber, steel, or concrete) recessed 50 mm below the surface slows invasion significantly.

**Fall armyworm** is well-established in Northland. Build surveillance into the summer routine: check the lawn at dusk, look for moths flying over the surface. NZLA Grub+ applied at dusk before the moths lay eggs and before caterpillars get large is the most efficient timing. Early treatment with small caterpillars is much more effective than treatment after the damage is done.

**Coastal properties** deal with salt spray from onshore winds. Species tolerant of coastal conditions (couch, tall fescue) are better suited to exposed coastal sections than ryegrass.
