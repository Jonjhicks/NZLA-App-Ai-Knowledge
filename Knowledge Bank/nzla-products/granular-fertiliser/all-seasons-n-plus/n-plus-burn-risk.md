# Will All Seasons N+ burn my lawn?

At label rate on a properly watered lawn with prompt watering-in, no. The slow-release coating keeps most of the nitrogen behind the coating where it's not available to burn the leaves.

Burn risk goes up when:
- Applied at higher than label rate. More immediately-available nitrogen on the surface = burn.
- Not watered in. Granules sitting dry on a hot lawn release the immediately-available portion onto dry leaves and roots.
- Applied to drought-stressed turf. The lawn isn't taking it up, the salt content on a stressed lawn can scorch.
- Applied unevenly. Concentrated patches at higher rate than the surrounding area show as burnt stripes or rings.
- Applied in a heatwave. Hot conditions plus surface nitrogen plus dry stressed lawn = burn.

If you do get burn (surface yellowing within a few days of application), water the lawn deeply to dilute and move the nitrogen down through the profile. Within 2 to 4 weeks the burn typically recovers as the chemistry moves through and growth resumes.

The simple rules: stick to label rate, apply to a well-watered lawn, water in straight after, even spreading.

For higher-risk situations (hot weather, stressed lawn, sandy soils that don't buffer nitrogen well), drop to half-rate or use Liquid Boost (foliar liquid feed) instead. The foliar route gives nitrogen response without the granular burn risk.
