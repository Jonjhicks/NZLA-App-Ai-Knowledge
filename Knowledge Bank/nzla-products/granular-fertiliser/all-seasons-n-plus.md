# NZLA All Seasons N+

High-nitrogen granular push and recovery feed. NPK 32-1.3-8.3 with 2% iron. Mostly fast-release nitrogen for a rapid green-up, with a PCSCU slow-release fraction for sustained feeding over 6 to 8 weeks.

## What it does

Where All Seasons is the steady balanced base, All Seasons N+ is built for when you want the lawn to push hard. 70% fast-release nitrogen produces visible green-up within days. 30% controlled-release carries the feed through the following 6 to 8 weeks.

The N to K ratio is 3.8:1 — far more nitrogen-dominant than All Seasons (1.3:1). That means it's a growth-driving feed, not a hardening feed. Use it where growth is the goal, not year-round.

## Rate and application

2 kg per 100m². Apply to moist soil, water in with 5 to 10 mm of irrigation-general or rainfall within 24 hours. The fast-release fraction can scorch the leaf if it sits dry on a hot lawn, so don't skip the watering-in step.

Use a calibrated spreader in a cross-pattern for even coverage. With a high-N product, uneven spreading shows up sharply.

## When to use

**Spring push (September to November):** thicken up after winter, push early growth, increase lawn density heading into summer.

**Post-renovation:** after scarifiers, coring, or overseed once new seedlings are 4 to 6 weeks old and robust. The nitrogen accelerates fill-in and turf recovery.

**Stress recovery:** after  damage (once controlled), drought stress (once the lawn is rehydrated), or heavy wear. Push the lawn through the recovery window.

**Late summer (March):** on lawns that have been hammered through summer, an autumn push helps bulk up before the cooler months set in.

## When not to use

Going into winter. Fast-release nitrogen forcing soft growth into cold, damp conditions is exactly the environment Fusarium and cool-season patch diseases thrive in. Autumn feeding (March) is fine — late May or June is not.

On heat-stressed or drought-stressed turf. A struggling lawn can't take up high nitrogen properly and the salt load can scorch.

On a new seedling lawn. Too aggressive for fragile seedlings — Starter is the establishment fertiliser.

As a year-round base on its own. N+ is a tool for specific moments in the calendar. Running it every 6 weeks all year will overdo the nitrogen and leave the lawn in a cycle of flushes and troughs.

## All Seasons N+ vs All Seasons

Standard programme: All Seasons as the year-round base, N+ brought in at 2 to 3 points per year where the growth push is wanted.

All Seasons (21-1-16) has better potassium balance for hardening and  resistance. All Seasons N+ (32-1.3-8.3) hits harder on nitrogen for growth response. They complement each other — one isn't a substitute for the other.

## All Seasons N+ vs Mini Prill

Similar role (high-N push feed), different prill size. Mini Prill (28-0-12, SGN 150 tiny prill) is for dense canopy lawns and low cutting heights where standard prills sit on the leaf surface. For most home lawns mown at 35 mm or above, All Seasons N+ at standard prill size is the simpler, more cost-effective choice.

## Common mistakes

Using it heading into winter. That's the highest-impact mistake — soft growth plus cool damp conditions plus Fusarium pressure.

Combining it with a foliar high-N application in the same week. Stack a Liquid Boost spray on top of an N+ granular and you'll overdrive the nitrogen.

Applying to a drought-stressed or -stressed lawn expecting it to fix the problem. Fix the stress first, then push growth.
