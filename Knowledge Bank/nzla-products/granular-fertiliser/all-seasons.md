# NZLA All Seasons

The flagship NZLA granular base fertiliser. NPK 21-1-16 with 5% iron, delivered through US-sourced PCSCU (polymer and sulphur-coated urea) for 6 to 8 weeks of steady feeding. The product the standard NZLA year-round programme is built around.

## What it does

All Seasons splits its nitrogen into two fractions: 40% immediately available for a visible green-up within days, and 60% slow-release for sustained feeding across 6 to 8 weeks. The PCSCU coating is the technology that makes this work reliably — polymer over sulphur coating, engineered to release at a consistent rate as soil moisture penetrates the granule.

The result is steady feeding without the spike-and-crash pattern of cheap fertiliser. The lawn stays greener for longer on fewer applications.

## Why the potassium matters

21% nitrogen gets the attention. The 16% potassium is what makes All Seasons genuinely useful year-round rather than just a spring feed.

Potassium drives plant strength, cold tolerance, and  resistance. A lawn fed with adequate K heading into winter is harder, holds its colour better through the cold months, and is less prone to the fungal diseases that attack nitrogen-soft growth. So while All Seasons will green up a lawn in spring, the K level makes it equally useful in March and May when hardening the plant is the priority.

## Rate and application

2 kg per 100m². A 20 kg bag covers 1000m², 10 kg covers 500m², 4 kg covers 200m².

Apply to moist soil. Water in with 5 to 10 mm of irrigation-general or rainfall within 24 hours — the coating needs moisture to start the release cycle. Apply with a calibrated spreader, cross-pattern (half north-south, half east-west) for even coverage. Uneven spreading shows as striped green response a few weeks later.

Don't apply to dry ground without watering in. The coating doesn't activate without moisture.

## When to use

3 to 4 applications per year on a typical NZ cool-season lawn:
- Early spring (September): first feed as the lawn wakes up.
- Late spring (November): carry into summer with good density.
- Autumn (March to April): the most useful application of the year on cool-season turf. Roots are growing through autumn, feeding now sets up the lawn for next spring.
- Late autumn (May, optional, half-rate): sustain colour and root growth through winter without forcing soft top growth.

Skip the height of summer in heat-stressed regions where the lawn isn't actively taking up nutrient.

## All Seasons vs All Seasons N+

All Seasons (21-1-16) is the year-round balanced base. All Seasons N+ (32-1.3-8.3, mostly fast-release) is the push and recovery feed.

Use All Seasons as the standard feed. Bring in All Seasons N+ for specific moments that call for a harder nitrogen push: spring green-up, post-renovation fill-in, recovery from stress damage. Most lawns need All Seasons N+ two or three times a year and All Seasons the rest of the time.

## Common mistakes

Not watering in. The coating needs moisture to activate — granules sitting dry on a dry lawn aren't doing anything.

Applying to dry ground in hot conditions. Surface granules drying on hot leaf can cause scorch from the immediately-available fraction.

Expecting instant response and re-applying too early. Slow-release means the feed is still running after week 6. Re-applying at week 3 overdoes it.

Applying to a new seedling lawn. Starter is the establishment fertiliser, not All Seasons. Switch to All Seasons after the first 3 mows.
