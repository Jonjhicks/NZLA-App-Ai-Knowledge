# NZLA Mini Prill

A high-nitrogen granular built around a tiny SGN 150 prill that drops through dense canopy to soil level. 28-0-12 with 3% iron, designed as a push-feed and dense-canopy alternative to All Seasons N+.

## What it is

The headline number is the prill size. SGN 150 is small enough that it doesn't hang up on the leaf canopy the way standard granulars do on a thick, fine lawn. The fertiliser actually gets to the soil where it can work.

The other key spec is the release profile, 70% of the nitrogen is immediately available, 30% is controlled release. So you get faster green-up than a pure slow-release, with enough duration to carry the feed for 10 to 12 weeks before you need to reapply.

## The numbers

28% nitrogen, 0% phosphorus, 12% potassium, 3% iron. Higher N, lower K and Fe than All Seasons or All Seasons N+, which puts it in the push-feed and recovery category rather than the steady-base category.

Rate is 2 kg per 100m².

## When to use

Dense lawns where standard granular sits on top of the canopy and doesn't reach soil.

Tight maintenance heights where the bigger prills physically don't fit between the leaves to the ground.

Recovery and push periods where you want both quick response and enough duration to carry the lawn for 2 to 3 months.

Spring and early autumn green-ups.

## How it sits next to All Seasons N+

Same role as All Seasons N+ (the high-nitrogen push feed), different delivery. Mini Prill is the call when canopy penetration is an issue. All Seasons N+ for everyday lawns where prill size isn't a problem.

## When not to use

Heading into winter. The high immediately-available N fraction will force soft growth into cold conditions, which is exactly what feeds Fusarium and the other cool-season patch diseases. Back off to the steadier K-and-Fe-heavier options for winter.

On stressed or dormant turf. High N on a struggling lawn pushes it harder when it can't keep up.

As your year-round base. Mini Prill is a push tool, not a foundation. Use it on top of a slow-release base like All Seasons or All Seasons N+.

## Common mistakes

Treating it like a pure slow-release. The 70% immediate fraction means it greens up quicker than a coated-only product. If you over-apply you'll get a flush.

Reaching for it in winter because the small prill seems handy on a dense lawn. The chemistry is wrong for the season.

Using it as a constant feed rather than a periodic push. Repeated 2 kg/100m² hits every few weeks will overdo the nitrogen.

## Best paired with

Slow-release base feed (All Seasons or All Seasons N+) underneath for the steady fertility, Mini Prill on top as the periodic push.

Iron+ as a foliar top-up if you want extra colour without more nitrogen.
