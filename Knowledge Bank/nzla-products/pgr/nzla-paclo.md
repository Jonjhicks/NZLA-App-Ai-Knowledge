# NZLA Paclo

Paclobutrazol at 250 g/L. The Poa annua specialist in the NZLA PGR range. Triazole chemistry that blocks gibberellic acid biosynthesis earlier in the pathway than trinexapac, giving deeper and longer-lasting suppression, and selectivity that hits Poa annua harder than desirable cool-season grasses.

## How it works

Paclobutrazol inhibits ent-kaurene oxidation, an early step in the gibberellic acid biosynthesis pathway. Because the block is earlier than trinexapac's, GA precursors don't accumulate as much — the plant simply makes less GA from the start. Suppression is deeper, longer-lasting, and the characteristic rebound effect of trinexapac is much less pronounced. The regulation winds down gradually rather than springing back.

The secondary activity is where Paclo stands apart from NZLA PGR. Paclobutrazol is a triazole, and Poa annua is more sensitive to it than ryegrass, fescue, and bentgrass. At standard turf rates, Poa annua is hit hard. The desirable grasses are modestly regulated but keep growing. Applied repeatedly across a season, the competitive balance shifts — desirable grasses fill the space that weakened Poa gives up. Long-term Poa management is what Paclo is specifically designed for.

## The DMI fungicide interaction — critical warning

This is the most important practical warning on the label.

Paclobutrazol is a triazole. Group 3 fungicides (DMIs — propiconazole, tebuconazole, triadimenol, triticonazole) are also triazoles and share related enzyme targets. Combining them — in the same tank or in the same application window — can produce severe over-regulation. The lawn yellows aggressively, stops growing entirely, and can take weeks to recover.

**Never mix Paclo with a Group 3 fungicide.** If a DMI fungicide application is needed, separate it from Paclo applications by several weeks.

## Soil persistence

Paclobutrazol binds tightly to soil organic matter and persists for months to over a year depending on conditions. This is both useful (the effect lasts without constant reapplication) and a constraint (the chemistry accumulates with repeated use).

Practical implications: start at lower rates and build up. Don't go heavy first time. A year of heavy use can over-regulate a lawn for the following year. If the lawn is still showing regulation when you don't want it, the chemistry is still in the soil and there's no quick fix.

## When to use

Cool-season turf with Poa annua pressure: greens, fairways, sports grounds, high-performance home turf.

For growth regulation: a single application gives 8 to 12 weeks of regulation. Late spring (October-November) for hardening into summer, or autumn (March-April) to slow growth heading into cooler months.

For Poa suppression: programme approach over multiple seasons is what produces results. Apply 1 to 2 weeks before Poa annua seed-head emergence — in NZ that's typically late winter to early spring depending on region. Multiple lower-rate applications through autumn to early summer, season after season, gradually shifts the turf composition.

Single applications don't shift Poa meaningfully. This is a season-long, multi-year tool.

## When not to use

With Group 3 DMI fungicides in the same tank or same week.

Above 32°C daytime temperature — the combination of heat stress and PGR causes severe damage.

On stressed turf (heat, cold, drought, waterlogged, diseased).

On warm-season turf where the Poa suppression mechanism is less relevant and the effect less predictable.

On a new lawn under 8 weeks.

Going into winter dormancy — apply early enough that the regulation has run its course before dormancy.

## Rate and application

Check the current label. Start at the lower end of the rate range on first use, then scale up based on the observed response.

Apply in 4 to 8 L carrier water per 100m². Foliar uptake plus some soil uptake. Light watering after application can assist soil uptake, but excessive irrigation-general can over-distribute the active — follow label guidance.

Don't stack multiple full-rate applications within short intervals. The residual builds in the soil.

Don't tank-mix with NZLA PGR — they work on the same pathway and stacking the effect produces over-regulation.

## Paclo vs NZLA PGR

NZLA PGR (trinexapac): general-purpose, 4 to 6 weeks of regulation, manageable rebound, forgiving for first-timers, suitable for all turfgrass species, no soil persistence issues.

Paclo (paclobutrazol): Poa suppression specialist, 8 to 12 weeks of regulation, slower rebound, significant soil persistence, selectivity against Poa, strict DMI fungicide interaction to manage.

For straight mowing frequency reduction on a home lawn, NZLA PGR is the right choice. Paclo comes in when Poa management is the objective, or when longer-duration regulation on fine turf is what the programme needs.

## Best paired with

Blockade pre-emergent herbicide to address the Poa germination window. Paclo manages what's already established in the turf; Blockade prevents new seedlings from establishing.

Cultural practices that favour desirable grasses: correct mowing height, good fertility, avoiding scalping.

NZLA PGR for general growth regulation at other times of year — rotated appropriately with Paclo, never combined.
