# PGR General

Plant growth regulators (PGRs) are a tool a lot of home users don't realise is available to them. They reduce mowing frequency, tighten the canopy, lift colour, and depending on the chemistry, selectively suppress problem species like Poa annua. This is the category-level overview, see NZLA PGR and NZLA Paclo for specific product detail.

## What a PGR actually does

A PGR slows vertical growth in the plant by interfering with gibberellic acid (GA), the hormone that drives stem and leaf elongation. The plant keeps photosynthesising and building biomass, but that energy gets redirected from top growth into lateral spread, denser canopy, deeper roots and carbohydrate storage.

The lawn ends up tighter, denser, often darker green, and you mow it 30 to 50% less often. Underneath, the root system is doing better than it would have without regulation.

## Two PGR chemistries in the NZLA range

**NZLA PGR (trinexapac-ethyl)** is the general-purpose regulator. Works across all turfgrass species at varying rates. 20 to 50% clipping reduction for 2 to 6 weeks. The standard tool for tightening up a home lawn or sports field.

**NZLA Paclo (paclobutrazol)** is the Poa annua specialist. Stronger and longer-lasting than trinexapac, and the selectivity shifts the competitive balance against Poa over time. Used on high-performance cool-season turf where Poa pressure is a problem.

## When PGRs make sense for home lawns

Lawns being mowed more often than the owner wants to.

Lawns where a tighter canopy is the goal (stripe definition, density, presentation).

Lawns under Poa annua pressure (Paclo specifically).

Pre-event preparation where you want the lawn to hold its appearance for longer between mows.

## When they don't make sense

Recovery situations. After scarification, renovation, wear damage, drought or disease, you want the lawn growing hard to recover, not regulated.

Heat-stressed turf. PGR plus heat stress causes severe damage.

Going into or out of dormancy. The plant needs to transition without further interference.

Newly established lawns. The lawn needs to grow in before you start holding it back.

## Resistance and rotation

PGRs aren't really at "resistance" risk in the same way herbicides and fungicides are. The chemistry works on plant hormones rather than killing the plant. But over-reliance on a single PGR can cause aesthetic issues like yellowing, especially at higher rates. Rotating between trinexapac and paclobutrazol where appropriate, or alternating PGR seasons with non-PGR periods, keeps the lawn out of cumulative regulation issues.

## Important warnings (apply to all PGRs)

Do not mix with Group 3 fungicides (DMIs) without confirming compatibility. Severe over-regulation can result.

Do not apply to stressed, dormant, or recovering turf.

Do not exceed label rates. PGRs are potent, more is not better.

Approved handler required for commercial use of most professional PGRs.
