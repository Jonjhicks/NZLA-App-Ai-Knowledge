# NZLA Paclo

The Poa annua specialist PGR. Paclobutrazol at 250 g/L. Triazole chemistry that inhibits gibberellic acid biosynthesis earlier in the pathway than trinexapac (NZLA PGR), giving deeper and longer-lasting growth suppression. The selectivity also differs — Poa annua is more sensitive than desirable cool-season grasses, which is exactly the trick you want for long-term Poa management.

## How it actually works

Paclobutrazol is a triazole fungicide derivative repurposed as a plant growth regulator. It inhibits ent-kaurene oxidation, an early step in the gibberellic acid biosynthesis pathway. By blocking GA synthesis at an early step (rather than the late step trinexapac targets), paclobutrazol produces deeper and longer-lasting suppression.

The plant produces less GA, less GA precursors accumulate (compared to trinexapac), and recovery after the chemistry wears off is slower and more gradual. So the rebound effect that's characteristic of trinexapac is much less pronounced with paclobutrazol — the regulation winds down rather than springs back.

## Why the DMI fungicide interaction matters

This is the most important practical warning on the label.

Paclobutrazol is a triazole. Triazoles also have DMI (demethylation inhibitor) fungicidal activity — the same chemistry class used in Group 3 fungicides (propiconazole, tebuconazole, triadimenol, triticonazole). Because both paclobutrazol and the DMI fungicides target related plant biosynthesis pathways (sterols and gibberellins both use related enzyme systems), combining them can produce severe over-regulation. The lawn yellows aggressively, stops growing entirely, and can take weeks to recover.

Never mix paclobutrazol with a Group 3 fungicide in the tank or in the same application window. If a Group 3 fungicide application is needed, separate it from paclobutrazol applications by several weeks.

## Why the Poa annua selectivity works

Paclobutrazol affects all GA biosynthesis in any plant it enters. The selectivity is differential sensitivity — Poa annua is more sensitive to a given rate than desirable cool-season grasses like ryegrass, fescue and bentgrass. At standard turf rates, Poa annua is hit hard while the desirable grasses are only modestly regulated.

That differential, applied repeatedly across seasons, shifts the competitive balance. The desirable grasses keep growing (slightly slower than normal), the Poa annua is severely suppressed, and over time the Poa population in the lawn declines while the desirable species fill the space.

## Soil persistence — significant

Paclobutrazol binds tightly to soil organic matter and persists for months to over a year depending on conditions. This is a strength (the effect lasts) and a constraint (the chemistry accumulates with repeated use).

Two practical implications. Single applications keep working for months — the effect doesn't end after weeks the way trinexapac does. And the chemistry builds up in the soil over multiple applications, so over-application becomes a real problem that can take a full year or more to clear from the lawn.

Start at lower rates and step up. Don't go heavy first time.

## When to use

Cool-season turf with Poa annua pressure — greens, fairways, sports grounds, high-performance home turf.

Apply 1 to 2 weeks before Poa annua seed-head emergence for best seed-head suppression. In NZ that's typically late winter to early spring depending on region.

Long-term Poa management programme over multiple seasons. One application doesn't fix Poa — repeated strategic applications shift the balance.

## When not to use

Mixed with Group 3 fungicides (DMIs — propiconazole, tebuconazole, triadimenol, triticonazole). Severe over-regulation can result.

In heat over 32°C. Heat stress plus PGR causes severe damage.

On turf already stressed by cold, drought or waterlogging.

On warm-season turf or fine fescues without checking specific tolerances.

In a programme where you need rapid recovery from damage in the following weeks — paclobutrazol's persistence means the regulation lasts.

## How it sits next to NZLA PGR

**NZLA PGR (trinexapac-ethyl):** general growth regulator for clipping reduction and canopy management. Late-stage GA inhibitor, more reversible, characteristic rebound effect, weeks of duration.

**NZLA Paclo (paclobutrazol):** Poa annua-specific tool. Early-stage GA inhibitor, deeper and longer-lasting, less rebound, months of soil persistence.

For straight growth management with no Poa target, use NZLA PGR. For Poa suppression, use Paclo. They can fit in a programme on the same turf at different times of year, but never in the same application.

## Common mistakes

Applying with a Group 3 fungicide in the tank or in the same week. Single biggest cause of paclobutrazol damage.

Treating it as a one-off Poa fix. Poa annua management is a multi-season programme — expect 2 to 3 applications per year for ongoing pressure.

Over-applying at first attempt. The chemistry is potent and persistent — start lower and step up rather than going heavy first time.

Applying in hot weather. The 32°C cap is firm.

Failing to plan for soil accumulation. Repeated applications build up in the soil; a year of heavy use can over-regulate a lawn for the following year.

## Best paired with

Cultural Poa management — dense healthy desirable turf, mowing height that favours the desirable species.

Pre-em Blockade in the lead-up to Poa germination windows. Paclo on what's already there, Blockade on what's coming.

NZLA PGR for general growth regulation through other times of the year (rotated, not combined).
