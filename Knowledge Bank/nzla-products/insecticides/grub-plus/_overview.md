# NZLA Grub+

The professional turf insecticide. Chlorantraniliprole at 200 g/L. The chemistry that's displaced older organophosphates and carbamates in turf insect control globally because it hits insects through a highly insect-specific receptor pathway — extremely effective on target pests, very safe around mammals, birds, bees and pets.

## How it actually works

Chlorantraniliprole binds to and activates the ryanodine receptor in insect muscle cells. The ryanodine receptor controls how calcium is released from internal storage (the sarcoendoplasmic reticulum) into the muscle to trigger contraction. Normally that release is tightly regulated — calcium pulses out, muscle contracts, calcium gets pumped back, muscle relaxes.

Activate the receptor continuously and calcium pours out of storage uncontrollably. Muscle stays contracted. The insect can't relax its muscles, can't move properly, stops feeding, and dies of paralysis within hours to days depending on dose.

Mammals (including dogs and cats) have ryanodine receptors too, but the chemistry of chlorantraniliprole binds the insect form of the receptor strongly and the mammalian form very weakly. The selectivity ratio is over 500-fold — the chemistry has to be 500 times more concentrated to affect mammalian receptors than to affect insect receptors. At label rates, the residue isn't anywhere near enough to affect pets, birds or wildlife.

Bees are similarly protected — they have a different ryanodine receptor form than the target pests, so chlorantraniliprole is considered one of the safer insecticides for use in landscapes with pollinators.

## What it controls

The NZLA Grub+ label and current marketing covers grass grubs, African Black Beetle larvae, Argentine Stem Weevil larvae, Argentinian Scarab, billbugs, and the surface-feeding caterpillars — cutworm, sod webworm, lawn armyworm, porina.

That's the full range of significant turf-damaging insects in NZ — soil-dwelling larvae (the various beetle grubs) and surface-feeding caterpillars (the various armyworms and webworms).

## Duration — up to 6 months

A single application of Grub+ provides up to 6 months of control. That's longer than older turf insecticides because chlorantraniliprole binds tightly to the target receptor and persists in insect tissue and soil for extended periods.

For most NZ home lawns, that means a spring application catches the peak grass grub larval feeding period (October through December) and provides residual through summer caterpillar pressure.

## The 1-application-per-year cap

Maximum one application per year is a hard label restriction. The reason is soil persistence — chlorantraniliprole binds tightly to soil organic matter and breaks down slowly. Repeated applications accumulate in the soil over successive seasons. The cap prevents long-term buildup.

In practical terms, time the one annual application for when grass grub larvae are at the right size for effective control (spring), which also catches the early caterpillar pressure of the season.

## Rate and application

Up to 1.5 L/ha (about 15 mL per 100m² in moderate carrier water), max 300 g active per hectare per year. Maximum 1 application per year is a hard label restriction.

**Soil-dwelling targets (grass grubs, black beetle larvae, stem weevil, billbugs, porina):** irrigate ~6 mm immediately after application to move the chemical into the soil root zone where the larvae are feeding.

**Surface-feeding caterpillars (cutworm, sod webworm, army worm):** delay watering and mowing 24 hours so the product stays on the leaf where the caterpillars are.

## Timing in NZ

**Grass grub and other soil larvae:** target the larval stage in spring (October through December) when grubs are feeding on roots near the surface. Smaller larvae are easier to control than mature ones. Adult beetles aren't on the label — Grub+ works on larvae.

**Caterpillars:** when damage is first visible, before population spreads. The chemistry also works preventatively if applied during the egg-laying period.

**Porina:** larvae feed on roots and at the surface depending on stage. Soil irrigation post-application moves the chemistry into the root zone.

## When not to use

More than once per year. Hard label restriction.

Without irrigation for soil-dwelling targets. Without the 6 mm water-in, the chemical sits on leaf surface where the soil-dwelling targets aren't.

Near waterways or drains. GHS aquatic Cat 1 — very ecotoxic to aquatic life. Buffer zones matter.

As a routine annual preventative on lawns that aren't showing damage. The 1-per-year cap plus soil persistence make this an inappropriate "just in case" tool.

## Common mistakes

Applying without watering in for soil targets. Defeats the purpose entirely.

Applying when grubs are too large. Mid-size active larvae are the right target window — late-instar grubs about to pupate are harder to kill and less worth treating.

Confusing it with the older insecticide chemistries. Chlorantraniliprole works differently — slower visible kill (hours to days, not minutes), but more durable control and dramatically safer profile around non-target species.

Using it on lawns with no actual insect damage. Sound monitoring (pulling a turf square periodically to check for grubs) is the right tool first.

## Best paired with

Cultural monitoring. A square spade of turf lifted in spring tells you whether grubs are present and at what density. Treatment is only warranted above damage thresholds (commonly 15+ grubs per 0.1m²).

Sound lawn management. A healthy thick lawn handles minor grub pressure without intervention — many lawns don't need Grub+ at all.

Spring application timing rather than calendar-based treatment.
