# NZLA Grub+

Chlorantraniliprole at 200 g/L. The professional turf insecticide. Covers the full range of significant soil and surface-feeding lawn  in New Zealand through a highly insect-specific mechanism — extremely effective on target very safe around mammals, birds, bees, and pets at label rates.

## How it works

Chlorantraniliprole binds to and activates the ryanodine receptor in insect muscle cells. The ryanodine receptor controls calcium release from the sarcoendoplasmic reticulum — normally a tightly pulsed process that lets muscles contract and relax. Activated continuously, calcium pours out uncontrollably. Muscle stays contracted. The insect can't move, stops feeding, and dies of paralysis within hours to days.

Mammals, birds, bees, dogs and cats also have ryanodine receptors, but the insect form of the receptor is fundamentally different. The selectivity ratio is over 500-fold — chlorantraniliprole has to be far more concentrated to affect mammalian receptors than to hit insect receptors. At label rates, there's no meaningful risk to pets or wildlife.

## What it controls

The main NZ lawn :
- Grass grub (Costelytra giveni larvae)
- African Black Beetle larvae
- Argentine Stem Weevil larvae
- Argentinian Scarab
- Billbugs
- Caterpillars: cutworm, sod webworm, lawn armyworm
- Porina

One product covers soil-dwelling larvae and surface-feeding caterpillars. For most NZ home lawns, that's the complete picture of insect damage risk.

## Application — critical difference between target types

**Soil-dwelling targets (grass grubs, black beetle larvae, stem weevil, billbugs, porina):** irrigate 6 mm immediately after application. The chemical must move into the root zone where the larvae are feeding. Without the water-in, it sits on the leaf and does nothing for soil targets.

**Surface-feeding caterpillars (sod webworm, lawn armyworm, cutworm):** delay watering and mowing 24 hours. The product needs to stay on the leaf where the caterpillars feed.

Apply to a well-hydrated lawn — water the day before application. Dry soil limits distribution of the active.

Check the current label for the exact rate.

## Recommended timing

NZLA recommends two applications per year, six months apart.

**Late September to early October.** The primary application. Targets the soil before egg hatch and catches early first-instar larvae as they emerge and start feeding. Getting the product in place before the new larvae are active is the most effective approach — it prevents damage rather than treating it after the fact.

**March.** The second application, six months after the spring treatment. Catches any remaining larvae and provides protection through the autumn period when larger, later-instar grubs are feeding hardest.

This twice-yearly programme keeps lawns ahead of the cycle. Waiting until turf is visibly damaged and lifting like carpet means the larvae are large and root damage is already done — much harder to recover from heading into winter.

**Caterpillars (armyworm, sod webworm, cutworm):** treat as populations build in late spring to early summer, or when damage first appears. Evening application suits the caterpillars' nocturnal feeding habits.

**Porina:** March to May. Apply in the evening. Porina caterpillars emerge at night.

The common principle: treat early, when the pest is small, before damage is established.

## Soil biology impact

Any soil-active insecticide affects non-target invertebrates to some degree, including earthworms and beneficial soil organisms. At label rates and used as directed, the effect is manageable but not zero.

Use Grub+ on lawns with actual or known seasonal pest pressure. Monitoring — pulling a turf square (around 0.1 m²) and counting what's in the soil — is good practice before treatment. A healthy lawn can tolerate low grub numbers. Thresholds of around 15+ grass grubs per 0.1 m² justify treatment.

Soil biology recovers in the months following an application. Humic+ and mulch mowing support that recovery.

## Common mistakes

**Not watering in for soil targets.** Single most common reason the product doesn't work.

**Treating too late.** By the time turf is obviously dying in patches and lifting like carpet, larvae are large and root damage is extensive. Spring timing prevents this situation.

**Applying on a lawn with no pest history or signs of pressure.** Monitor first.

**Comparing to older insecticide chemistries.** Chlorantraniliprole works more slowly than older products — visible kill over hours to days, not minutes — but the control is more durable and the safety profile around pets and beneficials is genuinely better.
