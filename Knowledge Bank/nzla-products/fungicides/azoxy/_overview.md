# NZLA Azoxy

A systemic, broad-spectrum fungicide for established turf. Azoxystrobin at 250 g/L. Globally one of the most-used turf fungicides because the chemistry delivers protectant, curative, and anti-sporulant activity in a single product. FRAC Group 11 strobilurin.

## How it actually works

Azoxystrobin is a QoI fungicide — Quinone outside Inhibitor. It binds to a specific site on the cytochrome bc1 enzyme complex (Complex III) in fungal mitochondria. That enzyme is essential for the fungus to produce ATP, the energy currency of every cell. With the enzyme blocked, the fungus can't make energy and can't sustain the energy-dependent processes it needs to grow and infect — spore germination, hyphal extension, infection establishment.

The fungus dies of energy starvation. That mechanism is highly specific to fungal mitochondria, which is why azoxystrobin is effective at very low concentrations and has very low mammalian toxicity.

The chemistry acts at early infection stages best. Spores germinating on the leaf surface, fungal hyphae trying to penetrate the cuticle, early-stage infection inside the leaf — all of these are vulnerable. Once infection is well established and tissue has died, azoxystrobin doesn't bring that tissue back. It prevents the spread.

## Three modes in one

**Protectant.** Sprayed on a healthy lawn, sits on and in the leaf, prevents new infection from establishing. The most common preventative use.

**Curative.** Active against early-stage infection already inside the plant. Hits the fungus before it does major damage, stops disease running.

**Anti-sporulant.** Even on infected tissue, suppresses spore production. Reduces the inoculum the disease has available to spread to surrounding healthy tissue.

That combination is why Azoxy is a workhorse globally. One product, three modes, broad disease spectrum.

## Movement in the plant

Azoxy is xylem-mobile. After foliar uptake, the active moves through the leaf via the xylem (the water-conducting tissue). It also moves translaminarly — from the upper leaf surface to the lower surface through the leaf tissue itself. That gives complete leaf coverage from a single side of application.

It does not move to roots via phloem. So root-targeting use of azoxystrobin (for Pythium root rot, spring dead spot) requires watering the product into the soil after application — different from foliar use, where you specifically don't water it in.

## Resistance management — critical

Strobilurins are at high resistance risk because the binding site is a single point on a single enzyme. A single mutation in the fungus can confer complete resistance. Microdochium patch has documented azoxystrobin resistance internationally, and using azoxystrobin repeatedly without rotation accelerates resistance development.

Resistance management is non-negotiable. Maximum 2 consecutive applications before rotating to a different FRAC group. Fungus Pro (thiram, FRAC M3, multi-site contact chemistry) is the standard NZLA rotation partner — it's almost impossible to develop resistance to multi-site chemistry because the fungus would need multiple simultaneous mutations.

A typical professional winter programme runs Azoxy as the curative knockdown on active disease, Fungus Pro as the protectant between sprays, and the two never used consecutively on the same lawn.

## Rate and application

23 mL per 100m² in 4 to 8 L of water. Apply to dry foliage for even coverage.

Don't water it in straight after — the fungicide needs to sit on and in the leaf to do its job. For foliar diseases, post-application irrigation washes the product into the soil away from where the pathogen is.

Intervals: 28 days for most diseases, 21 for Helminthosporium. Maximum 2 consecutive applications before rotating.

## What it does and doesn't cover

The label covers winter Fusarium (Microdochium patch), red thread, brown patch, anthracnose, grey leaf spot, Helminthosporium, Pythium (both leaf blight and root rot), seedling damping off, and spring dead spot.


## When not to use

Don't spray near apple trees. Some varieties are phytotoxic to azoxystrobin — real damage to the tree.

Don't expect dollar spot or rust control. Wrong tool.

Don't use back to back beyond two applications. Inviting resistance.

Don't water in immediately for foliar disease targets. Wrong direction for foliar diseases.

## Common mistakes

Watering in immediately after spraying for foliar disease. Defeats the purpose.

Using too little water and getting patchy coverage. Stick to 4 to 8 L per 100m² range.

Expecting it to bring back wiped-out turf. Azoxy stops disease spreading, doesn't grow dead tissue back.

Spraying repeatedly without rotation. The fastest way to develop resistance.

Spraying on wet foliage. The active needs to bind to the leaf surface, which it does poorly when the leaf is already wet.

## Best paired with

Fungus Pro (thiram) as the rotation partner — different FRAC group (M3 multi-site vs Group 11 single-site), so they together cover the resistance management needs.

A solid base fertility programme — disease pressure is much lower on well-fed turf, so the fungicide programme works better.

Cultural disease management (dew removal, airflow improvement, drainage fixes) — fungicide treats the symptom, cultural fixes address the cause.
