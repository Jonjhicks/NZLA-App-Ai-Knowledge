# NZLA Aerate

A professional-grade soil-amending polymer for heavy soils. Clay, loam, and compacted soils where the structure itself is the problem. Different category to a standard wetting agent — Aerate changes the soil, not just the water-soil interface.

## What it does

Heavy clay and loam soils pack so tightly that little space remains for water and air. The result is a soil that's either waterlogged in winter or rock-hard in summer, with roots that can't function properly in either state.

A standard surfactant wetting agent doesn't fix this because the problem isn't surface tension on hydrophobic particles. It's the structure of the soil. Aerate is built for this — it's part wetting agent (for water movement) and part soil-amending polymer.

The polymer is electrostatically attracted to clay and silt particles. It binds individual particles into larger, more stable aggregates. That opens up porosity between the aggregates, gives roots air, lets water move through, and allows excess water to drain rather than sitting on the surface.

The change persists in the soil rather than disappearing when the product washes through. That makes Aerate a slow-build product — you won't see results overnight. Over a season of monthly applications, a clay lawn becomes a different soil.

## Rate and application

10 mL per 100m². A 1 L bottle treats an entire hectare (10,000m²), which makes it economical even on larger areas.

Monthly during the growing season, or as needed depending on soil condition. Apply before rainfall or water in well after application to move the polymer into the top of the soil profile where it works.

Tank-mixes with NZLA Penetrate on clay and silt soils.

## When to use

Heavy clay or silt lawns that hold water in winter and crack in summer.

Lawns with persistent core-aeration that returns between mechanical aeration events.

As part of soil preparation for new lawn establishment on heavy soils.

Long-term soil improvement programmes alongside regular mechanical aeration.

Year-round for ongoing structural improvement once the programme is running.

## Aerate vs a wetting agent

Different problem, different tool.

A wetting agent (Wetter, Wetter 3W) addresses hydrophobicity on dry sandy or organic soils through summer. Water beading off the surface.

Aerate addresses core-aeration and poor structure on clay and loam soils. Water pooling after rain because the soil can't absorb it.

For a clay lawn, the right pairing is Aerate plus Penetrate: Aerate restructures the soil so pore space opens up, Penetrate keeps water moving through it. Wetter has limited value on a genuine clay soil.

For soils that go through dry-wet cycles and show both problems at different times, can-i-tank-mix Aerate and Wetter at lower rates of each covers both issues.

## Aerate vs mechanical aeration

Neither replaces the other — they work together.

Mechanical aeration (core or solid-tine) physically opens the soil. Aerate works on the chemistry of the clay particles to build stable aggregates and improve porosity between aeration events.

The combination over time produces far better results than either alone. If you're only doing mechanical aeration once a year without any soil chemistry support, the benefit diminishes quickly as the holes close up. Regular Aerate maintains the improvement.

## Common mistakes

Expecting overnight results. Soil structure changes over weeks to months of consistent application.

Using it on sandy soils. There's no clay or silt fraction for the polymer to bind. Aerate is for heavy soils — Wetter and Wetter 3W are for sandy soils.

Skipping the water-in. The polymer must reach the upper soil profile to bind the clay particles.

Confusing it with a wetting agent. Different product category, different mechanism, different target soil type.

## Where it sits in the range

Aerate is the clay and heavy soil specialist. **Penetrate** is its standard pairing — they tank-mix on heavy soils. **Wetter**, **Wetter 3W**, and **Wetter GR** are the tools for sandy and hydrophobic soils, a different problem class entirely.
