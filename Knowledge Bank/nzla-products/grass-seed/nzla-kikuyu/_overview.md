# NZLA Kikuyu

The premium kikuyu seed. Raw Pennisetum clandestinum, not blended with other turf species (which is what most market kikuyu products are at around 5% kikuyu content). For warm-season climates where kikuyu suits, this is the high-content option.

## What it is

Kikuyu is the dominant warm-season turf grass in the upper North Island. Aggressive lateral spread via both stolons and rhizomes, very high wear tolerance, deep root system for drought, dense canopy that outcompetes most weeds.

NZLA Kikuyu's positioning is the raw seed concentration. Many products marketed as "kikuyu" are actually 5% kikuyu blended with other species (often ryegrass for fast cover while the kikuyu establishes underneath). NZLA Kikuyu is the genuine high-content option for users who want pure kikuyu establishment.

## Winter performance

The selected variety has better winter colour retention than most warm-season grasses. It continues growing at temperatures down to about 8°C, which is lower than standard kikuyu and well below couch grass. In Auckland it stays green for more of the year than a typical warm-season lawn.

It tolerates one-off frosts but heavy frost regions are still outside its range. South Island is generally not kikuyu territory.

## Establishment

Under optimal conditions, full coverage in approximately 8 weeks from sowing. Warm soil (above 15°C) and consistent moisture are essential.

Sowing rate and method: refer to label. Kikuyu seed is small and needs careful sowing.

## When to use

Upper North Island lawns (Waikato and north) where the climate suits.

Heavy-wear sites in warm climates (sports fields, parks, high-use home backyards).

Drought-prone warm climates where cool-season grasses would brown out.

Sandy soils that won't hold cool-season turf well.

## When not to use

South Island. Wrong climate, especially the heavy-frost southern regions.

Heavy shade. Kikuyu is a full-sun species.

Mixed with cool-season grasses you want to keep. Kikuyu will outcompete and dominate.

Premium aesthetic lawns where fine texture matters. Kikuyu has a coarser leaf than ryegrass or fescue.

Anywhere that controlling kikuyu later would be a problem. Kikuyu is aggressive and hard to remove once established.

## Common mistakes

Buying "kikuyu" seed elsewhere and getting a 5% kikuyu / 95% ryegrass blend without realising it.

Sowing into cold soil. Below 15°C and the seed sits and rots rather than germinating.

Trying to establish kikuyu in the South Island.

Mixing with a cool-season grass and expecting both to thrive. The kikuyu will win.

Mowing too tight at establishment. Let the kikuyu fill in before going to maintenance height.
