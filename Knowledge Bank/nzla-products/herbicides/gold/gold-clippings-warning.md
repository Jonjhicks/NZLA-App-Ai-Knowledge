# Can I compost clippings from a Gold-treated lawn?

No. Picloram has a long residual in plant tissue and in soil, and it doesn't break down in a typical home compost system. Clippings from a Gold-treated lawn carry picloram residues that can persist in compost for 12 to 18 months or longer.

If that compost ends up in a vegetable garden, the picloram damages or kills sensitive crops, particularly tomatoes, capsicums, beans, peas, potatoes and many ornamentals. The damage shows up as twisted distorted growth that doesn't recover. It's not subtle.

What to do with the clippings after a Gold application:
- Mulch them back into the lawn with a mulching mower (best option). The picloram stays in the lawn ecosystem where it's not a problem.
- Send to landfill (green waste collection is not always safe, depends on whether they're composting).
- Don't compost at home, don't put on vegetable gardens, don't give to neighbours for their compost.

The 12 to 18 month figure isn't a hard cut-off either. Some persistence beyond that has been documented. Easier rule: clippings from a Gold-treated lawn never go into compost or vegetable gardens, full stop.

Same caution applies to any picloram-containing product (not just NZLA Gold). If you're not sure what's been on a lawn, treat the clippings as suspect.
