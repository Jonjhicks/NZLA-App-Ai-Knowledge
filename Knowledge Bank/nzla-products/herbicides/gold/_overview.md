# NZLA Gold

The heavy-duty broadleaf herbicide for established cool-season lawns. Two-way mix of picloram (50 g/L) and triclopyr (100 g/L). Both HRAC Group 4 synthetic auxins. Wider weed spectrum than BWC, particularly strong on the tough, waxy, or woody-stemmed weeds that other broadleaf herbicides leave behind.

## How it actually works

Both actives in NZLA Gold are synthetic auxins. They mimic the plant growth hormone auxin (indole-3-acetic acid, IAA) at toxic concentrations.

Auxin is the hormone that controls cell elongation, vascular development, root growth and many other coordinated growth processes in plants. Normally the plant carefully regulates auxin levels — small amounts in the right places drive directed growth. A flood of synthetic auxin at toxic levels swamps the regulatory system. Cells try to elongate in conflicting directions. Vascular tissue grows disorganised and disrupted. Stems twist, crack and bleed. Leaves curl and distort. The plant grows uncontrollably for a few days, then collapses.

Death takes 1 to 3 weeks depending on rate, species and conditions. The visual sign the chemistry is working is the twisted, contorted growth that appears within days of application.

## Why the two actives work together

**Picloram** (50 g/L) is a pyridinecarboxylic acid auxin. It moves extensively through the plant via both xylem and phloem, reaching growing points throughout the plant. Picloram is highly active on a wide range of broadleaf weeds and has long persistence — both in the plant (where it keeps working) and in the soil (where it provides residual control).

**Triclopyr** (100 g/L) is a pyridinyloxyacetic acid auxin. It's foliar absorbed and moves through phloem to growing points. Triclopyr brings particular strength on woody and waxy-leafed weeds — the chemistry penetrates waxy cuticles better than some other auxins, which is why it's effective on the tougher weeds that BWC can't always handle.

Together, the two actives cover both translocation pathways (broad systemic distribution), both rapid and persistent action (triclopyr fast, picloram persistent), and a wider weed spectrum than either alone.

## Targets

Long list. Cape daisy, catsear, chickweed, clovers, convolvulus, cotula, cudweed, daisy, dandelion, dichondra, dock, hawkbit, hawksbeard, hydrocotyle, mallow, mayweed, nettles, onehunga, creeping oxalis, parsley piert, plantain, pearlwort, penny royal, ragwort, shepherd's purse, sorrel, storksbill, thistles, twin cress, wireweed.

Particularly strong on storksbill, twin cress, wireweed, mallow, and convolvulus — weeds that hold out against BWC.

## Onehunga cross-resistance note

Documented clopyralid-resistant onehunga in NZ shows cross-resistance to other Group 4 herbicides including picloram. If BWC didn't perform on onehunga (suspect resistance), Gold may also be compromised through the picloram fraction. The triclopyr fraction is structurally different and may still have activity, but reduced efficacy is likely.

If onehunga keeps coming back through both BWC and Gold programmes, you're dealing with Group 4 resistance and need a different chemistry class (mecoprop, bentazone) — neither currently in the NZLA range.

## Soil persistence and drift — major considerations

**Picloram** persists in soil for 6 to 12 months or longer. It doesn't bind strongly to soil organic matter, which means it can move with water — leaching into groundwater, running off during heavy rain, drifting through soil to nearby garden beds. Long persistence is both a strength (residual control of weeds emerging weeks after application) and a real risk.

**Triclopyr** degrades much faster — 8 to 46 days typical half-life. Much shorter persistence reduces long-term residue risk.

**Drift damage** from Gold is severe. Both actives at higher rates move off-target via spray drift, runoff during rain, and (for picloram) leaching through soil. Damage to broadleaf garden plants — roses, hedges, vegetables, ornamentals — from minor exposure is well documented. Use the minimum spray pressure that gives adequate coverage, don't apply in wind, and direct sprays no higher than the target weeds.

**Sprayer residue** is a particular issue with picloram. The chemistry is so persistent that even thoroughly washed sprayers can carry damaging residue. The standard professional practice is to use a dedicated sprayer for hormone herbicides — a sprayer that has had picloram or aminopyralid through it is best not used on ornamentals afterward.

## Rate and application

Refer to label for specific rates. Apply when weeds are actively growing in mild conditions.

Cool-season turf only (ryegrass, fescue, browntop). Check the label before using on kikuyu or couch.

Don't apply if heavy rain is forecast within 48 hours — runoff risk is real with picloram.

## When not to use

On warm-season turf without checking the label.

On stressed lawns.

On new seedings — wait until the lawn has been mowed at least 3 times.

Anywhere near garden beds or ornamentals you want to keep. Drift and runoff cause severe damage.

In windy conditions.

In sprayer equipment used for ornamentals.

## Common mistakes

Spraying near garden beds in any wind. Drift can take out roses, vegetables, hedges, ornamentals.

Heavy watering in the days after application. Heavy water moves picloram residue into garden beds via runoff.

Repeated reliance on Gold alone. Rotate with BWC across seasons.

Using the same sprayer for ornamentals afterward without thorough cleanout. Even washed sprayers can carry damaging residue.

Spraying at higher rates than needed. More product = more drift, more runoff, more soil residue. Stick to label rates.

## Best paired with

BWC as the rotation partner across seasons. Different active mix, similar mode of action overall, but the rotation reduces resistance pressure on any single active.

Pre-em Blockade in spring (where label-appropriate) to stop new weeds germinating ahead of the next post-em pass.

Cultural control to thin the weed seed bank over time.
