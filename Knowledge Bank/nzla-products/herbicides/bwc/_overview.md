# NZLA BWC (Bow & Arrow)

The three-way broadleaf control. MCPA (300 g/L), clopyralid (20 g/L) and diflufenican (15 g/L). Two different chemistry groups (HRAC 4 for the MCPA and clopyralid, HRAC 12 for the diflufenican), three distinct modes of action working together. That combination gives both wide weed coverage and lower resistance risk than any single-active broadleaf herbicide can offer.

## How it actually works — three mechanisms in one application

**MCPA** is a synthetic auxin that mimics indole-3-acetic acid (the plant's natural growth hormone) at toxic concentrations. Foliar absorbed, translocated through the phloem to growing points throughout the plant. At growing points, the synthetic auxin floods the regulatory system that normally controls cell elongation and vascular development. Cells grow chaotically, stems twist and crack, leaves curl, vascular tissue is disrupted. Death follows in 1 to 3 weeks. MCPA is broadly active across most common broadleaf weeds.

**Clopyralid** is a more specialised synthetic auxin — a pyridine carboxylic acid. It's particularly active on weeds in the daisy family (Asteraceae — dandelion, catsear, hawksbeard, thistles, daisy) and on legumes (clover). The chemistry binds to auxin receptors more persistently than natural auxin, so the sustained binding causes prolonged disruption of growth regulation. Death takes 2 to 4 weeks but it's thorough on the target weed families.

**Diflufenican** is a completely different chemistry — a phytoene desaturase inhibitor (HRAC Group 12). It blocks carotenoid biosynthesis, which in turn destroys chlorophyll under light (similar mechanism to mesotrione but at a different enzyme in the carotenoid pathway). Susceptible plants bleach white. Diflufenican is absorbed by germinating shoots with limited translocation, so it's most active on weeds at very early stages — recently germinated seedlings rather than established plants.

The three actives together cover broadly emerged broadleaf weeds (MCPA, clopyralid) plus weeds germinating from the seed bank (diflufenican), with three distinct modes of action that no single mutation in the weed could defeat. That's the resistance-management value of the mix.

## Targets

Standard NZ broadleaf list — dandelion, plantain, catsear, hawksbeard, hawkbit, thistles, dock, oxalis, clovers, daisy. Particularly strong on the rosette-forming weeds (catsear, hawkbit, hawksbeard) where the clopyralid component drives effective kill, and on chickweed and similar early-season weeds where the diflufenican adds early-stage suppression.

## The onehunga resistance situation

This is the most important practical knowledge with BWC.

Onehunga weed (Soliva sessilis) populations in NZ have developed over 200-fold resistance to clopyralid. The resistant strains were originally identified in locations north of Auckland but are now being found in other parts of NZ — Mount Maunganui and beyond. Cross-resistance extends to other pyridine carboxylic acid auxins (picloram, which is in NZLA Gold) and somewhat to MCPA and dicamba. **No resistance to mecoprop.**

In practical terms:

If BWC stops working on onehunga in a particular lawn or region, suspect clopyralid resistance. Applying more BWC won't fix it. The published evidence is that an MCPA + mecoprop + dicamba mixture is effective on resistant onehunga (the mecoprop component does the work).

Mecoprop is not currently in the NZLA range. If you're dealing with confirmed clopyralid-resistant onehunga, the right call is to be honest with the customer — the chemistry that works isn't currently available in the NZLA range, and the resistance problem requires a different active.

Don't keep reapplying BWC expecting different results. That accelerates resistance development.

## Rate and application

5 L/ha across the lawn (50 mL per 100m² in normal carrier water).

Apply when weeds are actively growing in mild conditions. Cold (under 10°C) slows uptake and reduces kill. Heat-stressed weeds also don't take it up well. Mild, actively growing is the sweet spot.

Wait at least 4 weeks before reapplying.

## When not to use

On new seedings — wait until the lawn has been mowed at least 3 times.

On stressed lawns. Heat or drought stress reduces lawn tolerance.

On warm-season turf (kikuyu, couch) without checking the label.

When clover suppression isn't wanted. BWC will hit clover hard.

Where confirmed clopyralid-resistant onehunga is the target.

## Common mistakes

Spraying in marginal conditions (cold, hot, just-mowed, dry). The actives need actively growing weeds with intact leaf area for uptake.

Re-treating too quickly. Give it the full 4 weeks before another go.

Using on onehunga where clopyralid resistance is suspected — and reapplying repeatedly when it fails. Accelerates the resistance problem.

Substituting other multi-way broadleaf products on the assumption they're equivalent. The specific MCPA + clopyralid + diflufenican mix is what BWC is — different mixes have different strengths and gaps.

## Best paired with

NZLA Gold for tougher weeds (storksbill, twin cress, wireweed, mallow) that the BWC mix sometimes leaves. Note Gold contains picloram, also a pyridine carboxylic acid, so cross-resistance applies on resistant onehunga.

Cultural control (thick healthy turf, correct mowing height) for long-term weed pressure reduction.

Pre-em Blockade ahead of the next germination window where label-appropriate.

Rotation across seasons rather than continuous reliance on a single chemistry.
