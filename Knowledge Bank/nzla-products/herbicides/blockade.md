# NZLA Blockade

Prodiamine at 476.2 g/L. HRAC Group 3 pre-emergent herbicide. Stops weed seeds germinating by killing the developing root tip before the seedling can establish. One of the longest residuals in the pre-em class — 4 to 6 months from a single application.

## How it works

Prodiamine inhibits microtubule formation in germinating weed seedlings. Microtubules are the cellular scaffold that organises chromosome separation during cell division. When a seed germinates and the root tip pushes down through the soil, those dividing cells need functional microtubules. Prodiamine binds to the assembly proteins and prevents them forming. Cell division fails, the root can't extend, the seedling dies before it reaches the surface.

It only works on germinating seeds. Once a plant is established with a developed root system, prodiamine in the surface soil layer doesn't affect it — the established roots aren't dividing rapidly, and they're below the chemical barrier.

Prodiamine binds tightly to soil organic matter and clay particles in the top centimetre of soil. It doesn't leach significantly, which is why the residual is so long. The 4 to 6 month window from one application is longer than most dinitroanilines.

## NZ label restriction — warm-season turf only

In New Zealand, Blockade is registered for warm-season turf (kikuyu, couch) only. It is not labelled here for cool-season grasses (ryegrass, fescue, browntop).

Internationally, prodiamine is widely registered for cool-season turf in the USA and most other markets — ryegrass, fescue, bentgrass, bluegrass. The difference is a regulatory matter, not a chemistry or safety matter. But in NZ, the label is what governs, and off-label use can't be recommended.

For cool-season lawns with poa annua pressure, NZLA Etho is the labelled option.

## Timing — this is where most people go wrong

**Summer grass and crabgrass:** apply 2 to 3 weeks before soil temperatures at 50 mm depth hit 12°C and stay there. That's the germination trigger. Auckland: late August. Canterbury: late September to early October. Watch soil temperature, not the calendar.

**Poa annua:** apply in autumn before soil temperatures drop into the 18 to 22°C range where poa germinates. Late February to early April depending on region. Same principle — the barrier needs to be in place before germination starts, not after.

A second application 90 to 120 days later extends the barrier through the full germination window. Minimum 90 days between applications is a label requirement.

## Rate and application

10 to 30 mL per 100m² for most weed control, up to 40 mL per 100m² for poa annua. Check the current label.

**Activation is essential.** At least 6 mm of rain or irrigation-general within 7 days of application. Without that water-in, the chemistry doesn't bind into the soil profile and the barrier never forms. This is the single most common reason pre-em programmes fail.

Maximum 2 applications per 365 days.

Don't apply if heavy rain is forecast within 48 hours — the product will wash off before binding.

## The seeding window restriction

The 4 to 6 month residual catches people. Prodiamine kills germinating grass seed just as effectively as germinating weed seed. Apply Blockade in spring and the autumn seeding-and-over-seeding window is gone. Plan the renovation calendar before applying, not after.

## When not to use

On cool-season turf in NZ (off-label). On any lawn where seeding-and-over-seeding is planned within 4 to 6 months. On stressed turf. On putting or bowling greens (label exclusion). On newly seeded, sodded, or sprigged turf. As a post-emergent —  already up won't be touched.
