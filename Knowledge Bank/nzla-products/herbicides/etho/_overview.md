# NZLA Etho

The selective option for poa annua in ryegrass and tall fescue lawns. Ethofumesate at 500 g/L. HRAC Group 16 benzofuran chemistry. Pre and early post-emergent activity gives a wider application window than a pure pre-em like Blockade, and the selectivity on ryegrass and tall fescue is what makes it useful for cool-season home lawns where Blockade can't be used.

## How it actually works

Ethofumesate inhibits the synthesis of very long chain fatty acids (VLCFAs) in susceptible plants. VLCFAs are essential components of plant cell membranes and the waxy cuticles that protect leaves. Without functional VLCFA synthesis, susceptible plants can't build new cell membranes as cells divide, can't maintain the cuticle that prevents water loss, and the cellular machinery progressively fails.

The chemistry targets actively growing tissue specifically — newly germinating seedlings (where new cells are being built rapidly) and emerging shoots in the early post-emergent window. Once a weed is well-established, the chemistry has less to work with and effectiveness drops.

## Why the selectivity works

Same principle as mesotrione's selectivity. The chemistry of ethofumesate is the same in every plant it enters — turf or weed. What makes it selective on perennial ryegrass and tall fescue is **metabolism rate**.

Perennial ryegrass and tall fescue have efficient metabolic pathways that detoxify ethofumesate before it can disrupt lipid biosynthesis. The herbicide enters the plant, gets broken down, the plant carries on.

Fine fescues, warm-season grasses (kikuyu, couch), and most broadleaf weeds don't have the same metabolic capacity. The ethofumesate persists in the plant, inhibits VLCFA synthesis, and the plant dies.

That's why the species restriction matters absolutely. **Fine fescue can't metabolise ethofumesate efficiently and will die from a standard application.** Same for kikuyu and couch.

## The dual-stage advantage

Most selective herbicides are pre-emergent OR post-emergent. Ethofumesate has activity at both stages, which gives a wider application window.

**Pre-em phase.** Applied before weed seeds germinate, ethofumesate sits in the top centimetre of soil. Germinating shoots take it up as they push through. Lipid synthesis fails, the seedling can't establish, dies before reaching the surface. Water in with 5 to 7 mm of irrigation to move the active into the soil where the shoots are.

**Post-em phase.** Applied to early-stage weeds with limited leaf development, ethofumesate is absorbed through the foliage and through the developing root system. Lipid synthesis fails, the seedling dies. Let it dry on the foliage and don't irrigate immediately — wash-in moves the active away from the leaf surface where post-em uptake happens.

The two-application pattern (14 to 21 days apart) catches both staggered germination (new seeds coming up in the gap between applications) and weeds that escape the first application.

## Rate and application

40 mL per 100m² (4 L/ha), in a minimum of 4 L of water.

Two applications, with the second going down 14 to 21 days after the first.

**Pre-em use:** water in with 5 to 7 mm of irrigation immediately after spraying.

**Post-em use:** let it dry on the foliage, don't irrigate immediately.

Use water above 5°C — cold water can affect product behaviour. No surfactants — adjuvants can disrupt the formulation. Use the spray solution immediately after mixing.

Grazing withholding period is 28 days.

## Critical grass species restriction

DO NOT apply to fine fescue. It will damage or kill it. Same for kikuyu and couch.

Tall fescue can be knocked back if it's not actively growing. Apply only when the tall fescue is in active growth.

If you're unsure what grass mix is in the lawn, find out before spraying. A "fescue" lawn might be tall fescue (mostly fine for Etho) or fine fescue (will die from Etho). Different species, different outcomes.

## When not to use

On any lawn containing fine fescue.

On kikuyu, couch or any warm-season grass.

In a sprayer that's had surfactant in it.

With water below 5°C.

On heat-stressed or recently-mowed lawn.

Within 28 days of grazing animals being on the lawn (withholding period).

## Common mistakes

Not knowing what grass is in the lawn. The fine fescue restriction catches people out because "fescue" gets used loosely.

Skipping the second application. The 14 to 21 day follow-up catches staggered germination.

Watering in immediately after a post-emergent application. The active needs to stay on the leaf.

Adding a surfactant. The label specifically says no.

Mixing and storing the spray solution before use. The active works best applied immediately after mixing.

## Best paired with

Blockade ahead of the next poa germination window for a pre-em barrier on the new season's seed bank.

Cultural control (correct mowing height, dense healthy turf) to reduce the poa seed bank long-term.

Routine maintenance fertility that keeps the ryegrass or tall fescue dense enough to outcompete poa establishment.
