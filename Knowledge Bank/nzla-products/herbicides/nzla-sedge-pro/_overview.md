# NZLA Sedge Pro

The selective tool for nutsedge. Halosulfuron-methyl at 750 g/kg (HRAC Group 2, sulfonylurea). Hits purple and yellow nutsedge without taking out the surrounding lawn, which is the whole reason specific sedge herbicides exist.

## What it does

Halosulfuron-methyl inhibits the ALS enzyme, which susceptible plants need to make essential amino acids. Without those amino acids, growth stops and the plant dies. Most lawn grasses metabolise the herbicide before it does damage; sedges don't, which is the basis of the selectivity.

The product is a water-dispersible granule, mixed into spray water. Spot treatment for small patches, boom for larger areas.

## Targets

Purple nutsedge (Cyperus rotundus) and yellow nutsedge (Cyperus esculentus). These are perennial sedges that come back from underground tubers, so a single spray rarely kills them outright. Sedge Pro hits the plant above ground, but the tuber bank can keep producing new shoots for a year or more after first treatment.

## Rate and application

Spot treatment: 1.3 g per 100m² in 10 L water. Targeted spray on the sedge patch.

Boom application: 65 to 130 g/ha in minimum 80 L of water per hectare.

Apply to actively growing nutsedge once new leaf growth is 5 cm or more. Smaller plants don't have enough leaf area to absorb a useful dose.

Delay mowing for 2 days after application so the leaves absorb and translocate the active.

## Sprayer cleanout

Sulfonylureas are extraordinarily potent at very low concentrations. Trace residues in a sprayer can damage subsequent broadleaf crops or sensitive lawns. After using Sedge Pro, flush the sprayer thoroughly with chlorine bleach (commercial pattern), then rinse with clean water. Don't skip this step.

## When not to use

On newly established or stressed turf.

Near broadleaf garden plants. Spray drift will damage them.

In hot windy conditions where drift is unmanageable.

Expecting a one-spray fix on established nutsedge. Multiple applications across a season, and likely a second season, is normal.

## Common mistakes

Spraying nutsedge before it has reached 5 cm new leaf growth. Wastes product.

Mowing within 2 days of application. Removes the leaf the active is sitting on before it's fully translocated.

Skipping the sprayer cleanout. Trace residue can damage the next thing the sprayer is used on.

Assuming a single application has solved the problem. Sedge tubers can produce new shoots for years, multi-season programmes are normal.

## Best paired with

Cultural control. Improving drainage and reducing wet soil pockets reduces nutsedge pressure long-term.

Repeat applications across the season (label permitting) to deplete the tuber bank.
