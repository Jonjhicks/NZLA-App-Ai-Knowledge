# NZLA BWC

Three-way broadleaf herbicide. MCPA (300 g/L), clopyralid (20 g/L), and diflufenican (15 g/L). Two chemistry groups (HRAC 4 for MCPA and clopyralid, HRAC 12 for diflufenican) and three distinct modes of action in one application. That combination covers a wide weed spectrum and reduces the resistance risk of any single active.

## How the three actives work

**MCPA** is a synthetic auxin that mimics the plant growth hormone IAA at toxic levels. Foliar absorbed, moves through the plant via phloem to all growing points. At growing points, the hormone flood overrides normal growth regulation — cells elongate chaotically, stems twist, vascular tissue is disrupted. Death in 1 to 3 weeks. Active against most common broadleaf .

**Clopyralid** is a pyridine carboxylic acid auxin. More persistent in plant tissue than MCPA, with particular strength against Asteraceae (daisy family — dandelion, catsear, hawksbeard, thistles, daisy) and legumes (clover). The sustained receptor binding causes extended disruption over 2 to 4 weeks.

**Diflufenican** inhibits carotenoid biosynthesis (HRAC Group 12, phytoene desaturase inhibitor). Without carotenoids, chlorophyll is destroyed by sunlight. Susceptible plants bleach and die. Absorbed by germinating shoots with limited translocation — most active on recently germinated seedlings, which gives BWC some residual activity against seeds in the soil germinating in the weeks after application.

The combination covers both established  (MCPA, clopyralid) and seedlings emerging from the seed bank (diflufenican).

## Weed coverage

Standard NZ broadleaf list: dandelion, plantain, catsear, hawksbeard, hawkbit, thistles, dock, oxalis, clovers, daisy, chickweed, and many more. Particularly strong on the rosette-forming  (catsear, hawkbit) where the clopyralid component does reliable work.

## The onehunga resistance situation — important

Onehunga weed (Soliva sessilis) populations in NZ have developed over 200-fold resistance to clopyralid. Resistant strains have been identified north of Auckland and are spreading — Mount Maunganui and beyond. Cross-resistance extends to picloram (also HRAC Group 4, in NZLA Gold) and somewhat to MCPA and dicamba.

In practical terms: if BWC isn't controlling onehunga in a particular lawn or area, suspect clopyralid resistance. Reapplying more BWC won't fix it — it accelerates resistance development. The chemistry that handles resistant biotypes is mecoprop, which is not currently in the NZLA range. If confirmed clopyralid-resistant onehunga is the problem, that gap needs to be acknowledged honestly.

## Rate and application

Check the current label. Carrier water 4 to 8 L per 100m². Apply to dry foliage when  are actively growing in mild conditions (12 to 24°C). Wind low, no rain for 6 hours minimum.

Don't mow for 3 days before or after application — leaf area is what the herbicide absorbs through.

Don't water in for 24 hours. This is foliar chemistry, not soil-applied.

Wait at least 4 weeks before reapplying.

## Turf safety

At label rate on healthy established cool-season turf (ryegrass, tall fescue, browntop, fine fescue), BWC is safe and selective. Where damage shows up: over-rate, heat-stressed or drought-stressed lawns, lawns under 8 weeks, or tank-mix errors combining multiple hormone herbicides at full rates.

Brief yellowing of the lawn for a few days after spraying is normal — the grass processes the chemistry and greens back up.

## BWC vs Gold

BWC covers the standard broadleaf weed spectrum. Gold (picloram + triclopyr) handles tougher or woodier  that BWC can leave behind — oxalis, mallow, storksbill, twin cress, wireweed. But Gold is heavier chemistry, not safe on fine fescue or browntop, and the picloram has longer soil persistence.

For most NZ home lawns with a standard broadleaf mix, BWC handles the job. Gold is the step up for persistent or tougher .

Note: Gold also contains a Group 4 active (picloram), so clopyralid-resistant onehunga that has cross-resistance to Group 4 chemistry is a problem for both products.

## When not to use

On lawns under 8 weeks old. On stressed lawns. On warm-season turf without checking the label. When confirmed clopyralid-resistant onehunga is the target weed.
