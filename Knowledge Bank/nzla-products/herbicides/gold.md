# NZLA Gold

Picloram (50 g/L) and triclopyr (100 g/L). The heavy-duty broadleaf herbicide for established cool-season lawns and the tougher  that BWC leaves behind — woody stems, persistent rosettes, oxalis, mallow, and anything with a waxy cuticle that other auxin herbicides can't penetrate as well.

## How it works

Both actives are synthetic auxins (HRAC Group 4). They mimic the plant growth hormone auxin (indole-3-acetic acid) at toxic concentrations. Auxin controls cell elongation, vascular development, and growth regulation. A flood of synthetic auxin at toxic levels swamps those regulatory systems — cells grow chaotically, stems twist and crack, vascular tissue is disrupted. Leaves curl and distort. The plant grows uncontrollably for a few days, then collapses.

**Picloram** moves via both xylem and phloem, giving broad systemic distribution throughout the plant including into the root system. Long soil persistence (6 to 12 months or more). Broad activity across most common broadleaf .

**Triclopyr** is foliar absorbed, moves through phloem to growing points. Particularly effective on waxy-leafed and woody  where its ability to penetrate the cuticle is the key advantage. Degrades in soil in 8 to 46 days — much shorter persistence than picloram.

Together: two translocation pathways, both rapid and persistent action, wider weed spectrum than either alone.

## What it controls

Long list. Cape daisy, catsear, chickweed, clovers, convolvulus, daisy, dandelion, dichondra, dock, hawkbit, hawksbeard, hydrocotyle, mallow, mayweed, nettles, onehunga, creeping oxalis, plantain, ragwort, shepherd's purse, sorrel, storksbill, thistles, twin cress, wireweed, and more.

Particularly strong on storksbill, twin cress, wireweed, mallow, convolvulus, and established oxalis —  that hold out against BWC.

## The onehunga resistance note

Documented clopyralid-resistant onehunga in NZ (Harrington, Massey University) shows cross-resistance to picloram. Both actives in Gold belong to the same HRAC Group 4 auxin class as clopyralid. If BWC has stopped working on onehunga, Gold's picloram fraction may also be compromised. If both BWC and Gold fail on onehunga, the chemistry needed is mecoprop, which is not currently in the NZLA range.

## Drift and persistence — take these seriously

**Picloram soil persistence** is 6 to 12 months or longer. It doesn't bind strongly to soil organic matter and can move with water — leaching into groundwater, running off during heavy rain, moving through soil to nearby garden beds.

**Drift damage** from Gold is severe. Both actives damage broadleaf garden plants — roses, hedges, vegetables, ornamentals — from minor exposure. Twisted, distorted growth that doesn't recover. Apply at the minimum pressure that gives adequate coverage. No application in wind. Don't direct sprays higher than the target .

**Clippings:** don't compost clippings from a Gold-treated lawn. Picloram persists in plant tissue and in compost for 12 to 18 months or longer. Clippings composted into a vegetable garden will damage or kill sensitive crops — tomatoes, capsicums, beans, peas, potatoes. Mulch them back into the lawn or send to landfill.

**Sprayer residue:** use a dedicated sprayer for hormone herbicides. Even thoroughly washed sprayers can carry damaging picloram residue. A sprayer used for Gold should not be used on ornamentals afterwards without extremely thorough cleanout.

## Rate and application

Check the current label. Apply when  are actively growing in mild conditions (spring or autumn). Don't apply if heavy rain is forecast within 48 hours — runoff moves picloram off-target.

Cool-season turf only: safe on perennial ryegrass and tall fescue. Not safe on fine fescue, browntop, or warm-season grasses. Established lawns only — wait until the lawn has been mowed at least 3 times.

## When not to use

Anywhere near garden beds, ornamentals, trees, or vegetable gardens. On fine fescue, browntop, kikuyu, or couch. On stressed, drought-stressed, or newly seeded lawns. In wind. If the clippings will be composted.

## Gold vs BWC

BWC handles standard broadleaf  on most cool-season lawns. Gold is for the tougher end of the spectrum and heavy infestations where BWC isn't achieving control. Gold comes with more soil persistence, more drift risk, and more composting restrictions — use BWC as the routine tool and step up to Gold when the target weed genuinely requires it.
