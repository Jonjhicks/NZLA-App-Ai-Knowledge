# NZLA Blockade

The professional pre-emergent. Prodiamine at 476.2 g/L — one of the most-used turf pre-emergents globally because the chemistry is highly effective at very low rates and the residual lasts longer than most alternatives in the same chemistry class.

## NZ label vs international label

In New Zealand, Blockade is only labelled for warm-season turf (kikuyu, couch). Not labelled here for cool-season grasses (ryegrass, fescue, browntop).

In the USA and most international markets, prodiamine is widely registered for cool-season turf — ryegrass, fescue, bentgrass, bluegrass — and is used routinely on cool-season golf greens, fairways and sports turf globally.

The practical reality is that prodiamine is safe on cool-season grass. The NZ label restriction is a regulatory matter, not a chemistry or safety issue. But because products can only be recommended for their labelled uses, NZLA's official position is that Blockade is for warm-season turf in NZ. The customer can be told the difference between the NZ and international labels honestly, but off-label use can't be recommended.

## How it actually works

Prodiamine is a dinitroaniline herbicide that inhibits microtubule formation in germinating weed seedlings.

Microtubules are the cellular scaffold that organises chromosome separation during mitosis (cell division). When a seed germinates and the root tip starts dividing rapidly to push the root down through the soil, those dividing cells need functional microtubules. Prodiamine binds to the microtubule assembly proteins and prevents them forming. The result is failed cell division in the developing root tip — the seedling can't extend its root, can't establish, dies before reaching the soil surface.

The chemistry works on germinating seeds specifically because that's where rapid cell division is happening at the root tip. Once a plant is established with a developed root system, prodiamine in the surface soil layer doesn't affect it (the established roots aren't dividing rapidly any more, and they're below the chemical barrier anyway).

## Why the residual is so long

Prodiamine binds very tightly to soil organic matter and clay particles in the top centimetre of soil. It doesn't leach significantly, doesn't break down quickly under UV (because it's bound in soil), and is metabolised by soil microbes only slowly. Result: 4 to 6 months of effective pre-emergence barrier from a single application, longer than most pre-emergents in the dinitroaniline class.

The downside of the long residual is the seeding window restriction — you can't overseed for 4 to 6 months after application because the prodiamine will kill any new grass seed you put down just as effectively as it kills the target weeds.

## Timing in NZ — the critical decisions

This is the part most people get wrong.

**Summer grass and crabgrass control.** Get Blockade down before soil temperatures at 50 mm depth hit 12°C and stay there. That's the germination trigger for warm-season annual grass weeds. Application 2 to 3 weeks ahead of that gives the barrier time to form. In Auckland that's late August. In Canterbury it's late September into early October. Watch soil temperatures, not the calendar.

**Poa annua control.** Application in autumn before soil temperatures drop into the 18 to 22°C range where poa germinates. Late February to early April depending on region. Same logic — get the barrier in place 2 to 3 weeks before germination, not after.

**Second application 90 to 120 days later** carries the barrier through the peak germination window. The 90-day minimum interval is a label requirement, not a guideline.

## Rate and application

Established turf controlling annual grass weeds: 10 to 30 mL per 100m². Poa annua specifically: 10 to 40 mL per 100m².

Activation needs at least 6 mm of rain or irrigation within 7 days of application. Without that the chemical doesn't bind into the soil profile properly and the barrier never forms.

Maximum two applications per 365 days, with at least 90 days between them.

Don't apply if heavy rain is forecast within 48 hours, you'll wash the product off-target before it binds.

## When not to use

On cool-season turf in NZ. Off-label, cannot be recommended.

On new lawns. Blockade kills germinating seedlings, including your seed.

On putting greens or bowling greens (label exclusion).

On stressed turf — the roots are already compromised.

As a post-emergent. If weeds are already up, Blockade won't touch them.

Within 4 to 6 months of planned overseeding.

## Common mistakes

Putting it down too late. Once soil temps trigger germination, you're behind it. Apply 2 to 3 weeks before expected germination.

Not watering it in. The 6 mm activation is essential.

Re-seeding too soon after application. The 4 to 6 month residual catches people out — Blockade in spring and overseed in autumn doesn't work.

Applying to a stressed lawn.

Trying to use it as a rescue for weeds already up. Wrong chemistry — needs BWC, Gold, Meso or similar post-emergent.

## Best paired with

BWC or Gold for routine post-em broadleaf control on weeds that escape the pre-em barrier.

Cultural control (thick healthy turf, correct mowing height) to reduce the seed bank long-term.

A planned overseeding calendar that respects the 4 to 6 month residual.
