# NZLA Halo

The selective grass-in-grass herbicide. Haloxyfop-P-methyl at 100 g/L. HRAC Group 1 FOP (aryloxyphenoxypropionate). Takes out unwanted grass species — kikuyu, couch, summer grass, ryegrass, browntop — from fine fescue lawns where the fine fescue is the desirable species. Different from selective broadleaf herbicides in that it specifically kills grasses without harming broadleaf plants.

## How it actually works

Haloxyfop-P-methyl is an ACCase inhibitor — it blocks acetyl-CoA carboxylase, the enzyme that catalyses the first committed step in fatty acid synthesis in grasses. Without ACCase activity, the plant can't build new cell membrane lipids. As cells divide and grow, they need new membranes. Without functional fatty acid synthesis, the plant stops growing and dies.

The chemistry works on grasses specifically because broadleaf plants have a different form of the ACCase enzyme that's not inhibited by FOP chemistry. So Halo can be sprayed over broadleaf garden plants without damaging them, which is part of why it's used in agriculture for grass-weed control in broadleaf crops (cotton, canola, soybeans).

## Why the selectivity within grasses is tricky

The "kills grasses" part is broad, and that's the catch on turf.

Most turf grasses — perennial ryegrass, tall fescue, browntop (Agrostis), kikuyu, couch, summer grass — are susceptible to haloxyfop at standard rates. Spraying Halo over any lawn that contains these species will damage or kill them.

Fine fescue tolerates haloxyfop at restricted rates (the fine fescue ACCase enzyme has lower affinity for the chemistry). That's the basis of the selectivity NZLA Halo offers — selective grass-weed control in fine fescue lawns specifically.

**Headline restriction: fine fescue maximum 15 mL per 100m².** Going over rate damages even fine fescue. This is the most important number on the label.

## What it kills

The chemistry kills most grasses other than fine fescue (and some specific other label species).

For fine fescue lawn owners, that means Halo is the tool for removing kikuyu encroachment, couch invasion, paspalum, summer grass, and any other invading grass that would otherwise need full renovation to address.

For lawn owners with ryegrass, tall fescue, browntop, kikuyu or couch as the desirable lawn species, **Halo is not selective — it will kill the lawn**. Different chemistry needed.

## Soil residual and seeding window

Haloxyfop persists in soil for 6 to 8 weeks before breaking down to non-toxic levels. Sowing new seed within that window will damage or kill the new seedlings — the chemistry kills germinating grass just as effectively as established grass.

Plan any reseeding around the 6 to 8 week stand-down.

## Rate and application

Refer to label for rates by application method (broadcast L/ha, knapsack mL/10L) and by target weed.

Fine fescue rate cap: 15 mL per 100m². Hard limit.

6 to 8 weeks stand-down after application before sowing new seed.

Not for use in grazed areas.

GHS: eye irritation Cat 2, STOT repeat Cat 2, aquatic chronic Cat 2. Commercial use needs qualified handler (EPA HPC Notice 2017). PPE is non-negotiable — eyes and skin protected, no inhalation exposure.

## When to use

Established fine fescue lawns with kikuyu, couch, ryegrass, browntop, summer grass or paspalum encroachment.

Specific situations where you need to remove a grass weed from a fine fescue lawn without using a non-selective like glyphosate (which would kill everything).

## When not to use

On any lawn containing ryegrass, tall fescue, browntop, kikuyu, couch, or any other Halo-susceptible grass as the desirable species. Halo will kill them.

Above the 15 mL/100m² cap on fine fescue.

In grazed areas.

Within 6 to 8 weeks of planned seeding.

By an unqualified user in a commercial setting.

Without PPE.

## Common mistakes

Spraying it over a mixed lawn assuming "selective" means safe for all turf. It doesn't — Halo is selective for fine fescue specifically.

Exceeding the 15 mL/100m² cap.

Sowing seed too soon after application — residual carries 6 to 8 weeks.

Drift onto desirable grasses elsewhere. Halo will damage adjacent grass lawns from drift.

Confusing it with broadleaf herbicides (BWC, Gold). Halo is a grass herbicide, opposite chemistry, different uses.

## Best paired with

Renovation programme. If a fine fescue lawn has heavy kikuyu encroachment, Halo plus a renovation plan (kill, overseed, manage) is more effective than chemistry alone.

Cultural control. Mowing height, fertility and density that favour fine fescue reduce the underlying reason grass weeds are invading.
