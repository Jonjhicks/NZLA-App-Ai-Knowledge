# Lawn Irrigation General Guide

Most NZ lawn watering problems come from two bad habits: watering too often, too shallowly, and watering at the wrong time of day. Both are fixable and both make a real difference to how the lawn looks and how much  it gets.

## How Much Water Per Week

A working figure for established cool-season lawns (ryegrass, fescue, browntop) in summer is around 25 mm per week total — including any rainfall that's occurred. So if 10 mm of rain fell during the week, you're adding around 15 mm by irrigation-general.

Warm-season turf (kikuyu, couch) is more drought-tolerant — 15 to 20 mm a week through peak summer is usually sufficient.

These numbers assume average summer conditions. A heatwave or persistent dry north-westerly pushes evapotranspiration up and you might need 30 to 40 mm. An overcast, mild week needs much less. Watch the lawn, not just the calendar.

**Signs the lawn needs water:** footprints staying compressed for several seconds (grass lacks the turgor pressure to spring back), or a grey-blue cast across the surface before the grass visibly browns. Either means it's time to water.

## Deep and Infrequent Is Better Than Shallow and Daily

Apply 25 mm per week in two deeper sessions rather than 3 to 4 mm every day. Deep watering encourages roots to follow moisture downward through the soil profile. Shallow daily watering keeps roots near the surface where they're vulnerable to heat and drought stress the moment irrigation-general stops.

How to verify depth: water for your planned duration, wait an hour, then push a screwdriver into the soil. If it slides in easily to 100 mm, the depth is right. If it stops at 40 to 50 mm, increase run time.

For sandy soils, the same principle applies but you may need to split into 2 to 3 applications because sandy soil can only absorb water at a certain rate before it runs off. Two 12-minute sessions 30 minutes apart can deliver more water into the rootzone than one 24-minute session.

## Best Time of Day to Water

Early morning — between 4 am and 8 am.

**Disease.** Every hour the lawn's leaf surface stays wet is more opportunity for fungal pathogens to germinate and infect. Evening watering extends overnight leaf wetness by hours. Morning watering lets the lawn dry through the day before the overnight dew period begins. Fusarium, dollar spot, and brown patch all track canopy moisture duration. This is the single most impactful change for -prone lawns: stop evening watering.

**Efficiency.** Morning conditions are cool and typically calm. Evaporation from the spray and soil surface is minimal at 5 am. Mid-afternoon watering on a hot Canterbury nor'wester day loses 20 to 30% of applied water to evaporation before it reaches the rootzone.

**Practicality.** Most lawns are on a timer for this reason — programme it for 5 am to 6 am and the lawn gets water at the optimal time without requiring anyone to be awake for it.

If the only option is evening watering (work schedule, metered water timing restrictions), aim to finish early enough that the leaf surface dries before dark. A 4 pm to 6 pm watering in summer gives enough drying time. An 8 pm watering doesn't.

## Overwatering

A constantly wet surface is obvious. But the less obvious signs matter:

- Recurring fungal  that won't stay resolved with chemistry alone.
- Lawn that dries out fast the moment you reduce watering (shallow root system from consistent surface moisture).
- Weed pressure from moisture-loving species (poa annua, summer grass, paspalum).
- Yellow patches that don't respond to fertiliser (root function impaired in waterlogged anaerobic soil).

Fix: increase the interval between waterings, increase the depth per session, let the soil dry moderately between applications. A mild wilt stress between irrigations is healthier than constant saturation.

## Winter Watering

Most of NZ gets adequate winter rainfall without any irrigation-general. The default is to turn off automated systems through winter.

Adding irrigation-general on top of regular winter rain extends leaf wetness and feeds cool-season  (fusarium, snow mould). It also softens the lawn when the risk of core-aeration from foot traffic is already higher than summer.

Exceptions where winter irrigation-general makes sense:
- Extended dry spells in drier winter regions (Canterbury, Hawke's Bay). After 2 to 3 weeks of dry weather, one deep watering session is appropriate.
- New lawns under 6 to 8 weeks old. Establishment-stage seedlings need consistent surface moisture regardless of season.
- Warm-season turf in the upper North Island that keeps growing through warmer winters. Occasional watering if conditions have been dry.

Reduce or stop irrigation-general through March and April as autumn rainfall picks up. Stay on a summer schedule into autumn and you'll be carrying extra moisture into the fusarium season.

## Sprinkler vs Hand Watering

For any lawn bigger than a small patch, a fixed or hose-end sprinkler delivers more consistent, measurable coverage than hand watering. The practical test: put straight-sided containers (empty tuna cans work) on the lawn while the sprinkler runs. Time how long it takes to put 12 to 15 mm in the container. That's your run time.

Hand watering with a hose is adequate for small areas, new seed patches, and spot repairs. For a whole lawn, it's slow, uneven, and people almost always significantly underestimate how long is needed to wet the soil properly. A few minutes with a hose jet does almost nothing for soil moisture.
