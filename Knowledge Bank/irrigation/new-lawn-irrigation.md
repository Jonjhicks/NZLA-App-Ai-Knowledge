# Watering a New Lawn

New lawn irrigation-general has one job: keep the surface consistently moist from sowing until the seedlings are rooted. It's not about watering deeply during establishment — it's about keeping that top 5 to 10 mm from drying out.

Both extremes kill new lawns. A surface that dries out cracks and kills germinating seedlings before they can establish. Waterlogged soil drowns seedlings and encourages damping-off . The target is moist but not saturated.

## The Establishment Stages

**Sowing to germination (days 0 to 14):**
Light, frequent watering. Two to three short applications per day in warm weather. Each session might only be 3 to 5 minutes — enough to re-wet the surface without pooling. The goal is keeping the top 10 mm damp all day. Ryegrass germinates in 7 to 14 days in warm conditions (soil temperature 15°C+). Tall fescue is similar. Fine fescue is slower — up to 21 days.

**Seedlings through to first mow (days 14 to 35 approximately):**
Once seedlings are up and the second leaf is appearing, back off the frequency and start increasing the depth. Shift toward one good watering a day, letting the surface dry slightly between applications to encourage roots to grow downward. By three to four weeks post-sowing you should be watering every second day.

**After first mow through to established (weeks 5 to 8):**
Continue transitioning toward a standard established-lawn programme. By 6 to 8 weeks post-sowing, the lawn should be on deep, infrequent watering — 25 mm per week in two sessions, same as a mature lawn.

## Warm vs Cool Weather Establishment

**Warm weather (summer or early autumn sowing, soil temperature 15°C+):**
Higher evaporation means the surface dries out faster. More critical to maintain irrigation-general frequency. Two to three short daily sessions through the first two weeks. Watch the surface closely — a light windblown drying from the top can stress seedlings on a warm afternoon even if the soil below is moist.

**Cool weather (autumn or spring sowing, soil temperature 10 to 15°C):**
Evaporation is low and the surface stays moist longer between irrigations. Easier to manage but easy to over-water. Rainfall often does part of the work. One or two short daily sessions may be adequate, and if rain is regular you may not need to irrigate at all some days. Don't add irrigation-general on top of wet days — waterlogged seedlings are a real risk.

If it rains meaningfully during establishment, suspend irrigation-general and let the natural rainfall do the job. Re-evaluate the following morning.

## Equipment for New Lawns

**Fine spray is essential.** Heavy droplets from an impact sprinkler dislodge seed and create craters in the prepared soil. You want a fine, gentle spray that wets the surface without disturbing it.

**Oscillating sprinkler with fine spray setting:** good for most home lawn areas. Even coverage, adjustable width, cost-effective. Set water pressure down if possible.

**Hose with fan-spray nozzle (gentle setting):** adequate for smaller areas and spot seeding of repairs. Hands-on but precise.

**Pop-up spray system:** suitable if you already have it installed — just adjust run times to short frequent cycles during establishment rather than the longer deep-watering sessions you'd use on an established lawn.

Impact sprinklers and rotary heads with large droplets are not ideal for fresh seed. Fine spray through the establishment phase, then switch to your regular delivery method once the seedlings are established.

## Watching the Surface

The single most useful skill during establishment is simply looking at the surface before and during each irrigation-general. If the surface is still visibly moist from the previous watering, skip or shorten the current one. If the surface looks dry and the soil is beginning to crack slightly, add an extra session.

A new lawn in good conditions (autumn sowing, adequate warmth, consistent moisture) practically manages itself if you check it twice a day and respond to what you see. A lawn that's allowed to dry out between checks is the main avoidable failure.

## Post-Establishment Transition

Once the lawn is at first-mow stage (typically 6 to 8 weeks from sowing), begin transitioning to the established lawn programme. The objective from here is deep root development — water less often, deeper. Roots follow moisture. A root system that has chased water down to 100 to 150 mm depth is far more drought-tolerant than one that's stayed in the top 30 mm because water was always at the surface.
