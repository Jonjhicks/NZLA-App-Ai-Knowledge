# What is dry patch and how do I fix it?

Dry patch is an area of lawn that won't take up water properly even when you irrigate. The soil at the surface has become hydrophobic — water-repellent — so the water beads up and runs off rather than soaking in. The grass over that spot dries out and goes brown while the rest of the lawn looks fine.

Causes vary. Sandy soils after a long dry spell, lawns coming out of summer drought, thatchy lawns with a waxy surface layer, over-fertilised lawns with surface salt buildup, and lawns near exposed corners that bake dry in the sun. The common factor is a surface that water won't penetrate.

The fix is a wetting agent.

NZLA Wetter (heavy molecular weight non-ionic residual surfactant) is the standard tool. 250 mL per 100m² in 4 to 8 L of water, washed in with 15 to 20 mm of irrigation. The surfactant breaks the hydrophobic film on the soil particles and lets water enter the root zone normally. Repeat 7 to 10 days later for severe cases.

For high-performance turf or sand-based root zones, Wetter 3W (premium three-way action) is the upgrade. For non-irrigated lawns, Wetter GR (granular version) works without needing immediate water-in.

For ongoing prevention, monthly application of Wetter through the growing season stops dry patch rebuilding.

A few practical notes. You can spot-test by pouring a glass of water on the dry patch — if it sits on the surface for more than 30 seconds, that's hydrophobic. A wetting agent will fix it. Improving soil structure long-term (organic matter, aeration) reduces the recurrence.
