# How often should I water my established lawn in summer?

Less often than you think, but deeper when you do.

The standard for established cool-season lawns through summer is one to two deep waterings a week, each going deep enough to wet the soil to 100 to 150 mm depth. That encourages roots to chase moisture down through the profile, which builds drought tolerance and a more resilient lawn.

Daily light watering does the opposite. It keeps the surface moist but never penetrates to the root zone, so roots stay shallow and the lawn dries out fast the moment you stop watering. Shallow-rooted lawns are far more vulnerable to heat stress.

For most home lawns the practical rule is 25 to 35 mm of water per week total, split into one or two applications. In a hot dry week that might mean 30 minutes per zone twice a week. In a mild week, maybe once.

How to check depth properly: water for your planned duration, wait an hour, then push a screwdriver into the soil. If it goes in easily to 100 mm, you've watered enough. If it stops at 50 mm, you need more time.

Warm-season lawns (kikuyu, couch) need less water generally — deeper roots, more drought tolerance. Twice-weekly deep watering through peak heat is usually plenty.

NZLA Wetter or Wetter 3W as a wetting agent through summer if you've got dry patch — gets water into the root zone rather than running off the hydrophobic surface.

Mornings are best for watering. Evening watering extends overnight leaf wetness and feeds disease.
