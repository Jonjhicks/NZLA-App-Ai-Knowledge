# Irrigation Timers and Smart Controllers

A timer is the simplest upgrade to irrigation-general management — it runs the system at the right time of day (early morning) whether you remember to or not. A smart controller takes that further and adjusts the schedule based on actual weather conditions.

## Standard Timers

A basic battery-operated tap timer or controller runs your irrigation-general on a fixed schedule: zone A for 20 minutes at 5:30 am, zone B for 15 minutes at 6 am, and so on. It doesn't know whether it's rained or whether the next week is forecast to be a heatwave. It runs the schedule you set.

Done properly, a standard timer saves water compared to ad-hoc hand watering. Manual hose-end watering tends to be shallow and irregular. A programmed timer watering deeply at 5 am, twice a week, is more efficient than the same volume applied at 5 pm in several short bursts when someone happens to remember.

Add a rain sensor (typically $40 to $80) and the timer skips watering when a certain rainfall threshold is reached. A useful basic improvement that prevents the system running through heavy rain events.

**Best for:** single-zone hose-end systems, simple setups, situations where budget matters more than optimisation.

## Smart Controllers

Smart controllers use weather data — local rainfall, evapotranspiration (ET) calculations, and often forecast information — to automatically adjust irrigation-general schedules. They skip watering when it's not needed and increase run times when evapotranspiration is high.

**Hunter Hydrawise** and **Rain Bird ESP-Me** are the most commonly installed smart controllers in NZ residential settings. Both connect to Wi-Fi, use local weather station data or forecast APIs, and can be managed from a phone app.

**Water savings:** most properties with smart controllers see 20 to 40% less water use compared to a fixed-schedule timer. The savings are real because a fixed schedule typically over-waters during cool, cloudy, or wet periods. Smart controllers automatically remove those unnecessary waterings. On a metered water connection, this translates directly to cost savings.

**App control:** adjust schedules remotely, skip a zone from your phone if you're away and there's forecast rain, get alerts if a zone fails to run (indicating a solenoid or valve issue). Useful when travelling.

**Setup investment:** more involved than a standard timer. You need Wi-Fi at the controller location, and initial setup requires configuring each zone with plant type, soil type, head type, and sun exposure. That takes 30 to 60 minutes. Once set up, the system manages itself with minimal intervention.

**Best for:** permanent multi-zone pop-up systems, properties where summer water bills are significant, owners who want the system to be genuinely set-and-forget.

## Getting the Schedule Right

Neither a standard timer nor a smart controller compensates for a fundamentally wrong schedule. The most common mistake is setting the controller for short, daily runs — which produces shallow watering, encourages shallow roots, and creates exactly the vulnerable lawn that stresses fast under drought or heat.

The right schedule, regardless of controller type:
- Two to three deep watering sessions per week for established cool-season lawns.
- Each session running long enough to apply 12 to 15 mm (verify with containers).
- Timed to finish before 8 am.
- Total of approximately 25 mm per week including rainfall.

A smart controller running a correct deep-and-infrequent schedule is the best setup. A standard timer running the same correct schedule is nearly as good. A smart controller running daily shallow sessions still produces a shallow-rooted vulnerable lawn.

Get the programme right first, then let the technology make it consistent and weather-responsive.

## Practical Notes

For a simple one-zone garden hose setup: a basic battery tap timer ($60 to $100) handles the job. Set it for a 5 am start, run time calibrated to deliver 12 to 15 mm. Add a rain sensor if available.

For a permanent multi-zone system: the upgrade from a standard controller to Hydrawise or equivalent smart controller costs $300 to $500 for the controller unit plus installation. It pays back in water savings over a few summers and removes the need to manually manage schedule adjustments through seasons.

For new system installations: worth specifying a smart-controller-compatible system from the start. Pre-wiring for smart control at installation costs little more than a standard setup and makes the upgrade straightforward later.
