# Lawn Drainage and Soil Moisture

Drainage problems and hydrophobic soil are opposite problems that look superficially similar — both produce dry patches or areas where the lawn struggles. The distinction matters because the fixes are different.

## Water Pooling After Rain

If water sits on the surface after rain and takes hours or days to drain, the cause is one of three things.

**Compacted soil.** The most common cause for home lawns. Heavy clay, foot traffic, mower wheels on the same lines, kids playing in the same spots — all of it compresses soil over time until the pore spaces close up and water has nowhere to go. Core or solid-tine aeration opens the profile. NZLA Aerate applied after aeration (or worked into irrigation-general) is a penetrant surfactant that helps water break through surface tension into compacted soil. This is the right first step for most home lawn pooling.

**Hydrophobic soil.** Sandy or organic-matter-poor soils develop a waxy coating on the particles after a dry spell. Water beads off the surface rather than soaking in. The paradox: the soil is dry and refusing water. See the dry patch section below for the fix.

**High water table or poor subsoil drainage-and-soil-moisture.** Water has nowhere to escape. If your lawn sits at the bottom of a slope or on low-lying ground and pooling persists despite aeration, the issue is structural. Subsoil drainage-and-soil-moisture (slotted pipe in gravel trenches running to a stormwater outlet or soakpit) is the durable fix. That's earthworks territory.

Start with aeration and a wetting agent. Most home lawn pooling resolves at that level.

## Improving Drainage: The Three Levels

**Surface level.** Core or solid-tine aeration once or twice a year. Follow immediately with a sandy loam top dressing that falls into the cores and replaces compacted soil with more permeable material. NZLA Aerate applied with irrigation-general through summer maintains water movement between aeration events. Doing this consistently over 3 to 5 seasons makes a measurable difference on heavy clay lawns.

**Soil profile level.** Top dressing with a sandier mix over multiple seasons gradually shifts the composition of the top 100 mm from heavy clay toward a more permeable sandy loam. One application does little. Annual top dressing after core aeration over 4 to 5 years noticeably improves a heavy-soil lawn's drainage-and-soil-moisture.

**Subsoil level.** Where water table is the issue, where there's no exit path for water, or where a clay hardpan layer below the surface traps water regardless of surface management — physical drainage-and-soil-moisture installation. Slotted agricultural pipe in gravel trenches, laid to grade, run to an outlet. This solves what no product can.

## Hydrophobic Soil and Dry Patch

Hydrophobic soil is the opposite of a drainage-and-soil-moisture problem. The soil is bone dry, and water won't enter it. The organic matter coating on soil particles becomes waxy and water-repellent after a hot dry period. Water applied in irrigation-general or rain simply beads on the surface and runs off.

**The test:** pour a glass of water on the suspect area. If it sits on the surface for more than 30 seconds rather than soaking in, the soil is hydrophobic.

**Symptoms:** defined dry patches that stay brown while irrigated neighbours stay green. Cracked, hard surface. Grass thinning in the dry patches.

**The fix is a wetting agent.** NZLA Wetter (heavy molecular-weight non-ionic surfactant) breaks the hydrophobic film on soil particles and allows water to enter the root zone normally. Apply at 250 mL per 100 m² diluted in 4 to 8 L of water as a sprayer application, then irrigate with 15 to 20 mm immediately after to drive the product into the soil. For severe cases, repeat at 7 to 10 days.

NZLA Wetter 3W is the premium version with a three-way action and longer residual. Better choice for high-performance lawns or where you want maximum season-long persistence from a single application.

NZLA Wetter GR is the granular version — applies without needing immediate water-in, useful for lawns without irrigation-general or for applying on a schedule when you can't guarantee immediate watering.

NZLA Penetrate applied monthly through summer keeps surface water moving down through the profile between wetting agent applications.

**Prevention:** a pre-summer Wetter 3W application in October/November, before dry patch establishes, is more effective than fixing it after it develops. Monthly Wetter or Penetrate through the growing season keeps the soil receptive.

Don't use household dish soap as a substitute. It breaks down surface tension briefly but damages soil biology and can harm the grass at effective concentrations.

## Overwatering Symptoms

Drainage and moisture management go both ways. Overwatered lawns show their own symptoms, and they're worth knowing:

- Lawn squelches underfoot most days.
- Recurring fungal . Fusarium, dollar spot, brown patch all track canopy moisture duration. Constant wet conditions feed them.
- Weed pressure. Poa annua, summer grass, and other moisture-loving  establish more readily in consistently damp lawns.
- Shallow root systems. Roots follow moisture. Constantly surface-wet soil keeps roots shallow, making the lawn vulnerable to heat and drought stress when watering stops.
- Yellowing patches without obvious cause — roots sitting in waterlogged anaerobic soil don't function properly.

Fix: cut back irrigation-general frequency, water deeper but less often, and let the soil dry moderately between waterings.
