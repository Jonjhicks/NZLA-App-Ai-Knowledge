# Will an irrigation timer actually save me water?

A standard timer might not, by itself. It runs the schedule you set whether it's needed or not. If you set it long, it waters long. If it runs through a rainy week, it still runs.

What a timer does well is consistency. It waters early in the morning (5 or 6 am is ideal) when evaporation is low and disease pressure is reduced. It runs for exactly as long as you set it. That removes the guesswork of "did I water for long enough" and the overcompensation of "I'll give it an extra hour just in case."

Done properly, a timer plus a deliberate schedule (two or three deep waterings a week of 12 to 15 mm each) uses less water than ad-hoc hose-end watering, because most ad-hoc watering is shallow and frequent, which wastes water and stresses the lawn.

Add a rain sensor to a standard timer and you'll skip waterings when it's wet. Add a smart controller and you'll get further water savings through forecast-based adjustment.

The real water saving comes from getting the schedule right (deep and infrequent, not shallow and daily). A timer just makes that consistent. A smart controller makes it weather-responsive.
