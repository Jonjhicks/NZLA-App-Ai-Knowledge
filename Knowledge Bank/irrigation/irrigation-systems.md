# Lawn Irrigation Systems

For lawns where consistent summer performance matters, a permanent irrigation-general system pays its way. The choice of what to install depends on the lawn size and what level of consistency you want.

## Do You Actually Need an Irrigation System?

If you're comfortable with the lawn browning off in summer and recovering in autumn, no. Established cool-season turf in NZ survives most summer dry spells. It looks poor for a few weeks, but it comes back in March. This is a legitimate management approach for lower-maintenance lawns.

If you want the lawn looking good year-round through summer, irrigation-general is the biggest single factor. The right fertiliser programme, species choice, and mowing practice will not hold a lawn together through a Canterbury January without water. Drought-tolerant species (tall fescue, warm-season grasses in the upper NI) extend the threshold significantly, but most lawns need water to look their best in summer.

**The middle ground** is hose-end sprinklers. Adequate for small lawns and engaged owners. Falls apart on larger areas or when you're away for a week in February. A $50 oscillating sprinkler handles a 100 m² lawn reasonably well if you're disciplined about using it.

**Alternatives that reduce system dependency:** drought-tolerant species at establishment (tall fescue is the main one for cool-season lawns), soil organic matter building over time, and a consistent NZLA Wetter 3W programme through summer to maximise efficiency of water applied.

## System Options by Lawn Size

**Under 100 m²:** a portable oscillating or impact sprinkler on a hose timer works well. Cost is $50 to $150 for a decent setup. Measure output with containers, calibrate your run time, and it's a functional solution.

**100 to 500 m²:** permanent pop-up spray heads on a controller starts to make economic sense here. The time saved over a summer and the consistency of early-morning watering on a timer justifies the investment. Installed cost for a typical medium home lawn is $2,000 to $6,000 depending on layout, head count, and whether it's DIY or professionally installed.

**500 m²+:** pop-up rotary heads (Hunter PGP or similar models) throw water further with a rotating nozzle rather than a fixed spray, so you need fewer heads to cover more ground. Higher water pressure required. Better for larger open lawns.

## System Design Principles

**Head-to-head coverage.** The most common installation mistake is spacing heads too far apart. Each spray head should reach the next head — "head-to-head coverage." This ensures every part of the lawn gets the same amount of water. Under-spaced heads create dry strips between spray patterns.

**Matching precipitation rates.** All heads on the same zone should apply water at the same rate. Mixing spray heads with rotary heads on the same zone creates dry and wet patches because spray heads put out much more water per minute than rotary heads.

**Zone separation.** Lawn zones separate from garden bed zones. The lawn needs different application rates and timing to garden beds. Mixing them on the same zone means either over-watering one or under-watering the other.

**Check points for common problems:** low-throwing heads on the edge near fences or paths, areas that get too much from two overlapping heads, low-pressure zones on the end of a long run. Walk the system while it's running the first time.

## Pop-Up Spray Heads

Pop-up heads retract below the lawn surface when the system isn't running — they're the standard for residential lawns because they're out of the way for mowing. Standard pop-up height is 100 mm (4-inch pop-up) which is adequate for most home lawns. Taller pop-ups are available where the grass is regularly mown at a higher height.

Nozzles are interchangeable and come in fixed-arc options (90°, 180°, 270°, 360°) to match corner, edge, and full-circle positions. Matched precipitation-rate nozzles (available in the same arc options) are worth using — they apply water at the same rate per hour regardless of the arc, so a corner head and a full-circle head on the same zone apply the same depth in the same time.

## Rotary Heads (Hunter PGP and Similar)

Rotary heads use a spinning rotor rather than a fixed spray. Throw distances of 8 to 15+ metres compared to spray heads' 3 to 5 metre throw. Lower application rate per minute (water applied more slowly, allowing better absorption). Better for large open lawns where many spray heads would be needed.

The Hunter PGP is the industry standard in NZ residential and light commercial use. Adjustable arc, adjustable throw, durable. Replaceable nozzles for different output rates.

Not suitable for small lawns — the minimum throw distance is too great for tight spaces.

## Installing Before Sowing

If you're building a new lawn from scratch, install the irrigation-general system before preparing the soil and sowing seed. The pipe trenching disrupts an established lawn; doing it before sowing avoids that problem entirely. Plan the head layout first, trench the laterals, install the heads at finished grade, then prepare the soil around them and sow.
