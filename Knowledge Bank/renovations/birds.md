# Birds and Your Lawn

Bird damage comes in two forms, and they need different responses. Understanding which one you're dealing with saves a lot of wasted effort.

## Seed-eating birds on a new lawn

Sparrows and finches are the main culprits. They'll hoover up freshly sown grass seed within days of sowing. A properly-sown area can lose a significant chunk of its seed rate if you don't take precautions.

The most effective protection is simply burying the seed. Rake lightly after sowing to get it into the top 5 to 10 mm of soil — seed sitting on the surface is bird food, seed in the soil is largely inaccessible. Rolling after sowing improves seed-to-soil contact and does the same job.

For new lawns on open flat areas where birds are persistent, lay hessian or shade cloth over the sown area and weight it down at the edges. It keeps birds off, holds surface moisture, and improves germination. Remove it once seedlings are 30 to 50 mm.

You can also increase the sowing rate slightly to account for bird losses. Sowing 4 kg per 100m² instead of 3 kg provides some buffer against the inevitable.

## Birds digging up an established lawn

This is a different problem entirely. Blackbirds and starlings methodically working a lawn, flipping small chunks of turf, are hunting grubs. Magpies do the same thing but leave more dramatic damage — larger sections, more disruption. Pukeko on lifestyle blocks can tear up substantial areas going after pasture .

Bird damage to established lawn is one of the most reliable early warning signs of a soil pest problem. The birds are showing you exactly where the grubs are concentrated before the lawn visibly declines from root damage.

The diagnostic step is simple: lift a 30 cm square of turf in the affected area. Cream-coloured C-shaped grubs about 15 to 20 mm are grass grub or black beetle larvae. Brown segmented caterpillars are porina or armyworm. More than 15 grubs per 0.1 m² (roughly a dinner-plate-sized square) warrants treatment.

NZLA Grub+ (chlorantraniliprole 200 g/L) covers grass grub, black beetle, billbugs, porina and caterpillars. A single application gives up to 6 months control. Apply and then irrigate 6 mm immediately to move the product down into the soil where the larvae are living.

Once the pest population is treated, the birds move on. There's nothing to hunt.

If you dig up the turf and grub counts are low, the bird activity may be opportunistic — the birds are investigating rather than finding significant prey. Usually self-limiting, and within a week or two they've moved to better hunting grounds.

## What doesn't work

Trying to deter birds while leaving an active grub population is like treating the smoke and ignoring the fire. Scarecrows, reflective tape, fake predators — none of them address why the birds are there. Get the pest situation right and the birds manage themselves.

Birds are also protected wildlife in NZ. Trapping or poisoning is not an option.

## The bigger picture

A healthy, thick, well-fed lawn at the right mowing height resists both forms of bird pressure — there's no exposed seed to eat, and the dense root mat makes it harder for birds to break up the surface. Most established healthy lawns have some bird activity without significant damage. Problems tend to develop either during establishment (seed exposure) or when pest populations build to a level worth hunting.
