# New Lawns

## Best time to start

Autumn (March through April) for most of NZ. Spring (September through October) is the second window.

Autumn works well for several reasons at once. Soil temperatures are still warm enough for fast cool-season germination (12 to 18°C in most regions). Air temperatures are mild — no heat stress on seedlings. Rainfall picks up naturally, reducing irrigation-general load. Weed pressure is lower than spring because summer annual  have finished their cycle and winter  haven't fired up yet. The lawn establishes roots before winter, then hits spring with a developed root system ready to go.

Spring is a solid second option. Soil temperatures climb reliably through September and October. The lawn has a long growing season ahead. The issue is that spring-sown lawns face summer heat while still young (under 4 months), and weed pressure during establishment is heavier than in autumn.

Avoid mid-summer (December through February) and mid-winter (June through August). Summer heat kills new seedlings without serious irrigation-general commitment. Winter soil temperatures are below the germination threshold for most species and growth is too slow for reliable establishment.

For warm-season grass (kikuyu, couch): the timing shifts toward warmer months. These species need soil temperatures above 15°C minimum, ideally above 18°C, to germinate. October through early February for most of the country.

Regional adjustments: Auckland and the upper North Island have wider windows — earlier spring from early September, and autumn extending into May. Canterbury, Wellington, and southern regions have tighter windows. Otago and Southland are tighter again. In colder regions, missing the window by a few weeks has real consequences.

## Starting from scratch

Five steps. The single biggest reason new lawns disappoint is rushing the soil preparation. Every hour spent on soil prep saves a year of frustration.

**1. Clear the area.** Remove existing vegetation. Glyphosate kill (two passes, 2 weeks apart) for established lawns and . Wait for full kill before proceeding.

**2. Dig over the top 100 to 150 mm.** Rotary hoe for larger areas, fork or spade for smaller. This depth matters — shallow prep produces shallow-rooted lawns that struggle in summer. For clay soils, mix in sand at this stage. For sandy soils, mix in organic matter.

**3. Remove rocks, debris and old roots.** Anything larger than 25 mm. Buried debris creates dead spots. Rocks scalp under the mower.

**4. Level the surface.** Address dips and bumps. A long board dragged across should show a uniform surface without major variation. Take extra time here — it's almost impossible to fix levelling issues in an established lawn without starting over.

**5. Firm the soil.** Walk the area systematically or roll with a lawn roller. The test: footprints should sink no more than 10 mm. Too deep means more firming needed. No visible impression means the soil is ready.

Apply NZLA Starter granular at 1.5 kg per 100m², raked lightly into the top 10 mm of soil.

Sow NZLA Perennial Rye (or species appropriate to the situation) at 3 to 4 kg per 100m² for ryegrass varieties. Rake the seed in lightly. Roll if you have a roller available. Water consistently for the first 2 to 3 weeks.

NZLA Liquid Starter as a foliar at 4 to 5 weeks. Transition to All Seasons granular at 8 to 12 weeks once the lawn is established.

## Laying ready lawn over an existing lawn

Don't. This is worth being direct about because the advice still circulates.

Ready lawn laid over existing grass fails for a simple physical reason: the new root mat sits on a layer of foliage between itself and the underlying soil. Roots can't grow through that intervening layer. The new turf doesn't anchor and dies within weeks.

The right process is kill the existing lawn first (two glyphosate passes), remove the dead material, prepare the soil properly, and lay the new turf over bare prepared ground. Takes longer but actually works.

The only exception is small patch repairs (under 1 m²) where you're filling a bare spot. That's patching, not over-laying — the ready lawn squares can integrate with the surrounding established lawn at the edges and root down into prepared soil underneath.

## How long until it looks good

From seed: the lawn looks like bare soil with green fuzz at 1 to 2 weeks. Thin but recognisably grass at 3 to 4 weeks. First mow at 4 to 6 weeks. Properly filling in at 8 to 12 weeks. Established and looking mature at 4 to 6 months. Indistinguishable from long-established lawn at 9 to 12 months.

From ready lawn: looks finished immediately. Fully established with fading transition lines at 1 to 3 months. Mature at 3 to 6 months.

The biggest factor affecting how quickly it looks good isn't the establishment method — it's the watering programme through the first 4 to 6 weeks, and the soil prep underneath. Lawns with good soil and consistent moisture look right much faster than lawns that were rushed or underwatered.

Most failures and disappointments in new lawn establishment come from pushing the lawn too hard too soon: heavy traffic, aggressive early mowing, big fertiliser hits on young seedlings. Establishment is mostly patience and consistent gentle care.
