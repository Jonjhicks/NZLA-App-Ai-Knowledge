# What is wash out and how do I prevent it?

Wash out is the loss of seed, soil, or fertiliser from a lawn area due to heavy rain or runoff carrying material away. Particularly affects new lawns, sloped areas, or recently fertilised lawns.

Three forms of wash out:

**Seed wash out.** Heavy rain immediately after sowing carries the seed downhill, into garden beds, onto paths, or into stormwater drains. Sowing fails in the original area; seed germinates inappropriately wherever it ended up.

**Soil wash out.** Topsoil eroding from sloped lawns or newly-prepared lawn surfaces. Particularly bad on freshly tilled ground without grass cover. Creates ruts, exposes subsoil, undermines the lawn's foundation.

**Fertiliser wash out.** Granular fertiliser washed off-target by heavy rain shortly after application. Wasted product, possible damage to garden beds or waterways, missed feeding for the lawn.

Why it matters:

Beyond the immediate loss, wash out creates uneven establishment patterns that persist for years. Patches without enough seed never thicken properly. Eroded soil zones support grass poorly.

Prevention strategies:

**1. Check weather forecast.** Don't sow seed, lay topdressing, or apply granular fertiliser when heavy rain is forecast within 24 to 48 hours. Standard practice.

**2. Rake seed in lightly.** Seed buried in the top 5 to 10 mm of soil is largely protected from wash out. Seed sitting on the surface is vulnerable.

**3. Roll after sowing.** Improves seed-to-soil contact and reduces wash-out risk. A small hand roller suits home lawns.

**4. Cover with hessian on slopes.** For new lawn establishment on any noticeable slope, hessian or jute matting weighted in place protects seed and soil from rain impact. Allows water and light through while breaking the energy of falling water and preventing material being carried away.

**5. Sow at the right time.** Spring and autumn sowing windows typically have more moderate rainfall than summer or winter. The chance of catastrophic heavy rain is lower.

**6. Address slope angle.** For lawns over about 15 percent grade (significant slope), wash out is an ongoing risk. Consider terracing, drainage interception, or alternative landscape solutions for very steep sites.

**7. Water in granular fertiliser promptly.** Granular applied and then exposed to heavy rain within hours can wash off. Within 24 hours, light irrigation to wash into the soil reduces wash-out risk.

**8. Don't apply on saturated soil.** Wet, saturated soil can't absorb additional water — anything you put on top is at risk of washing away.

**9. Establish ground cover quickly.** Once grass is established (even at seedling stage), it holds soil in place and reduces erosion. The vulnerable period is between sowing and establishment.

For established lawns:

Mature established lawns generally resist wash out well because the dense root system holds soil and the canopy breaks the energy of falling water.

The exceptions are:

Lawns on steep slopes where periodic erosion happens regardless.

Lawns with bare patches that are vulnerable until they fill in.

Lawns where heavy fertiliser application has just occurred.

For sloped lawns:

Choose grass species with strong rooting habits. NZLA RSR Ryegrass (stoloniferous, dense canopy) handles slopes better than typical bunch-type ryegrass.

Consider using NZLA Starter combined with starting at higher mowing heights to maximise canopy and root development.

For established lawns in flood-prone or low-lying areas:

Drainage interception (swales, French drains) reduces water volume reaching the lawn during heavy rain. Worth investing in for chronic flooding situations.

NZLA Penetrate helps water move down through the profile, reducing surface flooding duration.
