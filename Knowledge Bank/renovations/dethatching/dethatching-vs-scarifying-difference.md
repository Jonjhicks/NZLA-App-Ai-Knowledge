# What is the difference between dethatching and scarifying?

Mostly the same thing in NZ usage. Both terms describe removing thatch from a lawn using a tool with vertical blades that cuts into the surface.

The technical distinction:

**Dethatching** is the goal — removing the brown organic layer (thatch) that builds up between the grass and the soil.

**Scarifying** is the method — using a powered tool with vertical rotating blades that cuts through the thatch and pulls dead material to the surface where it can be raked out.

So you scarify TO dethatch. Same outcome, different focus in the terminology.

In practice:

Most NZ lawn care professionals use both terms interchangeably. "Scarify the lawn" and "dethatch the lawn" describe the same operation.

The tool is called a scarifier (sometimes vertical mower or verti-cutter, more often than dethatcher in NZ). The action is scarifying. The result is dethatching.

If there's any common distinction:

"Dethatching" sometimes refers specifically to lighter raking work (manual or with a fan rake) to remove surface debris and thin thatch layers.

"Scarifying" tends to imply the more aggressive powered intervention with a proper scarifier.

But this distinction isn't universal and you'll see both terms used both ways across NZ lawn care.

What matters for your lawn:

The thatch level (under 15 mm fine; 15 to 25 mm needs intervention; over 25 mm is severe).

The right tool for the level of thatch you have. Light thatch — fan rake or light power scarify. Moderate thatch — proper powered scarifier set to depth of 10 to 20 mm into the thatch. Heavy thatch — aggressive scarifying or full renovation.

The right timing. Spring (September-October) or autumn (March-April) when growth is active enough to recover.

The recovery support after. NZLA Starter granular, Liquid Starter foliar at 4 to 5 weeks, oversowing if the scarifying left significant bare areas.

Whether the work is called dethatching or scarifying doesn't matter for outcome. The work that needs doing, the timing, and the recovery support are what matters.

Related terms you may hear:

**Verti-cutting** — vertical cutting with a different style of vertical-blade tool, sometimes shallower than full scarifying. Used on golf greens and fine turf.

**Slit seeding** — overseeding using a scarifier-style tool that cuts slots and drops seed into them simultaneously. Combines scarifying with seeding in one operation.

**Vertical mowing** — same as scarifying or verti-cutting depending on regional terminology.

All variations on the same theme — vertical blades into the lawn surface for thatch removal, seeding preparation, or both.
