# Should I topdress after core aeration?

Yes if you want to gradually amend the soil profile. The holes from aeration are an ideal opportunity to introduce sand, sandy loam, or compost into the root zone where it can actually do some good.

What topdressing after aeration achieves:

**Soil profile amendment.** New material fills the holes and gradually mixes with the existing soil as the cores break down. Over multiple aeration-plus-topdress cycles, the surface soil profile shifts toward whatever the topdressing material provides — sand for better drainage on clay, organic matter for better structure on sand.

**Smoothing.** Fills minor surface dips and bumps. Several rounds of topdressing across a season can noticeably level a slightly uneven lawn.

**Soil-grass contact.** Cores leave open holes that birds, weeds and pests can exploit. Topdressing fills the holes properly so the lawn returns to a uniform surface faster.

**Drainage improvement.** Sand topdressing in particular opens drainage channels in clay-heavy soils over time.

What to use:

**For drainage improvement on clay soils:** straight sand. Coarse builder's sand or specialised turf sand. The sand moves down the holes and into the upper soil profile, gradually creating a more open soil structure.

**For levelling and general soil amendment:** sandy loam mix (typically 70% sand, 30% loam). Levels well, doesn't smother the existing grass, gradually improves the profile.

**For low-organic-matter soils:** compost or organic-rich topsoil. Adds biological function to depleted soils. Source from reputable suppliers — cheap compost often contains weed seeds.

**For sand-based root zones (golf greens, sports turf):** straight sand of the specification matching the existing profile. Don't introduce different specification sand or organic material — would create layers in the profile.

How much:

5 mm or less of topdressing material across the lawn after aeration. Work it into the canopy with a stiff brush or drag mat. Heavy topdressing (more than 5 mm) smothers the existing grass and creates more problems than it solves.

When NOT to topdress after aeration:

If the soil is already sandy and well-drained — the holes will close naturally without intervention.

If you're not committed to following through. Topdressing without proper working-in leaves uneven surface that mowing reveals.

If the topdressing material is wet enough to clump rather than spread cleanly.

Recovery support:

NZLA Starter granular before or during topdressing supports the recovery. Light watering to settle the topdressing into the holes. Hold off mowing until the topdressing has settled.

Multiple rounds across a season:

A single topdressing after aeration is modest soil amendment. Multiple topdressings across a year (after each aeration in a multi-aeration programme, or spaced through a growing season) compound the effect meaningfully. Over 2 to 3 years of consistent aeration plus topdressing, even severely compacted clay lawns can be transformed.
