# How often should I core aerate my lawn?

Depends on the soil and the use level.

**Light home lawn use on well-drained soil:** every 2 to 3 years. Light traffic, sandy or loam soil, no obvious compaction signs. The lawn doesn't need annual aeration to stay healthy.

**Standard home lawn on average soil:** every 18 to 24 months. Some compaction builds up but slowly. Aerating once every couple of years keeps it ahead of accumulating compaction.

**Heavy use lawn (kids and dogs):** annually. Foot traffic compacts soil progressively. Annual core aeration keeps the soil open and the lawn healthy under the use.

**Clay or compacted soils:** annually or twice a year. Heavy clay soils naturally compact and need regular intervention to keep them workable. Spring and autumn aeration on serious clay lawns is common.

**Sports turf or commercial properties:** twice a year minimum. Often more, depending on use intensity. Sports fields and golf courses run on aeration programmes that can include multiple shallow aerations per season plus annual deeper work.

**Sand-based root zones (sports turf, golf greens):** less often than clay lawns but still routine. The sand profile compacts differently and aeration is mainly for thatch and surface compaction management.

How to tell if you need it:

Push a screwdriver or thin probe into the soil. If it goes in easily to 50 mm, the soil is fine. If it takes real force to push past 30 mm, compaction is restricting root growth and aeration is overdue.

Water test: pour a bucket of water onto the lawn. If it absorbs within 30 seconds, the soil is open. If it pools and runs off, compaction is restricting infiltration.

Lift a square spadeful of turf and look at the soil profile. Roots should reach 75 to 100 mm down. Shallow root systems indicate compacted soil restricting growth.

When to aerate:

Spring (September through November) or autumn (March through April) when growth is active enough to recover from the damage within 4 to 6 weeks. Don't aerate in summer heat or winter dormancy.

After aeration:

NZLA Starter for recovery support. NZLA Aerate (soil-amending polymer) tank-mixed with NZLA Penetrate fills the holes with chemistry that improves the soil structure beyond what the holes alone deliver. Topdress with sand or sandy loam to gradually amend the soil profile.

Frequency adjustment over time:

If annual aeration starts producing little visible benefit (soil already open, drainage already good), you can extend to every 18 months or longer. If annual aeration isn't keeping up with compaction (lawn still struggling), increase frequency or address structural issues.
