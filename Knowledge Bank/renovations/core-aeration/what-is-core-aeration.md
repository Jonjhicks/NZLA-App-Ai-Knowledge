# What is core aeration and do I need to do it?

Core aeration is using a machine with hollow tines that punch into the lawn and pull small plugs (cores) of soil and grass out. The plugs come out as you walk, leaving a pattern of holes about 15 mm in diameter and 75 to 100 mm deep, spaced 50 to 100 mm apart.

The job is opening up the soil profile. Compaction is the main target — soils that have been walked or driven on, sand bases that have settled, or clay soils that have packed tight. The holes let air, water, fertiliser and root growth penetrate the profile. The cores left on the surface break down over a couple of weeks and add organic matter back to the surface.

Do you need it? Depends on the soil.

**Yes** if the soil is compacted (push a screwdriver in — if it takes real force to push past 50 mm, you've got compaction), if water pools on the surface after rain rather than soaking in, if the lawn struggles despite proper fertility and watering, or if the lawn is on heavy clay or has had heavy traffic.

**No** if the soil is open and friable, water absorbs quickly, the lawn is growing well, or you've recently aerated within the last 18 months.

Frequency: most lawns benefit from core aeration every 2 to 3 years if compaction is moderate, or annually if compaction is severe.

When: spring or autumn when growth is active enough to fill the holes within 4 to 6 weeks. Don't core in summer (heat stress) or winter (slow recovery).

After coring: apply NZLA Starter to support recovery, water in. On heavy clay, NZLA Aerate (soil-amending polymer) tank-mixed with NZLA Penetrate fills the holes with chemistry that improves the soil structure beyond what the holes alone deliver.

Topdressing with sand or sandy loam after coring gradually amends the soil profile.
