# Establishment Decisions

## Check drainage-and-soil-moisture before you start

A lawn over waterlogged soil will underperform forever. The time to find out is before the establishment work, not after.

Dig a 300 mm test hole in the lowest and wettest part of the proposed lawn area. Fill it with water and time how long it takes to drain.

Under 4 hours: good drainage-and-soil-moisture. No intervention needed.

4 to 12 hours: moderate. Surface improvements (levelling, aeration) will help but the lawn will be workable.

Over 24 hours: poor drainage-and-soil-moisture. Installed drainage-and-soil-moisture is worth considering before you commit to a lawn.

Sites that genuinely need drainage-and-soil-moisture work first: standing water visible after rain for more than 24 hours, lawn area at the bottom of a slope with no natural exit for water, heavy clay subsoil with no permeability, or a previous lawn at the site that failed from waterlogging. Drainage installation is expensive (slotted pipe in gravel trenches runs $30 to $80 per linear metre), but it lasts decades. It's a better investment than years of struggling with a wet lawn.

For lifestyle blocks on poorly drained land, a tractor-mounted mole plough creates underground drainage-and-soil-moisture channels — a proven farm technique that applies well to larger lawn areas.

## Seed vs ready lawn

The cost difference is substantial. Seeding a lawn runs roughly $3 to $5 per m² all-in (seed, NZLA Starter, Liquid Starter, soil prep materials). Ready lawn costs $10 to $18 per m² installed, with the material alone at $15 to $25 per m² before delivery and labour.

That's a 5 to 10x cost difference on material. For a 300 m² lawn, the gap is $10,000 or more.

Ready lawn makes sense when: the area is small (under 50 m²), a quick result is genuinely needed (selling the house in 6 weeks), or the owner doesn't have the time or inclination to manage a 6 to 8 week establishment period.

Seed makes sense when: the area is medium to large, cost is a consideration, or you want a specific species that ready lawn growers don't stock (MDR, RSR Ryegrass, fine fescue, tall fescue blends). Seeding also gives full control of soil preparation before sowing, which matters when you're working from a compromised base.

For most NZ home lawn projects over 100 m², seed gives far better value if the establishment work is done properly.

## Removing an existing lawn

Glyphosate kill is the standard approach for most situations. Mow short, water 2 to 3 days before application, apply at label rate to actively growing turf. Wait 10 to 14 days for full kill, then a second pass if any green remains. Rake out dead material, cultivate, prep for new establishment.

The two-pass approach handles persistent  (dock, Californian thistle, kikuyu) that survive a single application. Don't save money by skipping the second pass —  growing back through a new lawn are harder to deal with than doing the kill properly.

For smaller areas (under 100 m²) where you want to avoid chemistry: hire a sod cutter, which slices under the turf at root depth. Roll up and remove the sod. More physical work and it doesn't address weed seeds in the soil, but it's faster than waiting for a chemical kill. Smothering with black plastic or cardboard (6 to 12 weeks) is also an option for non-urgent small areas.

## Paddock to lawn conversion

Paddock soils arrive with specific problems: core-aeration from livestock, variable fertility, a weed seed bank from years of pasture, and often uneven surfaces from stock movement. Two glyphosate applications 2 to 3 weeks apart are usually needed — one pass doesn't kill deep-rooted dock and thistle.

After kill, mow the dead vegetation low and rake out heavy thatch. Rotary hoe to 100 to 150 mm, cross-cultivate on heavy clay, then level with a tractor blade or harrow. A light topdressing of quality topsoil on the surface 50 mm gives a better seedbed than cultivated paddock soil alone.

A soil test before re-establishing is worthwhile on paddock conversions. Stock grazing concentrates fertility in some areas and strips it in others. pH can vary across the site depending on land use history. Knowing what you're working with costs $40 to $80 and removes the guesswork on lime and fertiliser inputs.

Apply NZLA Starter at 1.5 kg per 100m² before sowing. For large areas, NZLA Humic+ supports developing soil biology — the microbial function in cultivated paddock soil needs time to rebuild. NZLA Liquid Starter through the establishment phase.

For lifestyle block lawns over 1000 m², professional preparation with farm equipment is more practical than DIY. The principles are the same; the scale changes the economics.

Realistic timeline on a paddock conversion: kill plus preparation takes 4 to 6 weeks, establishment to first mow another 6 to 8 weeks, full fill-in 6 to 12 months.

## New build sites

Post-construction sites present predictable challenges. Topsoil is stripped during the build. Subsoil is compacted by heavy machinery. Building debris gets buried. Cement and plaster washings affect soil pH. Variable soil depth across the site.

Dig a 300 mm test hole in several spots around the lawn area before starting. Check topsoil depth, subsoil core-aeration, buried debris, and any obvious contamination.

If topsoil was stripped: import quality screened topsoil to at least 150 mm depth. Cheap topsoil from unknown sources brings weed seeds and contamination. Budget $30 to $60 per m³ delivered, plus spreading. Don't shortcut this — thin topsoil produces a thin lawn that declines over years.

Break up the compacted subsoil before adding topsoil. Without cultivating through the compacted layer, water won't drain and roots won't develop past the topsoil.

Remove any buried concrete, brick, or timber before laying new soil. These create dead spots in the finished lawn that persist indefinitely.

Cement and plaster residue creates high-pH zones. Apply sulphur to affected spots before establishing. NZLA Aerate during establishment supports soil structure on borderline soils. NZLA Humic+ helps rebuild biological activity in new topsoil that may have low microbial function.

Resist the urge to establish too quickly after a new build. The site needs to settle, particularly if significant earthworks or fill work was done. Most NZ new home owners establish lawn 3 to 12 months after move-in. Getting the preparation right is worth the wait.
