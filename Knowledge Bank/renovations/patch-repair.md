# Patch Repair

## The rule that matters most

Fix the cause before you fix the patch. Bare patches don't appear randomly. If you reseed without addressing why the area went bare, the patch comes back. Figuring that out first saves the time and money of repeated repairs.

Wear from foot traffic, pets, or toys: the traffic route needs changing, or the area needs a more wear-resistant species.

Dog urine: the nitrogen and salt concentration is the problem (see below).

Disease damage (Fusarium, dollar spot): the  conditions need addressing before reseeding into the same environment.

Grub damage: treat with NZLA Grub+ before seeding, otherwise the grubs eat the new seedlings' roots.

Excess shade: standard ryegrass struggles in deep shade. Patch reseeding with fine fescue (more shade tolerant) changes the outcome.

Compaction: fork or aerate the patch before seeding. Compacted soil kills new seedlings from the same cause.

Pre-emergent residual: Blockade applied within the last 4 to 6 months will kill new seedlings. Wait out the residual.

## Standard patch repair process

Rake or scrape out dead material from the bare patch. Loosen the top 20 to 30 mm of soil — fresh soil contact is what new seed needs.

Apply NZLA Starter granular at 1.5 kg per 100m² across the patch. Rake lightly into the top 10 mm.

Sow seed matching the surrounding lawn at 3 to 4 kg per 100m² for ryegrass varieties. Rake in lightly.

Water consistently for 10 to 14 days until seedlings establish. Keep the surface moist — don't let it dry out for even a single day during this window. Cover larger patches with hessian to retain moisture in full sun.

NZLA Liquid Starter as a foliar 4 to 5 weeks after sowing.

Don't mow the patch until the new grass reaches 60 to 80 mm. Mow around it if needed.

## Dog urine patches

Dog urine kills grass through concentrated nitrogen and salt burn. The bright green ring around spots (where dilute urine has spread) actually shows the nitrogen fertiliser effect. The dead centre is the salt damage from concentrated urine.

Fix the damage with two steps: flush the salt out, then re-establish.

Flush first. Pour a full bucket of water over the spot. For fresh spots (urine within the last few hours), heavy watering can prevent grass death entirely by diluting and washing the salt down. For established dead patches, deep watering helps move residual salt out of the root zone.

Then repair the patch using the standard process above. NZLA RSR Ryegrass is a good choice for dog-spot-prone areas — the stoloniferous habit means it actively repairs damage and spreads to fill gaps.

Prevention is more effective than repair. Train the dog to a designated area, or flush immediately after every urine event. Consistent flushing prevents salt buildup and largely eliminates new spots. Dietary supplements that reduce urine nitrogen are available from vets and have shown some effectiveness.

If dogs routinely use the lawn without management, expect ongoing repairs. A kikuyu lawn (warm climates) handles urine damage significantly better than ryegrass.

## Why repairs keep failing

If patch repairs are failing repeatedly in the same spots, one of these is usually the cause:

The underlying cause is still active. Same dog same spot, same wear, same .

Inadequate soil prep. Seed sown onto unprepared ground without soil contact produces weak patchy establishment.

Watering failure. The number-one patch repair failure mode. Seedlings need consistent surface moisture for 10 to 14 days. A single dry-out kills emerging seedlings.

Wrong seed for the conditions. Ryegrass in deep shade where fine fescue belongs. The wrong species simply can't establish where the rest of the lawn struggled.

Pre-emergent residual. Blockade within the last 4 to 6 months is a silent seedling killer.

Diagnostic approach for a repeating failure: if the seed germinated but seedlings died young, watering or pre-em residual. If nothing germinated at all, soil contact was wrong or seed was killed by chemical residue or birds.
