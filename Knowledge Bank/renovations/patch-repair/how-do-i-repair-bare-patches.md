# How do I repair bare patches in my lawn?

Five-step process for each patch.

**Identify the cause first.** Bare patches don't appear by accident. Wear (foot traffic, kids, dogs), pet urine (dog spots), disease damage (Fusarium, dollar spot patches), grub damage, drought, or excess shade. If you don't fix the cause, the patch comes back. Pet urine spots need flushing with water before reseeding. Disease patches need the disease addressed. Shade patches need the species changed (fine fescue tolerates shade better than ryegrass).

**Prep the soil.** Rake out the dead material and loosen the top 20 to 30 mm of soil in the patch. Fresh soil contact is what new seed needs to germinate. If the soil is compacted, fork it through to open it up.

**Apply NZLA Starter** at 1.5 kg per 100m² across the patch area. Rake it lightly into the top 10 mm of soil.

**Sow the seed.** Match the species to the surrounding lawn. NZLA Perennial Rye for general home lawn use. For ryegrass at typical home lawn rates, 3 to 4 kg per 100m². Rake in lightly so the seed sits in the top 5 to 10 mm of soil.

**Water consistently.** Keep the surface moist for 10 to 14 days until seedlings are up and growing. Light frequent watering through this window. Cover larger patches with hessian or sacking to retain moisture if the patch is in full sun.

Hold off mowing the patch until the new grass is 60 to 80 mm. Mow around it if needed.

Liquid Starter as a foliar 4 to 5 weeks after sowing supports the new seedlings.

Timing matters. Autumn and spring are the best windows. Summer patches will struggle without consistent irrigation. Winter patches won't fill in until growth returns.

Don't apply pre-emergent within 4 to 6 weeks of the patch repair — it kills the new seed.
