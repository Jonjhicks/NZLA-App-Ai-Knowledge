# How do I repair dog urine spots?

Two-step process: flush the salt out, then re-establish the patch.

Why dog urine kills grass:

Dog urine contains concentrated nitrogen and salts. Diluted across a wide area, the nitrogen would actually fertilise grass. Concentrated in a small spot, it scorches the grass like over-applying fast-release fertiliser. The bright green ring around dog spots (where dilute urine has spread) shows the nitrogen-fertiliser effect; the dead centre shows the concentrated salt-burn effect.

**Step 1 — Flush the salt out.**

Pour a bucket of water over the dog spot as soon as possible after the urine event. The water dilutes and washes the salt down into the soil where it disperses harmlessly. Done promptly, this can prevent grass death entirely.

For established dead patches where the urine event happened days or weeks ago, deep watering still helps move residual salt out of the root zone. 10 to 15 mm of water over the patch flushes residual salt down through the profile.

**Step 2 — Repair the patch.**

Rake out the dead grass and loosen the top 20 mm of soil.

Apply NZLA Starter granular at 1.5 kg per 100m² to the patch area. Rake in.

Sow seed matching the surrounding lawn. NZLA Perennial Rye for general home lawns. RSR Ryegrass if the patch is in a high-wear area where the stoloniferous habit helps with future damage recovery. 3 to 4 kg per 100m² sowing rate.

Rake lightly to get seed into the top 5 to 10 mm of soil.

Water consistently for 10 to 14 days. Light frequent watering.

NZLA Liquid Starter foliar at 4 to 5 weeks.

**Prevention going forward.**

Train the dog to use a designated area. Some owners create a small gravel or mulch area where the dog learns to go. Most effective long-term solution.

Flush every urine event with water immediately if you can — even just emptying a watering can over the spot within minutes prevents salt buildup.

Dietary supplements that reduce urine nitrogen content are available from vets. Some effectiveness reported but not universal. Worth investigating if dog spot management is a chronic problem.

Maintain healthy fertilised lawn overall. Well-fed lawns absorb minor urine events better than underfed weak lawns.

Choose grass species that recover better. RSR Ryegrass (stoloniferous habit) repairs wear and damage actively. Kikuyu (in warm climates) is highly resistant to urine damage.

Realistic expectations:

If the dog routinely uses the lawn for urine without management, dog spots will recur in similar locations. Patch repair becomes ongoing. Either change the dog's routine or accept the recurring repairs.

Mature established lawns tolerate the occasional dog event without significant damage. New lawns or lawns under other stress show urine damage more dramatically.

For households with multiple dogs or a dog regularly using the lawn, the dietary supplement plus consistent flushing plus annual patch repair is the typical management pattern. Permanent dog-resistant lawns aren't really achievable through chemistry — they're achieved through routing the dog away from the lawn.
