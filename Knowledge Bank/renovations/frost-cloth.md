# Frost Cloth

## What it is

A lightweight breathable fabric — typically 30 to 50 g/m² polypropylene — used to protect new or vulnerable lawn areas from frost damage. Sometimes called horticultural fleece or row cover.

It works by trapping a layer of warmer air against the soil surface and preventing direct frost contact with the foliage. It doesn't make the area warm — it keeps the microclimate 2 to 4°C above ambient at soil level. That margin is enough to prevent frost damage on most NZ overnight frosts.

Standard polypropylene frost cloth runs $10 to $30 per m² at garden centres, hardware stores, and rural supply outlets. It lasts 3 to 5 seasons before UV degradation becomes a problem. Treated cloth lasts longer. Store dry and out of UV when not in use.

## When to use it

Established mature lawns almost never need frost cloth. The plants handle most NZ frost without intervention and recover quickly from any discolouration.

Where frost cloth earns its place:

New lawns sown in autumn through winter. Seedlings establishing in cool conditions are vulnerable during the first few weeks. Cover the area if heavy frost is forecast until the plants are robust enough to handle it — usually after the first 2 to 3 mows.

Newly laid ready lawn in frost-prone regions. Fresh turf with new roots that haven't fully integrated into the soil profile can suffer frost damage to the root mat. Cover during the first few weeks if heavy frost is forecast.

Recently overseeded patches in existing lawns. New seedlings emerging through established turf in autumn are particularly vulnerable.

Post-renovation recovery. A recently scarified or aerated lawn going into a cold spell is more vulnerable than an undisturbed established lawn.

## How to use it

Apply in the evening before a forecast frost. Drape over the area to be protected and weight down the edges with stones or pegs to stop wind dislodging it.

Remove in the morning once frost has lifted from the surrounding area — usually once you can see frost has cleared off nearby surfaces. For a single cold night, that's 12 to 16 hours of coverage.

For extended cold spells, leave the cloth in place for the duration rather than removing and replacing daily.

## What not to do

Don't leave frost cloth on continuously for weeks. The lawn weakens from light deprivation faster than it would from intermittent frost exposure. Light is more important than frost protection for establishing grass. Remove on mild days to allow direct light.

Don't leave it on during warm sunny days — trapped heat on a sunny afternoon can scorch the lawn. Remove during daytime when temperatures climb above about 15°C.

Don't leave it wet against the lawn for extended periods. Damp cloth held against the surface creates ideal conditions for fungal . Let the lawn dry between uses.

## Decision rules for typical situations

Heavy frost forecast on an established lawn: don't bother. Mature grass handles it.

Light frost on a new lawn under 6 weeks old: cover for the night.

Heavy frost on a new lawn under 6 weeks old: cover and consider extending coverage if a cold spell is forecast.

Auckland and upper North Island: frost cloth is rarely needed. Most winter overnight temperatures don't reach damaging levels for establishing grass.

Canterbury, Otago, Southland: worth having on hand for new establishment during winter months. Frosts in these regions can be damaging to young seedlings.
