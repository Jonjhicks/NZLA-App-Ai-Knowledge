# Dethatching and Scarifying

## What's the difference?

Mostly the same thing. In NZ usage, dethatching and scarifiers describe the same operation — removing the layer of dead organic matter (thatch) that builds up between the grass and the soil. You scarify in order to dethatch. The terms are used interchangeably on the tools, by most contractors, and in most lawn care guides.

If there's a practical distinction, "dethatching" sometimes refers to lighter raking (manual or fan rake) on thin thatch layers. "Scarifying" implies the powered tool: a machine with vertical rotating blades that cuts through the thatch and pulls dead material to the surface for removal. Either way, the goal is the same.

You'll also hear "verti-cutting" and "vertical mowing" for related fine-turf operations on golf greens and sports surfaces. Same principle, different tool specification.

## How much thatch is too much?

The depth test is the quickest answer. Cut a small wedge out of the lawn — about 100 mm wide and 100 mm deep — with a sharp spade or knife. Look at the cross-section. Green grass at the top, brown layer between the grass and the soil (thatch), then the soil. Measure the brown layer.

Under 10 mm: healthy. Leave it alone.

10 to 15 mm: borderline. Watch it over the next season.

15 to 25 mm: intervention needed. Plan a scarify.

Over 25 mm: severe. Aggressive scarifiers or full renovation.

The spongy underfoot test is less precise but useful for a quick check. A lawn that bounces like a trampoline when you walk on it has significant thatch building. A lawn that gives slightly but feels firm is healthy.

If water pools on the surface after rain and takes more than a minute to absorb, or if your lawn responds weakly to fertiliser that should produce visible greening within a week, thatch is likely the cause in both cases — it's blocking the passage of water and nutrients down to the root zone.

Heavy thatch also creates habitat for fungal . Persistent Fusarium, moss, and other  pressure often correlates with thatch over 15 to 20 mm. The moisture held against the crown is the problem.

## Timing

Spring (late September through October) or autumn (March through April) for cool-season lawns. Those are the windows when soil temperatures support fast recovery from the mechanical damage scarifiers does.

For warm-season lawns (kikuyu, couch), scarify when those species are growing hardest — late spring through summer.

Don't scarify in mid-summer heat. The lawn is already under stress and the combination is too much. Don't scarify in winter — growth is too slow to fill the bare patches within a reasonable time. A heavy scarify in August can leave a lawn looking rough until well into spring.

If you want a presentable lawn for an event or sale, plan the timing with recovery in mind. A serious scarify needs 4 to 6 weeks of recovery before the lawn looks clean again. Don't rush it.

## What to expect

The lawn looks rough immediately after scarifiers. Significant brown material on the surface, bare patches, uneven height. That's normal. The extent of the roughness reflects how much thatch was there to remove.

Light thatch removal (15 to 20 mm layer) — recovers in 2 to 3 weeks with minimal visible damage.

Heavy scarifiers (20 to 25+ mm layer) — looks like a renovation for 4 to 6 weeks. Oversowing bare areas is often needed.

## Depth adjustment

For moderate thatch (15 to 20 mm), set the scarifier to a depth that pulls material without scalping through to the soil. The blades should be cutting through the thatch layer, not digging into the root zone.

For severe thatch (over 25 mm), you may need multiple passes — one at a shallower setting, then a deeper pass once the upper material is removed. Trying to remove 25 mm of thatch in one pass often scalps the surface badly.
