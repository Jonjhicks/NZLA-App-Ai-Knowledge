# Seedling Care

## The first 6 weeks

Three priorities: water consistently, keep traffic off, support gently. Everything else is secondary.

## Watering

New seedlings have tiny root systems and dry out fast — the entire plant is millimetres above the soil surface and developing roots barely reach into the soil. A single dry-out event can kill seedlings within hours.

First 2 weeks: light, frequent watering. Two to three times a day in warm conditions, once a day in cool weather. Enough to wet the top 5 to 10 mm of soil without pooling.

Weeks 2 to 4: back off frequency, increase depth. Twice a day in warm conditions, once a day in cool. Going slightly deeper as roots develop.

Weeks 4 to 6: transition to twice-weekly deep watering as the lawn establishes properly.

Watch the seedlings. Wilting in late afternoon or edges drying out means increase frequency. Yellowing or waterlogged patches mean back off.

This watering is the most important thing. It's also the most common failure. Set a reminder if needed.

## Traffic

Keep off the new lawn for 4 to 6 weeks. Foot traffic on seedlings damages developing roots and crushes plants before they're robust enough. Mark routes around the lawn if the area connects to something people need to get to. Keep dogs off for at least 6 weeks.

## Feeding

NZLA Starter granular at sowing provides the establishment phosphorus and trace elements. Don't apply more granular fertiliser on top of new seedlings — risk of burn.

At 4 to 5 weeks, NZLA Liquid Starter as a foliar at 100 to 200 mL per 100m² supports the seedlings through the vulnerable transition period. It's gentler than maintenance fertilisers and appropriate for this stage.

Don't apply high-nitrogen fertilisers on seedlings under 6 weeks old.

## First mow

Wait until seedlings reach 60 to 80 mm height — typically 4 to 6 weeks after sowing depending on species and conditions.

Three tests to confirm readiness. Height: consistently 60 mm or above across the lawn. Density: looks like grass rather than scattered green fuzz — the canopy is reasonably continuous. The pull test: grip a tuft of seedlings and pull gently. If they pull out easily, roots aren't established enough. If they tear at the leaf level but stay rooted, you can mow.

Don't mow earlier even if impatient. Mowing seedlings under 60 mm damages the developing crown, removes too much leaf area for photosynthesis, and can pull un-rooted seedlings out of the soil entirely.

First mow specifics: highest cut setting that takes meaningful material off (if seedlings are 80 mm, target 55 mm). Sharp blade — tearing leaf on new seedlings sets recovery back by weeks. Catch clippings rather than mulching — clippings can smother young grass. Mow when dry. Mow slowly.

The lawn looks rough for a week after the first mow. Uneven height, some stress. Normal. Recovers within 7 to 10 days.

Wait 10 to 14 days before the second mow. Continue applying the third rule each time. By the third or fourth mow (8 to 12 weeks from sowing), the lawn handles normal maintenance.

If your target height is significantly lower than the 60 to 80 mm first mow height, bring it down progressively over 2 to 3 mows. Never try to drop from 80 mm to 30 mm in one cut on new seedlings.

## Why seedlings fail

**Drying out** is the most common cause. Seedlings wilt, go brown and crispy, often noticeable late afternoon after a hot day. Fix: consistent surface moisture for the full 4-week establishment window.

**Damping off** (fungal ) happens when over-watering combines with warm conditions. Seedlings collapse at soil level — healthy one day, dead at the base the next. Often in patches where moisture pools. Fix: reduce watering frequency to allow partial drying between events, improve reduce any mulch or cover thickness.

**Pre-emergent residual**: Blockade applied within the last 4 to 6 months silently kills new seedlings. Fix: wait the full residual period.

**Birds eating seed** before germination. Visible as bare patches where seed was sown, no seedlings emerging. Fix: rake or roll seed in during sowing, cover with hessian or netting.

**Wrong species for conditions**: seedlings that germinate but don't thrive, slow growth, easy to lose. Match species to the site — fine fescue for shade, ryegrass for sun and wear, tall fescue for drought tolerance.

**Heat stress** on summer establishment: even properly watered seedlings struggle above 28°C. Increase watering frequency and consider hessian shade if sowing in warm weather.

## Patience

New lawns don't look like established lawns until 6 to 12 months in. The plant population is younger, less developed, less dense. That's normal. Don't compare a 6-week-old lawn to a 6-year-old lawn.

Most early failures trace back to people pushing the lawn before it's ready.
