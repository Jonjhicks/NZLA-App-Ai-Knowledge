# Do I need to install drainage before a new lawn?

Depends on the site. Many lawns don't need installed drainage; some do.

Sites that need drainage installed:
- Standing water visible after rain for more than 24 hours.
- Soil profile saturated within 100 mm of surface in winter.
- Lawn area sits at the bottom of a slope with no exit for water.
- Heavy clay subsoil that water can't move through.
- High water table (groundwater within 300 mm of surface).
- Previous lawn at the site failed from waterlogging.

Sites that don't need drainage installed:
- Reasonable surface fall (1% or more) for water to flow away.
- Permeable subsoil that drains naturally.
- No history of standing water.
- Soil tests well-drained when you dig a test hole.

How to check:
- Dig a 300 mm hole in the wettest part of the lawn area.
- Fill the hole with water.
- Time how long it takes to drain.
- Under 4 hours = good drainage.
- 4 to 12 hours = moderate, lawn will be ok with surface improvements.
- Over 24 hours = poor drainage, intervention needed.

Drainage installation options:

Surface drainage:
- Re-grade the site so water flows to an exit point.
- Sometimes possible to fix bad situations with grading alone.
- Cheaper than subsoil drainage.

Subsoil drainage:
- Slotted pipe in gravel-filled trenches laid in a herringbone or grid pattern.
- Pipes connect to a stormwater outlet or soakage area.
- Spacing depends on soil and conditions (3 to 5 m typical for home lawn).
- Cost: $30 to $80 per linear metre installed.

Soakpit / soakage area:
- Underground pit filled with gravel that holds water and lets it slowly drain into surrounding soil.
- Suitable as an exit point for drainage where stormwater connection isn't available.

For lifestyle blocks:
- Subsurface drainage on poorly drained land transforms lawn potential.
- Tractor-mounted mole plough creates underground channels that drain water.
- Long-established farm technique applicable to lawn areas.

Cost-benefit:
- Drainage installation is expensive but lasts decades.
- A new lawn over waterlogged soil will fail or perform poorly forever without drainage.
- One-time investment beats years of struggling with a wet lawn.

Cheaper alternatives if drainage installation isn't viable:
- Choose wet-tolerant species (tall fescue handles intermittent wetness).
- Higher mowing height to protect roots.
- Reduce traffic on the wet area.
- Accept lower lawn quality in poorly drained areas.

For typical NZ home lawn establishment, most sites don't need drainage installation. For the sites that do, doing it before the lawn is established is much cheaper and easier than retrofitting later.
