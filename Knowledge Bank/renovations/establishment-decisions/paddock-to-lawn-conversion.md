# How do I convert paddock to lawn?

A common project on lifestyle blocks and rural-fringe properties. The paddock has pasture grasses, weeds, and bare patches. Converting it to lawn takes specific steps.

Step 1: Kill existing vegetation

Glyphosate application at label rate is the standard approach. Two applications 2 to 3 weeks apart handles deep-rooted weeds (Californian thistle, dock, others) that one application doesn't kill.

Step 2: Remove dead material

After kill, mow the dead vegetation low and rake out heavy thatch. Leaving it slows establishment of new seed.

Step 3: Soil assessment and preparation

Paddock soils often have:
- Compaction from livestock (sheep, cattle).
- Pugging damage in wet areas.
- Variable fertility (high in concentrate areas, low in grazed areas).
- Weed seed bank in the soil.
- pH varying based on land use history.

Soil test before establishing helps identify any specific issues (low pH, low phosphorus, etc.).

Cultivation:
- Rotary hoe or plough to break up the surface and incorporate dead material.
- Depth 100 to 150 mm typically.
- Cross-cultivate if soil is heavy.
- Stone pick after cultivation if rocky.

Step 4: Levelling

Paddock surfaces are usually uneven. Levelling with a tractor blade or harrow gets the surface acceptable for mowing. For premium lawn quality, more detailed levelling needed.

Top dressing with quality topsoil for the surface 50 mm gives a better seedbed than cultivated paddock soil alone.

Step 5: Apply NZLA Starter

NZLA Starter granular at label rate, incorporated into the top 25 to 50 mm.

Step 6: Sow

Cool-season blend (NZLA Perennial Rye plus tall fescue) for general use.
NZLA RSR Ryegrass for self-repairing function.
NZLA Ultimate Tall Fescue plus Elfin for drought tolerance on dry sites.

Sow at standard rates (25 to 35 g per square metre).

Step 7: Establishment care

- Water lightly and frequently.
- Apply NZLA Liquid Starter at 7 to 10 day intervals.
- First mow when seedlings are 70 to 80 mm.
- Address any weed flush from the soil seed bank.

Timing:
- Best to convert in autumn (March to May) so the lawn establishes through cool moist conditions.
- Spring conversion works but the lawn has to handle summer heat in its first year.
- Summer conversion not recommended in most NZ regions.

For large areas (lifestyle block lawns of 1000+ m²), professional preparation with farm equipment is more practical than DIY. The principles are the same but scale matters.

Realistic timeline:
- Kill plus cultivation plus prep: 4 to 6 weeks.
- Establishment to first mow: 6 to 8 weeks.
- Full establishment (lawn fully fills in): 6 to 12 months.

Common mistakes:
- Sowing into an inadequately killed paddock - weeds and old pasture grasses come back through.
- Skimping on soil prep - lumpy uneven surface that's hard to mow.
- Sowing in wrong season - poor establishment, weak first year.
- Under-rate fertiliser - lawn struggles for first 12 months.
