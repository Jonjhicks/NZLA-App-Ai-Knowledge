# What is the difference between seeding and overseeding?

Both involve sowing grass seed, but different purposes and approaches.

**Seeding** is establishing a new lawn from scratch. Sown over prepared bare soil. No existing grass to compete with. The full plant population establishes from the new seed.

**Overseeding** is sowing seed into an existing lawn to thicken it or introduce additional species. The existing lawn stays; the new seed fills in gaps and adds to the population.

The key differences:

**Surface preparation.**

Seeding requires full soil prep — rotary hoeing or forking the top 100 to 150 mm, removing rocks, leveling, applying starter fertiliser, raking smooth.

Overseeding works on the existing lawn surface. The lawn is mowed short, lightly scarified or raked to expose soil between plants, starter fertiliser applied, seed sown over the top, lightly raked in.

**Sowing rate.**

Seeding for a new lawn: 3 to 4 kg per 100m² for ryegrass varieties. Full population establishment.

Overseeding: 2 kg per 100m² for ryegrass varieties. Filling gaps rather than starting from scratch.

**Establishment competition.**

Seeded lawns have no competition. All resources (light, moisture, nutrients) available to the new seedlings.

Overseeded lawns have the existing grass competing with the new seedlings for resources. Establishment is harder. Some seedlings fail to establish because larger surrounding plants out-compete them.

**Timeline to result.**

Seeded lawn looks like a proper lawn around 8 to 12 weeks from sowing. Bare ground to grass.

Overseeded lawn looks improved within 4 to 6 weeks (denser canopy, less visible thin spots). The existing lawn already provides the basic appearance; overseeding fills it out.

**Failure modes.**

Seeded lawn failure: usually water-related (drying out during establishment) or weed-related (weed competition in the bare soil).

Overseeded lawn failure: usually competition-related (existing grass out-competing the new seedlings) or pre-em residual (kills the new seedlings before establishment).

When to seed:

New lawn from scratch. Bare ground or after full renovation. Wherever there's no existing grass to overseed into.

When to overseed:

Thin patches in an existing lawn. After light scarifying or aerating that has thinned the canopy. To introduce premium species (MDR, RSR Ryegrass) gradually into an existing lawn with cheaper rye. After winter damage to refresh the lawn density.

Common pattern combining both:

Major renovation involves killing the existing lawn, then seeding the prepared soil. Subsequent years use overseeding to maintain density and gradually shift species mix toward premium varieties.

The right tool for each situation. Don't try to overseed onto compacted bare ground (full seeding works better) or seed into an existing patchy lawn (overseeding works better).
