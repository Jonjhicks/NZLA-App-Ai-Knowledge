# Why are my grass seedlings dying?

Seven common causes of seedling death. Most home lawn seedling failures trace back to one or two of these.

**1. Drying out (most common).** Seedlings dry out fast — the entire plant is millimetres above the soil surface and the developing roots are tiny. A single dry-out event during establishment can kill seedlings within hours.

Symptoms: seedlings wilt, then go brown and crispy. Often noticeable in late afternoon after a hot day. Dead within 12 to 24 hours of severe drying.

Fix: water consistently for the first 4 weeks. Light frequent watering — 2 to 3 times a day in warm conditions. Cover with hessian if needed to retain surface moisture.

**2. Damping off (fungal disease).** Over-watering plus warm conditions creates ideal conditions for fungal pathogens that attack seedling stems at soil level. The seedlings topple over and die.

Symptoms: seedlings looking healthy one day, then collapsed and dead the next. Often in patches where moisture pools. White or grey fungal threads sometimes visible at soil level.

Fix: reduce watering frequency to allow the surface to partly dry between watering events. Increase airflow. Reduce thickness of mulch or cover if used. In severe cases, NZLA Azoxy on label for seedling damping off.

**3. Heat stress.** Establishing seedlings can't tolerate the heat an established lawn handles. Hot afternoons over 28°C plus inadequate watering kills seedlings fast.

Fix: avoid sowing in summer if possible. If sowing in warm weather, increase watering frequency, shade with hessian if needed.

**4. Pre-emergent residual.** Blockade or similar pre-em applied within the last 4 to 6 months kills new seedlings as they germinate.

Fix: don't sow seed within the residual window of any pre-em application. Wait the full 4 to 6 months.

**5. Wrong watering balance.** Both too little and too much water kill seedlings.

Too little: drying out (covered above).

Too much: damping off (covered above), or waterlogging that prevents oxygen reaching developing roots.

Fix: aim for consistent surface moisture without pooling. Watch the lawn — adjust to conditions.

**6. Birds eating seed.** Sparrows, finches and other seed-eating birds remove freshly sown seed before it germinates.

Symptoms: bare patches in the lawn where seed was sown. No emerging seedlings in affected areas.

Fix: cover with hessian or netting during establishment. Rake or roll seed into soil contact (buried seed is largely safe from birds).

**7. Wrong species for conditions.** Sowing the wrong grass for the location.

Symptoms: seedlings germinate but don't thrive — slow growth, poor density, easy to lose to drought or disease.

Fix: match the species to the location. Fine fescue for shade, ryegrass for sun and wear, tall fescue for drought tolerance.

Diagnostic approach for a failing lawn:

Check moisture first. Push a finger into the soil where seedlings are dying. Is the soil moist (at 10 mm depth)? If dry, watering is the issue. If wet, over-watering or drainage is the issue.

Look at the seedlings themselves. Wilted and crispy = drying out. Collapsed at soil level = damping off. Discoloured but still standing = pre-em residual or species mismatch.

Check the patch pattern. Even across the lawn = species or condition issue. Patches that match watering coverage = irrigation problem. Patches in low spots = waterlogging.

Most failures fix in time:

Most seedling problems are fixable if caught within a week. Once seedlings are dead in significant areas, overseeding the dead patches is usually needed.
