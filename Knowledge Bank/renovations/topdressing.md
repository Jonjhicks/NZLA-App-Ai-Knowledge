# Topdressing

## What it is

Topdressing is applying a thin layer of sand, sandy loam, or compost over the lawn surface and working it down between the grass plants. The existing grass grows up through it. Done repeatedly across a season or over years, it levels out surface variations and gradually shifts the soil composition.

Three things topdressing can achieve: surface levelling, soil profile amendment, and filling in after aeration or scarifiers. Most applications do more than one of these at once.

## Material selection

**Sandy loam** (roughly 70% sand, 30% loam) is the standard for general home lawn topdressing. Levels well, settles into the canopy without smothering the existing grass, gradually improves drainage-and-soil-moisture on clay soils. Typical cost $40 to $80 per cubic metre delivered.

**Pure coarse sand** for clay lawns where drainage-and-soil-moisture improvement is the priority, for post-aeration fill on sand-based profiles, and for sports turf maintenance. Sand moving into clay soil over multiple applications progressively opens the drainage-and-soil-moisture structure. Don't use pure sand on already-sandy lawns — worsens drought stress.

**Compost or organic-rich topsoil** for low-organic soils (sandy, pumice, or thin post-construction profiles) where building water retention and soil biology is the goal. Source from reputable suppliers — cheap compost brings weed seeds.

**Quality loam or topsoil** for building soil depth on lawns where the profile is shallow, or for significant levelling.

Before committing to a bulk delivery, ask for a small sample. Good material crumbles in your hand, smells fresh and earthy, has no visible weed material or contaminants, and is at workable moisture — not soaking wet or bone dry. Cheap material from unknown sources causes more problems than the saving justifies.

Quantity guide: 5 mm of topdressing across 100 m² requires approximately 0.5 cubic metres of material. Most suppliers don't deliver fractions of a cubic metre, so round up.

## How much per application

5 mm or less per application. That's the practical limit before smothering risk becomes real. More than 5 mm and you're burying the grass rather than filling in around it.

Work the material down into the canopy with a stiff brush or drag mat so it settles between the grass plants rather than sitting on top. Seed that just sits on top won't germinate into the new layer if it's not in soil contact.

After aeration, fill the holes first — the material moves down into the cores where it's most useful for soil amendment.

## How often

The right frequency depends on what you're trying to achieve.

General maintenance on a healthy home lawn: every 2 to 3 years is enough. Light topdressing after aeration to refresh the surface and address minor unevenness.

Drainage improvement on clay lawns: 2 to 3 times per year for a few years. Repeated sand topdressing compounds the profile amendment. Meaningful change on heavy clay takes 2 to 3 years of consistent work.

Levelling minor unevenness: 2 to 3 applications across one or two growing seasons, then drop back to maintenance frequency once the surface evens out.

Organic matter building on sandy soils: annually with compost-rich material.

Sports and fine turf: every 4 to 8 weeks during the growing season is common. Sand-based root zone maintenance is a regular part of professional turf management.

Low-maintenance home lawns: rarely or never. Topdressing is intervention work. If the lawn is healthy, draining well, and looking how you want it — you don't need to topdress.

## When to topdress

Spring (September through November) or autumn (March through April). Active growth means the grass grows through the new material quickly.

Don't topdress in summer heat or winter — recovery is too slow.

## Recovery support

NZLA Starter granular at 1.5 kg per 100m² before topdressing supports the lawn through the recovery. Light watering to settle the material into the surface. Hold mowing for about a week.

## Indicators that topdressing would help

Water sitting on the surface after rain and taking over a minute to absorb. Lawn feeling spongy underfoot. Minor unevenness developing over time. Recent aeration or scarifiers leaving a rough surface. Visible poor soil profile when you dig (clay subsoil with minimal topsoil).

If none of those apply, the lawn probably doesn't need it.
