# What is frost cloth and when do I use it?

A lightweight breathable fabric (typically 30 to 50 g/m² polypropylene) used to cover new or vulnerable lawn areas to protect from frost damage. Sometimes also called horticultural fleece or row cover.

Material works by trapping a layer of warmer air against the soil surface and preventing direct frost contact with the foliage. Doesn't make the spot warm — just keeps it 2 to 4°C above ambient at the soil level. Enough to prevent frost damage on most overnight frosts in most NZ regions.

When to use frost cloth:

**New lawns sown in autumn through winter.** Seedlings establishing in cool conditions are vulnerable to overnight frost damage. Frost cloth over the establishing area protects the young growth until it's robust enough to handle frost on its own (typically after the first 2 to 3 mows).

**Newly laid ready lawn in frost-prone regions.** A fresh turf cap with new roots hasn't fully integrated into the soil profile. Frost on the surface can damage the root mat and slow establishment. Cover for the first few weeks if heavy frost is forecast.

**Recently overseeded patches in established lawns.** New seedlings emerging through an existing lawn in autumn are particularly vulnerable. Cover the patched areas if forecast suggests damaging frost.

**Post-renovation recovery.** A scarified or aerated lawn going into winter is more vulnerable than an undisturbed established lawn. Cover during the recovery weeks if frost is forecast.

When NOT to use frost cloth:

Established mature lawns. The mature plants handle most NZ frost without protection. The lawn may discolour temporarily but recovers.

In mild regions where frost is rare or light. Auckland and the upper North Island rarely need frost cloth.

For permanent or long-term cover. Frost cloth on a lawn for months reduces light reaching the grass and ultimately damages the lawn from light deprivation.

How to use it:

Apply in the evening before a forecast frost. Drape over the area to be protected. Weight down with stones or pegs to prevent wind dislodging it.

Remove in the morning once the frost has lifted from surrounding areas. Allow the lawn to breathe and receive direct light during the day.

For repeated use, leave the cloth in place for the duration of the cold spell rather than removing and replacing daily.

How much does it cost:

Standard polypropylene frost cloth is reasonably priced — typically $10 to $30 per m² depending on grade. Available from garden centres, hardware stores and rural supply outlets.

Specialty heavier frost cloth (50 g/m² and up) gives better protection but is more expensive. Standard 30 g/m² is plenty for most lawn applications.

For one-off use:

Cheap shade cloth or hessian over the protected area gives some frost protection if dedicated frost cloth isn't available. Not as effective as proper frost cloth but better than nothing on a forecast cold night.
