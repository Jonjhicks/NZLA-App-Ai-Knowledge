# Soil Temperatures for Lawn Work

## The numbers that matter

Different jobs need different temperatures at the 50 mm depth (the standard measurement point for most lawn applications).

**Cool-season grass germination** (ryegrass, fescue): 10°C minimum for reasonable timeframe germination (10 to 14 days). Below 5°C they sit dormant or germinate very slowly. Optimal range is 15 to 20°C. Sowing below 10°C produces slow patchy establishment.

**Warm-season grass germination** (kikuyu, couch): 15°C minimum, ideally above 18°C. Below that, the seed sits and rots rather than germinates. Sowing warm-season grass into cool soil wastes the seed.

**Summer grass and crabgrass germination trigger**: 12°C at 50 mm and rising. This is the point pre-em needs to be in place. NZ regional timing: late August in Auckland, early to mid-September in the Waikato and Bay of Plenty, late September to early October in Canterbury.

**Poa annua germination trigger**: 18 to 22°C and falling — typically March through April. Autumn pre-em timing for poa annua needs to land before this window.

**Active cool-season growth threshold**: around 7 to 8°C. Below that the lawn is effectively dormant. Above that, growth and recovery from renovation work becomes possible.

**Warm-season active growth threshold**: 15°C plus. Warm-season lawns don't grow meaningfully below this.

**Scarifying and aeration recovery**: cool-season turf needs soil above 10°C to recover from renovation work within a reasonable timeframe. Late September through late April for most of NZ.

## How to measure

A dedicated soil probe thermometer pushed to 50 mm depth is the standard. Wait 30 to 60 seconds for the reading to stabilise. Digital probe models are accurate to within 1°C and robust for repeated use. Available at garden centres and hardware stores for $20 to $80.

A kitchen meat thermometer with a probe works in a pinch — just as accurate, though less robust for repeated soil use.

NIWA (niwa.co.nz) publishes soil temperature data for many NZ regions. Useful for confirming regional conditions when you don't have a thermometer, or for tracking trends over a season.

For measuring, take readings in the same spot at the same time of day (mid-morning is standard) for consistent comparison. Choose a representative spot — not in deep shade, not in a heat-exposed corner. Take readings over several consecutive days to track the trend rather than relying on a single reading. Avoid measuring immediately after rain or heavy irrigation-general — the water temperature temporarily affects the reading.

## Practical timing guide

For most home lawn work, soil thermometers are useful but not essential. Regional calendar timing combined with NIWA data works for the majority of applications.

Spring pre-emergent: target when soil temperatures at 50 mm hit 12°C and are holding. Auckland typically late August to early September. Canterbury, Waikato, and most of the North Island mid-September to early October. Southern South Island late October.

Autumn sowing window: soil temperatures at 15 to 18°C and falling. For most of NZ this is March through April. Good germination with plenty of establishment time before winter.

Spring renovation (scarifiers, aeration, oversowing): soil above 10°C consistently — late September through October for most regions.

Autumn renovation: any time soil is above 10°C and you have 4 to 6 weeks before hard frosts — March through April generally.

If you're unsure whether conditions are right: the barefoot test is a reasonable approximation. If you can walk barefoot on the soil comfortably, it's warm enough for renovation work. If it feels cold underfoot, wait.

A soil thermometer is worth the $40 to $80 for anyone running a precise programme with PGR work, GDD tracking, or fine turf management. For standard home lawn use, regional timing guides are adequate.
