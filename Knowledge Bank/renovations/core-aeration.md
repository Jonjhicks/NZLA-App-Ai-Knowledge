# Core Aeration

## What it is

Core aeration is using a machine with hollow tines that punch into the lawn and pull small plugs of soil out. The plugs (cores) come out as you walk, leaving a pattern of holes roughly 15 mm in diameter and 75 to 100 mm deep, spaced 50 to 100 mm apart.

The job is opening up the soil profile. Compaction is the main target — soils that have had heavy foot traffic, sand bases that have settled, or clay soils that have packed tight over time. The holes let air, water, fertiliser and root growth penetrate the profile. The cores left on the surface break down over a couple of weeks and return organic matter to the surface.

## Do you need it?

There's a quick test. Push a screwdriver or thin rod into the soil. If it goes in easily to 50 mm, the soil is open. If it takes real force to push past 30 mm, core-aeration is restricting root growth.

A water test is equally revealing. Pour a bucket of water on the lawn surface. If it absorbs within 30 seconds, the soil is fine. If it pools and runs, core-aeration is restricting infiltration.

You can also lift a square spadeful of turf and look at the root depth. Healthy roots should reach 75 to 100 mm into the soil. If they're stopping at 30 to 40 mm, compacted soil is the likely barrier.

If none of those signs are present and the lawn is growing well, you don't need to aerate.

## How often

Light home lawn use on well-drained soil — every 2 to 3 years is enough. Some core-aeration builds up but slowly.

Standard home lawns with moderate use — every 18 to 24 months. Keeps the soil ahead of accumulation.

Heavy use lawns with kids and dogs — annually. Foot traffic compacts progressively. Annual aeration maintains soil function under the pressure.

Clay or consistently compacted soils — annually, or twice a year for severe cases. Heavy clay packs back quickly. Spring and autumn aeration is common on serious clay lawns.

Sports fields and heavily used commercial turf — twice a year as a minimum, sometimes more depending on intensity of use.

## When to aerate

Spring (September through November) or autumn (March through April). The lawn needs to be growing actively to fill the holes and recover within 4 to 6 weeks.

Don't core in summer heat (the holes stay open longer and heat stress compounds the mechanical stress) or in winter when growth is too slow for proper recovery.

## Topdressing after aeration

The holes from aeration are the best opportunity you'll get to introduce material into the root zone. Filling them with sand or sandy loam gradually amends the soil profile with every application.

Use straight coarse sand on clay soils where drainage-and-soil-moisture improvement is the priority. Sandy loam on other soils for general amendment and levelling. Apply no more than 5 mm of material and work it into the canopy with a stiff brush or drag mat so it falls into the holes rather than sitting on the surface.

Over multiple aeration-plus-topdress cycles across 2 to 3 years, even badly compacted clay lawns shift meaningfully toward better drainage-and-soil-moisture and root development.

If you're not going to topdress, the holes will close naturally on their own — just not as quickly or usefully.
