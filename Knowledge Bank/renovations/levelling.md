# Levelling

## Matching the method to the severity

How bumpy the lawn is determines which approach works. Trying to use topdressing on a 40 mm dip is the same mistake as digging up a 5 mm dip — both waste time and money.

Minor bumps and dips under 10 mm: topdressing over multiple applications. Apply 3 to 5 mm of sandy loam over the whole lawn or just the uneven areas, work it down into the canopy with a stiff brush or drag mat so it settles between the grass plants. The existing grass grows through the topdressing layer, dips fill in, the surface evens out. Two to three applications across a single growing season can address 10 to 15 mm of variation.

Moderate undulations 10 to 30 mm: combination approach. For high spots, lift the turf cap, remove soil from beneath, replace the turf. For dips, lift the turf cap, add sandy loam underneath, replace. More work than topdressing alone, but handles variations that would take too many applications to fill by other means.

Significant unevenness over 30 mm or across large areas: full renovation. Kill the existing lawn, regrade the soil mechanically (rotary hoe and level rake, or a contractor with appropriate equipment), then re-establish from seed or ready lawn. The only practical option for serious unevenness.

## What not to do

Don't apply 20 or 30 mm of topdressing in one go expecting it to smooth things out. The grass beneath suffocates and dies, leaving a worse problem than you started with. The 5 mm rule per application is the practical limit.

## Sand levelling

Sand topdressing (pure coarse sand rather than sandy loam) is the approach for clay lawns where drainage-and-soil-moisture improvement is the goal alongside levelling. Each application introduces sand into the upper soil profile. Over multiple applications across 2 to 3 years, the upper soil layer becomes increasingly sand-amended, improving drainage-and-soil-moisture and root development.

This is a multi-year programme, not a single-season fix. Four to six applications per year for 2 to 3 years produces meaningful soil profile change on heavy clay.

Use coarse builder's sand or specialised turf sand. Avoid fine masonry sand (drainage-and-soil-moisture too poor when it compacts) or marine sand (salt content).

Don't sand-topdress a lawn that already drains well or sits on sandy soil — you'll worsen drought stress without any drainage-and-soil-moisture benefit. Use sandy loam or organic-rich topdressing instead.

## Material selection

Sandy loam (70% sand, 30% loam) for general home lawn levelling and topdressing. Levels well, settles into the canopy without smothering, gradually improves clay soil drainage-and-soil-moisture.

Pure coarse sand for drainage-and-soil-moisture amendment on clay soils, post-aeration fill on sand-based profiles, and sports turf maintenance.

Compost or organic-rich topsoil for sandy or low-organic soils where building water retention and soil biology is the goal. Source from reputable suppliers — cheap compost brings weed seeds.

Loam or topsoil for building up soil depth where the existing profile is shallow.

Before committing to a bulk delivery, ask for a sample. Good material falls apart in your hand, smells fresh and earthy, has no visible weed material, and is at workable moisture — not soaking wet or bone dry.

## Timing and recovery

Spring (September through November) or autumn (March through April). Active growth supports the grass growing up through new topdressing material. Don't topdress in summer heat or winter.

NZLA Starter granular before topdressing supports recovery. Water lightly after to settle the material into the surface. Hold mowing for about a week to let the surface stabilise.

Quantity guide: 5 mm of topdressing across 100 m² requires about 0.5 cubic metres of material. Round up for delivery.
