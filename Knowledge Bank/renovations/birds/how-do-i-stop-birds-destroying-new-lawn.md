# How do I stop birds destroying my new lawn?

Two different bird problems on a new lawn. Seed-eating birds at sowing stage, and grub-hunting birds once the lawn is established. Different solutions.

**Seed-eating birds (at sowing).** Sparrows, finches and other small seed-eating birds will hoover up freshly sown grass seed. Can wipe out a serious chunk of your sowing rate within days if not addressed.

How to protect:

**Cover with hessian or shade cloth.** Lay over the freshly sown area and weight down. The birds can't reach the seed and the cover holds moisture in for better germination. Remove once seedlings are 30 to 50 mm.

**Rake the seed in lightly.** Seed sitting on the surface is bird food. Seed in the top 5 to 10 mm of soil is largely safe. Light raking after sowing buries the seed enough that birds can't easily reach it.

**Roll after sowing.** Pressing the seed into soil contact does the same job as raking — buries it enough to be inaccessible to most seed-eating birds.

**Increase the sowing rate slightly** to account for some inevitable bird loss. Sowing 4 kg per 100m² instead of 3 kg covers minor losses.

**Time sowing around bird activity.** Some regions have less intense seed-eating bird pressure than others. Local observation tells you the pattern.

**Grub-hunting birds (on established lawn).** Once the lawn is up, the bird issue switches from seed protection to grub damage. Blackbirds and magpies tear up turf going after grubs underneath.

If a young lawn is being torn up by birds:

Check for grubs (lift a square of turf). If grub counts are above threshold, treat with NZLA Grub+. Up to 6 months control.

If grub counts are low, the bird damage may be opportunistic — birds investigating the area without significant prey. Usually self-limiting; the birds give up when they don't find food.

What NOT to do:

Don't apply bird deterrents (scarecrows, reflective tape, fake predators) to a new lawn for grub-related damage. Removes the symptom without addressing the cause.

Don't poison or harm birds. They're protected wildlife in NZ and they're not the underlying issue.

Don't ignore persistent bird damage on a new lawn. Investigate the cause — usually grubs, sometimes seed protection failure, occasionally just a high local bird population.

Cultural prevention long-term:

Healthy thick mature lawn at the right mowing height resists both seed loss (it's already grass, no seed to eat) and grub damage (dense canopy and healthy root mat make grub damage less worth a bird's effort). Most established healthy lawns have minor bird presence without significant damage.
