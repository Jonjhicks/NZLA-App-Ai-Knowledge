# Soil Preparation

## Why it matters

Rushed soil prep is the single biggest reason new lawns disappoint. An uneven seedbed, shallow cultivation, or poor soil quality produces problems that persist for the entire life of the lawn. Regrading or amending an established lawn is vastly more disruptive than doing it right before you start.

## What good soil looks like for a lawn

Loam or sandy loam is the target — roughly 60 to 70 percent sand, 15 to 25 percent silt, 10 to 20 percent clay. Open and friable structure that crumbles in the hand. Water absorbs quickly and roots can grow freely. Organic matter at 3 to 5 percent. pH between 5.5 and 7.0. At least 200 mm of usable soil depth before hitting rock, heavy clay subsoil, or other restrictions.

Most NZ soils don't start there. That's fine. The goal is to work with what you have and improve it over time.

## What you're likely working with

Heavy clay is common across much of NZ (Auckland, Waikato, Manawatu, Canterbury lowlands). Clay holds nutrients but compacts badly, drains poorly, and makes root development difficult.

Sandy soils appear in coastal areas, parts of Hawkes Bay, Nelson, and Otago. Fast drainage-and-soil-moisture, good rootability, but prone to drought and nutrient leaching.

Pumice and volcanic soils in the central North Island are light, free-draining, often acidic, and have low nutrient-holding capacity.

Silt loams on river plains and floodplains are generally reasonable lawn soils with the right management.

Hand test: take a handful of moist soil and squeeze. Forms a ball that won't break apart = clay. Forms a ball that crumbles when poked = loam. Won't form a ball at all = sand.

For significant renovations or new lawns where you're unsure, a soil test ($40 to $80 from NZ labs) removes guesswork on pH, nutrient levels, and organic matter. Worth it before major investment.

## Preparation steps for new lawn establishment

**Clear existing vegetation.** Glyphosate kill (two passes, 2 weeks apart) handles established growth and . Wait for full kill before starting work.

**Till to 100 to 150 mm.** Rotary hoe for larger areas, fork or spade for smaller. This depth gives roots room to establish and breaks up surface core-aeration. Go shallower and the lawn develops shallow roots that struggle in summer.

For clay soils: mix in coarse sand at this stage to improve drainage-and-soil-moisture long-term. For sandy soils: mix in compost or organic-rich topsoil to improve water retention.

On severely compacted sites (post-construction, heavy clay after livestock), deeper tilling may be needed. Hire a more powerful rotary hoe or consider professional soil work.

**Remove rocks and debris.** Anything larger than 25 mm. Buried debris creates dead spots and root barriers. Surface rocks scalp under the mower.

**Level the surface.** Drag a long board across to identify high and low spots. Rake to fill dips and level high points. Take extra time here — this is the hardest thing to fix after the lawn is established. A properly levelled surface shouldn't show board-high or board-low spots.

**Firm the soil.** Walk systematically or roll. The test: footprints should sink no more than 10 mm. Too deep = more firming. No visible impression = ready. Newly tilled loose soil settles unevenly under watering and rain if it's not firmed first.

**Apply NZLA Starter granular** at 1.5 kg per 100m². Rake lightly into the top 10 mm. Now ready to sow seed or lay ready lawn.

## Tilling — when and how

For new lawn establishment and major renovation: yes, till. Opens the profile, mixes amendments, breaks core-aeration.

For seeding-and-over-seeding or patch repair: no. Tilling destroys the existing grass. Light scarifiers or hard raking is the right tool.

Don't till wet soil. Clay tilled wet packs hard when it dries. Wait for conditions where the soil crumbles in the hand rather than forming sticky balls.

After tilling, the soil is loose and lumpy. Roll or rake to level, then firm before sowing.

## Amending the soil over time on established lawns

You can't till amendments into an established lawn. But you can amend gradually through topdressing, core aeration, and biology support.

NZLA Aerate (soil-amending polymer) works into the holes from core aeration to improve soil structure and drainage-and-soil-moisture function on clay soils. NZLA Penetrate moves water through the profile better on hydrophobic soils. NZLA Restore supports soil microbial biology — the biological function that breaks down thatch, cycles nutrients, and builds soil structure over time.

These are multi-year programmes on established lawns. The changes compound with consistent application.
