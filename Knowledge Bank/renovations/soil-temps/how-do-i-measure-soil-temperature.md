# How do I measure soil temperature?

Three approaches, depending on accuracy needed.

**1. Soil thermometer.** A dedicated probe-style thermometer designed for soil. Push to 50 mm depth (the standard reading depth for most lawn applications) and wait for the reading to stabilise. Typically 30 to 60 seconds.

Available from garden centres, hardware stores, or scientific equipment suppliers. $20 to $80 depending on the model. Analog dial models are cheaper; digital models more accurate.

For lawn use, the digital probe-style models are the standard. They're robust, accurate to within 1°C, and easy to read.

**2. Cooking probe thermometer.** A kitchen meat thermometer with a probe works for soil temperature in a pinch. Push to 50 mm depth, wait, read.

Less robust than dedicated soil thermometers (the probe isn't designed for repeatedly pushing into soil) but functional for occasional measurements.

**3. Online or app-based data.** NIWA (National Institute of Water and Atmospheric Research) publishes soil temperature data for many NZ regions. Check niwa.co.nz for current and historical soil temperature data for your area.

Weather apps and gardening apps increasingly include soil temperature estimates based on weather data. Accuracy varies — the apps estimate from air temperatures and recent weather rather than measuring directly.

Best for general information when precision isn't critical. Not as accurate as a direct measurement on your specific lawn.

What depth to measure:

**50 mm depth** is the standard for most lawn applications — pre-em timing, seed germination assessment, fertiliser response. The temperature at the root zone level where biological processes are happening.

**Shallower (25 mm)** for some specific applications — surface-level disease forecasting, immediate seed germination conditions.

**Deeper (100 mm)** for deep-root assessments and dormancy break timing.

For routine home lawn use, 50 mm is the right depth.

When to measure:

**Spring pre-em timing.** Daily checks through August into September to catch when soil temperatures hit 12°C and stay there (the summer grass and crabgrass germination trigger).

**Autumn pre-em timing.** Daily checks through February into March to catch when soil temperatures fall to 18 to 22°C (poa annua germination window).

**Spring seeding decision.** Check whether soil temperatures are above 10°C minimum for cool-season grass germination, or above 15°C for warm-season grass.

**Disease forecasting.** Some disease models use soil temperature alongside other factors. For most home use, this is overkill.

How to take a representative reading:

Take readings in the same spot at the same time of day (typically mid-morning) for consistency.

Choose a spot representative of the whole lawn — not in deep shade, not in a hot exposed corner.

Take readings on multiple days to track the trend rather than relying on single readings.

Avoid taking readings immediately after rain or heavy irrigation — the water temperature affects readings briefly.

For lawn timing applications:

Daily readings are unnecessary for most home use. Weekly readings during the critical windows (spring transition into warmer soil, autumn transition into cooler soil) catch the timing changes.

For most home lawn owners, simple calendar-plus-region timing works fine. Auckland late August through early October for spring pre-em. Canterbury late September through early November. NIWA data for confirmation when you want precision.

Investing in a soil thermometer makes sense for owners running precise programmes (fine turf, premium lawn, PGR programmes with GDD tracking).

For everyone else, regional timing guides are adequate.
