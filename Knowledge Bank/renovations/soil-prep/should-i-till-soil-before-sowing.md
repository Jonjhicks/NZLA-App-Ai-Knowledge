# Should I till the soil before sowing?

For full new lawn establishment, yes — tilling (rotary hoeing or forking) is part of proper soil prep. For minor overseeding or patch repair, no — light scarifying is enough.

When to till:

**Full new lawn establishment.** Rotary hoe the top 100 to 150 mm of soil. Opens the profile so new roots can establish, mixes in any amendments, breaks up surface compaction, makes levelling easier.

**Major lawn renovation.** Same as new lawn — kill the existing lawn, then rotary hoe before laying new lawn.

**Significant soil amendments.** When adding sand to clay, compost to sandy soil, or other major mixing in. Tilling integrates the amendment with existing soil.

**Compacted areas being rebuilt.** Where the existing soil is too compacted to support new lawn establishment.

When NOT to till:

**Overseeding into existing lawn.** Tilling destroys the existing grass. Use light scarifying or hard raking instead to expose seed-soil contact without removing the existing lawn.

**Patch repair on small bare areas.** Hand fork or rake the patch only — don't till the surrounding established lawn.

**Established lawn maintenance.** Aeration (core or solid tine) opens the profile without the destructive impact of tilling.

How to till for new lawn:

**Rotary hoe** is the standard tool. Hire one for $80 to $150 per day from rural supply or equipment hire shops. Works the top 100 to 150 mm of soil into a workable tilth.

**Forking by hand** suits smaller areas. Push a garden fork in 150 mm deep and rock to lift and break up the soil. Slower than rotary hoe but doesn't require equipment hire.

**Avoid roto-tillers** with very small or fine attachments. They produce dust-fine soil that compacts back hard, losing the open structure you wanted.

How deep to till:

100 to 150 mm is standard. Goes deep enough to give roots room to develop and to break up surface compaction. Doesn't disturb deeper soil structure that's actually fine.

For very compacted soils with hardpan layers, deeper tilling (to 200 mm) may be needed. Hire a more powerful tiller or consider professional soil work.

For sand-amended profiles (adding sand to clay), till deeper to mix the sand thoroughly with the existing clay.

After tilling:

The soil is loose and lumpy. Roll or rake to level the surface and bring it to a workable smoothness for sowing. Some firming through walking or rolling is needed — newly tilled loose soil settles unevenly under rain and watering.

Test the level by dragging a long board across — should reveal a uniform surface without major dips or bumps.

Apply NZLA Starter granular at 1.5 kg per 100m² after levelling. Rake into the top 10 mm.

Now ready to sow seed or lay ready lawn.

Common tilling mistakes:

**Tilling wet soil.** Clay soil tilled wet packs hard once it dries. Wait for soil to be dry enough to crumble in the hand rather than form sticky balls.

**Tilling too deep.** Going past 150 mm typically isn't necessary and can disturb deeper structure unnecessarily.

**Skipping levelling after tilling.** Loose tilled soil never settles into a smooth surface without active levelling work.

**Tilling without amendments.** If your soil needs sand, compost or other amendments, add them BEFORE the final tilling pass so they integrate with the soil.

For most home lawn renovations, half a day of rotary hoe work plus a couple of hours of levelling produces a good base for new lawn establishment.
