# What soil do I need for a good lawn?

Loam or sandy loam is ideal. Most NZ soils don't start there but can be amended over time.

What ideal lawn soil looks like:

**Texture.** Sandy loam (60 to 70 percent sand, 15 to 25 percent silt, 10 to 20 percent clay). Good drainage but holds enough moisture for grass growth. Workable, doesn't crack open or pack hard.

**Structure.** Open and friable. Crumbles in your hand rather than forming clumps. Water absorbs quickly. Roots can grow through it freely.

**Organic matter.** 3 to 5 percent organic content. Supports soil biology, holds nutrients in plant-available forms, retains moisture without becoming waterlogged.

**pH.** 5.5 to 7.0 for cool-season grass species. Slightly acidic to neutral. Most NZ soils sit in this range without intervention.

**Depth.** At least 200 mm of usable soil before hitting rock, clay subsoil or other restrictions. Deeper is better — gives roots room to develop and the lawn drought resilience.

What you typically actually have in NZ:

**Heavy clay.** Common in many parts of NZ — particularly Auckland, Waikato, Manawatu, Canterbury (parts), and many lifestyle blocks on rural land. Clay holds water, drains poorly, compacts badly, but holds nutrients well.

**Sandy soil.** Coastal areas, parts of Hawkes Bay, Nelson, parts of Otago. Drains fast (sometimes too fast), holds little nutrient, prone to drought stress, but easy to work.

**Pumice and volcanic soils.** Central North Island. Light, free-draining, often acidic, low cation exchange capacity. Distinct profile that needs specific management.

**Silt loams.** Many river plains and floodplains across NZ. Generally good lawn soils with the right management.

How to assess your soil:

The hand test. Take a handful, wet it slightly, squeeze. If it forms a ball that doesn't break apart — clay. If it forms a ball that crumbles when poked — loam. If it won't form a ball at all — sand.

Get a soil test. Hill Laboratories, R.J. Hills, and other NZ labs offer affordable soil tests. Texture, pH, nutrient levels, organic matter — all measured properly. Worth doing before a significant renovation.

Amendments to improve your soil:

**Clay soils.** Add sand and organic matter through topdressing, aeration, and over time. NZLA Aerate (soil-amending polymer) on clay soils helps soil structure. NZLA Penetrate moves water through the profile better.

**Sandy soils.** Add organic matter through topdressing with compost or rich topsoil. Improve water retention with NZLA Wetter wetting agents. Build soil biology with NZLA Restore.

**Compacted soils.** Core aeration combined with topdressing gradually opens the profile. Multi-year programme.

For new lawn installations:

Where the existing soil is significantly wrong (e.g. solid clay with no topsoil), consider importing 100 to 150 mm of quality topsoil and spreading over the existing surface. Significant cost ($200 to $500 per cubic metre of quality topsoil delivered) but creates a lawn-friendly profile to work from.

For established lawns with imperfect soil:

Work with what you have. Aerate annually, topdress periodically, build soil biology with NZLA Restore, manage moisture with appropriate wetting agents. Over years, the soil profile improves.

Don't expect perfect lawn soil. Most NZ lawns succeed on imperfect soil with consistent good management.
