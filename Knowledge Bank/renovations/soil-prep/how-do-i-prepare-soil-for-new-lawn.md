# How do I prepare soil for a new lawn?

Six steps. The single biggest reason new lawns disappoint is rushed soil prep. Time spent here saves years of frustration.

**1. Clear the area.** Remove any existing vegetation, including dead grass, weeds, debris, rocks. Use glyphosate to kill any persistent existing growth. Wait 2 weeks for full kill, then a second pass if needed.

For larger areas, hiring a turf cutter speeds the removal of existing turf cleanly.

**2. Dig over the top 100 to 150 mm.** Loosen the soil profile so new roots can establish. Rotary hoe for larger areas, fork or spade for smaller areas. The depth is critical — shallow prep produces shallow-rooted lawns that struggle in summer.

For clay soils, consider mixing in sand at this stage to improve drainage long-term. For sandy soils, mix in compost or organic-rich topsoil to improve water retention and nutrient cycling.

**3. Remove rocks, debris and old roots.** As you dig, take out anything larger than about 25 mm. Rocks at the surface scalp under the mower. Buried debris creates dead spots where grass struggles. Roots from removed plants can resprout.

A heavy garden rake works for surface debris. A soil sieve for fine work if budget allows.

**4. Level the surface.** Spread the loosened soil to a uniform level. Address dips and bumps. The surface should be smooth enough that a long board dragged across shows no major variations.

For lawns that will be used for play or any precision work, take extra time here. Levelling is much harder to fix later than to do right initially.

For larger areas, hire a landscape level rake (large tool with rigid frame) to speed the levelling process.

**5. Firm the soil.** Walk the area systematically, applying even pressure. Or roll with a lawn roller. The goal is soil that's firm enough to walk on without sinking, but not so compacted that water won't penetrate.

The settling test: walk the area in different directions. If footprints sink more than 10 mm, firm further. If footprints make no visible impression, the soil is suitable.

**6. Apply NZLA Starter granular** at 1.5 kg per 100m². Rake lightly into the top 10 mm of soil. The phosphorus drives early root development in the new seedlings; trace elements support seedling vigour.

Now you're ready to sow seed or lay ready lawn.

What good soil prep produces:

A surface that's level, firm without being compacted, well-drained, and ready to support consistent germination across the entire area.

What rushed soil prep produces:

Uneven lawn surface that can't be fixed without removing the established lawn. Bumpy mowing that scalps high spots and misses low spots. Patches that don't establish because the soil underneath is wrong. Years of ongoing problems.

Time investment:

A typical 200 to 400 m² home lawn needs about 8 to 16 hours of physical labour to prep properly (one to two weekend days), depending on the existing surface condition.

Professional soil prep on the same area runs $1,500 to $4,000 depending on complexity.

The trade-off between cost and effort here is real, but skipping or rushing the prep is the false economy.

Once the prep is done properly, sowing seed and watering for 4 to 6 weeks brings you to a properly established lawn.
