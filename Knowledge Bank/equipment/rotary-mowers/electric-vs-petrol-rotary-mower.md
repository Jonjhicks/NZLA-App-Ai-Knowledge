# Should I buy an electric or petrol rotary mower?

Depends on lawn size and use pattern. Both serve well in their right applications.

**Petrol rotary mowers.**

Pros:

More cutting power than equivalent electric. Handles longer grass, thicker growth, rough conditions better.

No battery limitations. Run as long as you keep fuel in the tank.

Longer service life in heavy use. Quality petrol mowers last 10 to 15 years with maintenance.

Better suited to larger lawns and rough conditions.

Self-propelled options widely available.

Cons:

Louder operation.

Requires fuel, oil changes, spark plug maintenance, air filter replacement.

Heavier than equivalent electric.

Exhaust and fuel smell.

Carburetor problems if fuel goes stale.

Higher operating cost (fuel) over time.

**Electric mowers (corded or cordless).**

Pros:

Quieter than petrol.

Less maintenance — no fuel, oil, or spark plugs.

Push button start.

Lower vibration.

Lighter than equivalent petrol (cordless models particularly).

Cleaner — no exhaust or fuel smell.

Excellent for early morning or evening use without neighbour disturbance.

Cons:

Corded models limited by cord reach.

Cordless models limited by battery life (typically 30 to 60 minutes per charge on quality units).

Less power than petrol equivalents at the same price point. Can stall in heavy grass.

Battery replacement cost when batteries wear out ($150 to $400+).

Battery degradation over years of use.

How to decide:

**Lawn under 300m², light grass:** quality cordless electric. Sufficient power for the area. Better neighbour relations, less maintenance.

**Lawn 300 to 600m², standard conditions:** depends on use pattern. Cordless electric works for owners willing to manage battery time. Petrol for owners wanting consistent power without battery concerns.

**Lawn over 600m², heavy use, rough conditions:** petrol. Battery limitations become impractical at this scale.

**Frequent rough or wet conditions:** petrol. Electric mowers are less tolerant of difficult conditions.

**Owners with multiple battery-powered tools (trimmer, blower, etc.):** consider battery system compatibility. Some brands share batteries across tool ranges, increasing the value of investing in that brand's ecosystem.

Specific considerations:

**Corded electric mowers** are the cheapest option ($150 to $400) but the cord is genuinely awkward. Practical only for small simple-shaped lawns where the cord doesn't tangle around obstacles.

**Cordless battery mowers** ($400 to $1,500) have transformed the electric mower market. Quality cordless units with 4Ah+ batteries cover 200 to 400m² on a single charge. Spare batteries extend coverage.

**Petrol mowers** range from $300 cheap units to $2,000+ premium. Quality matters substantially — cheap petrol mowers fail within 3 to 5 years; quality units last 10 to 15.

Battery technology evolution:

Cordless battery mowers in 2026 are dramatically more capable than the same category 5 years ago. Modern cordless quality units rival mid-range petrol mowers in cutting performance for typical home lawn use.

Battery costs continue to decline. Replacement batteries are no longer the prohibitive cost they once were.

For users buying their first quality mower, cordless electric is often the right choice for typical NZ home lawn use.

Brand considerations:

Quality brands in both categories include Honda, Toro, Husqvarna, Stihl (mainly petrol), Ryobi, Makita, DeWalt, Greenworks (mainly electric), and others.

Avoid budget brands at any price — the quality difference is real.

Self-propelled vs push:

For larger lawns or owners wanting less physical effort, self-propelled mowers reduce the work substantially. Available in both petrol and electric categories. $100 to $300 premium over push equivalents.

Practical NZ recommendation:

**Small lawn (under 300m²):** quality cordless electric ($400 to $700). Modern technology handles this size well.

**Medium lawn (300 to 600m²):** depends on personal preference and budget. Both petrol and quality cordless work.

**Large lawn (over 600m²):** petrol typically, or ride-on for over 1500m².

Avoid:

Cheap mowers in any category. Performance and lifespan don't justify the savings.

First-generation or budget cordless models. The market matured rapidly — buy current models from established brands.

Petrol mowers without dealer support for service. When carburetor or other issues develop (and they will), having local service access matters.

For most NZ home lawn owners:

A quality cordless electric mower from an established brand is increasingly the right choice. Petrol remains the right call for larger properties and heavy users.

Don't buy more mower than your lawn needs. A premium push mower outperforms a cheap ride-on on a 500m² lawn.
