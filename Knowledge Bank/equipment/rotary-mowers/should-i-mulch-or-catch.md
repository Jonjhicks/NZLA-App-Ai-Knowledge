# Should I mulch or catch my clippings?

Generally mulch. Three exceptions where catching makes sense.

The default — mulch:

Mulch mowing returns clipping nutrients (particularly nitrogen) to the soil. Over a season this reduces your fertiliser requirement by 20 to 30 percent.

A well-designed mulching mower chops clippings into small pieces that work down between grass plants and into the soil surface. The small pieces break down within days during active growing season.

For mulching to work well:

Mow frequently (apply the third rule — never take more than a third of the leaf at once).

Sharp blades — torn clippings clump and don't mulch effectively.

Dry conditions — wet clippings clump.

Right mower type — dedicated mulching mowers have curved decks and special blades that chop clippings finer than standard rotary mowers.

Most modern home mowers handle mulching adequately. Premium models with proper mulching design do it much better than budget alternatives.

When to catch instead:

**Disease in the lawn.** If you have Fusarium, dollar spot, red thread, rust, or any other foliar disease active, catch the clippings to remove infected leaf material from the lawn. Mulching infected clippings spreads the disease.

**Long grass after a missed cut.** If you've been a long time between mows and have a thick mat of clippings, mulching produces clumps that smother the lawn rather than incorporate. Catch this time, then return to mulching once you're back on a regular schedule.

**Going to seed (poa, browntop, etc.).** If the lawn has gone to seed visibly, catching the clippings removes the seed heads before they disperse. Reduces next year's weed pressure.

When side-discharge makes sense:

**Very long grass that needs to be brought down before mulching is viable.** First mow at higher setting with side-discharge to spread the long clippings without clumping; subsequent mows can return to mulching as the lawn comes back to normal height.

**Large open lawns where the slight unevenness of side-discharge clippings doesn't show.** Faster than mulching or catching because the mower can run at full speed without restriction.

What's actually happening:

A modern mower with mulching capability has a closed deck design and a "mulching plug" that blocks the side discharge port. Clippings stay under the deck longer, get cut multiple times by the blade, and exit very small.

A mower in catching mode discharges clippings through the side port directly into the attached catcher bag.

A mower in side-discharge mode sprays clippings out the side without re-cutting or collection.

Most modern home mowers can switch between modes by removing/replacing the mulching plug and attaching/removing the catcher.

The deck design affects mulching quality more than people realise. Cheap mowers with simple flat decks don't mulch as well as quality designs with curved decks and proper blade geometry.

What about the "clippings cause thatch" myth:

Mulched grass clippings don't cause thatch buildup. Thatch is primarily from stems, roots, and slowly-decomposing plant material — not from clippings.

The clippings argument is a misunderstanding that persists despite turf research being clear on this. Mulch with confidence.

What about leaving clippings on the lawn:

In active growing season with proper mowing frequency and sharp blades, mulched clippings disappear within 24 to 48 hours. They don't sit on the surface long enough to be a nuisance.

In slow growth conditions (winter, drought) clippings persist longer. Reduce frequency or catch during these periods if appearance bothers you.

Special cases:

**Robot mowers** mulch by design — they cut so frequently that clippings are tiny and incorporate immediately.

**Reel mowers** typically don't have catching capability. Clippings spread on the lawn as the mower passes. Acceptable for short well-managed turf.

**Mulching attachments retrofitted to standard rotary mowers** vary in effectiveness. OEM mulching kits work; aftermarket plugs are hit-and-miss.

For most NZ home lawns:

Mulch as the default. Catch when disease is present, when the lawn has gone too long between mows, or when seed heads need removing.

Sharp blades, regular mowing frequency, and dry conditions transform mulching performance.

Stop bagging routinely — return the nutrients to the lawn and save the disposal hassle.
