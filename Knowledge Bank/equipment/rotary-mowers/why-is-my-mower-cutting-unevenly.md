# Why is my mower cutting unevenly?

Six common causes. Most uneven cutting traces to one of these.

**1. Blunt or damaged blades.** Worn, chipped, or unbalanced blades cut unevenly. One section of the blade cuts cleanly while damaged sections tear or skip.

How to check: turn off mower, disconnect spark plug or remove battery. Tilt mower (oil side up to prevent leaks), inspect blade edges. Look for chips, rolled edges, visible damage.

Fix: sharpen or replace blade. Balance after sharpening.

**2. Unbalanced blade.** Even a sharp blade that's unbalanced (one side heavier than the other) causes vibration that produces uneven cutting and damages the mower over time.

How to check: remove blade, balance it on a nail or balancing tool. A balanced blade sits level. An unbalanced blade tips toward the heavier side.

Fix: rebalance by removing material from the heavier side with a file or grinder.

**3. Uneven tyre pressure.** Different tyre pressures on each side of the mower change the deck height on each side. One side cuts lower than the other.

How to check: inspect tyres for visible different inflation. Check with a gauge — should be equal pressure across all tyres.

Fix: equalise tyre pressures. Check more often during heavy use periods.

**4. Deck height incorrectly adjusted.** Adjustment mechanisms on different corners of the deck can drift over time, resulting in one corner higher than others.

How to check: place the mower on a flat surface. Measure the deck height from the ground at each corner. All four corners should be equal (within 5 mm).

Fix: adjust the height mechanisms to equalise. Refer to the mower manual for specific adjustment locations.

**5. Worn deck wheels or guide rollers.** Some mowers have additional wheels or guides on the deck that contact the ground. Wear in these affects cutting height.

How to check: inspect deck rollers and wheels for wear, cracks, or unevenness.

Fix: replace worn rollers.

**6. Bent or damaged deck.** Impacts (hitting rocks, tree roots) can bend the deck. The blade no longer rotates in a flat plane relative to the ground.

How to check: turn off mower. Look at the deck from underneath. The blade should rotate parallel to the deck surface. Spin the blade by hand and observe whether it stays at a consistent height.

Fix: significant deck damage typically requires professional repair or deck replacement.

Other contributing factors:

**Speed of mowing.** Too fast means the blade has less time to cut each grass plant cleanly. Some unevenness comes from rushing.

Fix: slow down. Especially for thick or rough grass.

**Wet grass.** Cuts poorly. The blade tears rather than cuts cleanly.

Fix: wait for the grass to dry before mowing.

**Mowing pattern.** Always mowing the same direction can produce visible patterns over time.

Fix: alternate mowing direction each session — north-south one week, east-west the next.

**Blunt mower making one direction worse.** When blades are dull, mowing in the direction that disadvantages the blade angle produces worse cuts than the other direction.

Fix: sharpen blades.

**Lawn surface unevenness.** Some "uneven cutting" is actually the lawn being uneven. The mower cuts at consistent height; the ground varies.

Fix: address the lawn unevenness through topdressing or levelling — different fix to mower issues.

Diagnostic approach for uneven cutting:

Inspect blade first. Most common cause.

Check tyre pressures.

Measure deck heights at each corner.

Examine deck for damage.

Consider the cutting pattern — direction, speed, grass condition.

Once you've identified the issue, the fix is usually straightforward and the mower returns to clean cutting.

For mowers with persistent uneven cutting despite addressing all the above:

Take it for professional service. Deck damage, internal mechanism issues, or transmission problems may need expert diagnosis.

Maintenance prevents most uneven cutting:

Sharpen blades twice a season minimum.

Check and equalise tyre pressures monthly during use.

Clean the underside of the deck after each session.

Inspect deck rollers and adjustment mechanisms quarterly.

Take seriously hitting rocks or tree roots — inspect for damage immediately rather than assuming everything is fine.

A well-maintained mower cuts evenly. Most uneven cutting is fixable.
