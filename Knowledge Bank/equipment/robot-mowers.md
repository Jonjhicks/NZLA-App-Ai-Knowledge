# Robot Mowers

A robot mower cuts little and often, sometimes daily. That mowing pattern is actually good for the lawn. Constant light tipping pushes grass to tiller sideways rather than stretch up, which builds density over time. The clippings are tiny and return straight to the soil as mulch, bringing nitrogen back to the lawn rather than the green waste bin — effectively 20 to 30% of your fertiliser programme for free.

Done right, a robot mower improves the lawn over time.

## How they work

**Boundary wire**: a thin insulated wire laid around the lawn perimeter (30 to 50 mm deep, or pegged at the surface). The wire carries a low-voltage signal the robot uses to stay within the area. Garden beds, paths, and areas you want excluded are defined by routing the wire around them.

**The robot**: battery-powered, typically 18V to 28V, with small rotating blades underneath. Multiple sensors handle the boundary wire, bumps, lift (blades stop immediately when picked up), rain, and slope angle. Navigation is usually random-pattern — mows in random directions until the battery runs low, then returns to the charging station. Premium models use GPS or sensor mapping for systematic patterns.

**Charging station**: connected to the boundary wire, plugged into standard 240V household power. The robot returns here automatically, recharges, and resumes.

## Setup requirements

Lawn shape needs to allow boundary wire installation. Slopes within the manufacturer's limit (usually 25 to 35%). Reasonable obstacle situation. Power supply available for the charging station. Installation takes 2 to 4 hours for a typical home lawn — DIY or professional.

## What it doesn't do

Edge trimming. The wire sits 100 to 200 mm from the actual lawn edge, so that strip needs periodic attention from a line trimmer. Long grass recovery: if the robot stops working for a week (holiday, fault), you'll need a conventional mower to bring the grass back down before it can resume.

Clipping return reduces nitrogen demand but doesn't replace potassium and iron. NZLA All Seasons a few times a year still earns its place. NZLA Iron+ for colour. During an active  outbreak, you can't remove infected clippings with a robot. Factor this in when deciding on a fungicide programme.

## Safety — kids, pets, and hedgehogs

Kids: blade stop on lift and tilt sensors make robots substantially safer than push or ride-on mowers for children. Basic supervision is sensible around young children.

Dogs and cats: most animals adapt quickly and largely ignore the robot within days.

Hedgehogs: this is the genuine problem. Hedgehogs roll into a ball when threatened. The robot's obstacle sensors may detect them but the blades can pass over before they react. Wildlife rescue organisations in NZ regularly report robot mower injuries to hedgehogs.

**The fix is straightforward: set the robot to mow during daylight hours only.** Hedgehogs are nocturnal. Daytime mowing eliminates the risk. All quality robot mowers allow scheduling — this should be the default setting for any NZ owner.

## Costs

Entry-level ($1,500 to $3,000): suits lawns up to 1000 m². Mid-range ($3,000 to $6,000): better sensors and coverage. Premium ($6,000 to $15,000+): GPS mapping, large lawns. Major brands in NZ: Husqvarna Automower, Worx Landroid, Robomow, Stihl iMow, Honda Miimo.

Annual electricity running cost is typically $20 to $60.

## Suits well

Open lawns with manageable obstacles. Lawns 500 to 5000 m². Owners away frequently. Owners wanting fully automated maintenance.

## Suits poorly

Heavily obstructed or fragmented lawns. Steep terrain. Owners wanting traditional systematic mowing patterns or stripe definition.

Set up properly, a robot mower is one of the better things you can do for a lawn long-term. Set it to run days only and let it work.
