# How do robot mowers work?

Three main components — boundary wire, the robot itself, and the charging station.

**Boundary wire.** A thin insulated wire laid around the perimeter of the lawn area you want mowed. Buried just under the surface (typically 30 to 50 mm deep) or pegged on top. The wire carries a low-voltage signal that the robot detects to stay within the boundary.

Areas you don't want mowed (garden beds, paths, water features) are excluded by routing the boundary wire around them — the robot won't cross the wire.

Installation: 2 to 4 hours for a typical home lawn. Either DIY (kits include wire, pegs, instructions) or professional installation.

**The robot.** Battery-powered mower (typically 18V to 28V lithium battery, 30 to 90 minutes runtime per charge). Multiple small blades on a rotating disk underneath, cutting via a slicing action similar to a rotary mower.

Built-in sensors detect:

The boundary wire (stays within the mowing area).

Bumps and obstacles (avoids collisions).

Lift sensors (stops the blades if picked up — safety feature).

Rain (some models pause in heavy rain).

Slope angle (avoids rolling on steep terrain).

Navigation logic:

Most robot mowers use random-pattern navigation — they mow in random directions, gradually covering the whole lawn over multiple sessions. Doesn't look organised but covers the area efficiently over time.

Premium models use GPS or sensor-based mapping to mow in systematic patterns. More efficient coverage but significantly more expensive.

When the battery runs low, the robot returns to the charging station automatically, charges, then resumes mowing.

**The charging station.** Located at a convenient point connected to the boundary wire. The robot returns here to recharge. Some models also use the station as a starting point for mowing sessions.

Power consumption: typically 5 to 20 watts during charging. Annual electricity cost $20 to $60 depending on use pattern.

What it does:

Mows little and often. The robot cuts only 2 to 5 mm at a time but does so frequently — daily or more during growing season. The frequent micro-mowing keeps the lawn at a consistent target height continuously.

Clippings are tiny and fall back into the lawn (mulching). No catching, no disposal. The clippings break down quickly because they're so small, returning nutrients to the soil.

Operates autonomously. Once set up, the robot manages itself — no daily operator input required.

What it doesn't do:

Edge trimming. The boundary wire is typically placed 100 to 200 mm in from the actual lawn edge. The unmowed strip around the edge needs trimming with a line trimmer or edger periodically.

Long grass mowing. Robot mowers can't handle grass much taller than 50 to 60 mm. If grass gets ahead of the robot (after holiday, illness, equipment problem), you need a regular mower to bring it back.

Complex terrain. Steep slopes (typically over 25%), large bare patches, water features all challenge robot mowers.

Setup requirements:

Lawn shape needs to allow boundary wire installation. Highly fragmented lawns with many small sections become impractical.

Slopes within manufacturer's limits — usually 25 to 35% maximum.

Reasonable obstacle situation — paths and beds the wire can route around.

Power supply available for the charging station — typically standard 240V household power.

What suits robot mowers:

Open lawns with manageable obstacles. Rectangular or simple-shaped lawns easier than complex shapes.

Lawns 500 to 5000m² in size — most quality robot mowers are sized for this range.

Owners who want maintenance automation but have appropriate lawn type.

Owners away frequently — robot continues maintenance during owner absence.

What doesn't suit:

Lawns with very irregular shapes or many separate areas.

Heavily sloped lawns.

Lawns with many child or pet hazards.

Owners wanting traditional lawn appearance from systematic mowing patterns.

Quality and price range:

Entry-level: $1,500 to $3,000. Capable for typical home lawn up to 1000m².

Mid-range: $3,000 to $6,000. Better cutting performance, sensor accuracy, larger lawn capacity.

Premium: $6,000 to $15,000+. Mapping navigation, GPS, advanced sensors, large lawn capacity.

Major brands in NZ: Husqvarna (Automower range), Worx Landroid, Robomow, Stihl iMow, Honda Miimo.

For most NZ home lawn owners considering robot mowing:

The technology has matured to the point where reliability and performance suit most home lawn situations. Setup investment is significant but ongoing operating cost is minimal.

Worth considering as a serious alternative to traditional mowing for owners with suitable lawn types and budget.
