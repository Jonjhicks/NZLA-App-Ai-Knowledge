# Are robot mowers safe for pets and kids?

Mostly yes for kids with sensible supervision. Mixed for pets and definite concerns for hedgehogs.

**Children.**

Robot mowers have multiple safety features:

Blade stop on lift — picking up the robot immediately stops the blades.

Tilt sensors — tipping the robot stops the blades.

Bumper sensors — collision with obstacles stops forward motion and changes direction.

The cutting blades themselves are small (typically 50 to 90 mm diameter) and protected by the mower body. Children can't easily reach the blades without significant deliberate effort.

That said:

Don't let young children play with or around the robot while operating. Same rule as for any motorised equipment.

Older children can interact with the robot safely once they understand it's not a toy and not to deliberately defeat the safety features.

Schedule robot mowing for times when supervised play isn't happening if you have very young children using the lawn.

Most home users report robot mowers as substantially safer than ride-on or push mowers around children because of the multiple sensors and automatic shutdown features.

**Cats and dogs.**

Generally safe. Animals quickly learn to avoid the robot or treat it as an unremarkable feature of the lawn. The robot's slow movement and obstacle sensors mean collisions are gentle.

Some considerations:

Dogs particularly may bark at or chase a new robot mower initially. Most adapt within days.

Animals that sleep on the lawn may not move away from an approaching robot. The robot's obstacle sensors detect them and change direction, but it's worth scheduling robot mowing for times animals are inside or active.

Tools to do is not ideal — playing with the robot's wheels or controls. Once interest wears off, this resolves.

Animal urine or faeces left on the lawn get mulched by the robot. Not ideal hygienically. Pick up animal waste before scheduled mowing if possible.

For most households with pets, robot mowers integrate well after a brief adaptation period.

**Hedgehogs (significant concern).**

This is the genuine safety issue with robot mowers in NZ. We have hedgehog populations across most of the country, and robot mowers mowing at night injure them — sometimes severely.

Hedgehogs roll into a defensive ball when threatened. The robot's obstacle sensors detect a stationary ball-shaped object as an obstacle and try to navigate around it — but the blades can pass over the hedgehog before the sensors react. Result: serious injury to the hedgehog.

Multiple NZ wildlife rescue organisations report robot mower injuries to hedgehogs.

The fix:

**Schedule mowing for daylight hours only.** Hedgehogs are nocturnal. Mowing only during the day (9 am to 5 pm, roughly) eliminates the hedgehog risk.

All quality robot mowers allow scheduling — set daytime-only operation as a permanent setting.

This is essentially the only ethical position on robot mowing in NZ. Mowing at night when hedgehogs are active is irresponsible regardless of how convenient the timing is.

**Other native wildlife.**

Skinks, geckos, native invertebrates may be affected by robot mowing. Less documented than the hedgehog issue but worth considering.

Daytime-only mowing reduces impact on most nocturnal native species.

Some owners further limit robot mowing to short sessions rather than continuous operation, reducing wildlife exposure.

**Snake-related concerns (not relevant in NZ).**

NZ has no native snakes. This is a non-issue here, unlike in countries where robot mowers and snakes overlap.

**Older or disabled adults.**

Robot mowers can be a real benefit for people physically unable to push or operate ride-on mowers. The automation removes the physical demand of lawn care.

Safety considerations are the same as for other adults — keep clear during operation, allow the safety sensors to do their job.

**General safety practices.**

Read the manufacturer's safety guidelines. Different models have specific safety features and limitations.

Maintain the robot's safety sensors. Damaged sensors compromise the safety features. Service or replace promptly.

Keep boundary wire intact. A break in the wire causes the robot to lose its boundary detection, potentially mowing outside the intended area.

Don't defeat safety features. Disabling sensors or modifying the robot creates real risks.

Schedule responsibly. Daylight only, accounting for the wildlife and household considerations.

For most NZ households, robot mowers are safe with sensible setup and scheduling. The hedgehog issue is the most important consideration — daytime-only operation resolves it.
