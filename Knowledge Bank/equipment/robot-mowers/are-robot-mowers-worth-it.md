# Are robot mowers worth it?

For most lawns, yes — done right, a robot mower improves the lawn over time, not the opposite.

The reason is the mowing pattern. A robot cuts little and often, sometimes every day. Constant light tipping pushes the grass to tiller and spread sideways instead of stretching up, so the lawn ends up denser and finer. The clippings are tiny and get dropped straight back as mulch, returning the nitrogen they contained to the soil rather than to the green waste bin. Over a season that's a real feed you're getting for nothing.

The problems are about setup and conditions, not the machine itself.

**Wheel tracking.** Robots follow similar paths and will mow in the wet because they don't know any better. On soft or poorly drained ground that leaves wear lines and a bit of compaction along the boundary wire and around tight corners.

**Cutting height matters.** Set it too low and you'll get scalping stress every day. Keep cool-season grass like ryegrass and fescue at 30 to 50 mm. Warm-season lawns (kikuyu, couch) can sit lower.

**Disease management.** Because robots don't collect, you can't lift clippings out during a disease outbreak. The cutting itself isn't a big disease driver if blades stay sharp, but worth knowing.

**Hedgehogs.** Robots running at night injure them. Set it to mow during the day. We've got plenty of hedgehogs in NZ.

On fertility, the clipping return reduces nitrogen need by 20 to 30 percent but doesn't supply potassium and iron. All Seasons a few times a year still earns its place. Iron+ for colour pickup.

Get the height right, mow in daylight, watch tracking on soft ground, and a robot mower is one of the better things you can put on a lawn.
