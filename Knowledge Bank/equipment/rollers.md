# Lawn Rollers

A lawn roller earns its place for specific jobs. For routine established lawn care, it sits unused. Know when you need one.

## When rolling is useful

**New lawn establishment.** Rolling after sowing seed improves seed-to-soil contact and promotes even germination. Standard professional practice for new sowings.

**After ready lawn laying.** Firms the turf cap against the soil below and improves root contact.

**After topdressing.** A light pass settles the material into the canopy consistently.

**Subtle levelling.** Heavy rolling presses down small high spots. Effective on minor unevenness; won't fix anything more than a few millimetres.

**After frost heaving.** Spring rolling in cooler regions presses lifted patches back down.

**Sports turf.** Cricket pitches and bowling greens use rolling regularly as part of surface preparation.

## When not to roll

Wet ground. Rolling wet soil creates core-aeration damage and surface ruts. Dormant turf in winter. Lawns with thatch problems (rolling compacts thatch). Heavy clay soils prone to core-aeration (adds to the problem).

Established home lawns generally don't need rolling unless you're doing specific establishment or levelling work.

## Buying a roller

**Manual push roller** ($80 to $300): water-filled drum, pushed by hand. Adjustable weight by varying the water fill. Suits small home lawns for occasional establishment and topdressing work.

**Tow-behind** ($200 to $800): towed behind a ride-on mower. Covers larger areas efficiently for owners who already have a ride-on.

Look for: water-fillable drum (adjustable weight is more versatile than fixed fill), width of 400 to 600 mm for home use, quality materials that won't rust and leak within a season.

Cheap rollers fail quickly. Decent ones last decades with minimal maintenance.

## Hire vs buy

Rollers are inexpensive enough that buying makes sense if you're doing new lawn establishment or topdress regularly. Hire is also easy — a day's hire is adequate for most single projects.

## Using a roller correctly

Roll on slightly damp soil (the day after sowing is ideal). One or two passes is enough — over-rolling compacts unnecessarily. Work in two directions (north-south then east-west) for even coverage. Don't roll if the soil surface is forming ruts — stop and wait for better conditions. Empty the roller before storing to prevent flat spots on the drum.
