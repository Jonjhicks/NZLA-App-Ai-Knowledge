# Do I need a leaf blower?

Depends on whether you have leaf-dropping trees and how much leaf accumulation affects your lawn.

When a leaf blower earns its place:

**Lawns under or near deciduous trees.** Significant leaf drop in autumn (April-May for most NZ regions) requires regular leaf removal. Leaves left on the lawn block light, hold moisture, and create disease conditions — particularly Fusarium pressure heading into winter.

Hand raking works but is slow. A leaf blower covers ground 5 to 10 times faster than raking.

**Larger lawn areas with significant fallen debris.** Beyond leaves, blowers handle grass clippings, debris, small twigs efficiently.

**After hedge or tree trimming work** — quick cleanup of fallen material from lawns and paths.

**Routine path and driveway clearing** through autumn and winter — combined function with lawn use.

When you can skip:

**No nearby deciduous trees.** Lawns away from deciduous trees have minimal leaf drop. Routine mowing handles whatever falls.

**Small lawn with few trees.** Hand raking 5 to 10 minutes a week handles light leaf drop adequately. Buying equipment for occasional use doesn't justify cost.

**Owners prioritising quiet operation.** Leaf blowers are loud. Some neighbourhoods have restrictions on use during certain hours.

Types of leaf blowers:

**Handheld blowers** — smaller, lighter, easier to manoeuvre. Suitable for most home lawn cleanup.

Cordless battery: $150 to $400. Quiet, easy to maintain. Battery life can be limited on larger areas.

Petrol: $200 to $500. More power, longer runtime. Louder, requires fuel maintenance.

Corded electric: $80 to $200. Cheapest but limited by extension cord reach.

**Backpack blowers** — larger fuel tank and more power than handheld. Better for larger properties or extended sessions.

Petrol typically: $400 to $1,500. Substantial power, comfortable to carry for long sessions, but expensive.

**Wheeled blowers** — for large commercial properties or substantial leaf accumulation. Overkill for most home lawns.

**Vacuum-mulcher functions** — some leaf blowers convert to vacuum mode, sucking leaves up and mulching them in a bag. Convenient for collecting leaves but the bagging slows the process versus blowing.

What to consider:

**Power (CFM and MPH).** Cubic feet per minute (volume of air moved) matters more than mph for moving leaves. Look for 400+ CFM for adequate power on substantial leaf drop. 600+ CFM for heavier work.

**Weight.** Significant for handheld models. Lighter is easier on arms during longer sessions.

**Noise level.** Some models are dramatically quieter than others. dB ratings on packaging — under 70 dB is acceptably quiet, over 80 dB is loud.

**Battery vs petrol.** Petrol for large properties and extended use. Battery for typical home use, quieter operation, easier maintenance.

For most NZ home lawn owners with autumn leaf drop:

A quality cordless electric handheld leaf blower ($200 to $400) handles autumn cleanup efficiently. The investment pays back in time saved over hand raking, particularly if you have multiple sessions per autumn.

For owners without significant leaf drop:

Skip the leaf blower. Rake what falls, mow what stays. Don't buy equipment for occasional use.

Practical use tips:

Blow leaves toward a collection area — tarpaulin, garden bed, compost pile.

Use lower power settings when possible — quieter and less leaf-scattering.

Avoid wet leaves — wet material sticks and resists blowing.

Wear hearing protection for extended use of louder models.

Consider neighbour-friendly hours for use. Avoid early morning and late evening.

Leaf management broader picture:

Fallen leaves are valuable garden material. Compost them rather than disposing if you have garden beds or vegetable production.

Mulched leaves (run over with mower) can be returned to the lawn in small quantities as soil amendment.

Substantial leaf accumulation that's blocking lawn light is a problem regardless of how you remove it. Don't leave it to accumulate.
