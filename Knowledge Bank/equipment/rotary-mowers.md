# Rotary Mowers

The rotary mower is the right tool for most NZ home lawns. A single blade spinning horizontally cuts the grass as the mower moves forward. Simple, reliable, handles a wide range of heights, tolerates long grass and rough conditions. The trade-off against a reel mower is cut quality — at lower heights especially, a rotary tears more than it slices. But for lawns mowed at 30 to 50 mm, a well-maintained rotary does the job well.

## Electric vs petrol

**Petrol** mowers offer more cutting power, unlimited runtime, and longer service life under heavy use. They handle longer grass and rough conditions better than equivalent electric. The trade-offs: louder, heavier, require fuel, oil changes, and periodic spark plug and filter maintenance. Carburettor problems can develop if the mower is stored with old fuel.

**Cordless electric** has changed significantly in recent years. A quality modern 40V or 56V cordless mower rivals mid-range petrol performance on typical home lawns. Quieter, push-button start, much lower maintenance, lighter. Battery life on a 4Ah+ battery covers 200 to 400 m². The limitation is battery runtime — larger lawns need a spare battery or a petrol mower.

**How to choose**: lawn under 300 m² on standard grass — quality cordless electric. 300 to 600 m² — either works, depends on personal preference. Over 600 m², heavy use, or rough conditions — petrol. Corded electric mowers are the cheapest option but the cord is genuinely awkward — only practical on small simple lawns.

Quality matters far more than the electric/petrol distinction. A cheap petrol mower from a budget brand fails in 3 to 5 years. A quality electric or petrol from Stihl, Honda, Toro, Husqvarna, or Makita lasts 10 to 15 years with basic maintenance.

## Blade sharpening

Sharpen blades roughly every 20 to 25 hours of mowing — twice a season for most home users. But the real indicator is the cut. Crouch down the day after mowing and look at a grass tip. A sharp blade cuts cleanly and the tip stays green. A blunt blade tears the leaf, leaving a ragged white or brown end. That frayed tip loses water faster, heals slowly, and gives fungal pathogens an easy entry point. Heading into cool damp autumn conditions, sharp blades are a genuine  prevention step.

For rotary blades: sharpen with a file or bench grinder, then balance the blade on a nail before refitting. An unbalanced blade vibrates and damages bearings over time.

After hitting a rock or root, inspect the blade immediately. A bent or chipped blade causes vibration and gives an uneven cut regardless of sharpness.

## Mulch or catch?

Mulch as the default. Mulching returns clipping nutrients to the soil — primarily nitrogen — reducing your fertiliser input by 20 to 30% over a season. A proper mulching mower with a curved deck and mulching blade chops clippings small enough that they disappear into the sward within 24 to 48 hours during active growth.

Mulching works best when you mow frequently (never removing more than a third of the leaf), blades are sharp, and the grass is dry.

**Catch when**:  is active in the lawn (Fusarium, red thread, dollar spot — catch to remove infected material rather than recirculating it). The lawn has gone too long between mows and would produce clumping clumps rather than fine mulch. Seed heads are visible in the lawn and you want to reduce reseeding.

Side discharge is useful for very long grass on the first pass to bring it back down before returning to mulching.

Clippings don't cause thatch. That's a persistent myth. Thatch comes from stems, roots, and slowly decomposing plant material — not from grass clippings.

## Uneven cutting — common causes

Blunt or damaged blade (most common). Unequal tyre pressures (each side at different pressure changes deck height on that side). Deck height adjustment uneven across corners. Bent or damaged deck from an impact. Walking too fast (blade has less time per blade of grass). Wet grass (blade tears rather than cuts).

Check tyre pressures, inspect the blade, measure the deck height at each corner. Most uneven cutting is fixable. A persistent problem despite addressing all of these warrants a professional service.
