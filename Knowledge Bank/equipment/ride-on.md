# Ride-on Mowers

For lawns over 1500 m², a ride-on mower isn't a luxury. Mowing 2000 m² with a push mower takes 2 to 3 hours. A ride-on does it in 45 minutes. Beyond a certain size, push mowing becomes a weekend-consuming chore rather than maintenance.

## When a ride-on makes sense

1500 m²+ of mowed area is the realistic threshold for most owners. Lifestyle blocks, rural properties, and larger home lawns are the natural fit. Also worth considering for owners with physical limitations that make push mowing difficult.

For lawns under 1000 m², a ride-on usually creates more problems than it solves. It manoeuvres poorly around obstacles, doesn't improve on a good push mower for quality, and takes up significant shed space.

## Types

**Lawn tractors** (steering wheel, deck underneath, attachment options): the standard for home and rural properties. $3,000 to $10,000+ for quality units. Suit most situations.

**Zero-turn mowers** (dual lever controls, pivots on the spot): much faster around obstacles, common on larger commercial properties. $5,000 to $20,000+.

**Rear-engine ride-ons**: compact and cheaper ($2,000 to $4,000), but less capable. For medium-sized lawns where storage space is limited.

## What to look for

Deck size: 38" (950 mm) or larger for efficient coverage. Smaller decks suit obstacle-heavy areas. Engine brand matters — Briggs & Stratton, Kohler, Kawasaki all have established service networks. Hydrostatic transmission is standard on quality units (smoother and more reliable than gear-driven). Check the cut height range covers your target height. Measure gates and access points before buying — some quality units are too wide for standard residential access.

## Costs

Entry-level quality: $3,000 to $5,000. Mid-range: $5,000 to $10,000. Premium or light commercial: $10,000 to $20,000+. Annual operating cost (fuel, oil, blades, service): $200 to $600.

## Maintenance

**Each use or weekly during the season**: check oil level, tyre pressure, blade condition, air filter, belts. Keep cooling fins clear of grass clippings.

**Every 25 to 50 hours of use**: oil and filter change, spark plug inspection, fuel filter replacement, battery terminals.

**Annually**: professional service. Engine compression test, carburettor cleaning, transmission fluid check, deck spindle bearings, blade balancing. $200 to $500 — worth it. Catching problems early costs much less than fixing them after failure.

**End of season**: drain or stabilise fuel, change oil before storage, clean thoroughly, sharpen blades, disconnect battery, cover.

**Spring start-up**: reconnect battery, check oil and tyre pressures, inspect blades, add fresh fuel, test run before serious work.

The most common maintenance mistake is skipping oil changes and annual service. A $300 service prevents $3,000 repairs. That calculation is straightforward.
