# Aerators

An aerator punches holes in the lawn surface so air, water, fertiliser, and roots can move more freely through the soil profile. Most NZ lawns need it at some point — core-aeration builds gradually from foot traffic, mower wheels, rain, and natural soil settling.

## Core aerator vs solid tine

These are different tools doing different jobs.

A **core (hollow tine) aerator** uses hollow tube tines that physically pull plugs of soil out and leave them on the surface. It removes material, which creates lasting voids in the soil profile. Compaction relief is real and durable. The lawn looks rough for 2 to 4 weeks while the plugs break down and holes fill in. Recovery takes 4 to 6 weeks in active growth.

A **solid tine aerator** uses solid spikes that punch in and back out, pushing soil sideways rather than removing it. The holes gradually close up as soil rebounds. Relief is real but less lasting. Low disruption — the lawn barely shows it.

For genuine core-aeration relief, core aeration is the right tool. Solid tine works for routine surface maintenance between major core aeration sessions.

## How often

Light use, good soil: every 2 to 3 years. Standard home lawn: every 18 to 24 months. Heavy foot traffic (kids, dogs, vehicles): annually. Clay soil: annually, sometimes twice. Sports turf or high-use areas: twice a year minimum.

Signs the lawn needs aeration: water pools instead of soaking in, soil feels hard when you push a screwdriver in, roots are shallow when you dig a section, the lawn doesn't respond to fertiliser like it should.

## Timing

Spring (September to November) or autumn (March to April). Active growth supports recovery within 4 to 6 weeks. Don't aerate in summer heat or mid-winter — the lawn can't recover.

## Manual aeration

Works for small areas. A garden fork pushed in 150 mm and slightly levered gives solid tine relief on patches under 20 m². Aerator shoes (spiked sandals) provide light surface benefit on lawn up to a few hundred square metres. Practical for spot treatment between major hires rather than for whole-lawn work.

## Hire or own

Core aerators cost $2,000 to $5,000+ for quality units. Using one once or twice a year, hire is the clear answer for most home users. $100 to $200 per day from equipment hire shops or rural supply. Book in advance for spring and autumn — demand is high.

## After aeration

Apply NZLA Aerate (soil polymer) and NZLA Penetrate (wetting agent) into the holes — the open channels let both products work directly in the compacted zone rather than sitting at the surface. Topdressing with sand or sandy loam through the holes gradually amends the soil profile over time. NZLA Starter granular at 1.5 kg per 100m² supports recovery.

Water normally through the recovery period. The lawn fills in within 4 to 6 weeks during active growing season.
