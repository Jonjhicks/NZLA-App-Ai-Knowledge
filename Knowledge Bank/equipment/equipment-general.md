# Lawn Care Equipment Guide

## What you actually need

In priority order, for any home lawn:

**Mower.** The fundamental tool. Rotary mower for most home lawns. Reel mower for premium showcase lawns at low heights.

**Line trimmer.** Edges and obstacles. Cordless electric works well for most home lawns.

**Hose and watering setup.** Irrigation through dry periods. A simple oscillating sprinkler handles most home lawns. Permanent irrigation-general for larger or busier situations.

**Rake.** Fan rake for clearing thatch, debris, and seedbed prep. Metal tines outlast plastic.

**Garden fork.** Manual aeration of small areas, general soil work.

Beyond that, adding a spreader and sprayer substantially improves lawn care quality. A decent **rotary spreader** for accurate fertiliser and seed application, and a **15 L knapsack sprayer** for foliar fertiliser, herbicide, and fungicide, cover most of what a home lawn owner needs to do well.

Specialist equipment like aerators and scarifiers: hire them once or twice a year rather than owning.

For larger lawns or serious care, add a soil thermometer, soil test access, and a roller for establishment work.

## Buy or hire

**Buy** equipment you use weekly or monthly through the growing season — mower, trimmer, spreader, sprayer, hand tools.

**Hire** what you use once or twice a year — aerator, scarifier, ride-on mower if your lawn occasionally needs one, topdressing spreader.

The economics are clear. A core aerator hire costs $100 to $200 per day. Owning one costs $3,000 to $5,000. You'd need to use it more than 25 years to justify ownership at hire rates. Hire it.

A push mower used 40 times a year is a different calculation. Own it.

For specialist equipment used the first time, hiring before buying gives you hands-on experience before committing. Not all equipment suits all users or all lawns.

## Winter storage — petrol equipment

Petrol mowers, trimmers, and ride-ons need attention at the end of the season:

**Fuel.** Either drain the fuel completely (run the engine dry), or add fuel stabiliser to the tank and run for 5 minutes to circulate it through the system. Old fuel goes stale within 30 days and causes carburettor problems.

**Oil.** Drain and replace with fresh oil before storage. Old oil contains contaminants that corrode the engine over months.

**Blades.** Sharpen at the end of the season so they're ready for spring without delay.

**Battery.** Disconnect electric-start batteries and store in a cool dry place.

**Underside.** Clean grass clippings, mud, and debris off the deck. Spray exposed metal with light oil to prevent rust.

**Cover.** Keep equipment covered and off damp concrete floors.

## Winter storage — battery equipment

Charge batteries to around 50% before storage. Fully charged or fully flat are both bad for lithium battery longevity. Remove batteries from the equipment and store separately in a cool dry location.

## Winter storage — sprayers

Drain all chemical from the tank. Triple rinse with clean water. Allow to drain and dry completely. Lubricate pump seals before long storage. Frozen water in hoses and valves damages components — drain completely if there's any frost risk in your storage area.

## Spring start-up

Test the battery, check oil level, check tyre pressures, inspect blades, add fresh fuel. Run the engine for 5 to 10 minutes before relying on it for serious work. Equipment stored properly starts cleanly. Equipment stored poorly often needs work before it's usable.

## Quality matters

Cheap equipment fails fast. A quality $250 mower blade outlasts three $80 blades. A quality $200 knapsack sprayer outperforms a $40 one and is still working a decade later. The shed full of cheap tools is a false economy.

Buy quality equipment you'll use regularly. Hire quality professional equipment for the occasional specialist jobs.
