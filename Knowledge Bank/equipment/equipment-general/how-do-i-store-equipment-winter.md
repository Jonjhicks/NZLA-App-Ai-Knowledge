# How do I store lawn equipment for winter?

Proper winter storage prevents damage and ensures equipment starts cleanly next season.

**Petrol equipment (mowers, line trimmers, ride-ons).**

**Drain or treat the fuel.** Old fuel goes stale within 30 days and creates carburetor problems. Two options:

Drain the fuel completely (run the engine until it stops to clear the fuel system).

Add fuel stabiliser to the tank, run the engine for 5 minutes to circulate it through the system. Stored equipment with stabilised fuel starts cleanly in spring.

**Drain the oil and refill** with fresh oil before winter storage. Old oil contains contaminants that can corrode the engine over months of storage. Fresh oil prevents this.

**Clean the equipment thoroughly.** Remove grass clippings, mud, and debris. Particularly clean the underside of mower decks where grass builds up. Allow to dry completely.

**Sharpen blades.** Easier to do at the end of the season when you remember the blade condition. Ready for spring use without delay.

**Disconnect the battery** on equipment with electric start. Store the battery in a cool dry place if possible — extends battery life.

**Spray exposed metal surfaces with a light coating of oil or rust preventative.** Particularly the underside of mower decks. Prevents rust over winter.

**Cover the equipment.** Use a fitted cover or tarp to prevent dust accumulation and protect from moisture.

**Battery-powered equipment.**

**Charge batteries to about 50%** before storage. Storing batteries fully charged or fully discharged shortens their life. Half-charged is the sweet spot.

**Remove batteries from the equipment** and store in a cool dry place. Don't leave batteries in equipment over months — risk of slow discharge or terminal corrosion.

**Clean the equipment** as you would petrol equipment. Remove grass and debris.

**Store in a dry location.** Battery equipment is generally less robust to moisture than petrol equipment.

**Sprayers and chemistry equipment.**

**Drain all chemicals.** Don't store equipment with chemicals in the tank or hoses over winter.

**Rinse thoroughly with clean water** — multiple rinses to remove any chemical residue.

**For equipment used with persistent chemicals** (picloram in Gold, sulfonylureas in Sedge Pro), do a more aggressive cleanout: ammonia rinse or specialised sprayer cleaner per the chemical manufacturer's guidelines.

**Allow to dry completely** before storage.

**Lubricate moving parts** (pump, valves, trigger mechanisms).

**Store hoses uncoiled** or in large coils. Tight coils create kinks that fail next season.

**Hand tools.**

**Clean off dirt, sap and rust.** Wire brush or steel wool for stubborn buildup.

**Apply a light coat of oil** to metal surfaces. Linseed oil works well; standard machine oil also fine.

**Sharpen edges** if needed (shovels, edgers, secateurs).

**Store hanging where possible** — prevents bending and damage from being stacked.

**Storage location.**

Dry. Above all, dry. Moisture is the enemy of lawn equipment.

Frost-free if possible. Cold doesn't damage most equipment but liquid in cooling systems can freeze and damage components. Manual handling of cold metal is also unpleasant.

Off the ground. Concrete floors can be cold and damp. Pallets or shelving underneath helps.

Accessible. You'll want to retrieve equipment in spring — store in a way that's not buried behind seasonal items you won't need.

**Spring start-up.**

After winter storage:

Check oil levels and top up or replace if needed.

Test the battery (electric start equipment) and recharge if needed.

Inspect blades for damage or rust.

Add fresh fuel.

Check tyre pressures.

Test run before relying on the equipment for serious work.

Equipment properly stored in winter starts cleanly and lasts longer. Equipment left in poor condition over winter typically needs significant intervention before spring use.
