# Should I buy or hire lawn equipment?

Depends on frequency of use and cost per use.

The general rule:

**Buy** equipment you'll use weekly or monthly. Mower, line trimmer, basic hand tools — own these.

**Hire** equipment you'll use once or twice a year. Aerator, scarifier, large-format spreader — hire these.

**Borrow or share** with neighbours for equipment used by multiple households (sometimes practical for ride-on mowers in adjoining properties).

Buy vs hire economics:

**Buy makes sense when:**

The equipment is used 10+ times per year.

The lawn or property requires regular intervention with the specific tool.

The total cost of ownership over the equipment's lifespan (typically 5 to 10 years for quality lawn equipment) is less than the cumulative hire cost.

You value the convenience of immediate availability over the hire process.

**Hire makes sense when:**

The equipment is used 1 to 3 times per year.

The upfront cost is significant and the equipment sits unused most of the time.

Storage space is limited.

The equipment requires expertise to operate safely (some specialist equipment is genuinely safer to hire-with-instruction than to buy).

Worked examples:

**Push rotary mower.** Used weekly through growing season (40+ uses per year). Buy. Total cost over 5 years $500 to $2,000 depending on quality. Hire would cost $50 to $80 per use = $2,000 to $3,200 per year. Easy buy decision.

**Core aerator.** Used annually or biennially. Buy cost $2,000 to $5,000 for quality unit. Hire cost $100 to $200 per use. Hire pays off until you've used it 10 to 25 times — that's 10 to 25 years. Easy hire decision for most home users.

**Scarifier.** Used annually. Same logic as core aerator. Hire.

**Line trimmer.** Used weekly. Buy. Quality electric or petrol $200 to $500.

**Spreader.** Used 3 to 6 times per year for fertiliser application. Buy if you'll keep using it; hire if you're testing whether you'll commit to a fertiliser programme. Quality rotary spreader $200 to $500.

**Ride-on mower.** Depends entirely on lawn size. Owners with 2000m²+ lawns mowing weekly: buy. Owners with smaller lawns: definitely hire if needed at all.

**Knapsack sprayer.** Used 4 to 8 times per year for fertiliser and chemistry application. Buy. Quality unit $80 to $250.

**Topsoil or compost spreading equipment.** Used once or twice a year. Hire.

**Trailer for transporting hired equipment.** Borrow, hire, or rent ute from neighbour for the day of equipment use.

Quality matters more than buy vs hire:

A cheap version of the equipment doesn't serve well whether you bought or hired it. Better to hire a quality professional unit than to buy a cheap consumer version of specialist equipment.

For owned equipment, prefer quality brands and pay for durability. Cheap mowers fail quickly; quality mowers last 10+ years with maintenance.

Hire process:

Most NZ regions have rural supply outlets, equipment hire shops, and Mitre 10 / Bunnings hire desks.

Book in advance for seasonal high-demand equipment (aerators in spring, scarifiers in autumn).

Inspect equipment before taking it. Note any existing damage.

Get instruction on operation if you're unfamiliar.

Return promptly to avoid late fees.

For first-time users of any specialised equipment, hire is often better than buy regardless of economics — lets you learn the equipment without commitment.

The realistic NZ home lawn pattern:

**Buy:** mower, line trimmer, spreader, sprayer, hand tools, blade sharpening tools.

**Hire:** aerator, scarifier, topdressing spreader, occasional specialist equipment.

**Either:** soil testing equipment, irrigation timers, premium specialist equipment depending on commitment level.

Don't accumulate equipment you'll rarely use. Storage space, maintenance burden, and unused capital are real costs.
