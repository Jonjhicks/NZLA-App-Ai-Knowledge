# What is the difference between a knapsack and pressure sprayer?

Both are manually pumped sprayers. The differences are capacity, ergonomics and pressure delivery mechanism.

**Knapsack sprayer.**

Backpack-style sprayer worn over both shoulders. Pump handle accessible while spraying so you can maintain pressure continuously.

Capacity: typically 12 to 20 litres.

Pumping mechanism: continuous lever or piston pump on the side of the sprayer that you operate while walking. Keeps pressure consistent during use.

Comfort: distributes weight across both shoulders. Suitable for extended use periods.

Cost: $80 to $300 for quality home models.

Best for:

Larger lawn areas requiring substantial water volume.

Extended spraying sessions where consistent pressure matters.

Owners who want to walk and spray simultaneously without stopping to repressurise.

**Pressure sprayer (also called hand pump sprayer or compression sprayer).**

Hand-carried sprayer with a built-in pump. Pumped before use to pressurise the tank, then sprayed until pressure drops.

Capacity: typically 1 to 8 litres for hand pump versions. Some larger 12 to 20 litre wheeled units exist but they're less common.

Pumping mechanism: separate pump handle on top of the tank. You pump to build pressure before spraying.

Comfort: hand-carried, so weight is in your hand. Tiring during longer sessions.

Cost: $20 to $150 for quality home models.

Best for:

Smaller applications or spot treatment.

Occasional use where simplicity matters more than extended capacity.

Budget-conscious users who don't need premium features.

How to choose:

**Lawn under 200m² or occasional spot treatment:** pressure sprayer is fine. 5 litre hand pump unit covers small applications and is easy to store.

**Lawn 200 to 600m² with regular spraying:** quality knapsack sprayer. 15 litre capacity covers most home lawn applications in one tank.

**Lawn over 600m² or commercial use:** larger knapsack or specialised wheeled sprayer.

What to consider when buying:

**Capacity for your lawn size.** A 15 litre knapsack at 8 L/100m² covers 187 m² per fill. Plan refills accordingly.

**Build quality.** Cheap sprayers leak, fail mechanically, or have plastic that degrades from chemical contact. Quality brands cost more but last longer and apply more accurately.

**Nozzle quality and options.** Quality sprayers come with multiple nozzle types (flat fan, air induction, cone) for different applications. Cheaper sprayers may have only one nozzle.

**Chemical compatibility.** Make sure the sprayer is rated for the chemistry you'll use. Some cheap sprayers degrade with herbicide use; quality units handle a wide range.

**Comfort features.** Adjustable straps, padding, drip-stop valves, pressure gauges, comfortable trigger. Add to cost but improve usability over thousands of applications.

Brands to consider:

**Knapsack:**

Cooper Pegler, Solo, Stihl, Birchmeier — professional and prosumer quality.

Hozelock, Spear & Jackson — home-grade options.

**Pressure sprayer:**

Solo, Stihl, Hozelock, Gardena — wide range from home to professional.

Avoid:

Cheap unbranded sprayers from budget retailers. Failure rate is high, accuracy is poor, chemical compatibility is unreliable.

Maintenance:

Both types need cleaning after each use:

Empty the tank completely.

Rinse with clean water (multiple rinses for chemical residue removal).

Drain hoses and nozzles.

Allow to dry before storage.

Lubricate pump seals periodically.

Replace nozzles when worn (typically every 100 hours of use).

Specialist applications:

**For very small precise applications** (specific spot treatment): 1 litre handheld trigger sprayers work. $10 to $30. Use for individual weeds or specific patches.

**For larger commercial work:** powered sprayers (battery or petrol) reduce the manual effort. Significantly more expensive but justify themselves for high-volume users.

**Wheeled or trolley sprayers** in 25 to 100 litre capacity ranges suit larger lawns or contractor work. $400 to $2,000+.

For typical NZ home lawn owners:

A quality 15 litre knapsack sprayer ($150 to $250) is the standard tool. Handles most home lawn spraying needs efficiently for many years with proper maintenance.

Hand pump pressure sprayer in 5 litre capacity ($50 to $100) is fine for owners with small lawns or limited spraying needs.

Either way, quality matters more than the type distinction. A quality pressure sprayer outperforms a cheap knapsack.
