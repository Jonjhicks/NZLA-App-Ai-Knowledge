# How do I clean my spreader?

Essential maintenance — fertiliser residue corrodes spreader mechanisms within months if left in place. Two cleaning approaches for two situations.

**Routine cleaning (after each use).**

Empty all remaining product from the hopper. Don't store with product still in it.

Brush out loose residue from the hopper, deflector, and feed mechanism. A stiff brush works for most residue.

Wipe down the metal surfaces with a damp cloth.

Allow to dry completely before storage.

This routine takes 5 to 10 minutes after each use. Done consistently, prevents most corrosion problems.

**Thorough cleaning (periodically, or after wet conditions).**

When the spreader has been used in damp conditions, with corrosive products, or has visible buildup:

Empty completely.

Rinse the hopper with water. For metal hoppers, take care not to leave standing water that promotes corrosion.

Use a stiff brush in the rinse water to scrub out residue.

For stubborn buildup, a wire brush or steel wool removes encrusted material.

Rinse the deflector plate and feed mechanism thoroughly.

Lubricate moving parts (gate mechanism, hopper hinge, calibration adjuster) with light oil or lubricant after cleaning.

Allow to dry completely (24 hours+) before storage.

Why this matters:

Most lawn fertilisers contain salts that are corrosive to metal. Granular fertiliser residue holds moisture against the metal surface and accelerates corrosion.

Spreaders left uncleaned with fertiliser residue inside develop:

Rust on the hopper and deflector.

Sticky residue that affects accurate calibration.

Frozen or sticking feed mechanisms.

Eventual mechanical failure of moving parts.

A spreader that costs $200 to $500 new can be ruined within 2 to 3 years by lack of cleaning. The 5 minutes per use prevents this.

Special cases:

**Seed in the spreader.** Seed residue can be left in the spreader temporarily but should be removed before storage — birds and rodents can detect even small seed quantities and damage the equipment trying to access them.

**Pre-emergent herbicide residue.** Some pre-em chemistry leaves residue that's harmful to grass seed in subsequent uses. Thorough cleaning between herbicide and seed applications.

**Different products that might mix.** Cleaning between application of different products prevents cross-contamination. Particularly important when switching between fertiliser types (e.g. high-N to low-N) or between fertiliser and herbicide-impregnated products.

**Wet conditions during use.** If you've spread in damp conditions, more thorough drying and cleaning is essential. Damp residue accelerates corrosion.

Storage considerations:

Store cleaned spreader in dry covered location.

Hang or store off the ground if possible — promotes drying and prevents moisture from reaching the underside.

Cover with a tarp or fitted cover to prevent dust accumulation.

For winter storage (if no use planned for months):

Complete thorough cleaning.

Lubricate all moving parts more generously than routine.

Store in a frost-free dry location if possible.

Inspect before first spring use — sometimes problems develop during storage.

Spreader-specific notes:

**Rotary spreaders** have more moving parts (gear drive, throwing impeller) and need more attention to lubrication after cleaning.

**Drop spreaders** are simpler — basically a hopper with a regulated outlet. Less to clean and lubricate.

**Tow-behind spreaders** for ride-on mowers need similar cleaning to walk-behind units, plus attention to the tow attachment hardware.

For typical NZ home lawn use:

Quick clean after each use. Thorough clean monthly during active use season. Storage clean at end of season.

This pattern keeps a quality spreader functioning accurately for 10 to 15 years.

Common mistakes:

**Storing with product in the hopper.** The cause of most preventable spreader damage.

**Hosing out and storing wet.** Worse than leaving product in. Provides moisture for corrosion without removing the residue.

**Skipping lubrication.** Moving parts seize over time without periodic lubrication.

**Using wrong brushes.** Don't use brushes contaminated with herbicide on spreaders that will spread seed or sensitive products. Dedicated cleaning brushes for spreader maintenance.

A well-maintained spreader is a precise tool that delivers accurate application year after year. A neglected spreader becomes increasingly inaccurate and eventually fails.

The cleaning routine is short and prevents real problems. Worth the habit.
