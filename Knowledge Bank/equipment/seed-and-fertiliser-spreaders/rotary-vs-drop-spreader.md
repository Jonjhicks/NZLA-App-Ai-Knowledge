# What is the difference between a rotary spreader and a drop spreader?

Two different mechanisms with different strengths.

**Rotary spreaders** (also called broadcast spreaders) use a spinning disk at the bottom that flings the fertiliser or seed out in a wide arc as you walk. Coverage is wide — typically 2 to 4 metres in a single pass — which makes them fast to use across large areas. Trade-off is that the spread pattern isn't perfectly even; product falls heaviest right in front of the spreader and lighter at the edges of the throw. Standard practice is to walk overlapping passes (each pass overlapping the previous by about 50%) so the lighter edges combine into a consistent application.

**Drop spreaders** have an open slot along the bottom of the hopper that drops product straight down as you walk. Coverage is the exact width of the spreader (typically 400 to 800 mm). The application is perfectly even — every square centimetre under the spreader gets the same amount. Trade-off is that it's slower to cover large areas, and any gap between passes shows up as a visible stripe of unfertilised lawn (or seed) for weeks.

**When to use rotary:** large lawns, general fertiliser application where small variations in rate don't matter, situations where speed of coverage is the priority.

**When to use drop:** small or premium lawns where even application matters, edges of beds and paths (no broadcast onto garden), or sensitive areas where overdosing edges would damage other plants.

Calibrate either type for your specific product and walking pace before relying on label rates.
