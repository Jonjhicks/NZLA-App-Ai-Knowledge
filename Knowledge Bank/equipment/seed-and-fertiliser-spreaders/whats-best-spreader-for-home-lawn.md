# What is the best spreader for a home lawn?

Depends on lawn size and use pattern.

**For most NZ home lawns (200 to 800m²): quality push rotary spreader.**

A walk-behind broadcast spreader with a rotating disk that throws fertiliser in a wide arc as you walk. Covers ground efficiently, suits typical home lawn shapes, manageable cost.

Brands to consider:

Scott's, Earthway, Spyker, AccuPro — quality professional and prosumer brands.

Cost range: $150 to $400 for quality home models.

Capacity: 10 to 25 kg hopper handles most home lawn fertiliser applications.

Throwing width: 2 to 4 metres on standard rate settings.

What to look for:

Quality construction — metal frame and gearing rather than plastic where possible. Cheap spreaders fail within 2 to 3 years.

Adjustable rate settings with numbered markings — supports calibration for different products.

Side deflector for use near beds and edges — prevents overspread onto garden areas.

Wide pneumatic tyres rather than narrow plastic wheels — easier to push on uneven lawn.

Quality plastic or stainless steel hopper that resists corrosion.

**For premium small lawns (under 200m²) where precision matters: drop spreader.**

Walk-behind unit that drops fertiliser straight down through an opening at the bottom of the hopper. Coverage is the exact width of the spreader (typically 400 to 800 mm). Application is perfectly even — every square centimetre under the spreader gets the same amount.

Cost: $200 to $500 for quality home models.

Advantages over rotary:

Perfectly even application — no light edges or hot spots.

Excellent near garden beds and paths — no broadcast onto adjacent areas.

Better for sensitive applications (granular weed control, premium starter fertiliser).

Disadvantages:

Slower than rotary for covering ground.

Any gap between passes shows as a stripe of unfertilised lawn (or seed).

Limited to width of the spreader — multiple passes needed for larger areas.

Best for small lawns where the slower pace is manageable, or for premium applications where even distribution matters more than speed.

**For larger lawns (800m²+): tow-behind broadcast spreader.**

Larger capacity hoppers (25 to 80 kg) towed behind a ride-on mower or quad bike. Faster coverage of large areas.

Cost: $400 to $1,500 for quality tow-behind units.

Suited to properties with a ride-on mower already in use.

**For occasional use only: handheld spreader.**

Small (3 to 5 kg capacity) handheld unit, sometimes crank-operated, suitable for spot fertiliser application or small areas.

Cost: $30 to $80.

Not really a spreader for routine lawn use — too small and crude for accurate application. Useful for spot applications or very small lawn areas.

Practical decision guide:

**Lawn under 200m², premium care:** drop spreader.

**Lawn 200 to 800m², standard care:** rotary push spreader. The default for most NZ home lawns.

**Lawn 800m²+:** tow-behind spreader if you have a ride-on, otherwise larger rotary push.

**Multiple lawns or commercial use:** larger capacity rotary or specialised commercial equipment.

What to spend:

A quality $250 rotary spreader from a recognised brand handles typical home lawn use for 10 to 15 years.

A cheap $50 spreader from a budget retailer typically fails within 2 to 3 years.

The cost difference per year of use favours quality substantially. Buy once if possible.

What to avoid:

**Cheap "bargain" spreaders** — often have inadequate gearing, plastic frames that crack, hopper materials that corrode, and inaccurate calibration.

**Spreaders without calibration adjustment** — can only apply at one rate, which limits versatility.

**Spreaders without quality construction** — even good design doesn't save a spreader made of cheap materials.

Hire vs buy:

Quality home spreaders are affordable enough to buy if you'll fertilise regularly. Hire is rarely worth the trip for a spreader specifically.

Used spreader market is reasonable — quality used spreaders often available at moderate prices on online marketplaces.

For routine NZ home lawn use:

A quality rotary push spreader is the default tool. Calibrate it for the products you use, clean after each use, store properly, and it handles years of regular fertiliser application accurately.

The combination of quality rotary spreader plus consistent calibration plus proper cleaning produces accurate professional-grade fertiliser application on home lawns.
