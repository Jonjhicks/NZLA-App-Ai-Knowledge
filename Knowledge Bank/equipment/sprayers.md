# Sprayers

A sprayer is essential for foliar fertiliser, selective herbicide, and fungicide. Hand application isn't accurate enough for chemistry with tight rate requirements, and walking pace calibration on a proper sprayer produces far more consistent results than guessing.

## Knapsack vs pressure sprayer

**Knapsack sprayer** (backpack-style, 12 to 20 L): worn on both shoulders with a pump lever on the side you operate while walking. Maintains consistent pressure during a spray run. The right tool for most home lawn spraying.

**Pressure sprayer** (hand-carried, 1 to 8 L): pumped before use to build pressure, then sprayed until pressure drops. Fine for small applications and spot treatment. Hand-carried weight gets tiring over large areas. Best for lawns under 200 m² or occasional use.

For lawns 200 m² and over with any regular spraying programme: a quality 15 L knapsack is the standard tool. A 15 L tank at 8 L per 100 m² application rate covers 187 m² per fill — most home lawn applications in one or two tanks.

Quality brands: Cooper Pegler, Solo, Stihl, Birchmeier for professional use; Hozelock, Spear & Jackson for home. Avoid cheap unbranded units — inaccurate application, short life, unpredictable chemical compatibility.

## Calibration

Walking pace and nozzle output together determine your application rate per 100 m². Before using any product, calibrate by spraying water and measuring output over a known area at your normal walking pace. Adjust the flow control to match the target rate on the label. Do this once per product — don't assume the same setting works for different chemistry.

NZLA Indicate (spray colourant) is useful when doing a new application. You can see exactly where you've sprayed, which eliminates missed strips and double-application spots.

## Nozzle selection

Flat fan nozzles for most herbicide and fungicide work — consistent coverage across the spray width. Hold at 400 to 600 mm above the target and maintain consistent height during the pass.

Air induction nozzles reduce drift in moderate wind by producing larger droplets. Worth using for spot herbicide work near garden beds.

Replace nozzles annually for active users — worn nozzles change the spray pattern and application rate even if the opening looks intact.

## Cleaning — critical

Herbicide residue in a sprayer causes serious damage to subsequent applications. Even a rinse-clean sprayer that last held Gold (picloram + triclopyr) will injure broadleaf garden plants if used for liquid fertiliser next.

**After every use**: empty remaining spray, triple rinse with clean water. First rinse: 25% fill, pump and spray through the whole system. Second rinse: repeat. Third rinse: final flush. Wipe down exterior.

**When switching from herbicide to fertiliser or fungicide**: triple rinse, then fill with a 100 mL ammonia per 10 L water solution, circulate through the system, let stand an hour, drain, then triple rinse again.

**After Sedge Pro (halosulfuron)**: this chemistry is extraordinarily potent at trace concentrations. Flush with a 250 mL chlorine bleach per 10 L water solution, circulate, let stand an hour, drain, triple rinse. Thorough cleanout after every use.

**Dedicated sprayers**: for households with regular herbicide use alongside regular fertiliser and fungicide applications, running two separate sprayers (one herbicide only, one everything else) removes cross-contamination risk entirely. Quality knapsacks are affordable enough that two dedicated units are worth it.

## Uneven spray — common causes

Worn nozzle (replace it). Inconsistent pressure (pump continuously, maintain rhythm). Variable walking pace. Nozzle held at inconsistent height. Wind deflecting the spray. Use NZLA Indicate to reveal coverage gaps and confirm your technique is working.

## Safety during cleaning

Cleaning is when chemical exposure risk is highest — residue in rinse water can be concentrated. Wear chemical-resistant gloves and eye protection during sprayer cleanout. Wash hands and exposed skin immediately after.
