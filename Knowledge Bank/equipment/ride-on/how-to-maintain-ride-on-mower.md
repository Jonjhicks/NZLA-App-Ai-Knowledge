# How do I maintain a ride-on mower?

Five maintenance categories matter — routine checks, regular servicing, annual major service, seasonal preparation, and ongoing care.

**Routine checks (each use or weekly during growing season).**

**Oil level.** Check before each use. Top up with manufacturer-specified oil grade if low. Don't overfill.

**Tyre pressures.** Check weekly. Uneven tyre pressure causes uneven cutting heights — one side scalped, the other shaggy.

**Blade condition.** Inspect for damage, sharpness, balance. Replace damaged blades immediately — bent or chipped blades cause vibration that damages bearings and the mower deck.

**Air filter.** Check weekly during use. Replace when visibly dirty.

**Belt condition.** Inspect belts (transmission, deck drive) for wear and tension. Replace before they fail.

**Fuel.** Use fresh fuel. Don't run on old fuel that's been sitting for over 30 days.

**Cooling fins and engine.** Keep clean. Grass clippings and debris clogging the cooling fins cause engine overheating.

**Regular servicing (every 25 to 50 hours of use, or every 6 to 12 months for home users).**

**Oil and filter change.** Drain old oil while engine is warm, replace with fresh oil and new filter. Standard interval for most ride-ons.

**Spark plug.** Inspect, clean, replace if worn. Annual replacement for most home users.

**Air filter.** Replace at service interval regardless of appearance. Filters degrade internally even when they look clean.

**Fuel filter.** Replace at service interval.

**Battery.** Check terminals for corrosion. Clean if needed. Test battery health — replace every 3 to 5 years.

**Annual major service (typically pre-season).**

**Professional service** by dealer or qualified mechanic. Includes:

Engine compression test.

Carburetor cleaning and adjustment.

Transmission fluid check (hydrostatic models).

Deck spindle bearings inspection.

Blade balancing.

PTO clutch inspection.

Steering and suspension components.

Cost: $200 to $500 for typical home ride-on annual service.

Worth it. Catching issues early prevents major repairs later.

**Seasonal preparation.**

**End of season (autumn winding down):**

Clean equipment thoroughly. Remove grass clippings, mud, debris.

Sharpen blades for spring use.

Drain or stabilise fuel (run engine to clear fuel system, or add fuel stabiliser).

Change oil before winter storage.

Disconnect battery, store in cool dry location.

Cover the mower to prevent dust accumulation.

**Start of season (spring start-up):**

Reconnect and test battery.

Check oil level (top up or replace if needed).

Check tyre pressures.

Check blade condition.

Add fresh fuel.

Test run for 5 to 10 minutes before serious work.

If equipment hasn't started in months, give it a careful inspection and start sequence rather than expecting it to fire up immediately.

**Ongoing care (between services).**

**Keep clean.** Brush off grass clippings after each use. Hose off the deck periodically. Allow to dry before storage.

**Lubricate moving parts.** Steering joints, deck adjustment mechanisms, PTO engagement — light oil or grease as the manual specifies.

**Sharpen blades regularly.** Twice a season minimum for home use. After major impacts (hit a rock or tree root) inspect for damage and rebalance after sharpening.

**Watch for issues.** Listen to the engine — unusual sounds indicate developing problems. Vibration suggests blade or deck issues. Catch problems early.

Common maintenance mistakes:

**Ignoring oil changes.** Old oil contains contaminants that damage the engine. Stick to the service interval.

**Running on cheap or stale fuel.** Quality fresh fuel costs little; engine damage from stale fuel costs a lot.

**Skipping the annual service.** A $200 service catches problems that become $2,000 repairs if ignored.

**Mowing wet ground.** Causes corrosion, deck buildup, and traction issues. Wait for the lawn to dry.

**Mowing too low or hitting obstacles.** Damages blades, deck, and drive components. Set realistic mowing heights and watch for hazards.

**Improper storage.** Damp environments, fuel left in tank over months, batteries left connected — all cause damage that proper storage prevents.

For typical NZ home ride-on owner:

Weekly: visual checks, blade condition, tyre pressures.

Monthly during use: clean, check oil, check air filter.

Annually: professional service.

Seasonally: storage and start-up preparation.

This pattern keeps a quality ride-on running cleanly for 10 to 15 years. Neglect shortens lifespan dramatically and creates expensive repair bills.

Maintain it well and it'll outlast cheap equipment by years.
