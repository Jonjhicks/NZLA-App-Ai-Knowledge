# What is the difference between a core aerator and solid tine aerator?

Different tools for different jobs.

**Core (hollow tine) aerator** removes plugs of soil from the lawn. Tines are hollow tubes that punch in, capture a core of soil and grass, then pull it out as the machine moves forward. The cores sit on the surface as small plugs of soil and grass.

Result: actual space created in the soil profile. The removed soil leaves voids that don't refill from compression. Compaction reduces because the affected soil is physically removed.

Recovery: 4 to 6 weeks in active growing season. The lawn looks rough with visible holes and surface cores for 2 to 4 weeks until the cores break down and the holes start filling in.

Use for: compacted lawns, serious soil amendment, professional renovation work. The standard tool for genuine compaction relief.

**Solid tine aerator** punches holes without removing soil. Solid spikes push into the lawn and back out, displacing soil sideways rather than removing it.

Result: holes for a short time, but the soil around them eventually pushes back into the hole through normal compression and rain. Compaction relief is real but less durable than core aeration.

Recovery: minimal — solid tine work doesn't visibly damage the lawn the way coring does. Can be done with little disruption to lawn appearance.

Use for: routine maintenance, between major core aeration sessions, lawns that aren't severely compacted, situations where surface disruption needs to be minimised.

A third type worth knowing:

**Slit or sliced aerator** (verti-cutting style). Vertical knife blades cut narrow vertical slits in the lawn surface. Creates channels for water and air but doesn't remove soil.

Use for: golf greens and fine turf where precision is needed and recovery time is limited. Less common on home lawns.

How to choose:

For most home lawn aeration work — core aerator is the right tool. The real compaction relief justifies the recovery time. Hire annually or biennially.

For premium showcase lawns wanting frequent intervention — solid tine for routine maintenance plus annual core for the real work.

For sports turf — multiple aerator types in the maintenance pattern. Core for major work, solid tine for routine, slit for specific applications.

Equipment hire vs ownership:

Core aerators are expensive to own ($3,000 to $10,000+ for a quality unit) and only used a couple of times a year. Hiring is the realistic option for most home users — $100 to $200 per day from rural supply or equipment hire shops.

Solid tine aerators are cheaper and some home owners buy ($300 to $800 for a tow-behind or push model). Used more frequently because they're easier on the lawn.

For most home lawn owners:

Hire a core aerator annually or biennially. Don't bother with solid tine equipment unless you have a specific use case for frequent intervention.

A good annual core aeration combined with proper fertility programme and topdressing addresses most home lawn soil issues.
