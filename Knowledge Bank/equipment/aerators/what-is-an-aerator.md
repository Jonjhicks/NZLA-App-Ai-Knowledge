# What is an aerator and what does it do?

An aerator is a tool with tines that punch into the lawn surface to create holes in the soil. The holes let air, water, fertiliser and root growth penetrate the profile more easily — addressing soil compaction that builds up over time.

Two main types:

**Core (hollow tine) aerator.** Tines are hollow tubes that pull plugs (cores) of soil and grass out as you walk. The plugs come out and sit on the surface as you work, then break down over a couple of weeks. Creates roughly 15 mm diameter holes about 75 to 100 mm deep.

This is the standard professional choice for serious compaction relief. The removed cores create real space in the soil profile for roots, water and air. Recovery from coring takes 4 to 6 weeks in active growing season.

**Solid tine aerator.** Tines are solid spikes that punch holes without removing soil. Push the soil sideways rather than removing it. Less aggressive than core aeration but quicker and less mess.

Useful for routine maintenance on lawns that aren't seriously compacted. Doesn't address compaction as effectively as core aeration because no soil is removed.

Why aerate at all:

Soil under lawn compacts over time from foot traffic, mower wheels, settling of soil after rain, root growth pressing soil particles together. Compacted soil:

Restricts root growth (roots can't push through dense soil)

Reduces water infiltration (water runs off rather than soaking in)

Reduces oxygen at root zone (anaerobic conditions kill roots)

Restricts fertiliser uptake (granular fertiliser sits on the surface)

Holds water at the surface (creates ideal moss conditions)

Aeration relieves these issues by creating physical channels through the compacted layer.

Frequency:

Light home lawn use on good soil: every 2 to 3 years.

Standard home lawn: every 18 to 24 months.

Heavy use lawn (kids and dogs): annually.

Clay or compacted soils: annually or twice a year.

Sports turf or commercial: twice a year minimum.

When to aerate:

Spring (September through November) or autumn (March through April) when active growth supports recovery within 4 to 6 weeks.

Don't aerate in summer heat (lawn stressed) or winter (slow recovery).

After aeration:

NZLA Starter granular at 1.5 kg per 100m² to support recovery.

NZLA Aerate (soil-amending polymer) on heavy clay to fill the holes with chemistry that improves the soil structure beyond what the holes alone deliver.

Topdress with sand or sandy loam to gradually amend the soil profile through the open holes.

Watering normally — the lawn will recover within 4 to 6 weeks during growing season.
