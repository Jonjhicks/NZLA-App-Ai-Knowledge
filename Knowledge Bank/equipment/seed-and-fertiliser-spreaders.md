# Seed and Fertiliser Spreaders

A spreader applies granular fertiliser, seed, and granular products evenly across the lawn. Spreading by hand is inaccurate — you'll get hot spots and gaps that show for weeks. A calibrated spreader produces consistent application every time.

## Rotary vs drop

**Rotary (broadcast) spreaders** use a spinning disk that throws product in a wide arc as you walk. Coverage is 2 to 4 metres wide per pass. Fast for covering large areas. The spread pattern is heaviest in the centre and lighter at the edges, so overlapping passes by about 50% evens it out. Minor variation in application rate across the lawn isn't a problem for most fertiliser applications.

**Drop spreaders** release product straight down through a slot at the bottom of the hopper. Coverage is the exact width of the spreader (400 to 800 mm). Application is perfectly even under the spreader. Slower to cover area. Any gap between passes shows as a visible unfertilised stripe.

Use rotary for most home lawn fertiliser and seed applications — efficient, fast enough, adequate accuracy. Use drop for premium small lawns where even application really matters, or near garden beds where broadcast onto garden plants would cause damage.

## Which spreader for a home lawn

**Most NZ home lawns (200 to 800 m²):** quality push rotary spreader, $150 to $400. Metal frame and gearing where possible, adjustable rate settings with numbered markings, side deflector for use near garden edges, wide tyres for pushing on uneven lawn.

**Premium small lawn (under 200 m²) or accuracy-critical applications:** drop spreader, $200 to $500.

**Lawn 800 m² or larger with a ride-on:** tow-behind rotary spreader, $400 to $1,500.

Buy quality. A cheap $50 spreader from a budget retailer typically fails within 2 to 3 years from corrosion and worn gearing. A quality $250 model from Scotts, Earthway, Spyker, or AccuPro handles a decade of regular use.

Calibrate the spreader for each product and your walking pace before relying on label rates. Different product densities and particle sizes behave differently through the same spreader setting. Most NZLA granular products have recommended spreader settings on the label.

## Cleaning — essential maintenance

Fertiliser residue left in a spreader after use corrodes the metal within months. Salts in fertilisers hold moisture against steel and accelerate rust.

After each use: empty remaining product, brush loose residue from the hopper and mechanisms, wipe down metal surfaces with a damp cloth, allow to dry completely before storing.

Periodically (after use in damp conditions or any visible buildup): rinse the hopper, scrub with a stiff brush in water, rinse mechanisms, lubricate moving parts with light oil.

Never store with product in the hopper.

Before winter storage: thorough clean, lubricate all moving parts, store in dry covered location.

Common mistakes: storing with product in the hopper (most common cause of spreader damage), hosing out and putting away wet, skipping lubrication.

A well-maintained spreader is precise for 10 to 15 years. A neglected one becomes inaccurate and mechanically unreliable within a few seasons.
