# What is a reel mower?

A reel mower (also called cylinder mower) cuts grass using a horizontally rotating cylinder of blades that meets a fixed bedknife at the bottom. Each rotation of the cylinder produces a scissor-style cut as the blades pass over the bedknife — clean, precise, and dramatically different from the chopping action of a rotary mower.

How it works:

The reel (cylinder of curved blades, typically 5 to 11 blades depending on quality) rotates rapidly as the mower moves forward. The leading edge of each blade meets the fixed bedknife at the bottom of the mower, cutting any grass between them.

Grass blades caught between the reel and bedknife are sliced through cleanly — like a pair of scissors.

The contact between reel and bedknife is precisely set. Too tight and the blades clash, damaging both reel and bedknife. Too loose and the grass folds over instead of being cut cleanly.

Adjustment of reel-to-bedknife contact is the critical maintenance task on reel mowers — typically called "lapping" or "backlapping" when done properly.

Advantages of reel mowers:

**Cleaner cut quality.** Scissor cut produces a clean tip on each blade. Rotary mowers chop and tear, producing rougher cuts that look noticeably less polished.

**Lower mowing heights possible.** Reel mowers cut cleanly down to 5 to 15 mm. Rotary mowers struggle below 25 mm without scalping.

**Better stripe definition.** The clean cut and the roller behind the reel bend grass blades in one direction, creating sharp stripe definition. The signature look of premium showcase lawns and sports turf.

**Quieter operation than petrol rotary mowers** — particularly the manual push reel mowers, which are very quiet.

**Less stress on grass.** The clean cut heals faster than a torn rotary cut, reducing disease pressure.

Disadvantages:

**Higher cost.** Quality reel mowers $1,000 to $5,000+ for petrol-powered. Premium professional models much more. Manual push reel mowers $200 to $600.

**More maintenance.** Reel-to-bedknife adjustment, blade sharpening, regular inspection. Not as user-maintainable as rotary mowers.

**Doesn't handle long grass well.** A reel mower can't cut grass much taller than its own cylinder diameter. If grass gets ahead of you, you need a rotary mower to bring it back.

**Doesn't handle rough conditions.** Sticks, stones, hard objects damage the reel and bedknife. Lawn surface needs to be smooth.

**Slower than equivalent petrol rotary mowers** at comparable price points.

When a reel mower makes sense:

**Premium home lawns mowed at 20 to 30 mm or lower.** The cut quality justifies the investment.

**Showcase lawns where stripe definition matters.** Photography, presentation, garden displays.

**Sports turf and golf turf.** Standard equipment in these applications.

**Lawns with premium grass varieties** (MDR, Dark Perennial Rye) where the cut quality showcases the cultivar's potential.

**Owners committed to mowing 1 to 3 times per week** during active growth.

When a reel mower doesn't make sense:

**Standard home lawns at 30 to 50 mm.** A rotary mower is fine for this height range.

**Lawns with rough conditions, uneven surfaces, debris.** Reel mowers don't tolerate this.

**Lawns mowed less than weekly during active growth.** Reel mowers can't catch up if the grass gets long.

**Budget-conscious owners.** The cost difference between rotary and reel is substantial.

For most NZ home lawns:

A quality rotary mower is the right tool. Reel mowers are specialty equipment for specific applications.

For premium lawn enthusiasts willing to commit to the maintenance and aesthetic standards reel mowing requires:

The transformation in lawn appearance is real. Worth the investment for owners who care about the difference.
