# What is the difference between a manual and petrol reel mower?

Power source and lawn size suitability.

**Manual push reel mowers.**

The reel is driven by the wheels — pushing the mower forward rotates the wheels, which drive the reel via internal gearing. No engine, no fuel, no electricity.

Pros:

Quiet operation — almost silent.

Zero emissions.

No fuel costs.

Light weight (typically 8 to 15 kg).

Minimal maintenance — just keep the reel sharp and clean.

Good exercise.

Cost-effective ($200 to $600 for quality units).

Cons:

Requires physical effort. Pushing a reel mower across a lawn takes more energy than walking with a power mower.

Limited to small lawns. Practical use limit around 200 to 300m². Larger lawns become a chore.

Doesn't handle long grass well — the reel jams if grass is too tall.

Requires firm consistent push pace for clean cutting.

Best for:

Small home lawns under 200m².

Owners who mow weekly or more during growing season.

People wanting quiet equipment.

Environmentally conscious owners.

Premium small lawns at low mowing heights (15 to 30 mm).

**Petrol-powered reel mowers.**

A petrol engine drives the reel separately from the wheels. The mower self-propels or requires light pushing to move forward.

Pros:

Handles larger areas efficiently — much faster than manual.

Consistent cutting action regardless of operator pace.

Handles longer grass than manual (still less than rotary, but more than manual reel).

Self-propelled models cover ground with minimal physical effort.

Cons:

Significant cost ($2,000 to $10,000+ for quality units).

Loud operation — petrol engine plus reel rotation.

Petrol and fuel mixing required.

Heavier than manual (40 to 80 kg+).

More complex maintenance — engine plus reel.

Best for:

Premium home lawns 200 to 1500m² where cut quality matters.

Larger properties wanting reel cut aesthetic.

Sports turf, golf turf, professional applications.

Owners with budget for premium equipment.

**Electric reel mowers (a middle ground).**

Some battery-powered reel mowers now exist. Quieter than petrol, more capable than manual, but currently limited in NZ market availability and at premium prices.

How to decide:

**Lawn under 200m²:** manual push reel works well if you mow weekly. Affordable, simple, effective.

**Lawn 200 to 500m²:** depends on commitment and budget. Manual works for fit owners on smaller end of this range. Petrol for larger end or convenience-focused owners.

**Lawn 500m²+:** petrol generally. Manual mowing at this size becomes impractical for most users.

**Multiple lawns or commercial use:** petrol, ride-on reel models, or contractor equipment.

Realistic NZ market:

Manual push reel mowers are readily available — Brill, Fiskars, Scott's, various brands $200 to $600.

Petrol reel mowers are more specialised — Toro, Honda, Allett, McLane and others. Less commonly stocked at general retailers; check specialist turf suppliers or commercial mower dealers.

Battery reel mowers are limited but growing in availability.

For NZ home lawn owners interested in reel mowing:

Start with manual push reel if your lawn is small enough. Test whether you enjoy reel mowing and commit to weekly mowing before investing in petrol equivalent.

For owners committed to premium lawn care on larger areas, petrol reel mower is the next step up.

Maintenance considerations:

Both types require regular reel-to-bedknife adjustment.

Both require periodic blade sharpening (typically annually for home use).

Both benefit from professional servicing periodically — reel mowers are precise equipment that benefits from expert maintenance.

Quality of cutting depends on adjustment as much as on the mower itself. A well-adjusted manual reel mower outperforms a poorly-adjusted expensive petrol unit.
