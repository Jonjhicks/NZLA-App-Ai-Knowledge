# What is a scarifier and do I need one?

A scarifier is a powered tool with vertical blades that cut through the thatch layer and pull dead material out of the lawn. Different to mowing — a mower cuts the leaf tips horizontally; a scarifier slices vertically into the lawn surface.

The job is removing thatch (the dead, partially decomposed organic layer that builds up between the grass and the soil over time). A thick thatch layer holds moisture against the crowns of the plant, restricts air movement, prevents fertiliser and water reaching the soil, and provides a perfect environment for fungal disease.

A thin thatch layer (5 to 10 mm) is fine and actually beneficial — protects roots, conserves moisture, cycles nutrients. The problem is when it builds beyond 15 to 20 mm.

Do you need one? Depends on the lawn.

**Yes, you need scarifying** if the lawn feels spongy underfoot, water beads on the surface instead of soaking in, you can see a thick mat of brown dead material when you part the grass, or the lawn is on a slow decline despite proper fertilising.

**No, you don't need it** if the lawn is healthy, fertilising and watering response are normal, and the thatch layer is under 15 mm.

For frequency, most lawns benefit from light scarifying once a year (autumn or spring) and a heavier scarify every 2 to 3 years if thatch is building.

Hire one rather than buy unless you've got several lawns to do. Petrol scarifier hire is reasonably priced from most equipment hire shops.

After scarifying, the lawn looks rough for 4 to 6 weeks. NZLA Starter granular and Liquid Starter as a foliar support the recovery.
