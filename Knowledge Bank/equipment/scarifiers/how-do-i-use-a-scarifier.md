# How do I use a scarifier?

Four steps from preparation to follow-up.

**Step 1 — Prepare the lawn.**

**Mow short the day before scarifying.** Drop the mowing height to 20 to 25 mm. The shorter grass exposes the surface and lets the scarifier reach the thatch underneath.

If your lawn is at 50 mm or higher, don't drop straight to 20 mm in one cut. Reduce over 2 to 3 mows in the week before scarifying.

**Check soil moisture.** Soil should be slightly damp but not saturated. Dry soil makes scarifying ineffective (the tines can't penetrate properly). Wet soil tears the lawn and creates muddy mess.

If the soil is dry, water the lawn lightly 24 to 48 hours before scarifying to bring moisture into the right range.

**Mark or note obstacles.** Sprinkler heads, manhole covers, garden bed edges. Plan your route to avoid hitting them.

**Step 2 — Set the scarifier depth.**

Most scarifiers have adjustable depth settings.

**Light maintenance scarifying:** depth 5 to 10 mm into the thatch. Removes surface thatch without major lawn damage. Suitable for routine annual work.

**Moderate renovation scarifying:** depth 10 to 20 mm. Aggressive removal of significant thatch. Lawn looks rough for several weeks after.

**Heavy renovation scarifying:** depth 20+ mm. Major thatch removal verging on renovation. Lawn looks damaged for 4 to 8 weeks during recovery.

Set the depth based on your thatch level and how aggressive you want to be. Start at lighter setting and increase if needed rather than starting too deep.

**Step 3 — Scarify the lawn.**

Walk the scarifier across the lawn at moderate pace. Don't rush — the scarifier needs time to engage the thatch and pull it to the surface.

**Walk two directions for thorough work.** First pass in one direction (north-south), then a second pass at right angles (east-west). The two passes catch thatch the single pass misses.

For very heavy thatch, three passes (adding a diagonal direction) provides the most thorough work.

The scarifier pulls thatch and dead material to the surface as it moves. After the first pass, the lawn looks rough and covered in dead material.

**Step 4 — Cleanup and recovery support.**

**Rake out the scarified material.** Use a fan rake or leaf rake to collect the pulled-up thatch and dead grass. Significant quantity from substantial scarifying — wheelbarrow loads of material on heavy work.

The lawn underneath looks rough and exposed.

**Apply NZLA Starter granular** at 1.5 kg per 100m². Drives root recovery in the stressed lawn.

**Apply seed if substantial bare areas have been exposed.** Use seed matching the existing lawn at 2 kg per 100m² oversowing rate. Rake in lightly.

**Water consistently** if conditions are dry. Recovery needs adequate moisture.

**Hold off mowing for 1 to 2 weeks** after substantial scarifying. Lets the lawn start recovering before mowing stress is added back.

**Apply NZLA Liquid Starter** as a foliar at 4 to 5 weeks after scarifying. Supports establishment of new seed and recovery of damaged grass.

**Resume normal mowing** at lifted height (40 to 50 mm for ryegrass) once the lawn looks established. Returns to normal heights over 2 to 3 mows.

Common mistakes:

**Scarifying at too aggressive a depth.** First-time users often set the depth too deep. Start light, go deeper next time if needed.

**Skipping the depth adjustment.** Default factory depth on rental scarifiers may not suit your lawn. Adjust before starting.

**Scarifying in wrong conditions.** Wet ground tears the lawn. Dry hard ground makes scarifying ineffective.

**Not cleaning up the pulled material.** Leaving scarified material on the lawn smothers recovery. Rake out promptly.

**No recovery support.** Skipping NZLA Starter, watering, and seeding leaves the lawn struggling rather than recovering well.

**Wrong timing.** Don't scarify in summer heat or winter cold — recovery requires active growing season.

Hire vs own:

Most home users hire scarifiers — annual or biennial use doesn't justify owning. $80 to $150 per day from equipment hire shops.

Book in advance for the spring and autumn high-demand windows.

Inspect before taking the equipment. Note depth settings and any existing damage.

For owners doing significant scarifying work regularly (some sports turf, large lifestyle properties), owning may be justified. Quality petrol scarifiers $1,500 to $5,000+ for home-grade units.

For typical NZ home lawn:

Annual or biennial light to moderate scarifying in spring or autumn keeps thatch under control. The work is straightforward with proper preparation and recovery support.
