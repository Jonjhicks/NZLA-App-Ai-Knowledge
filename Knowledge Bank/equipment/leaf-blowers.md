# Leaf Blowers

Whether you need a leaf blower depends on whether you have deciduous trees dropping significant leaf volume onto your lawn. If you do, it earns its place fast. Leaves left on the lawn block light, hold moisture, and set up  conditions heading into autumn and winter. Raking works, but a leaf blower covers ground 5 to 10 times faster.

If you don't have meaningful leaf drop, a leaf blower is equipment for occasional use — hard to justify.

## Types

**Cordless battery handheld** ($150 to $400) is the right choice for most NZ home lawns. Quieter than petrol, easy to maintain, enough runtime for a typical autumn session. A 4Ah+ battery on a quality 36V+ unit handles most suburban lawns without needing a recharge.

**Petrol handheld** ($200 to $500) delivers more power and longer runtime with no battery limits. Worth it on larger properties or for extended sessions. Louder and requires fuel maintenance.

**Backpack petrol** ($400 to $1,500) for larger properties or extended autumn clearing. Carries the weight on your back, which helps on long sessions.

What to look for: CFM (cubic feet per minute) matters more than MPH. 400+ CFM for meaningful leaf movement. Under 70 dB is acceptably quiet; over 80 dB is going to generate neighbour comments.

## Practical use

Blow leaves toward a collection point — tarpaulin, garden bed, compost pile. Work with a dry lawn; wet leaves stick and resist blowing. Use lower power settings where possible — less noise, less scatter. Wear hearing protection for extended sessions.

If you're in a suburban area, check what time restrictions apply and stick to reasonable hours.

## Leaf management

Fallen leaves are useful. Compost them if you have garden beds. Small volumes of mulched leaves (shredded by running the mower over them) can be left on the lawn as organic matter — but significant leaf accumulation blocking light needs to come off. Don't leave piles of damp leaves sitting on turf for days heading into winter.
