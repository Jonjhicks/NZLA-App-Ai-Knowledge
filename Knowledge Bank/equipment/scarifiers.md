# Scarifiers

A scarifier (sometimes called a dethatcher, power rake, or vertical mower — the terms are used interchangeably in NZ) removes the thatch layer that builds up between the grass and the soil over time. It does this with vertical rotating blades that cut through the surface and pull dead material out.

Some thatch is fine — 5 to 10 mm protects roots, conserves moisture, and cycles nutrients. The problem starts above 15 to 20 mm. Thick thatch restricts water and air movement, holds -promoting moisture against crowns, and blunts the response to fertiliser.

## Do you need to scarify?

**Yes** if: the lawn feels spongy underfoot, water beads on the surface rather than soaking in, you can see a thick brown mat of dead material when you part the grass, or the lawn is on a slow decline despite regular fertilising and watering.

**No** if: the lawn is healthy, fertilising and watering responses are normal, and thatch is under 15 mm.

## How to use a scarifier

Mow the lawn short (20 to 25 mm) the day before. Don't go straight from 50 mm to 20 mm in one cut — step down over two to three mows in the preceding week.

Soil should be slightly damp but not wet. Bone-dry soil resists the tines; saturated soil tears and creates muddy mess.

Set the depth based on your thatch level. Light maintenance (5 to 10 mm): removes surface thatch without major disruption. Moderate renovation (10 to 20 mm): aggressive removal of significant build-up, lawn looks rough for several weeks. Start conservative — you can always go deeper next time.

Walk two passes at right angles (north-south then east-west) for thorough coverage.

Rake out all the pulled material immediately. Don't leave it on the surface. The lawn underneath looks rough and exposed — that's normal.

Apply NZLA Starter granular at 1.5 kg per 100m². Oversow bare areas with NZLA Perennial Rye at 2 kg per 100m² if significant bare patches appeared. Water consistently. Hold off mowing for 1 to 2 weeks after heavy scarifiers. Apply NZLA Liquid Starter as a foliar at 4 to 5 weeks into recovery.

## Timing

Spring (September to October) or autumn (March to April). Active growth drives recovery within 4 to 6 weeks. Don't scarify in summer heat or winter — the lawn can't recover adequately.

## Scarifier vs dethatcher

Technically, scarifiers cut deeper and more aggressively with fixed steel blades. Dethatchers use spring-tined or flexible blades that rake rather than cut. In NZ practice, both terms are used for essentially the same equipment. When hiring, ask about depth capability rather than worrying about the name.

## Hire vs own

Annual or biennial use doesn't justify owning. Quality petrol scarifiers cost $1,500 to $5,000. Hire from equipment hire shops or rural supply for $80 to $150 per day. Book in advance for the spring and autumn windows — demand is high.
