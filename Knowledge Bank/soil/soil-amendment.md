# Soil Amendment for Lawns

Soil amendment means adding something to change the physical or chemical properties of the soil. The options homeowners most often ask about are gypsum for clay, sand top dressing, and sulphur for alkaline soils.

## Gypsum on Heavy Clay

The claim that gypsum improves clay soil is widespread but significantly overstated for typical NZ lawn conditions.

Gypsum (calcium sulphate) works by a specific mechanism: the calcium it supplies displaces sodium from the soil's exchange sites. When sodium is the dominant cation in a clay soil (sodic soil), the clay particles disperse rather than flocculate, resulting in poor structure, waterlogging, and hard setting. Replacing sodium with calcium restores the clay's ability to flocculate and form aggregates. The result is improved drainage-and-soil-moisture and aeration.

**Where gypsum genuinely helps:**
- Sodic soils with measurably high sodium (sodium saturation above 5% on a soil test).
- Soils with high magnesium relative to calcium (gypsum restores the Ca:Mg balance).
- Surface-sealing soils where calcium helps maintain pore stability.

**Where gypsum doesn't help:**
- Standard NZ clay soils without sodium or magnesium imbalance. The clay structure problem is from particle size and density, not chemistry, and gypsum doesn't change particle size.
- Compacted clay where the issue is physical core-aeration, not chemistry.
- Acidic clay soils where lime is more appropriate (gypsum is pH-neutral; it doesn't correct acidity).

For most NZ home lawn clay problems, the more effective tools are: core aeration to physically open the soil, NZLA Aerate liquid applied into aeration holes to improve water movement through the clay profile, and sandy loam top dressing over multiple seasons to gradually improve the surface layer.

If you're dealing with a soil that's had sodium ingress (coastal flooding, irrigation-general with high-sodium bore water), gypsum is worth a soil test to confirm and then a targeted application. Without that specific chemistry condition, you're better served by physical management.

## Sand Top Dressing for Clay Improvement

Regular sand top dressing genuinely improves heavy clay soil, but it takes years and requires the right material.

The process: fine sand or sandy loam applied to the lawn surface gets worked into the top layer of soil by aeration, worm activity, rain, and gravity. Over repeated applications, the proportion of sand in the surface 100 mm gradually increases. The resulting mixed layer drains better than pure clay and develops better structure for root development.

**Realistic timeline:**
- Years 1 to 2: minimal visible change, but the process is underway.
- Years 3 to 5: noticeably better drainage-and-soil-moisture after heavy rain.
- Years 5 to 10: surface soil substantially improved.

**Practical programme:**
- Light top dress every autumn or spring — 3 to 5 mm depth per application.
- Pair with core aeration immediately before top dressing so the material falls into the cores and makes physical contact with the soil rather than sitting on the thatch layer.
- 3 to 5 mm per application: light enough not to smother the grass, heavy enough to contribute to the cumulative improvement.

**Material matters.** Use sandy loam (60 to 80% sand, 20 to 40% loam) rather than pure builders' sand or beach sand. Pure coarse sand on top of clay creates a layered profile that can actually reduce drainage-and-soil-moisture by sitting as a distinct layer rather than mixing with the clay. Sandy loam mixes more naturally with the existing soil.

For a brand new lawn on heavy clay, the faster path is importing 100 to 200 mm of sandy loam and incorporating it into the top layer before sowing. Years of top dressing won't achieve what good soil preparation at establishment provides.

## Sulphur for Alkaline Soils

Sulphur has two applications for lawns: correcting alkaline soil pH and addressing sulphur deficiency.

**pH lowering:** where soil pH is above 7.5 and persistent iron and manganese chlorosis is present, elemental sulphur applied over time gradually reduces pH toward the 6.0 to 6.5 target. Soil bacteria convert elemental sulphur to sulphuric acid, which reacts with soil carbonates and brings pH down.

This is slow. Expect 6 to 12 months for the bacterial conversion to happen and pH movement to begin, and multiple seasons of application to shift pH by a meaningful amount (0.5 to 1.0 pH units). It's also difficult to over-apply on genuinely alkaline soils, but on neutral or already slightly acid soils, excess sulphur causes its own problems. Test first.

Typical rate for elemental sulphur to lower pH: 2 to 5 kg per 100 m² to target a 0.5 unit pH reduction. Apply in autumn, water in, then monitor over the following 12 to 24 months.

Ammonium sulphate as a fertiliser provides sulphur alongside nitrogen and produces a mild acidifying effect over time. It's a good fit for lawns that need both nitrogen and a gradual pH reduction.

**Sulphur deficiency:** on ex-pasture sites and lifestyle blocks with a history of low fertiliser input, sulphur can be limiting. The visual sign is uniform pale colour across the whole lawn that doesn't respond to nitrogen application. Standard NZLA granular fertiliser programmes cover sulphur for regularly maintained lawns.

**In the meantime:** while sulphur works on the underlying pH problem, NZLA Iron+ chelated iron treats the immediate chlorosis symptom. Chelated iron remains plant-available at high pH where free iron is locked up in the soil. Apply foliar on a monthly schedule during the growing season while the longer-term pH correction is underway.
