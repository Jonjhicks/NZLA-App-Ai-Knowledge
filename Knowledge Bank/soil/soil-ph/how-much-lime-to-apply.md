# How much lime do I apply to my lawn?

Depends on how far the pH needs to move and the soil type.

General rule for NZ mineral soils: 1 tonne per hectare of good quality lime (80% CaCO3 or better) raises pH by about 0.1 units. That's 10 kg per 100 m² to raise pH by 0.1 units.

Practical conversion for home lawns:
- pH 5.5 to target 6.5 = needs 1.0 pH unit lift = 100 kg per 100 m² total.
- pH 5.0 to target 6.5 = needs 1.5 pH unit lift = 150 kg per 100 m² total.

That's a lot of lime on a small area, so split it across multiple applications over several seasons. A single heavy application can shock the soil biology and burn the lawn.

Practical programme for a typical home lawn:
- Spread 5 to 10 kg per 100 m² each autumn for 2 to 3 years.
- Total over the programme: 15 to 30 kg per 100 m².
- Retest after the third year, adjust the programme based on actual movement.

Soil type matters:
- Sandy soils need less lime than the calculation suggests (low CEC, less buffer).
- Heavy clay soils need more (high CEC, more buffer).
- High organic matter soils need more.

Lime types:
- Agricultural lime (calcium carbonate, CaCO3): standard, slow-acting, most common.
- Dolomite lime (calcium plus magnesium carbonate): adds magnesium too, useful if magnesium is low.
- Hydrated lime (calcium hydroxide): much faster acting but harsher on the lawn, use sparingly and at lower rates.
- Quicklime (calcium oxide): industrial application, not for home lawn use.

For NZ home lawn use, standard agricultural lime is the go-to. Spread with a calibrated spreader or by hand on small areas, water in lightly to settle the dust.

Always combine with a soil test before and after. The actual pH movement varies with soil type and the math is only a starting point.
