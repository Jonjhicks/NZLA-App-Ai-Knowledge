# Can I over-apply lime to my lawn?

Yes, easily. Over-liming a lawn is one of the most common mistakes from well-intended advice.

What happens with too much lime:
- pH goes above 7.0 to 7.5, making iron, manganese, zinc, copper less available to the plant.
- Lawn turns pale and chlorotic (yellow-green) even with adequate iron present in the soil.
- Phosphorus availability drops too.
- Soil biology shifts, beneficial fungi reduce, some pathogens favoured.
- Recovery takes years and may need sulphur application to bring pH back down.

How over-liming happens:
- Liming based on "I heard lime is good" instead of soil test.
- Adding lime annually as part of a "programme" without testing.
- Inheriting a lawn that's already at neutral pH and adding lime.
- Single heavy application instead of split applications over time.
- Confusing lime with gypsum (gypsum doesn't change pH significantly, lime does).

If you've over-limed:
- Stop adding any more lime immediately.
- Test the soil to confirm pH.
- If pH is above 7.5, sulphur application can bring it back down (slow process over years).
- Use NZLA Iron+ (chelated iron) to address the immediate chlorosis. Chelated iron stays plant-available at high pH where free iron doesn't.
- Switch fertilisers to ammonium-based products (ammonium sulphate, urea) which gradually lower pH through their nitrogen chemistry.

Prevention:
- Soil test before applying lime.
- Don't lime "just in case".
- Don't apply more than 5 to 10 kg per 100 m² in any one application.
- Wait 2 to 3 years between programme applications to let the soil respond.
- Retest after each programme cycle to track actual movement.

For lawns that have always been on the acidic side and have only had standard fertiliser programmes, light lime over a few seasons is unlikely to over-shoot. Lawns near concrete, in lime-rich regions, or on shell-based fill are more vulnerable to over-liming.
