# Why is my soil acidic?

Most NZ soils are naturally on the acidic side. A few reasons.

NZ rainfall. Wet climate leaches calcium and magnesium out of the topsoil over thousands of years, pH drops over geological time. NZ soils overall are more acidic than dry climate soils like those in central Australia or the western US.

Parent material. Volcanic-origin soils in upper NI tend toward acidic. Granite and greywacke-origin soils in much of NZ are also naturally acidic.

Native vegetation history. Native NZ podocarp and beech forests historically dropped acidic litter that accumulated in topsoil over centuries.

Nitrogen fertiliser. Long-term use of nitrogen fertilisers (urea, ammonium-based) gradually lowers soil pH. A lawn that's been fertilised regularly for 30 years is more acidic than one that hasn't been fertilised at all over the same time.

Acid rain (minor contribution in NZ but real). Industrial pollution adds slightly to soil acidification.

What this means for NZ lawn owners:
- Don't assume acidic soil is a problem someone caused. It's often the natural state.
- Periodic lime application maintains pH against the natural acidifying drift.
- A lawn that's been fertilised for years without lime probably has a pH around 5.0 to 5.5.
- A lawn on virgin soil (new build, ex-paddock) often has pH around 5.5 to 6.0 before any lawn programme.

Soil testing tells you where you actually are. Worth knowing before applying lime or sulphur, you can over-correct.

For lawns where pH is at 6.0 to 6.5, the soil is fine and lime isn't needed. Adding lime to a lawn that's already at target pH weakens the lawn and encourages chlorosis. Test first, then act.
