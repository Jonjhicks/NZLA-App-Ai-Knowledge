# What pH should my lawn soil be?

6.0 to 6.5 is the target for most NZ lawn grasses (ryegrass, tall fescue, fine fescue, kikuyu, couch). This is mildly acidic and matches what most NZ soils naturally sit at.

What happens outside that range:

Below 5.5 (acidic):
- Nutrient availability drops. Phosphorus gets tied up. Aluminium becomes more available and can be toxic to roots.
- Soil biology slows. Microbial activity that breaks down organic matter and cycles nutrients reduces.
- Lawn looks pale, thins out, weeds move in.
- Fix is lime application.

Above 7.5 (alkaline):
- Iron, manganese, zinc availability drops. Lawn looks pale (chlorosis) even with adequate iron applied.
- Phosphorus availability drops at very high pH.
- NZLA Iron+ chelated iron foliar feed helps because chelated iron stays plant-available at high pH.
- Fix for soil pH is sulphur application, slow to take effect.

Different species have slightly different optimums but they all overlap in the 6.0 to 6.5 zone:
- Ryegrass: 5.5 to 7.5 (tolerates wide range).
- Tall fescue: 5.5 to 7.5.
- Fine fescue: 5.5 to 6.5 (prefers slightly acidic).
- Kikuyu: 5.5 to 7.0.
- Couch: 5.5 to 7.5.
- Browntop: 5.0 to 6.5 (prefers acidic).

If pH is in the 6.0 to 6.5 range, no action needed. Spend your time on other things.

For lawns that are just slightly off (5.5 to 6.0 or 6.5 to 7.0), the issue is usually minor and the cost-benefit of correcting it is low. Bigger gaps from optimum (below 5.5 or above 7.5) are worth addressing.
