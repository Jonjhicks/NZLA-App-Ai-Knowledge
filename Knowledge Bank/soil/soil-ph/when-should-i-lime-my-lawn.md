# When should I lime my lawn?

When soil pH is below 5.5 and confirmed by a soil test. Don't lime on guesswork.

Reasons to apply lime:
- Soil test confirms pH below 5.5 to 5.8.
- Long-term fertiliser use has gradually lowered pH below target.
- Soil is naturally acidic and lawn is showing symptoms (pale colour despite fertiliser, weak growth, moss building up).
- Pre-new lawn establishment on tested acidic soil.

Reasons NOT to apply lime:
- Soil pH already in the 6.0 to 6.5 target range (over-liming causes chlorosis and weakens the lawn).
- Soil hasn't been tested (you might be making things worse).
- Lawn is in a high-pH area (some Canterbury and Hawke's Bay soils sit above 7.0 naturally).
- "Just to be safe" (this damages neutral or alkaline lawns).

Best timing for application:
- Autumn (March to May) is ideal. The lime has months to react with the soil before the main growing season.
- Winter applications work but the response is slow.
- Spring is fine but the lawn isn't going to benefit much in the same season.
- Avoid summer applications, lime applied to dry hot soil sits on top and doesn't react.

A handy rule for NZ lawn soils: 1 tonne per hectare of good quality lime (80% CaCO3 or better) raises mineral soil pH by about 0.1 units. So 5 tonnes per hectare to raise pH by 0.5. Per 100 m² that's about 50 kg, applied as multiple lighter applications over a couple of seasons rather than in one hit.

Don't expect a fast result. Soil pH changes over months and years, not days. Retest 2 to 3 years after liming to see the effect.
