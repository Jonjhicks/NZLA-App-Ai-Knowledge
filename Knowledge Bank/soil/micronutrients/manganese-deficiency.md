# Can my lawn be manganese deficient?

Yes, particularly on alkaline soils. Manganese deficiency is less common than iron deficiency but worth knowing about, especially in regions with naturally high pH soils (parts of Canterbury, Bay of Plenty, Hawke's Bay).

How it looks:
- Pale interveinal chlorosis similar to iron deficiency, but the pattern is more uniform across leaf age (iron deficiency shows mostly on younger leaves).
- Lawn responds partially to iron applications but doesn't fully resolve.
- Often appears alongside iron deficiency on alkaline soils (both micronutrients become less available at high pH).

What drives manganese deficiency:
- High soil pH (above 7.0 to 7.5).
- Recently limed soils where pH has spiked.
- Sandy soils with low organic matter.
- Very wet soils where manganese has been leached.

Diagnosis:
- Soil test that includes manganese can confirm.
- If iron applications haven't fully resolved chlorosis on an alkaline lawn, manganese is worth investigating.
- Visual diagnosis alone is hard because the symptoms overlap with iron deficiency.

Treatment:
- Manganese sulphate (commercial fertiliser supply) as foliar spray.
- NZLA Iron+ doesn't supply manganese specifically.
- Mn rate is much lower than iron rates - check the product label.
- Long-term fix is bringing pH down to 6.0 to 6.5 range with sulphur.

For NZ home lawns where lime has been over-applied or soil is naturally alkaline, suspect both iron and manganese deficiency together. Address pH long-term with sulphur, foliar treat with chelated iron and manganese sulphate in the meantime.

For most home lawns on slightly acidic NZ soil, manganese deficiency isn't an issue. This is one for the small percentage of lawns where the soil chemistry is genuinely on the alkaline side.
