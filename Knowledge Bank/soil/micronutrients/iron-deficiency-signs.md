# How do I tell if my lawn has iron deficiency?

Look for chlorosis - pale yellow-green colour that contrasts with healthy green tissue.

Specific signs:
- Newer leaves (younger growth) are paler than older leaves. Iron is immobile in the plant, so deficiency shows in new tissue first.
- Interveinal chlorosis: the veins stay green while the tissue between yellows. This is the classic iron deficiency pattern but it's often subtle on grass.
- Overall lawn colour is pale despite adequate nitrogen (you've fertilised and growth is fine but colour is off).
- Lawn responds quickly to NZLA Iron+ application - colour back in 1 to 3 days. That's a good confirmation if you weren't sure.

What it's NOT:
- Whole lawn pale and not growing = nitrogen deficiency, not iron.
- Pale patches with defined edges = disease, not iron.
- Pale stripes from spreader application = uneven fertiliser.
- Newer leaves yellow with older leaves dying back = could be heat stress or a different deficiency.

What drives iron deficiency in NZ lawns:
- Alkaline soil (pH above 7.0): iron in soil but locked up, not plant-available.
- Heavy phosphorus applications: phosphorus interferes with iron uptake.
- Cold spring soils: iron uptake is temperature-sensitive, slow in cold soil.
- High organic matter being broken down: temporary lock-up of iron in the chemistry.

For NZ home lawns the picture is usually one of:
1. Slightly low iron all year - corrected with monthly NZLA Iron+ through growing season.
2. Spring chlorosis from cold soil - corrected as soil warms, Iron+ helps in the meantime.
3. Persistent chlorosis on alkaline soil - sustained Iron+ programme plus longer-term sulphur application to bring pH down.

Iron+ is the foliar fix that works in days. Soil amendments are the longer-term answer where pH is the underlying problem.
