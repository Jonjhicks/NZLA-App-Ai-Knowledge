# Soil pH for Lawns

Soil pH is the single most useful number on a soil test for a home lawn. It determines how available nutrients are to the plant regardless of what's been applied. Fertilising an out-of-range pH soil is inefficient — the nutrients are present but locked up in forms the plant can't use.

## The Target Range

**6.0 to 6.5** is the target for most NZ lawn grasses: ryegrass, tall fescue, fine fescue, kikuyu, couch. This range maximises the availability of nitrogen, phosphorus, potassium, and key micronutrients including iron, manganese, and zinc simultaneously.

Individual species tolerate some variation:
- Ryegrass and tall fescue are tolerant of a wide range (5.5 to 7.5) but perform best around 6.0 to 6.5.
- Fine fescue and browntop prefer slightly more acidic conditions (5.0 to 6.5).
- Kikuyu and couch sit in the same general range as ryegrass.

If pH is between 6.0 and 6.5, leave it alone and focus attention elsewhere.

## Why NZ Soils Tend Toward Acidic

Most NZ soils are naturally on the acidic side. High rainfall leaches calcium and magnesium out of the topsoil over geological timescales, and most NZ parent rock (greywacke, granite, volcanic) has an acidic character. Native podocarp and beech forests historically deposited acidic litter.

On top of this, long-term use of nitrogen fertilisers (particularly ammonium-based products) gradually lowers soil pH. A lawn that's been regularly fertilised for 20 to 30 years without any lime application will typically have drifted below the 6.0 target. This isn't a problem anyone caused deliberately — it's the expected result of standard management without periodic pH correction.

Most NZ home lawn soils without any amendment history sit around 5.0 to 5.8. This is the normal starting point for most liming programmes.

## Acidic Soil: When and How to Lime

**When to lime:** when a soil test confirms pH below 5.5. Don't apply lime without testing — over-liming a neutral or alkaline soil causes chlorosis and weakens the lawn.

The threshold is 5.5 not 6.0 because lime takes time to react and pH moves slowly. By the time the lime has fully reacted, the pH will have moved from 5.5 toward 6.0 to 6.5. If pH is between 5.5 and 6.0, a light liming programme is reasonable but not urgent.

**How much to apply:** a general starting point for NZ mineral soils is that 10 kg per 100 m² of quality agricultural lime (80% CaCO3 or better) raises pH by approximately 0.1 units. To move from pH 5.0 to pH 6.5 requires approximately 150 kg per 100 m² total — but you don't apply that all at once.

A sensible programme for a typical home lawn:
- Apply 5 to 10 kg per 100 m² each autumn for 2 to 3 years.
- Total application of 15 to 30 kg over the programme.
- Retest in year 3, then continue or pause based on actual movement.

Sandy soils need less lime than the calculation suggests (lower buffering capacity). Heavy clay soils need more. High organic matter soils need more.

**Lime types:**
- Agricultural lime (calcium carbonate): the standard. Slow-acting, safe, appropriate for lawns.
- Dolomite lime (calcium plus magnesium carbonate): worth choosing if the soil has low magnesium as well as low pH.
- Hydrated lime (calcium hydroxide): fast-acting but harsher. Lower rates, more care needed.
- Quicklime: industrial. Not for home lawn use.

**Timing:** autumn is best — the lime has months to react before the main growing season. Winter and spring applications work but the response is slower. Avoid applying in summer to dry hot soil.

**What you won't get:** fast results. Soil pH changes over months and years. A test 2 to 3 years after liming shows how the programme is tracking.

## Alkaline Soil: When to Worry and What to Do

Soils above pH 7.5 lock up iron, manganese, zinc, and reduce phosphorus availability. Lawn growing in genuinely alkaline soil shows chlorosis (pale yellow-green) despite adequate fertiliser because the micronutrients are in insoluble forms the plant can't access.

Causes of alkaline lawn soil in NZ:
- Natural alkaline parent material in some regions (parts of Canterbury, coastal areas with shell influence).
- Over-liming — the single most common cause.
- Concrete, mortar, or builder's rubble influence in fill or near structures.
- High-calcium irrigation-general water over many years.

**Immediate fix:** NZLA Iron+ chelated iron applied as a foliar feed. Chelated iron stays plant-available at high pH where free iron is locked up. Apply monthly during the growing season. Colour improvement happens within days.

**Long-term fix:** elemental sulphur to gradually lower pH. Soil bacteria convert elemental sulphur to sulphuric acid, which reacts with carbonates and reduces pH. Rate: 2 to 5 kg per 100 m² per treatment targeting a 0.5 unit pH reduction. Apply in autumn, water in. Expect 6 to 12 months before meaningful pH movement.

Ammonium-based fertilisers (like urea, ammonium sulphate) also contribute a mild acidifying effect over time. Using these as part of the standard programme while sulphur does the heavy lifting is a reasonable approach.

## Over-Liming

Over-liming is common and worth knowing to avoid. It usually comes from applying lime "because it's good for the lawn" without testing, or applying lime annually as routine maintenance when the soil is already at or above target pH.

Signs: lawn turns pale and chlorotic (iron and manganese locked up) even with adequate iron. Pale colour doesn't respond to nitrogen. Sometimes confused with iron deficiency, but Iron+ only partially helps because the soil chemistry is the underlying problem.

Recovery: stop adding lime, test pH, apply sulphur if pH is above 7.5, switch to chelated iron (NZLA Iron+) for the immediate colour issue, use ammonium-based fertilisers rather than products with a liming effect. Recovery is slow — it takes years to bring pH back down with sulphur.

The lesson is to test before applying. Lime is not universally beneficial and not a regular maintenance input for lawns that are already at target pH.
