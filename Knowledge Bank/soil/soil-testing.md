# Soil Testing for Lawns

A soil test is the most reliable way to understand what's actually going on under your lawn. Without one, you're guessing at the cause of problems that might have straightforward solutions once you know the soil chemistry.

For most home lawns, a test every 3 to 5 years is adequate. Test more frequently if you've made significant changes — a liming programme, drainage-and-soil-moisture installation, heavy top dressing — and want to track the effect. Test when the lawn is persistently underperforming despite normal management.

## What a NZ Soil Test Tells You

**pH.** The most useful single number for most lawns. Target 6.0 to 6.5 for most grass species. Below 5.5 needs lime. Above 7.5 needs sulphur and chelated iron. In the 6.0 to 6.5 range, no pH action needed.

**Olsen P (phosphorus).** NZ's standard plant-available phosphorus test. Target around 20 to 30 mg/kg for established lawns. Below 15 means phosphorus is limiting and a phosphorus-containing fertiliser helps. Above 30 means adequate phosphorus is present — many established lawns that have been fertilised regularly are at or above this, and there's no benefit in continuing to add phosphorus at that level.

**Exchangeable potassium.** Target roughly 5 to 8 me/100g. Low potassium produces poor stress tolerance, increased  susceptibility, and weak recovery from wear. NZLA All Seasons and All Seasons N+ supply 16% potassium as part of the standard programme.

**Calcium, magnesium, sodium.** Major cations used to calculate base saturation — the proportion of each major element occupying the soil's exchange sites. NZ lawn targets: roughly 60 to 70% calcium, 10 to 15% magnesium, under 3 to 5% sodium. High sodium (above 5%) indicates a salt or sodic soil issue.

**CEC (cation exchange capacity).** The soil's capacity to hold nutrients. Low CEC (sandy soils) means more frequent fertiliser applications at lighter rates. High CEC (clay soils) means nutrients stay available longer between applications. See soil basics article for practical implications.

**Organic matter.** Often included in comprehensive tests. Target 4% or above for a healthy, productive lawn soil. Under 2% indicates a depleted soil that benefits from organic matter building programme. New build fill soils are commonly well below 1%.

**What the test doesn't tell you:** nitrogen levels (nitrogen moves too fast in soil to give a useful snapshot reading), soil core-aeration (measure physically), soil structure (visual assessment), or biological activity (separate specialist tests available from NZ labs).

## Reading the Report

Work through in this order:

1. **pH first.** If it's outside the 5.5 to 7.0 window, that's the priority issue. Fix it before chasing other numbers. Everything else depends on nutrients being available, and availability is pH-dependent.

2. **Olsen P.** Below 15 — add phosphorus. Above 30 — back off phosphorus inputs. In between — standard programme with a balanced granular is appropriate.

3. **Potassium.** Below 4 me/100g — potassium is limiting. NZLA All Seasons covers this in the standard programme.

4. **Cation ratios.** Only act on these if they're clearly out of range. Sodium above 5% suggests salt management needed. Magnesium very low relative to calcium suggests dolomite lime or magnesium sulphate.

5. **CEC.** Inform your fertiliser frequency decision, not an action in itself.

Don't try to correct everything at once. Identify the biggest single issue and address it. Retest in 2 to 3 years to see what's moved.

## How to Take a Good Sample

The quality of the test result is only as good as the sample. A sample that's not representative of the lawn gives you numbers that don't reflect the real situation.

**Tools:** clean spade or trowel, clean plastic bucket (no fertiliser or chemical residue), sealable plastic bag.

**Process:**
1. Identify 10 to 15 points spread across the lawn. Avoid unusual areas — the spot under the rainwater downpipe, the patch where the dog focuses, the edge right against the concrete path. You want the lawn, not the exceptional bits.
2. At each point, dig down 75 to 100 mm. Discard the top 25 mm of grass and thatch — you only want the soil from 25 to 100 mm depth.
3. Collect all sub-samples into the clean bucket and mix them together.
4. Take a cup-sized portion from the mixed bucket as your final sample.
5. Bag, label with name, date, and "lawn", and post or drop to the lab.

**Don't sample:** within 6 weeks of a fertiliser application, from waterlogged soil, or within a few weeks of heavy chemical use.

**Separate samples for different areas:** if the front and back lawn look genuinely different, or one section is consistently struggling, take separate samples. Don't mix a struggling area with healthy lawn — you'll average out the signal you need.

## Where to Get a Test Done in NZ

**Commercial labs:** Hill Laboratories (hill-laboratories.com) based in Hamilton is the most commonly used by home lawn owners in NZ. Eurofins NZ and Analytical Research Laboratories (ARL) are alternatives. All accept home lawn samples through their standard submission process.

A basic lawn soil test (pH, Olsen P, exchangeable cations, CEC) typically costs $60 to $100 and gives you the core information you need for most programme decisions. More comprehensive tests including trace elements cost more but are worth it for stubborn problems that haven't responded to standard management.

**DIY pH kits:** garden centre pH test strips or chemical kits cost $10 to $20 and give a quick pH estimate. Less accurate than lab tests but adequate if you only need to confirm whether the soil is acidic or alkaline.

When submitting to a lab, specify "lawn turf" as the soil use. They'll send appropriate sampling instructions and can suggest additional tests based on the symptoms you describe.

For most NZ home lawns, the basic test once every few years plus following the results is enough. Chasing elaborate micronutrient panels without a reason adds cost without much return on most maintained lawns.
