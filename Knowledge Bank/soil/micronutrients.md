# Micronutrients in Lawn Soil

Micronutrients are the elements lawns need in small amounts: iron, manganese, zinc, copper, boron, molybdenum, chlorine. They don't get the same attention as nitrogen, phosphorus, and potassium, but deficiencies cause visible symptoms and can stump you if you don't know what you're looking at.

For most NZ home lawns, micronutrients aren't the limiting factor — the soil supplies adequate amounts and a standard granular programme (NZLA All Seasons contains 5% iron, All Seasons N+ 6% iron) covers typical requirements. The exceptions are worth knowing.

## Iron Deficiency

Iron deficiency is the most common micronutrient issue in NZ lawns. The diagnostic sign is chlorosis — a pale yellow-green colour in the presence of adequate nitrogen. The specific pattern matters:

- Newer leaves (youngest growth) are paler than older leaves. Iron is immobile in the plant once fixed, so deficiency shows in new growth first. This distinguishes iron deficiency from nitrogen deficiency, which affects older growth first.
- The lawn is growing fine but the colour is wrong. If nitrogen is adequate and growth looks normal but the lawn looks washed out, iron is the likely culprit.
- Quick response to NZLA Iron+ is a confirming test. A colour improvement within 1 to 3 days after Iron+ application suggests iron was indeed the limiting factor.

What causes iron deficiency in NZ lawns:

**Alkaline soil (pH above 7.0).** Iron is present in the soil but locked up in insoluble forms at high pH. The grass can't access it. This is the most common cause of persistent iron deficiency. Chelated iron (as in NZLA Iron+) remains plant-available at higher pH because the chelate protects the iron from reacting with the soil chemistry. For permanent resolution, bring pH toward 6.0 to 6.5 with sulphur over time.

**Cold spring soils.** Iron uptake is temperature-dependent. Soil temperatures below 8 to 10°C slow root metabolism and reduce iron uptake. Spring chlorosis that resolves as the soil warms is typically this cause. NZLA Iron+ as a foliar feed bypasses the cold soil issue.

**Excess phosphorus.** High phosphorus levels interfere with iron uptake. Lawns that have been heavily fertilised with phosphorus over many years can show iron chlorosis even on soils that aren't alkaline.

**Treatment:** NZLA Iron+ is the fast solution. It works as a foliar feed, bypasses soil chemistry, and produces visible colour response in 1 to 3 days. Apply through a sprayer at the label rate. ## Manganese Deficiency

Less common than iron deficiency but worth knowing, particularly on alkaline soils. Manganese becomes unavailable at high pH, often alongside iron. The visual symptoms are similar — pale interveinal chlorosis — but with an important difference: manganese deficiency shows more uniformly across leaf ages rather than being concentrated in the youngest growth.

If an iron deficiency programme (regular Iron+ applications) isn't fully resolving chlorosis on a high-pH lawn, manganese should be considered. A soil test that includes manganese analysis confirms it.

**Treatment:** manganese sulphate applied as a foliar spray. NZLA Iron+ doesn't supply manganese specifically. Check commercial foliar product labels for combined iron and manganese formulations. The long-term fix for both iron and manganese deficiency on alkaline soils is sulphur application to bring pH down toward 6.0 to 6.5. This is a years-long process.

## Other Micronutrients

**Sulphur deficiency** is occasionally seen on lifestyle blocks and ex-pasture sites that haven't been fertilised in years. It shows as uniformly pale colour across the whole lawn, looking similar to nitrogen deficiency — the difference is it doesn't respond to nitrogen application. Regular fertiliser programmes with NZLA All Seasons cover sulphur requirements for most lawns.

**Zinc deficiency** is rare on maintained lawns. More common on long-cultivated cropping ground. Not typically a home lawn issue.

**Boron and copper deficiencies** are very rare on lawns and mostly horticultural concerns.

## When to Test and When to Apply

Don't supplement micronutrients speculatively. Most NZ lawns under a standard programme don't need additional micronutrient inputs beyond what's in All Seasons and a regular Iron+ schedule.

Specific deficiency signs — particularly iron chlorosis — are worth responding to with targeted products. Persistent chlorosis that doesn't resolve with Iron+ applications suggests either a pH problem, a manganese issue, or something else worth testing for.

A full soil test including trace element panel (available from Hill Laboratories and similar NZ labs) gives definitive answers when the standard programme isn't resolving visible problems.
