# Does gypsum help on heavy clay lawns?

Sometimes, but the claim is often overstated. Gypsum (calcium sulphate) can improve clay soil structure under specific conditions, but it's not a universal solution.

Where gypsum genuinely helps:
- Sodic soils with high sodium content. The calcium displaces sodium from the soil's exchange sites, improving structure. Real benefit but uncommon in NZ home lawn situations.
- Soils with high magnesium relative to calcium. Gypsum corrects the calcium-magnesium balance.
- Surface crusting on heavy clay where the calcium helps maintain pore space.

Where gypsum doesn't help much:
- Standard NZ clay soils without sodium or magnesium imbalance. The structure problem is from particle size, not chemistry, and gypsum doesn't change particle size.
- Acidic clays where lime would be more appropriate.
- Surface compaction (gypsum doesn't decompact soil, only mechanical aeration does).

Better tools for typical clay lawn issues:
- Mechanical aeration (core or solid tine) physically opens up compacted clay.
- NZLA Aerate penetrant wetting agent helps water move through compacted clay between aeration events.
- Sand top dressing over multiple seasons gradually improves clay structure.
- NZLA Humic+ supports soil biology that contributes to better structure over time.

Honest framing for customers asking about gypsum:
- Soil test for sodium and the calcium-magnesium ratio before assuming gypsum is the answer.
- If sodium is genuinely high (over 5% of base saturation), gypsum helps.
- If the issue is clay particle size and compaction, mechanical aeration plus surfactants does more.

For most NZ home lawn clay problems, the answer is aerate, apply NZLA Aerate, top dress with sandy loam over a few seasons. Gypsum is a niche tool for specific soil chemistry situations, not a general clay improver.
