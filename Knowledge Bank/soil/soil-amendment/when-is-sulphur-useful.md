# When is sulphur useful on a lawn?

Two situations.

Lowering pH on alkaline soil. Where soil pH is above 7.5 and the lawn is showing chlorosis (pale yellow-green despite adequate iron), elemental sulphur applied over time gradually drops pH back toward target. Slow process, takes years to shift pH by a meaningful amount.

Correcting sulphur deficiency. Some NZ soils, particularly on lifestyle blocks and ex-pasture sites that haven't been fertilised in years, can be sulphur-deficient. Lawn shows uniformly pale colour that doesn't respond to nitrogen.

Rates and types:

Elemental sulphur for pH lowering: 2 to 5 kg per 100 m² for a soil expected to drop 0.5 pH units. Apply in autumn, water in, expect to wait 6 to 12 months for soil bacteria to convert elemental sulphur to plant-available sulphate which is the form that affects pH.

Ammonium sulphate (sulphur plus nitrogen) as a fertiliser: typical lawn rates apply, the sulphate component provides ongoing mild acidification of the soil. Good fit for lawns on the alkaline side that need both nitrogen and slight pH drop.

Gypsum (calcium sulphate) provides sulphur without affecting pH much. Useful for sulphur deficiency on a lawn that doesn't need pH change.

For NZ home lawns:
- Most don't need sulphur. The soil is naturally slightly acidic and sulphur deficiency is rare on regularly fertilised lawns.
- The main use is for the small percentage of lawns on naturally alkaline soils (some Canterbury, Hawke's Bay, Bay of Plenty pumice soils) where pH is genuinely high.
- Soil test first before applying. Over-applying sulphur on already-acidic soil makes things worse.

If iron deficiency chlorosis is the main symptom, NZLA Iron+ chelated iron is a faster solution than sulphur. Iron+ delivers plant-available iron that works at high pH where free iron is locked up. Sulphur addresses the underlying pH problem if you want a longer-term fix.
