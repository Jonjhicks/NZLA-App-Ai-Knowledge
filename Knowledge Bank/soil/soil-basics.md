# Soil Basics for Lawn Management

Understanding the soil under your lawn isn't complicated once you know what you're looking at. Two concepts matter most for making practical programme decisions: cation exchange capacity (CEC) and organic matter.

## Cation Exchange Capacity

CEC is the soil's ability to hold positively charged nutrients — calcium, magnesium, potassium, ammonium — and release them to plant roots. It's measured in milliequivalents per 100 grams of soil (me/100g) and appears on soil test reports.

**What it means in practice:**

High-CEC soil holds more nutrients. A fertiliser application to a clay or organic-rich soil stays available for weeks. You don't need to apply as often, but each application needs to be sufficient to fill the exchange sites.

Low-CEC soil holds fewer nutrients. On sandy or pumice soil, fertiliser nutrients leach out after rainfall and the programme needs to compensate with more frequent lighter applications. A standard twice-yearly granular programme on sandy soil is leaving the lawn underfed through long stretches.

**Typical NZ ranges:**
- Sandy soils: 5 to 15 me/100g. Low CEC. High leaching. More frequent feeding.
- Loam soils: 15 to 25 me/100g. Moderate CEC. Standard programmes work well.
- Clay soils: 25+ me/100g. High CEC. Nutrients held well. Less frequent feeding needed, but structural and drainage-and-soil-moisture issues more common.
- High organic matter soils: 25 to 40+ me/100g. Best of all worlds: high nutrient holding with better structure than mineral clay.

**What you can change:** soil texture (sand to clay) doesn't change on any meaningful timeframe. Organic matter content does change, and organic matter contributes significantly to CEC. Building organic matter is the practical way to improve a low-CEC sandy soil's nutrient-holding capacity over time.

## Organic Matter

Organic matter is what living and recently living material contributes to soil. It does several things simultaneously.

**Water retention.** Soil at 5% organic matter holds roughly twice the water of soil at 1% organic matter. This is why top-dressing with quality compost and mulch-mowing both contribute to drought tolerance — not just by the physical addition but by building the soil's long-term water-holding capacity.

**Nutrient holding.** Organic matter has high CEC. It holds positively charged nutrients at exchange sites and releases them to plant roots. Soils with higher organic matter are more efficient with fertiliser inputs.

**Soil biology support.** Worms, fungi, bacteria, nematodes — all need organic matter as a food source. Without it, soil biology drops off, nutrient cycling slows, and the soil becomes biologically inert. NZLA Humic+ and Amino+ support soil biological activity alongside organic matter inputs.

**Structural improvement.** Organic matter binds soil particles into aggregates. This improves drainage-and-soil-moisture in clay (aggregation creates pore spaces) and water retention in sand (aggregates hold moisture around their surfaces). NZLA Nurture supplies a biological inoculant that helps establish beneficial soil biology.

**Buffering.** High organic matter soils resist pH swings from fertiliser applications and rainfall. More stable soil chemistry means more predictable response to the programme.

**Typical organic matter levels in NZ:**
- Native bush soil: 8 to 15%. Accumulated over centuries.
- Established pasture: 5 to 8%.
- Well-managed home lawn (mulch-mowed, regular compost top dress): 4 to 6%.
- Average home lawn catching clippings, no regular top dress: 2 to 4%.
- New build over subsoil or scraped fill: under 1%. Very low, and it shows.

## Building Organic Matter

The single biggest lever is mulch mowing — returning clippings to the lawn rather than catching and removing them. Clippings are 80% water and decompose quickly back into organic matter. Every catch-and-remove cycle removes that organic matter input from the system.

Top dressing with quality compost every 2 to 3 seasons accelerates organic matter accumulation, particularly on new builds with poor subsoil base.

NZLA Humic+ supports the humus formation process. Humic acids are stable, high-CEC compounds derived from the decomposition of organic matter — applying them supplementally contributes to long-term CEC improvement.

Avoiding heavy tillage and aggressive cultivation preserves the organic matter structure that's already there. Repeated deep scarifiers without compensating top dressing gradually depletes organic matter.

Realistic timeline: moving from 2% to 4% organic matter takes 5 to 10 years of consistent practice. Losing it is faster — one season of heavy treatment and catch-only management makes a measurable dent. The programme is about consistent inputs over years, not a single soil amendment event.

## Programme Decisions Based on Soil Type

For sandy or pumice soils (low CEC): apply NZLA All Seasons or All Seasons N+ at moderate rates every 6 to 8 weeks rather than heavy applications twice a year. Supplement with monthly foliar feeding (NZLA Charger or Liquid Boost) between granular applications. Wetting agent programme (Wetter or Wetter 3W) is important — sandy soils become hydrophobic and water efficiency drops without it.

For loam soils (moderate CEC): standard programme — three to four granular applications per year with supplementary foliar feeding as wanted. Most NZ home lawns fall into this category.

For clay soils (high CEC): granular applications last longer. Focus less on feeding frequency and more on structure and drainage-and-soil-moisture. Core aeration and top dressing are the most productive investments. Humic+ and Amino+ support the soil biology that helps clay structure over time.

For new builds on poor subsoil: import topsoil with at least 3 to 4% organic matter before establishment. The investment at establishment saves years of trying to build a programme on biologically dead fill soil.
