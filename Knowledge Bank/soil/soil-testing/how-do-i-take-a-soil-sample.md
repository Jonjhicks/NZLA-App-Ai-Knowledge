# How do I take a soil sample from my lawn?

Easier than it sounds. The lab does the analysis, you just need to get a representative sample.

Tools: a clean spade or trowel, a clean bucket, a sealable plastic bag or sample bag from the lab.

Process:
1. Pick 10 to 15 spots across the lawn. Avoid weird areas (the spot under the wheelbarrow, the patch where the dog goes). You want a representative sample of the lawn as a whole.
2. At each spot, dig down 75 to 100 mm. The top 25 mm of grass and thatch goes back on the lawn, you only want the soil from 25 to 100 mm depth.
3. Put each sample in the bucket. Mix them together to make one composite sample.
4. Take about a cup-sized portion from the mixed bucket as your final sample.
5. Bag it, label it with your name, date, and "lawn", post or drop it to the lab.

Don't sample from:
- Areas recently fertilised (wait 6 weeks minimum).
- Areas right next to garden beds, paths or concrete.
- Wet saturated soil.
- The lawn within a few weeks of any chemical application.

If you've got distinct areas (a front lawn that's struggling, a back lawn that looks fine), take separate samples for each. Don't mix them.

Spring and autumn are the standard times for sampling. Results back in a week or two depending on the lab.

For most home lawns, one test every 3 to 5 years is enough. More frequent testing makes sense if you've made big changes (lime application, top dressing programme, drainage installation) and want to track the effect.
