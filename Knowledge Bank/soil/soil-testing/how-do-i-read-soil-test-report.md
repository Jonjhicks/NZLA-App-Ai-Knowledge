# How do I read a soil test report?

A typical NZ lab report has the values, the units, and usually a target range or comment.

Work through it in this order.

pH first. If it's below 5.5 or above 7.5, that's the priority issue and you fix it before chasing other nutrients. Below 5.5 needs lime. Above 7.5 needs sulphur. In between, no action needed.

Olsen P next. Below 15 mg/kg means phosphorus is limiting and a phosphorus-containing fertiliser (NZLA Starter at renovation, or a balanced granular with some P) helps. Above 30, you've got plenty, don't keep adding phosphorus.

Potassium third. Below 4 me/100g is low and the lawn will lack stress tolerance. NZLA All Seasons (1% P, 16% K) or All Seasons (0% P, 16% K) supply potassium as part of the routine programme.

Then the cations (Ca, Mg, Na) for base saturation. Most NZ soils sit within reasonable ranges unless something's unusual (recent sodium ingress from saltwater, heavy magnesium-only fertiliser). Sodium over 5% of base saturation is a salt problem.

CEC for context. Tells you how the soil holds nutrients. A low-CEC sandy soil with adequate Olsen P and K is more vulnerable to leaching than a high-CEC clay with the same readings. Doesn't change what you do today, but informs future programme decisions.

Don't try to fix everything at once. Pick the biggest single issue, address it, retest in 2 to 3 years. Soil chemistry changes slowly.

If the report is hard to interpret, most NZ labs offer paid interpretation services or you can post the results to the NZLA app forum for input from Jon and the regulars.

Common patterns in NZ home lawn reports:
- Low pH (5.0 to 5.5): widespread, particularly on older lawns. Lime application addresses it.
- Adequate P and K: most lawns under a regular fertiliser programme show these at adequate levels.
- High Olsen P: long-fertilised lawns sometimes accumulate phosphorus. Cut back P-containing products.
