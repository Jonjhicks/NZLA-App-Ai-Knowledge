# What does a soil test tell me about my lawn?

The basic NZ lawn soil test gives you several pieces of information.

pH. The acidity or alkalinity of the soil. NZ lawn grasses prefer 6.0 to 6.5. Below 5.5 needs lime. Above 7.5 needs sulphur. This is the single most useful number on the test for most lawns.

Olsen P (phosphorus). NZ standard test for plant-available phosphorus. Target around 20 to 30 mg/kg for established lawns. Lower means phosphorus is limiting, higher means there's plenty (often the case on long-established lawns with regular fertiliser).

Exchangeable potassium. Plant-available potassium. Target around 5 to 8 me/100g for lawns. Low potassium shows as poor stress tolerance.

Calcium, magnesium, sodium. Major cations in the soil. Used to calculate base saturation (the proportion of each in the soil's exchange sites). NZ lawn ideals are roughly 60 to 70% calcium, 10 to 15% magnesium, less than 3% sodium.

CEC (cation exchange capacity). The soil's capacity to hold nutrients. Sandy soils have low CEC (5 to 15), loams middle (15 to 25), clays high (25+). High-CEC soils hold more nutrients and respond more slowly to applications. Low-CEC soils respond fast but lose nutrients to leaching.

Organic matter. Sometimes reported. Higher organic matter improves CEC, water-holding, soil biology. Target 4% or higher for healthy lawn soil.

What the test doesn't tell you:
- Nitrogen levels (nitrogen moves too fast in soil to be useful in a snapshot test).
- Soil compaction (you measure this physically).
- Soil structure (visual assessment).
- Biological activity (separate tests for that).

For most lawns, the test confirms whether the fertiliser programme is working or whether something specific needs adjusting (lime if pH is low, etc.). It's a diagnostic tool, not a prescription.
