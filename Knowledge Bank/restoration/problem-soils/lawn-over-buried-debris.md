# My lawn won't grow over an area - is there buried debris?

Possibly. Buried debris is one of the common reasons a section of lawn struggles permanently while the rest of the lawn is fine.

Signs of buried debris:
- Persistent brown or thin patch despite watering and fertilising.
- Lawn dies out in dry weather faster than surrounding areas.
- Hard to push a screwdriver in to any depth.
- Shape of the problem area is suspiciously regular (square, rectangle, line).

What's often buried:
- Old concrete (driveway, patio, slab).
- Building rubble from construction (brick, mortar, plaster).
- Old asphalt driveways or paths.
- Buried gravel layers from drainage installations.
- Tree stumps and large roots from cleared sites.
- Iron pipes, old septic tank outlets, removed services.

How to confirm:
- Probe the area with a metal rod or thick wire.
- Dig a test hole at the affected spot, 200 to 300 mm deep.
- Check the soil profile - is there a hard layer at depth?

What to do about it:

Light buried debris (small stones, occasional brick):
- May not need any action.
- Lawn struggles but survives.
- Top dress over several seasons to build soil depth above the layer.

Significant buried debris (concrete slab, large rocks, big rubble):
- Dig out and remove if practical.
- Replace with quality topsoil.
- Reseed or relay turf.

Major buried structures (old foundations, septic tanks):
- Professional excavation may be needed.
- Sometimes the area can't be returned to lawn quality.
- Consider converting the area to garden beds or paving instead.

For brand new lawns going onto post-construction sites:
- Test for buried debris before establishing.
- Removing it now is far cheaper than dealing with the consequences later.

For inherited lawns with mysterious bad patches:
- The previous owner may know what's there.
- Council building records can show old structures.
- Archaeological "test pit" approach (small careful dig) reveals what's underneath.

Common buried debris examples in NZ home lawns:
- 1960s and 70s suburbs: builders often buried concrete chunks and rubble on-site rather than disposing.
- Properties with renovations or additions: building debris from old additions still in the ground.
- Sites with cleared sheds or garages: foundation remnants.
- Older urban properties: old driveway materials below current lawn surface.

If the problem area is small and you can live with it, sometimes the easiest path is converting that patch to mulch, paving stones, or garden bed rather than continuing to fight the underlying issue.
