# Problem Soils

Grass will grow in almost any soil given enough time, but it won't thrive in genuinely difficult conditions without some correction. Most NZ lawns sit on suboptimal soil — clay that drains poorly, sand that holds nothing, pumice that's nutrient-poor, construction fill that's compacted rubble, or shallow topsoil over hardpan. Understanding what you're dealing with is the first step.

## Clay soils

New Zealand has significant areas of heavy clay — parts of Auckland, Waikato, Manawatu, and lower South Island. Clay holds water and nutrients but drains slowly, compacts under foot traffic, and cracks badly in drought. Roots struggle to penetrate, waterlogging causes and surface pooling after rain is common.

**What to do:**

Aeration is the most immediate fix. Core aeration twice a year (autumn and spring) keeps clay soils open. Solid-tine aeration more frequently helps with surface hardness.

NZLA Aerate (penetrant soil wetter) and NZLA Penetrate (deeper-profile penetrant) applied 3 to 4 times per year reduce surface hardness and help move water through the profile. They don't structurally fix clay, but they buy time and reduce surface waterlogging.

Sand topdressing over multiple years gradually amends the profile. 5 mm per application, worked into the aeration holes. Over 3 to 5 years of consistent topdressing, the soil profile in the top 100 mm shifts meaningfully. This isn't a quick fix — it's a multi-year programme.

Organic matter improves clay soil structure. NZLA Humic+ (12% humic acid + 2% fulvic acid) and Restore applied regularly through the season support the microbial activity that builds aggregated clay structure over time. Better soil biology creates better soil structure.

Avoid working clay soils when wet — it smears and compacts the structure. Aerate and topdress when the soil is moist but not saturated.

## Sandy soils

Sandy soils (common in parts of Bay of Plenty, Hawke's Bay coastal areas, and some Canterbury plains sites) drain freely but hold very little water or nutrition. Nutrients wash through quickly, the soil dries out fast, and grass establishment on raw sand takes longer.

**What to do:**

Frequent, smaller fertiliser applications rather than large infrequent ones. Nutrition that's applied in large amounts leaches past the root zone before the grass can use it. NZLA Charger every 2 to 3 weeks is better than All Seasons every 8 weeks on very sandy profiles.

Organic matter is the key amendment. Compost-rich topdressing builds water retention and cation exchange capacity over time. Annual compost applications in combination with NZLA Humic+ improve retention season by season.

Wetting agents are often necessary on dry sandy soils in summer. NZLA Wetter 3W at spring application, followed by NZLA Wetter as maintenance through summer. Sandy soils can develop water repellency in dry periods where hydrophobic organic coatings on sand particles cause water to bead off rather than penetrate.

NZLA Amino+ supports beneficial biology and helps new roots establish faster in low-organic sandy profiles.

## Pumice soils

Upper North Island — particularly central plateau, Rotorua area, Taupo — sits on pumice. Low natural fertility, high drainage-and-soil-moisture, low cation exchange capacity. Potassium and phosphorus are often deficient.

**What to do:**

Soil test first. Pumice soils frequently show significant K and P deficiencies. NZLA Starter at establishment addresses the phosphorus side. Ongoing programmes need regular potassium as well as nitrogen — NZLA All Seasons (with its 16% K) is well-suited.

Higher fertiliser rates are often needed on pumice than on mineral soils. The soil holds less, so more regular feeding is required.

Lime may be needed — pumice soils often run acidic. Check pH before liming. If pH is below 5.8, lime is justified. Above 6.0, leave it.

The same organic matter strategy as sandy soils applies: building OM over time improves retention and nutrient holding.

## Compacted soils (construction fill, high-traffic areas)

Common on new sections where topsoil was stripped for construction. The surface layer may be only 50 to 100 mm of topsoil over compacted fill, clay subsoil, or building rubble.

**What to do:**

Core aeration is essential — through whatever machine can penetrate the core-aeration. Deep-tine aeration (400 to 600 mm tines) can break up serious hardpan if accessible. For shallow topsoil over construction fill, deep-tine aeration is the only way to create root pathways into the zone below the topsoil.

Where topsoil depth is genuinely inadequate (less than 100 mm), additional topsoil is needed before the lawn will perform. 100 mm minimum to establish cool-season grasses well; 150 mm is better.

NZLA Penetrate used regularly helps reduce surface core-aeration on high-traffic areas where deep aeration isn't practical.

## pH problems

**Acidic soil (below 5.8):** Most NZ soils are mildly acidic and grasses handle this fine. Below 5.8, nutrient availability (particularly phosphorus and molybdenum) drops, and root development slows. Apply agricultural lime at 1 to 2 kg per m² worked into the soil at establishment, or top-dressed and watered in on existing lawns. pH correction is gradual — retest in 6 to 12 months.

**Alkaline soil (above 7.0):** Less common in NZ but occurs in some Canterbury soils and on sites with concrete or limestone rubble. Above 7.0, iron and manganese become less available and lawns often show chlorosis (yellowing). Sulphur applications bring pH down gradually — 100 to 200 g per m² as a starting point, retesting in 6 months. Take-all patch and other root diseases are more active in alkaline conditions. Don't lime alkaline soils.

## Reactive soils — swelling clay

Some NZ clays (smectite and montmorillonite types in parts of Waikato and Northland) swell dramatically when wet and shrink and crack when dry. The lawn surface heaves and moves, roots are sheared, and the surface is uneven.

Limited options short of soil replacement. Work with the soil by maintaining consistent moisture and avoiding letting it fully dry. NZLA Wetter 3W as a year-round programme can help maintain more even moisture distribution. Aerate only when soil is moist, not when dry or waterlogged.

## Getting a soil test

Before spending money on amendments for unknown soil, a basic soil test from a NZ lab covers pH, Olsen P, K, Ca, Mg, organic matter, and cation exchange capacity. $30 to $60 delivered to a lab and you know exactly what you're dealing with. Most problems are obvious from the results.

Decisions made without a soil test are guesses. Decisions made with one are targeted.
