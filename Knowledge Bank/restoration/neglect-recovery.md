# Neglected Lawn Recovery

A neglected lawn isn't always a lost cause. Weeds, thatch, thin coverage, bare patches — most of these are fixable. How much work it takes depends on how long the lawn has been neglected and what's underneath.

## Assess first

Before doing anything, work out what you're dealing with.

**Weeds.** What percentage of the lawn is grass versus ? Above 60% renovation from scratch is usually faster than trying to selectively kill the  and work with what's left. Below 40% a herbicide programme and seeding-and-over-seeding can recover the lawn without starting over.

**Soil.** Is there actually soil worth saving? Push a screwdriver into the ground in several spots. If it goes in 100 mm without hitting rock or core-aeration so severe it won't penetrate, you have enough depth to work with. If it bounces off 30 mm down, the soil needs attention before anything else.

**Grass species.** Is the existing grass worth keeping? Ryegrass, fescue, tall fescue — worth working with. A lawn that's 80% browntop with take-all patch problems is a candidate for species change rather than renovation.

**Thatch.** Cut a small plug and look at the base of the plant. More than 15 mm of thatch (the brown spongy layer between crown and soil) means the lawn needs scarifiers before seeding-and-over-seeding or fertilising.

## The recovery sequence

### Step 1 — Weeds first

Don't fertilise or overseed into a weed-heavy lawn. You're just feeding the competition.

Broadleaf : NZLA BWC at 50 mL per 100m². For a heavily infested lawn, Gold gives a wider spectrum. Wait 3 weeks after application before seeding-and-over-seeding — the residual activity can affect germination.

Grass  (poa annua, summer grass, paspalum): Etho for annual grasses in ryegrass and fescue lawns. For summer grass that hasn't yet emerged, NZLA Blockade as a pre-emergent stops the next generation. Check label — Blockade is registered for warm-season turf in NZ.

Moss: address the underlying cause (shade, drainage-and-soil-moisture, low fertility) alongside treatment. NZLA Moss if the lawn has a genuine moss problem, noting the label restriction to fine turf and cotula.

### Step 2 — Scalp and scarify

Once  are dead or under control, mow the lawn hard. Drop 2 to 3 mowing heights below where you've been mowing. The object is to expose the soil surface and cut through the thatch layer.

Follow with scarifiers if thatch is significant. A hired scarifier pulled through in two directions opens the surface dramatically. Remove the debris — don't leave it sitting on the lawn.

After scarifiers, the lawn looks terrible. That's expected and correct. This is where most people lose nerve. Push through it.

### Step 3 — Aerate

Core aeration after scarifiers. Two passes at 45 degrees. This opens the soil, relieves core-aeration, and creates the seed-to-soil contact that germination needs.

On clay soils, NZLA Aerate or Penetrate helps with the penetration if the soil is hard.

### Step 4 — Topdress

5 mm of sandy loam or good quality topsoil across the whole lawn, worked into the cores and surface with a stiff brush or drag mat. Fills the aeration holes, levels minor surface irregularities, gives incoming seed a good bed.

### Step 5 — Fertilise and seed

NZLA Starter granular at 1.5 kg per 100m² into the prepared surface. This is the foundation feed — high phosphorus for root development.

Seed at renovation rates: NZLA Perennial Rye or appropriate species at 25 to 35 g per m² for the full lawn. For patchy areas where existing grass still covers more than 50%, oversow at 15 to 20 g per m².

Rake or drag the surface lightly to get seed into soil contact. Roll if you have access to a garden roller.

### Step 6 — Establish

Irrigate 3 to 5 mm twice daily until germination is consistent (7 to 14 days for ryegrass). Keep the surface moist. A dry-out during germination can lose the whole seedbed.

First mow when seedlings hit 60 to 80 mm — not earlier.

Liquid Starter at 4 to 5 weeks at 100 to 200 mL per 100m² foliar. The seedlings now have enough leaf area to absorb it.

## Timing

Autumn (mid-March to end of April) is the best window for renovation in most of NZ. Warm soil, cooling air, reduced  pressure, 6 months of growth before the next summer. The second-best window is early spring (September to October) but summer heat can set back an imperfect establishment.

## When to start from scratch

Sometimes the honest answer is to kill everything and start over.

Situations that warrant complete renovation:
- Soil pH is badly wrong (below 5.5 or above 7.5) and needs correcting before grass will succeed
- Drainage is so poor the lawn sits wet for days after rain — structural fix required before species fix
- Take-all patch on a browntop lawn that's been deteriorating for years
- The lawn is more than 60 to 70% particularly perennial  with root systems (californian thistle, dock, kikuyu in cool-season turf)
- The soil layer is less than 100 mm with rubble or clay immediately underneath

For a complete redo: glyphosate the existing cover, wait 10 to 14 days, then cultivate, address soil issues, and establish fresh. A clean start is faster and less frustrating than fighting a bad existing situation with renovation techniques.

## What to expect

A properly executed renovation programme in ideal conditions (autumn, good soil, consistent irrigation-general) produces a presentable lawn in 6 to 8 weeks. Full density and coverage takes one full growing season. The second spring after an autumn renovation is when the lawn typically reaches its best.

Neglected lawns didn't get bad overnight. They won't fully recover overnight either.
