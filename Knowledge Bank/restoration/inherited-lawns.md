# Inherited and Neglected Lawns

## Assessment first — spend 30 minutes before spending money

Most new owners overspend on the wrong things in the first 6 months because they didn't read the lawn properly before acting.

Walk through and record:

**Species**: pick clumps and identify them. Single species (ryegrass, kikuyu) suggests deliberate establishment. Mixed species (ryegrass, browntop, poa, fescue in patches) means years of seeding-and-over-seeding or natural recruitment. This determines what chemistry is safe to use.

**Density**: thick uniform sward needs maintenance, not intervention. Thin patchy sward is underfertilised or stressed. Bare patches with  need renovation.

**Weed pressure**: few  means the lawn was being managed. Moderate  (scattered plantain, dandelion) is 1 to 2 seasons of neglect — standard herbicide programme fixes it. Heavy broadleaf dominance is years of neglect — a 12-month herbicide and fertility programme is needed.

**Colour**: pale or yellow across the whole lawn is nitrogen deficiency. Specific dead spots need further investigation (dog urine, buried debris, grub damage).

**Soil**: push a screwdriver 50 mm into the soil easily — no problem. Hard to push 25 mm — compacted. Note clay vs sandy vs loam texture.

**Drainage**: walk after rain. Any standing water or squelchy patches need addressing.

## Inherited lawn full of 

Identify the dominant  before applying anything.

**Broadleaf ** (dandelion, plantain, daisy, clover): NZLA BWC at label rate, spring or autumn timing. One application handles around 80% of typical broadleaves. Follow up 6 to 8 weeks later for survivors. Then build lawn density through a fertility programme to resist re-invasion.

**Annual poa** (grassy, small pale seedheads in patches): NZLA Etho if the lawn is ryegrass or tall fescue. Check species first — Etho damages fine fescue and browntop. Programme of 2 to 3 applications through autumn and winter.

**Onehunga** (prickle weed): standard timing (early spring, before seeds harden). BWC or Gold works on standard populations. Clopyralid resistance has been documented in parts of northern NZ — if BWC doesn't work after a proper application, resistance may be the reason.

**Heavy grass weed contamination** (kikuyu in a cool-season lawn, browntop patches): selective control is limited. Full renovation is often the most practical solution for heavy infestations.

Avoid heavy herbicide applications on stressed or thin turf — it damages the grass as much as the . Address weed pressure, then build density through fertility.

## Recovering from neglect

**Light neglect (under 12 months)**:  present but lawn density still reasonable. Standard programme (fertiliser plus herbicide cleanup in spring or autumn) returns it to standard condition within 6 months.

**Heavy neglect (2 to 5+ years)**:  dominate, significant thinning, thatch build-up possible, soil chemistry may be off.

Year 1 programme: soil test for baseline, first herbicide application on dominant start NZLA All Seasons three times a year. Reseed bare patches with NZLA Starter and seed. Autumn: scarify, core aerate, overseed.

Year 2: continue fertility programme. Address remaining  as they emerge through the new turf. Monthly foliar feeds with Liquid Boost and Iron+ to push growth.

Year 3: maintenance phase. The lawn should look like a well-managed lawn by this point.

For lawns over about 30% bare or weed-dominant, full re-establishment (glyphosate kill, cultivate, re-sow) is often more cost-effective than recovery. Calculate the comparison before committing to a restoration programme.

## Scalped or over-mowed recovery

Scalping removes all photosynthetic leaf tissue, exposing the crown. Light scalping recovers in 2 to 4 weeks. Heavy scalping (crowns damaged) produces dead patches needing reseeding.

**Immediate**: stop mowing for 1 to 2 weeks. Water deeply. No traffic on the scalped area.

**Week 2 to 6**: new leaf from surviving crowns. Apply NZLA Liquid Boost to push regrowth. NZLA Iron+ for colour as leaves emerge. Raise cutting height for the next several mows.

**Week 6 onwards**: assess what survived. Dead patches need NZLA Starter and seed. Address the underlying cause (uneven ground, mower height set too low, mower hit a high spot).

## Buried debris

A persistent dead or thin patch that doesn't respond to watering and fertilising may have buried debris underneath. Signs: regular shaped problem area (square, rectangular, straight-line), hard to probe with a screwdriver, dies out in dry weather faster than the rest of the lawn.

Common culprits in NZ: old concrete slabs or paths, builders' rubble from 1960s-70s construction, old driveway material below current surface, tree stumps or large roots.

Dig a test hole at the affected spot to 250 mm depth. If you hit concrete, rubble, or a hard impermeable layer, that's your answer.

Small buried material: top dress consistently over several seasons to build soil depth above the obstruction. Large buried structures: excavate and remove, replace with quality topsoil, reseed.
