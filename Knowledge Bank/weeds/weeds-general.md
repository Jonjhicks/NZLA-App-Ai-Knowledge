# Weed Control — General Principles

## Best time to spray

Spring (October to November) and autumn (March to April) are the primary windows for most weed control work.

Both seasons share the same advantages: mild temperatures (15 to 25°C) when  are actively growing, adequate soil moisture, calm weather more common than summer, and the lawn in active growth that tolerates chemistry better than during heat or cold stress.

Specific timing within those windows:

**Spring:** Apply broadleaf herbicides (BWC, Gold) once  are actively growing but before they've produced substantial seed. Apply pre-em Blockade in late August through early October depending on region — targeting the summer grass germination window before soil temperatures at 50 mm hit 12°C.

**Autumn:** Apply Blockade pre-em in February-March (Auckland) through March-April (Canterbury) before soil temperatures fall into the poa annua germination range (18 to 22°C). Apply broadleaf herbicides on visible  while growth is still active before winter slows things down. Onehunga should be targeted in late winter through early spring at the rosette stage.

**Summer (December to February):** Avoid selective herbicide in most cases. Heat-stressed  don't absorb chemistry effectively. Heat-stressed lawn has less tolerance for herbicide. Volatility and drift increase. Plan around summer — handle it in spring, follow up in autumn.

**Winter (June to August):** Most broadleaf weed control suffers because cold slows metabolism and uptake. Some exceptions: poa annua is still growing through NZ winters and responds to Etho (ethofumesate) in ryegrass and tall fescue lawns. Onehunga is also active in cool months. Spot glyphosate works on established  even in slow growth. But general broadleaf programmes are better left until spring.

The realistic annual pattern: pre-em in late August to October (spring), broadleaf control in October to November (spring), pre-em in February to March (autumn), broadleaf control in March to April (autumn). Spot treatment for individual issues between windows.

## Spraying in hot weather

Don't spray selective herbicide above 28°C as a general rule. Several problems compound in hot conditions.

Heat-stressed  have reduced metabolism. Reduced metabolism means reduced uptake and translocation of herbicide through the plant. Kill rates drop substantially compared to cooler conditions.

The lawn's tolerance for selective chemistry also reduces under heat stress. Most selective herbicides are safe under normal conditions; the safety margin narrows above 25°C.

Volatility increases in hot dry conditions, particularly picloram (the active in NZLA Gold). Volatile chemistry drifts and damages garden beds and ornamentals.

Spray applied to foliage in hot dry conditions can evaporate off the leaf surface within minutes — before the weed has had time to absorb it.

If you're in summer and weed pressure is significant, spray in early morning before 9 am or late evening after 6 pm when temperatures are lower. Mid-day in summer is the worst possible application window.

Specific product cautions: NZLA Liquid N should not be applied above 26°C (chemistry instability). NZLA Paclo (PGR) should not be applied when daytime highs consistently exceed 32°C.

## Winter herbicide use

Winter application of selective broadleaf herbicide (BWC, Gold) produces generally poor results because weed growth is too slow for effective uptake. Save it for spring.

What does work in winter:

NZLA Etho for poa annua in ryegrass and tall fescue lawns — poa is actively growing through NZ winters. Two applications 14 to 21 days apart.

Onehunga rosettes are active in cool conditions — late winter application on mild days catches the plant before it produces prickly seed heads.

Glyphosate spot treatment on established  that need removing regardless of season — the systemic uptake is slower in cold but still works.

For everything else, wait for spring.

## How long to wait before mowing

At least 48 to 72 hours after applying selective herbicide. Longer is better — 5 to 7 days gives the best results.

The reason is translocation. Selective herbicides need time to move from the sprayed leaves through the plant to the growing points where they cause death. Mowing within the first 24 to 48 hours removes the sprayed tissue before the chemistry has finished working.

Specific product guidance: BWC needs at least 24 to 48 hours. Gold needs 48 hours for tough . Meso (mesotrione) needs 7 days minimum for the best bleaching result. Pre-em Blockade is soil-applied and unaffected by mowing timing.

Best practice: mow the day before spraying, leaving  at maximum leaf area. Then don't mow for 7 days. Resume normal mowing cycle afterward.

For the first 7 to 14 days after spraying, catch clippings rather than mulching. Removes herbicide-treated leaf material from the lawn.

## Pre-emergent residual

NZLA Blockade (prodiamine) has a residual of 4 to 6 months in soil. This is one of the longer residuals in its chemistry class — the active binds tightly to soil organic matter and clay particles in the top centimetre of soil, doesn't leach significantly, and breaks down slowly.

The practical implications:

**Overseeding restriction.** Can't sow grass seed for 4 to 6 months after Blockade application. Prodiamine kills germinating grass seed just as effectively as it kills target . A spring Blockade application prevents autumn seeding-and-over-seeding in the same area that year. Plan the programme accordingly — if autumn seeding-and-over-seeding is on the schedule, the spring Blockade timing must account for it.

**Activation.** The residual only establishes if the product activates properly — at least 6 mm of rain or irrigation-general within 7 days of application. Without activation, the chemical sits on the surface, breaks down faster under UV, and doesn't develop full residual into the soil.

**Second application.** The label allows maximum 2 applications per 365 days, minimum 90 days apart. The 4 to 6 month residual means a single application handles most of a season — a second application is for extending coverage through to the next germination window.

Autumn poa annua applications carry residual through the autumn-winter germination window and are largely dissipated by spring, so spring seeding is generally possible after autumn pre-em. Spring summer-grass applications carry through summer — plan autumn seeding-and-over-seeding accordingly.

## Why  keep coming back

Three reasons, often in combination.

The soil seed bank. Every lawn has years of accumulated dormant weed seeds. Dandelion seed persists 5 to 10 years. Plantain 10 to 20 years. Annual grass  3 to 7 years. Killing visible  doesn't empty the seed bank — it keeps germinating each season. The cycle ends only when you've killed incoming seedlings consistently long enough for the seed bank to exhaust.

The conditions haven't changed. Thin lawn, low fertility, core-aeration, shade, wrong species, drought stress — each creates niches that  exploit. Address the visible  without addressing the conditions and new  replace them. The niche still exists.

Continuous reintroduction. Weed seeds blow in from neighbours, parks, pastures, wildlife. Soil amendments and seed mixes can carry seeds. You'll never eliminate this entirely, but you can reduce it through boundary management, clean soil sourcing, and certified seed.

The realistic response to recurrence: combined programme over 2 to 3 years. Chemistry on visible . Pre-em on annual species. Cultural improvement (fertility, mowing height, irrigation-general, aeration) as the foundation. Year one reduces visible . Years two and three reduce ongoing germination as the seed bank depletes. After that, ongoing maintenance is much less intensive.

## Why herbicide fails

Seven common causes.

Wrong timing — application during dormancy, heat stress or cold reduces uptake. Active growth in mild conditions is the requirement.

Inadequate coverage — spray that doesn't contact weed leaves can't work. Use sufficient water volume. NZLA Sticker adjuvant for waxy or hairy .

Premature mowing or watering — mowing within 24 to 48 hours or rain within 4 hours of application removes chemistry before uptake.

Wrong product — broadleaf herbicide doesn't control grass . Pre-em doesn't work on established plants.

Rate too low — diluting product beyond label rates reduces effective concentration. Calibrate sprayers properly.

Resistance — real in NZ for specific species. Documented clopyralid resistance in onehunga populations in northern NZ (clopyralid is the active in BWC). Some poa annua resistance to specific chemistries. If a product that worked previously is now failing on a specific species, resistance is worth investigating.

Wrong conditions — rain within hours washing off the spray; hot dry conditions causing evaporation before uptake; cold slowing metabolism.

For persistent failures: check timing first (most common cause), then coverage, then product match, then rate. Resistance is real but less common than user-error causes.

## Will pulling  by hand solve it?

For individual occasional  in an otherwise clean lawn, yes. For a lawn with an established weed population, no — hand pulling removes the symptom without addressing the seed bank or the conditions favouring .

Annual  (summer grass, some broadleaf annuals) pulled completely before they set seed don't regrow. The individual plant is dead.

Perennial  with taproots (dandelion, dock) regrow from any root fragment left behind. Complete taproot extraction is difficult.

Rhizomatous perennial  (sorrel, twin cress, yarrow) regrow from underground rhizome systems that extend well beyond the visible plant. Pulling the above-ground portion does almost nothing.

The seed bank remains regardless. Even if every pulled plant is dead, new germination from the seed bank continues.

Hand pulling makes sense for: small numbers of isolated  in otherwise clean lawn, weed catching before establishment (young seedlings are easy), and sensitive areas near edibles where herbicide isn't suitable. As the entire weed programme for a lawn with established weed pressure, it doesn't resolve the problem.

## Long-term weed prevention

A dense, well-managed lawn is the primary weed prevention tool. Chemistry supports but doesn't replace it.

Key elements: right species for the conditions, steady fertility programme (All Seasons every 6 to 8 weeks), mowing at the higher end of the species range (40 to 50 mm for ryegrass rather than 20 to 30 mm), deep infrequent watering to build root depth, regular aeration on compacted soils, and seeding-and-over-seeding any thin areas before they become weed habitat.

Pre-em Blockade twice annually reduces annual weed germination from the seed bank.

Most weed problems are downstream of lawn health problems. A genuinely healthy, thick lawn at the right mowing height has minimal weed pressure naturally. The investment in the lawn's overall health reduces the need for chemistry over time.

Achieving genuinely low weed pressure takes 2 to 3 years of consistent management. After that, maintenance is much less intensive.
