# Can I spray weeds when it is hot?

Generally avoid spraying selective herbicide in hot weather. Several issues compound to reduce effectiveness and increase damage risk.

The issues with hot-weather application:

**Reduced effectiveness on stressed weeds.** Weeds under heat stress slow their metabolism. Reduced metabolism means reduced uptake and translocation of herbicide. Kill rates drop substantially compared to cooler conditions.

**Increased risk of lawn damage.** Selective herbicides are generally safe on labelled grass species under normal conditions. Under heat stress, the safety margin reduces. Lawn damage from selective herbicide is more likely above 25°C.

**Volatility and drift.** Hot weather increases volatility of some herbicide components. The spray evaporates faster, creating drift risk that affects neighbouring garden beds and ornamentals. Picloram (in NZLA Gold) is particularly prone to volatility.

**Evaporation before uptake.** In hot dry conditions, the spray dries on the leaf surface within minutes — before the weed has had time to absorb the chemistry properly. Effective uptake needs the spray to remain on the leaf for hours.

NZLA label guidance on hot weather:

Most NZLA selective herbicide labels recommend avoiding application:

- When temperatures exceed 25 to 28°C during application
- When the lawn is heat-stressed (visible wilting, dry appearance)
- In drought conditions where the weeds are also stressed

Specific products:

**NZLA Liquid N** — do not apply when temperature exceeds 26°C. The ammonium stabilisation chemistry loses stability at high temperatures.

**NZLA Paclo (PGR)** — do not apply when daytime highs consistently exceed 32°C. Heat plus PGR causes severe damage.

What's "hot" for spraying:

Above 25°C — caution. Plan application for cooler parts of the day or wait for cooler weather.

Above 28°C — generally avoid selective herbicide application.

Above 30°C — wait for cooler conditions. Damage and ineffectiveness risk too high.

Best application times in summer:

**Early morning** (before 9 am). Cooler conditions, weeds may have absorbed overnight dew, low wind.

**Late evening** (after 6 pm). Cooler conditions as day declines, lower drift risk.

**Mid-day in summer is the worst window** for herbicide application. Hot, often windy, weeds stressed, evaporation high.

For lawns with significant weed pressure during hot summer:

Wait for cooler conditions. Most NZ regions have multiple cooler windows even through summer. Spot treatment in cool early mornings rather than blanket spraying.

Glyphosate has somewhat different rules — works in hotter conditions than selective broadleaf herbicides but still benefits from cooler weather. The systemic uptake is faster than selectives.

For specific weed pressure that can't wait:

Mow first (reduces weed leaf area to lower stress level), water the day before to reduce drought stress, apply early morning, accept reduced kill rates.

For specific cases:

Onehunga sometimes appears in late summer/autumn when conditions are still warm. Cool morning applications work.

Most broadleaf weed control programs are better timed for spring (October-November) and autumn (March-April) anyway — the mild conditions favour both lawn health and herbicide effectiveness.

Plan around weather forecasts. Selective herbicide on the right day produces dramatically better results than the same product applied in difficult conditions.
