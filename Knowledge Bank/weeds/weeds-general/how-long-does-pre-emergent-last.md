# How long does pre-emergent last in the soil?

Depends on the active. The numbers that matter for NZLA's pre-em range:

**Prodiamine (NZLA Blockade) — 4 to 6 months.** This is one of the longer-residual pre-emergents in its chemistry class. The active binds tightly to soil organic matter and clay particles in the top centimetre of soil, doesn't leach significantly, and breaks down slowly through microbial action. Single application gives a barrier that holds through most of a growing season.

**Pendimethalin (not in NZLA range, comparison) — a few weeks.** Much shorter residual than prodiamine. Useful where you want short barrier and faster turnover.

The practical implications of the long Blockade residual:

**Seed-sowing restriction.** Can't overseed for 4 to 6 months after Blockade application. The prodiamine kills germinating seedlings just as effectively as it kills the target weeds. A spring Blockade application means no autumn overseeding that year. Plan around it.

**Second application timing.** The label allows max 2 applications per 365 days, minimum 90 days apart. The 4 to 6 month residual means a single application covers most of a season; a second application is for extending coverage through to the next major germination window.

**Activation matters.** The residual only forms if the chemical activates properly — at least 6 mm of rain or irrigation within 7 days of application. Without that, the chemical sits on the surface, breaks down faster under UV, and never gives full residual.

For autumn applications targeting poa annua germination, the residual carries through the autumn-winter germination window and is largely gone by spring, so spring overseeding is generally possible.

For spring applications targeting summer grass, residual carries through summer. Overseeding into the same area waits until autumn at the earliest.
