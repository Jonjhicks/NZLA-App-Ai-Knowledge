# What is the best time of year to spray weeds?

Spring (October to November) and autumn (March to April) are the optimal windows for most weed control programs.

Why these windows:

**Mild temperatures.** Both spring and autumn typically have day temperatures in the 15 to 25°C range — ideal for selective herbicide uptake without heat stress on lawn or weeds.

**Active weed growth.** Both windows have most weed species in active growth phase. Active growth means active uptake and translocation of herbicide. Kill rates are high.

**Lawn tolerance.** The lawn species are also actively growing and can tolerate the herbicide without damage. Damage risk is highest in stressed conditions (heat, drought, cold dormancy).

**Calm weather more common.** Less wind makes spray application more accurate and reduces drift risk.

**Adequate but not excessive moisture.** Both seasons typically have enough rainfall to support post-spray activity without washing off freshly applied herbicide.

Specific timing within these windows:

**Spring (October-November in NZ).**

Apply broadleaf control (NZLA BWC, Gold) once weeds are actively growing but before they've set substantial seed. Catches weeds at vulnerable growth stage.

Apply pre-em Blockade in late August through early October depending on region — before soil temperatures hit summer grass germination threshold.

For lawns with poa control needs, finish autumn pre-em application timing before October.

**Autumn (March-April).**

Apply pre-em Blockade in February-March (Auckland) through March-April (Canterbury) before soil temperatures drop into poa annua germination range.

Apply broadleaf control on visible weeds before winter dormancy slows them. Targets autumn-growing weeds (onehunga, late-season summer annuals).

When to AVOID spraying:

**Summer (December-February).** Heat stress on weeds reduces uptake. Heat stress on lawn increases damage risk. Volatility and evaporation increase. Plan around summer — get treatment done in spring, do follow-up in autumn.

**Winter (June-August).** Cold weather slows weed growth and herbicide uptake. Lawn is also less tolerant of chemistry in cool conditions. Some specific applications work (Etho for poa, onehunga at warmer winter days) but general broadleaf control suffers.

**Drought conditions.** Stressed weeds don't take up herbicide effectively. Wait for rainfall before treating.

**Heat stress days.** Even within optimal seasons, individual hot days (28°C+) reduce effectiveness. Plan around forecasts.

**Wind.** Drift risk damages garden beds, ornamentals, vegetables. Wait for calm conditions.

**Within 24-48 hours of forecast rain.** Heavy rain washes herbicide off before adequate uptake.

For pre-emergent specifically:

NZLA Blockade timing is driven by soil temperatures rather than calendar.

Spring (summer grass and crabgrass): apply 2 to 3 weeks before soil temperatures hit 12°C at 50mm depth and rise.

Autumn (poa annua): apply 2 to 3 weeks before soil temperatures fall into 18 to 22°C range.

Regional adjustments — Auckland earlier than Canterbury.

For post-emergent broadleaf:

Spring is ideal for most broadleaf weed control. Active growth, mild conditions, good kill rates.

Autumn second-best for residual weeds and targeted late-season pressure.

The realistic annual pattern:

**Late August to early October:** spring pre-em (Blockade).

**October-November:** spring broadleaf control (BWC, Gold).

**February-March:** autumn pre-em (Blockade for poa).

**March-April:** autumn broadleaf control (BWC, Gold for persistent species).

For most home lawn weed problems, twice-annual broadleaf application plus twice-annual pre-em handles ongoing pressure. Spot treatment for individual issues between these windows.

Don't spray in summer or winter unless specific situations call for it.
