# Will weeds come back if I just pull them out?

Sometimes the individual weed doesn't come back. The weed problem in your lawn almost certainly will.

Two different questions:

**Does the individual pulled weed come back?**

Depends on the species and how thorough the pulling was.

**Annual weeds** (summer grass, crabgrass, some broadleaf annuals): once the plant is dead, it doesn't regrow. Pull thoroughly and the individual plant is gone.

**Perennial weeds with taproots** (dandelion, dock, mallow): regrow from any taproot fragment left in the soil. Hand pulling rarely extracts the full taproot — regrowth within weeks is common. The individual weed comes back unless you've removed every root fragment.

**Perennial weeds with rhizomes or stolons** (sorrel, kikuyu encroachment, twin cress): regrow from underground rhizome system that extends well beyond the visible plant. Hand pulling the visible portion does almost nothing — the rhizome system produces new shoots quickly.

**Stoloniferous weeds** (creeping bentgrass, some broadleaf species): regrow from rooted nodes on stolons. Pulling visible plants leaves rooted nodes that regenerate.

For these reasons, hand pulling works on annual weeds and isolated young plants but rarely fully addresses established perennial weeds.

**Does the lawn's weed problem come back?**

Almost always yes, regardless of how thoroughly you pulled.

The reasons:

The soil seed bank still contains thousands of dormant weed seeds. Each season germinates new weeds from the seed bank.

The conditions that favoured the weeds haven't changed. Thin lawn, low fertility, compaction, shade — the niches that allowed the original weeds to establish remain.

New weed seeds blow in continuously from neighbouring areas, wildlife, the air.

So even if every pulled weed is dead, new weeds keep appearing. The lawn-level weed problem isn't solved by hand pulling alone.

When hand pulling makes sense:

**Small numbers of individual weeds in an otherwise clean lawn.** A dandelion in your lawn isn't a weed crisis — pull it and move on.

**Isolated young weeds before establishment.** Catching weeds at early growth stage means easier extraction and less seed production.

**Weeds in sensitive areas where herbicide isn't suitable.** Near edible plants, water features, children's play areas. Pulling avoids chemistry concerns.

**As a follow-up to herbicide treatment.** Pulling weeds that survived or weakly responded to spray completes the kill.

When hand pulling alone isn't enough:

**Established weed populations.** More than a handful of weeds across the lawn needs systematic control — chemistry plus cultural management.

**Persistent perennial weeds.** Dock, dandelion, mallow with their deep taproots; sorrel and twin cress with their rhizomes. Hand pulling fights an uphill battle.

**Lawns with chronic weed problems.** The conditions favouring weeds need addressing, not just individual plant removal.

The realistic combined approach:

Hand pulling for occasional isolated weeds in clean lawn.

Selective herbicide for established weed populations (NZLA BWC, Gold).

Pre-emergent prevention (NZLA Blockade where label-appropriate) for annual weeds.

Cultural management (fertility, mowing height, irrigation) as the foundation.

For most NZ home lawns, the right balance is:

Spot treatment of occasional weeds (some hand pulling, some spot spray).

Twice-yearly broadleaf herbicide application during establishment of the management programme.

Ongoing cultural management to reduce ongoing weed pressure.

Hand pulling alone as the whole weed control programme rarely produces a satisfying result on lawns with established weed presence.
