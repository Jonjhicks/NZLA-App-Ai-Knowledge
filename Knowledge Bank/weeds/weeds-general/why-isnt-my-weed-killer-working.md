# Why is my weed killer not working?

Seven common reasons herbicide applications fail. Most home lawn herbicide failures trace to one or two of these.

**1. Wrong timing.** Application during weed dormancy, drought stress, or heat stress reduces uptake dramatically.

Fix: apply in active growth conditions. Mild temperatures (15 to 25°C), adequate soil moisture, weeds actively growing.

**2. Inadequate coverage.** Spray that doesn't actually contact the weed leaves can't be absorbed.

Fix: ensure thorough coverage with appropriate water volume. Use NZLA Sticker as adjuvant for waxy or hairy weeds. Spray when wind is calm for accurate placement.

**3. Premature mowing or watering.** Mowing within 24 to 48 hours or watering immediately after application removes the spray before uptake.

Fix: mow before spraying. Wait 7+ days after spraying before mowing. Don't water for at least 24 to 48 hours after.

**4. Wrong product for the weed.** Selective broadleaf herbicide doesn't work on grass weeds. Pre-emergent doesn't work on established weeds.

Fix: identify the weed correctly. Match the product to the target.

**5. Application rate too low.** Diluting product more than recommended reduces effective concentration.

Fix: follow label rates exactly. Calibrate sprayers properly. Don't try to economise on product.

**6. Resistance.** Some weed populations have developed resistance to specific herbicides.

Particularly relevant in NZ:

**Onehunga clopyralid resistance.** Documented 200+ fold resistance in populations north of Auckland and spreading. BWC contains clopyralid. If BWC isn't working on onehunga, resistance is likely. Mecoprop is the alternative (not in NZLA range).

**Poa annua resistance.** International evidence of resistance to multiple herbicide classes. Rotation between modes of action reduces resistance pressure.

Fix: rotate herbicide chemistry. Don't rely on single product repeatedly. Combine with cultural management.

**7. Wrong conditions.** Rain within hours of application washes spray off. Hot dry conditions cause evaporation before uptake. Cold conditions slow metabolism. Stressed weeds don't take up effectively.

Fix: time applications for stable mild conditions with adequate post-application weather (no rain forecast for 24 hours, no heat extremes).

Specific diagnostic approach:

**Did anything happen to the weeds at all?**

Some response visible: chemistry worked partially. Rate too low or coverage incomplete. Reapply.

No response at all: timing wrong, resistance, wrong product, or application failure. Diagnose the cause before retreating.

**Did the same weed types die in some areas and survive in others?**

Suggests coverage or application issues rather than chemistry failure. Improve application technique.

**Are surviving weeds the same species across the lawn?**

Suggests resistance — that species in your lawn has developed resistance. Switch chemistry class.

**Did the weeds initially die but new ones appeared from the same areas?**

Killed individual plants but seed bank or rhizomes producing new weeds. Repeat treatment of new plants while also addressing soil conditions.

For lawns with chronic herbicide failure:

Reconsider the lawn's overall management. A weak underfed lawn keeps producing weed problems regardless of chemistry. Cultural improvement (fertility, mowing height, irrigation) makes chemistry more effective.

Consider professional consultation if the issue persists. Sometimes local conditions or specific resistance issues need expert assessment.

For onehunga specifically:

If BWC isn't working, suspect resistance. Mecoprop-based products are the proven alternative — not currently in NZLA range. Flag the gap honestly rather than escalating doomed chemistry.

Most herbicide failures fix with attention to timing, application technique, and matching product to target. Resistance is real but less common than user-error causes.
