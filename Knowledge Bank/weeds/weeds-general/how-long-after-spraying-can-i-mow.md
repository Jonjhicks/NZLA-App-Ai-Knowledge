# How long after spraying weeds before I can mow?

Wait at least 24 to 48 hours after applying selective herbicide before mowing. Longer is better — 3 to 7 days for best results.

Why:

Selective herbicides work by being absorbed through the weed's leaves and translocated through the plant. The translocation takes time — typically 2 to 7 days to move the herbicide from the spray point to the growth points where it does its damage.

Mowing within the first 24 to 48 hours removes the sprayed leaf tissue before the herbicide has fully translocated. The weed survives the spray because the chemistry was cut off and bagged before doing its job.

NZLA product label guidance:

**NZLA BWC** — wait at least 24 hours before mowing.

**NZLA Gold** — wait at least 48 hours before mowing for tough broadleaf weeds.

**NZLA Etho** — depends on application. Post-emergent application: don't water in immediately, let the spray dry on foliage. Mowing within 2 to 3 days reduces effectiveness.

**NZLA Sedge Pro** — delay mowing 2 days after application.

**NZLA Meso** — 7 days minimum between application and mowing for best bleaching effect.

The general rule:

Longer wait = better kill on tough weeds. If the weeds are dense and well-established, wait 5 to 7 days if possible.

For routine post-spray management:

Mow the lawn before spraying — the day before is ideal. Leaves the weeds at maximum leaf area for spray contact. Then no need to mow for at least a week.

After 7 to 10 days, resume normal mowing on the usual cycle.

If you've already sprayed and need to mow before the recommended wait:

Damage to herbicide effectiveness varies by product and conditions. Probably some loss of kill but not necessarily failure.

Plan to retreat in 4 weeks if you observe poor kill. Most selective herbicides allow re-application after a 4-week interval.

For pre-emergent applications:

Different consideration. NZLA Blockade is a pre-em sitting in the soil, not absorbed through leaves. Mowing is fine immediately after pre-em application — doesn't affect the chemistry.

What about catching the clippings:

For 7 to 14 days after applying selective herbicide, catch the clippings rather than mulching. Removes any residual herbicide-treated leaf material from the lawn surface. Don't compost herbicide-treated clippings — they can damage plants in the compost.

For spot treatment with glyphosate:

Glyphosate translocates faster than selective broadleaf herbicides — typically 2 to 4 days for full uptake. Wait at least 48 hours before mowing the spot-killed area.

What about wet conditions:

If rain falls within 4 hours of selective herbicide application, the chemistry may be washed off before adequate uptake. Wait 4 weeks and retreat if the weeds survived.

If you spray on wet foliage, herbicide dilution reduces effectiveness. Wait for foliage to dry before spraying.

The standard pattern:

Mow before spraying. Spray when weeds are actively growing. Wait 7 days. Resume normal mowing.

Most failures come from rushing the post-spray window. Patience produces consistent results.
