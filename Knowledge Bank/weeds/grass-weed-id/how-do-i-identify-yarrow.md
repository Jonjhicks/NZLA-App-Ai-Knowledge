# How do I identify yarrow?

Yarrow (Achillea millefolium) is a broadleaf weed often categorised with grass weeds because of its fine feathery foliage that resembles grass at a distance. Common in NZ lawns and pastures.

Identification:

Distinctive finely divided feathery leaves, 50 to 150 mm long. The leaves are deeply pinnate — divided into many small narrow segments along a central stem. Overall appearance is fern-like or feather-like.

The leaves have a strong aromatic scent when crushed — yarrow has historical herbal use and the smell is distinctive.

Stems and leaves grey-green or dull green, often with a slightly velvety texture.

Tall flat-topped flower clusters in summer, 100 to 600 mm tall. Multiple small white (sometimes pink) flowers in flat clusters at the top of stems.

Spreads via creeping rhizomes underground as well as seed. Forms patches that expand over time.

Where it grows: throughout NZ lawns and pastures. Particularly common in low-fertility lawns, dry exposed sites, and acidic soils. Tolerates drought and poor conditions well.

Despite the fine grass-like foliage, yarrow is a broadleaf weed (dicot) — important for herbicide selection.

Why it matters:

Yarrow in a lawn creates patchy texture differences — the fern-like leaves contrast with surrounding grass. Patches become more visible after mowing as the cut yarrow looks different to cut grass.

Yarrow is more drought-tolerant than typical lawn grass, so during dry summers the lawn can look "patchy" because the grass struggles while the yarrow patches stay green.

Control:

NZLA BWC (MCPA + clopyralid + diflufenican) at 50 mL per 100m² gives reasonable control of yarrow. Apply when actively growing.

NZLA Gold for established yarrow patches that BWC hasn't cleared.

The rhizomatous spread means even effective chemistry may need multiple applications. Visible plants killed, but new shoots from underground rhizomes emerge over weeks following treatment.

Adding NZLA Sticker as adjuvant improves penetration on yarrow's slightly waxy fine leaves.

For individual yarrow patches:

Spot treatment with glyphosate plus NZLA Sticker. Kills completely; reseed the patch.

Cultural side:

Yarrow often indicates low-fertility and dry conditions. Address the underlying issues — fertility programme, irrigation, possibly soil amendment for very poor soils.

A dense well-fed lawn at the right mowing height crowds out new yarrow establishment.

For lawns with established yarrow:

Multi-season programme. Spring or autumn application of BWC or Gold combined with cultural improvement. New shoots from rhizomes may require re-treatment.

In some applications:

Yarrow is intentionally used in low-maintenance lawn mixes (yarrow lawns are a thing in some sustainable landscaping). Drought tolerance and low fertility requirements suit some specific situations.

For traditional grass lawns where yarrow is unwanted, treat as a persistent broadleaf weed requiring multi-season management.
