# Why is my lawn full of different grasses?

Most older lawns end up as mixed-species over time. Several pathways lead there.

**1. Wind-borne seed.** Grass species blow in from neighbouring lawns, pastures, parks, sports fields. Browntop and creeping bentgrass particularly travel on wind. Annual species like poa annua and summer grass disperse seed widely.

**2. Contaminated seed.** Cheap or poor-quality grass seed mixes often contain other species mixed in — sometimes deliberately, sometimes as contamination. A bag labelled "ryegrass" from a budget supplier may contain 20% other species.

**3. Lawn renovation.** Each renovation introduces new seed and sometimes new species. Over years, lawns drift away from their original single-species composition.

**4. Soil seed bank.** Many grass weed species produce thousands of seeds per plant that persist in soil for years. Each season germinates some of the seed bank.

**5. Neighbouring property influence.** Kikuyu from neighbours encroaches via stolons. Various species spread along fence lines and through gaps in fences.

**6. Original installation.** Some lawns are intentionally mixed-species from the start — different species for different conditions (sun vs shade), or blends for resilience.

What it means for your lawn:

**Mixed-species lawns are normal.** Most lawns over 5 years old contain at least 2 to 4 different grass species. This is the typical state of established home lawns in NZ.

**Some mixing is positive.** Different species cope with different conditions. A lawn with multiple species often has better resilience to drought, disease and use than a single-species lawn.

**Some mixing creates problems.** Heavy contamination with aggressive invaders (kikuyu in cool-season lawn, browntop in ryegrass) creates uneven appearance and competitive pressure on the desirable species.

How to assess what you've got:

Walk the lawn and look at distinct patches. Different colour, leaf width, texture, or growth height usually indicates different species.

For specific identification, take samples to a knowledgeable garden centre, NZLA forum post with photos, or local turf consultant.

Common patterns in NZ home lawns:

- Original perennial ryegrass with patches of browntop, summer grass in summer, poa annua in autumn-winter
- Mixed rye and tall fescue with creeping bentgrass contamination
- Upper North Island lawns transitioning from cool-season seed to kikuyu dominance
- Older lawns with so many species mixed they no longer resemble the original installation

What to do about it:

**If the lawn looks acceptable and functions well:** leave it. Mixed-species lawn that's doing its job doesn't need intervention.

**If specific weed species are causing visible problems:** target them with appropriate chemistry. Browntop with Meso. Poa with Etho or Blockade. Kikuyu with Halo (fine fescue only) or spot glyphosate.

**If the lawn has degraded to an unacceptable state:** full renovation. Kill the existing lawn, re-establish with desired species, manage to maintain composition over time.

**If the change is gradual and natural:** accept the lawn's evolution toward equilibrium species mix for your conditions. Often the most realistic long-term approach.

For premium showcase lawns where pure species composition matters:

Ongoing management required. Regular inspection, targeted chemistry, frequent overseeding, and ultimately periodic renovation to maintain the standard.

For routine home lawns:

A mixed lawn that's dense, green, and tolerates use is meeting its purpose regardless of species composition.
