# How do I control kikuyu in a cool-season lawn?

Kikuyu (Pennisetum clandestinum) is a warm-season grass that's the dominant species in upper North Island lawns but a serious invader in cool-season ryegrass or fescue lawns elsewhere.

Why it's hard to control. Kikuyu spreads aggressively via both stolons (above ground runners) and rhizomes (underground runners). Cutting it doesn't slow it down. Pulling it leaves rhizomes that regrow. Standard broadleaf herbicides don't touch it. Once established, kikuyu is genuinely difficult to remove from a lawn you want to keep cool-season.

Options:

**Selective chemistry — NZLA Halo (haloxyfop) on fine fescue only.** Halo takes kikuyu out of a fine fescue lawn while leaving the fine fescue intact (with the 15 mL/100m² rate cap). Doesn't work on ryegrass, tall fescue or browntop lawns — Halo will kill those species along with the kikuyu.

**Non-selective spot treatment.** Glyphosate with NZLA Sticker as adjuvant on the kikuyu patches. Kills everything in the spray area, then overseed the dead patches with cool-season seed once the residue has cleared. Practical for small to moderate infestations.

**Full renovation.** For heavy kikuyu invasion, the realistic answer is killing the whole lawn with glyphosate, waiting 2 to 3 weeks for any regrowth from rhizomes, possibly a second glyphosate pass, then re-establishing the lawn from seed. Significant project but the only durable fix for serious infestations.

Cultural side: kikuyu loves bare patches, low mowing heights and warm conditions. A thick well-mowed cool-season lawn at 40 mm plus through summer slows kikuyu establishment.

In upper North Island lawns where kikuyu is the established species and ryegrass is encroaching, the reverse problem applies — and NZLA Etho would be the tool except Etho damages kikuyu. Different chemistry conversation entirely.

Heavy kikuyu in a cool-season lawn rarely has a quick fix. Plan for full renovation if you want it gone properly.
