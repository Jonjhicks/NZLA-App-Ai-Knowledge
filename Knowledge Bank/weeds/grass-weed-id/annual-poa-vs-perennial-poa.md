# How do I tell annual poa from perennial poa?

Annual poa (Poa annua) and perennial poa (Poa pratensis, sometimes Poa trivialis) look similar at a glance but behave very differently and matter for control choices.

**Annual poa (Poa annua).** Light bright green colour, often paler than surrounding grass. Short stature (50 to 150 mm if left to grow). Flowers and sets seed prolifically, almost continuously through cool conditions — fluffy white seed heads visible most of autumn through spring. Each plant lives a few months then dies, replaced by seedlings from the seed bank. Acts as an annual or short-lived perennial weed.

**Perennial poa (Kentucky bluegrass and related).** Darker green, taller habit, more upright. Leaf tips often slightly hooded or boat-shaped (diagnostic feature). Long-lived perennial that spreads slowly via rhizomes. Less seed production, less aggressive than annual poa as a weed.

For most NZ lawn problems, annual poa is the issue. It's a contaminant in fine turf, a yearly nuisance in cool-season home lawns, and a serious problem on golf greens and sports turf.

Control approach for annual poa:

**Pre-em — NZLA Blockade.** Apply autumn before soil temps drop into the 18 to 22°C poa germination window. 10 to 40 mL per 100m².

**Post-em selective — NZLA Etho (ethofumesate) for ryegrass and tall fescue lawns.** 40 mL per 100m² × 2 applications 14 to 21 days apart. DO NOT use on fine fescue.

**Long-term PGR programme — NZLA Paclo (paclobutrazol)** for cool-season high-performance turf. Selectively suppresses annual poa more than desirable grasses, shifting competitive balance over multiple seasons.

Cultural side: dense healthy desirable grass at the right mowing height is the foundation. Annual poa exploits thin spots, low mowing heights and stress.

Perennial poa is usually accepted as part of the lawn rather than controlled. It's not aggressive enough to dominate, and selective control of it would damage other desirable grass species.
