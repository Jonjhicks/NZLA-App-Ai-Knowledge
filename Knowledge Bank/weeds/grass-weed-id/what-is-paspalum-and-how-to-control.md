# What is paspalum and how do I control it?

Paspalum (Paspalum dilatatum mostly, sometimes other Paspalum species) is a coarse warm-season grass that invades cool-season home lawns and looks awful in them. Wide flat leaves (10 to 15 mm), distinctive seed heads with two or three "fingers" running along the stem in late summer through autumn, and a tough fibrous habit that resists mowing.

Why it invades. Paspalum is a perennial that handles a wide range of soils and tolerates dry conditions. In a thin or stressed cool-season lawn, paspalum establishes in the gaps and then spreads outward each year. By the time most home owners notice, it's already a significant patch.

Control is harder than for annual grass weeds. Two approaches.

**Pre-em on germinating seed.** NZLA Blockade (prodiamine 476.2 g/L) covers paspalum at the germinating seed stage. 10 to 30 mL per 100m². Useful for preventing new paspalum establishment but won't touch existing plants. Note the NZ label restriction — Blockade is registered for warm-season turf only here.

**Selective grass-in-grass kill.** NZLA Halo (haloxyfop) takes paspalum out of fine fescue lawns specifically. Hard limit on fine fescue: 15 mL per 100m². Will damage or kill most other turf species (ryegrass, tall fescue, browntop, kikuyu, couch), so Halo only works on a fine fescue lawn.

For paspalum in ryegrass or tall fescue lawns, the chemistry options are limited. Spot treatment with glyphosate (plus NZLA Sticker as adjuvant) kills the paspalum and the surrounding lawn — overseed the dead patches afterward. Or accept a small patch and manage it by keeping the rest of the lawn dense enough to limit spread.

Cultural control matters. Thick healthy turf at the right mowing height prevents new paspalum establishing. Address compaction and bare patches that give it a foothold.

Heavy paspalum infestation often needs full renovation rather than spot chemistry.
