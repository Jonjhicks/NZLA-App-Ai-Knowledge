# How do I identify crabgrass vs summer grass?

Crabgrass (Digitaria sanguinalis) and summer grass (Digitaria ciliaris) are closely related annual grass weeds. Similar enough that the names are often used interchangeably in NZ. Both controlled identically.

Identification:

**Habit.** Both are annual warm-season grasses that germinate in late spring, grow aggressively through summer, and die off in autumn frosts. Each new season starts from seed.

**Growth pattern.** Low-growing, spreading habit. Stems creep across the ground and form patches. In unmowed conditions can reach 100 to 400 mm tall.

**Leaves.** Wider than typical lawn grass species, 5 to 10 mm wide. Bright green colour, sometimes slightly hairy.

**Seed heads.** Distinctive finger-like seed heads at the tops of stems in summer — typically 3 to 8 finger-like spikes radiating from a central point. Each finger 30 to 100 mm long. Looks like a small open hand at the top of the stem.

**Roots.** Shallow fibrous root systems. Easy to pull out by hand on individual plants.

Subtle differences between species:

Crabgrass (Digitaria sanguinalis): leaves and stems often have some reddish-purple tinge. Hairier than summer grass.

Summer grass (Digitaria ciliaris): generally smoother, more bright green. Less hairy.

For practical lawn management, the distinction doesn't matter. Same control methods, same timing, same chemistry.

Where they grow:

Throughout NZ lawns, particularly:
- Thin underfed lawn areas where the canopy doesn't shade out germinating seedlings
- Bare patches and disturbed soil
- Lawn edges
- Areas with heavy summer use
- Newly established lawns through the first summer

Annual lifecycle:

Spring (October-November in most NZ): seeds germinate as soil temperatures hit 12°C and rise.

Summer (December-February): aggressive growth, flowering, seed production.

Autumn (March-April): seed dispersal, plants die in first frosts or as conditions cool.

Winter: dormant in soil as seed.

Control:

The whole game is timing pre-emergent before germination.

NZLA Blockade (prodiamine 476.2 g/L) at 10 to 30 mL per 100m². Apply 2 to 3 weeks before soil temperatures hit 12°C and stay there.

Auckland late August. Central North Island mid-September. Canterbury late September to early October.

Note the NZ label restriction — Blockade is registered for warm-season turf only. Off-label use on cool-season turf can't be officially recommended.

Activate with 6 mm of rain or irrigation within 7 days.

Second application 90 to 120 days later carries the barrier through peak germination.

For lawns where pre-em was missed:

Post-emergent control of established crabgrass in cool-season turf is difficult — no fully selective NZLA option. Spot glyphosate kills it but takes out surrounding lawn too.

Mow regularly through the season to prevent seed set. Reduces next year's pressure.

Cultural side:

Dense well-fed lawn at the right mowing height prevents most crabgrass establishment. Crabgrass exploits thin and stressed areas.

Address compaction in patches where crabgrass concentrates.
