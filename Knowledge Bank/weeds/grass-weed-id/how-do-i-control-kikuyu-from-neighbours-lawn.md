# How do I control kikuyu invading from my neighbour's lawn?

Common problem in upper North Island and warm coastal areas where kikuyu is the dominant species on many lawns. Kikuyu spreads aggressively via stolons and rhizomes and doesn't respect property boundaries.

The realistic options:

**1. Selective chemistry — Halo on fine fescue only.**

NZLA Halo (haloxyfop-P-methyl) takes kikuyu out of a fine fescue lawn while leaving the fine fescue intact (with the strict 15 mL/100m² rate cap).

But this only works on fine fescue lawns. If your lawn is ryegrass, tall fescue, or browntop, Halo will kill those species along with the kikuyu.

For most home lawns (predominantly ryegrass or tall fescue), this option doesn't apply.

**2. Non-selective spot treatment.**

Glyphosate with NZLA Sticker as adjuvant on the kikuyu encroachment patches. Kills everything in the spray area — including your surrounding lawn. Reseed the dead patches with cool-season seed once the residue has cleared.

Practical for small to moderate infestations. Maintains the property boundary line by killing whatever crosses it.

**3. Physical barriers.**

The most permanent solution. Install a vertical barrier along the property boundary — typically 200 to 300 mm deep — to prevent rhizome spread underneath.

Galvanised steel edging, dedicated lawn edging products, or concrete kerbing work. Significant upfront cost but eliminates the problem permanently.

**4. Regular boundary management.**

Mow a defined strip along the property boundary at very low height (5 to 10 mm) weekly. Kikuyu can't establish at scalped mowing heights. Effectively keeps kikuyu suppressed in the boundary zone.

Less than ideal aesthetically but works for owners not wanting permanent infrastructure.

**5. Accept and adapt.**

For lawns substantially encroached by kikuyu, switching to a kikuyu lawn deliberately is sometimes the realistic answer. Kikuyu has many positive attributes — drought tolerant, wear-resistant, dense canopy.

Talk to the neighbour about kikuyu management on their side. Joint management of the boundary is more effective than one-sided efforts.

What doesn't work:

Selective herbicide on ryegrass or tall fescue lawns to remove kikuyu. The chemistry that kills kikuyu (Halo) also kills these species.

Hand pulling. The rhizome system extends far underground. Pulled plants regrow within weeks.

Single chemical application. Kikuyu's rhizome and stolon network requires multiple treatments over months for complete control.

For lawns with active boundary kikuyu pressure:

Combined approach. Regular boundary mowing to suppress new advance. Periodic spot kill with glyphosate on patches that establish. Annual review of whether physical barriers might be worth the cost.

Long-term, the realistic answer depends on the neighbour. If the neighbour's kikuyu lawn is maintained, the boundary pressure is manageable. If their lawn is neglected and kikuyu is running wild, more aggressive intervention on your side is needed.

Note: this is a reverse-direction problem from the more common situation. Where kikuyu is your lawn species and ryegrass/fescue invades, NZLA Etho would be the tool — different chemistry, different conversation.
