# Broadleaf Weed Identification and Control

Broadleaf  (dicots) are controlled selectively in grass lawns with products like NZLA BWC and NZLA Gold. These products don't harm most grass species but move through broadleaf weed tissue and kill the plant.

NZLA BWC (MCPA + clopyralid + diflufenican) at 50 mL per 100m² handles most common broadleaf . NZLA Gold (picloram + triclopyr) is the stronger option for tougher species, resistant populations, or where BWC has had only partial success.

Apply when  are actively growing in mild conditions (15 to 25°C). Avoid hot-weather spraying (above 28°C) and cold dormancy. Wait at least 48 to 72 hours before mowing. Add NZLA Sticker as an adjuvant for waxy or hairy-leafed  where coverage is poor.

## Clover

Clover in a lawn is a nutrition signal as much as a weed problem. Clover fixes its own nitrogen from the atmosphere, giving it a competitive advantage on underfed lawns where the grass is running short. A lawn full of clover is a lawn that needs more feeding.

For control, BWC at 50 mL/100m² is highly effective. Visible damage within a week, usually dead within 2 to 3 weeks. Gold for tough infestations.

Long-term management: NZLA All Seasons on a proper 6 to 8 week feeding cycle drives grass density that outcompetes clover. The chemistry handles the current population; the fertility prevents the next one.

Some people deliberately oversow micro-clover into lawns for the nitrogen-fixing benefit and accept a mixed lawn. That's a legitimate choice. If you want pure grass, BWC and consistent feeding.

## Dandelion

Easy to identify: jagged deeply lobed leaves in a flat rosette, bright yellow flowers on hollow single stems, classic puffball seed heads. Deep taproot sometimes 250 mm or more — hand pulling requires extracting the full taproot or it regrows. Any fragment left behind produces a new plant.

BWC is highly effective on dandelion. Apply during active growth in mild conditions. Full kill within 2 to 3 weeks.

Cultural prevention: regular mowing prevents puffball seed heads forming, reducing the seed bank year by year. A dense well-fed canopy prevents new seedlings establishing from wind-blown seed.

## Plantain

Two species: broadleaf plantain (Plantago major) with broad oval leaves and narrowleaf plantain (Plantago lanceolata, ribwort). Both have prominent parallel veins running leaf length-ways — the diagnostic feature. Broadleaf plantain favours compacted areas particularly.

BWC handles both species well. Apply when actively growing. Damage visible within a week.

Where broadleaf plantain clusters in specific patches, consider core aeration to address underlying core-aeration. The plantain is exploiting the conditions the core-aeration creates.

---

## Thistles

Scotch, nodding, californian, and variegated thistles are common in NZ lawns. Spiny leaf edges are the diagnostic feature — painful to handle, can't be confused with other . Californian thistle spreads via underground rhizomes as well as seed, making it significantly more persistent.

BWC is effective on most thistle species, particularly at the rosette stage before flowering stems develop. Gold for resistant or mature plants.

Californian thistle often requires multiple applications across seasons because the rhizome system survives where the above-ground growth is killed. Spot treatment with glyphosate plus NZLA Sticker can be more effective on isolated plants.

Wear gloves when handling thistle. Mow regularly to prevent seed production.

## Catsear

Catsear (Hypochaeris radicata, also called false dandelion or flatweed) is one of the most common NZ lawn  and frequently confused with dandelion. Key differences: catsear leaves are hairy (run your finger over them — bristly surface), less deeply lobed, and the flower stems are solid, branched, and carry multiple smaller flowers rather than one large flower per stem.

BWC with clopyralid is highly effective — catsear is in the Asteraceae family that clopyralid targets. NZLA Sticker adjuvant improves coverage on the hairy leaves.

Widespread in low-fertility lawns. A steady fertility programme (All Seasons every 6 to 8 weeks) substantially reduces ongoing catsear pressure. More difficult to permanently eliminate than dandelion — larger seed bank in most NZ lawns.

## Daisy

Common lawn daisy (Bellis perennis) is among the easiest broadleaf  to identify: small rosette of slightly hairy leaves, white-petalled flowers with yellow centres, 15 to 25 mm across on short stems. Spreads via stolons as well as seed.

BWC is highly effective. Apply when actively flowering for maximum uptake.

Worth being direct with customers: daisy is difficult to permanently eliminate because of its widespread seed bank and easy re-establishment in any thin area. Twice-yearly BWC application keeps the population suppressed. Some people simply accept daisies in their lawn — they're not aggressive enough to dominate, and for many people they're not objectionable.

## Dock

Broadleaf dock and curled dock both have large leaves with wavy edges and prominent central veining, growing as flat rosettes before sending up tall flowering stems. The deep taproot (sometimes 600 mm) and abundant rust-coloured seed heads make dock particularly persistent.

Mature dock with established taproots is one of the harder  to clear chemically. BWC at rosette stage works well; established plants often need multiple applications. NZLA Gold (triclopyr component) is more effective on mature dock than BWC.

Spot treatment with glyphosate plus NZLA Sticker is sometimes more practical on individual large plants. Mow regularly to prevent the rust-coloured seed heads maturing and dispersing.

## Hawkbit, hawksbeard, and the yellow-flower rosette group

Several  produce similar yellow flowers and are commonly confused. A quick identification guide:

**Hawkbit (Leontodon taraxacoides):** narrow lance-shaped slightly hairy leaves, thin wiry stems with single flowers per stem, smaller flowers than dandelion.

**Hawksbeard (Crepis capillaris):** tallest of the group (often 500+ mm), smooth branched stems, multiple smaller flowers in clusters.

**Catsear (described above):** hairy leaves, branched solid stems, 2 to 4 flowers per stem.

**Dandelion:** deep jagged lobes, single large flower on hollow unbranched stem, hairless leaves.

All are in the Asteraceae (daisy) family. All are controlled with BWC or Gold. The clopyralid component in BWC targets this family specifically. For most lawn owners, exact species identification within this group doesn't change the control programme.

## Hydrocotyle (pennywort)

Distinctive round coin-shaped leaves 10 to 40 mm across, glossy bright green, on long thin stems. Creeps along the ground rooting at nodes. The round leaf shape is diagnostic.

Thrives in damp shaded conditions — under trees, near irrigation-general, poor drainage-and-soil-moisture. Presence almost always signals conditions favouring the weed over grass.

BWC gives partial control. NZLA Gold is more effective on established mats. The creeping rooting habit means single applications often need to be followed up as rooted nodes regrow.

For established mats, spot treatment with glyphosate plus NZLA Sticker then overseed is sometimes more practical. Longer-term, address the conditions: improve drainage-and-soil-moisture, reduce shade where possible, adjust irrigation-general patterns. Where shade and damp can't be changed, hydrocotyle may be more realistic to accept than to fight indefinitely.

## Mallow

Round to broadly heart-shaped leaves with wavy lobed edges and slightly velvety texture. Pink to lavender 5-petalled flowers through the growing season. Distinctive round "cheese" seed pods are the easy identification feature.

Mallow is one of the tougher  for BWC. NZLA Gold (triclopyr) is more effective, particularly on established plants. NZLA Sticker adjuvant helps penetration on the waxy leaves.

Spot treatment with glyphosate on individual mature plants is often more efficient than selective herbicide on established mallow.

## Onehunga weed

Finely divided fern-like or parsley-like leaves in a low spreading rosette — that's the early-stage identification. By the time the prickly seed heads have formed (felt when walking barefoot), the seeds are already maturing.

Control timing is everything. Treat in late winter through early spring while the rosette is developing but before seed head formation. September through October typically.

**Resistance warning.** Documented clopyralid-resistant onehunga populations exist in NZ, particularly north of Auckland and now spreading through the upper North Island. BWC contains clopyralid. If BWC is failing on onehunga in your area, resistance is the likely cause. NZLA Gold contains picloram which has documented cross-resistance in some of these populations and may also underperform.

The proven alternative is mecoprop, which is not currently in the NZLA range. This is a genuine gap — don't substitute unrelated chemistry and expect results. In affected areas, cultural management (building dense lawn to prevent establishment) becomes more important.

## Sorrel

Sheep sorrel (Rumex acetosella) has small arrow-shaped or spear-shaped leaves with two distinct pointed lobes at the base. Spreads via creeping rhizomes. Can have a reddish tinge.

Sorrel presence often indicates acidic soil — below the ideal 5.5 to 7.0 pH range for cool-season grass. If sorrel is widespread across the lawn, a soil test ($40 to $80 from NZ labs) is worthwhile.

BWC handles younger plants well. Established patches with rhizome systems often need multiple applications. Gold for resistant patches.

Address soil pH if test confirms acidity: lime application at 1 to 2 kg per 10m² of agricultural lime corrects pH. Cultural solution is more durable than ongoing chemistry.

---

## Storksbill

Finely divided fern-like leaves in a low rosette, small pink to purple flowers, and the diagnostic feature: long beak-like seed pods 20 to 50 mm that split into spiral-tailed seeds that drill into soil and attach to clothing.

Storksbill often resists BWC. NZLA Gold is the more reliable option. Young plants respond better than established ones.

The spiral-tailed seeds attach readily to socks, dogs and clothing — one reason storksbill spreads widely once established. Watch for it spreading into new areas from neighbouring dry exposed patches.

---

## Twin cress

Low-growing plant with finely divided fern-like leaves, creeping stems with characteristic two-lobed seed pods. Strong cabbage smell when crushed — it's in the Brassicaceae (mustard) family. Often grows in compacted areas and lawn edges.

Resists BWC regularly. NZLA Gold is the appropriate product. NZLA Sticker adjuvant improves uptake. Multiple applications across a season typically needed.

Spot glyphosate on isolated patches is often more practical than selective control. Where twin cress is widespread, multi-season management with Gold plus cultural improvement.

---

## Wireweed (prostrate knotweed)

Wiry tough stems creeping across the ground with small narrow alternating leaves and tiny white flowers. Distinctive small papery sheaths at each node where leaves join the stem — the diagnostic feature of the Polygonum family.

Tolerates heavy foot traffic and core-aeration better than almost any other weed. Its presence in specific patches strongly indicates core-aeration.

BWC gives partial control on young plants. Gold is more effective. For established mats in compacted areas, spot glyphosate then reseed plus core aeration to address the underlying core-aeration.

NZLA Blockade has some pre-emergent activity on wireweed for the spring germination period. Note the NZ label restriction — Blockade is registered for warm-season turf only.

---

## Creeping oxalis

Small heart-shaped leaflets in groups of three (similar to clover but smaller and distinctly heart-shaped, not oval), small yellow flowers, slight sour-lemon smell when crushed. Creeps via stolons that root at nodes, forming low spreading mats.

BWC at 50 mL/100m² is the first tool. Multiple applications across a season are typically needed for heavy infestations — the rooting stolons mean killed visible plants leave behind rooted nodes. Gold for BWC-resistant populations.

Spot glyphosate plus NZLA Sticker works well for localised mats. Thick healthy lawn prevents new establishment — oxalis exploits bare patches and thin areas.

## General notes on broadleaf weed control

Most broadleaf  respond to BWC as the first tool and Gold as the escalation for resistant or established populations. The pattern of failure with BWC is more often application conditions (wrong timing, inadequate coverage, premature mowing) than chemistry resistance.

NZLA Sticker adjuvant improves results on any species with waxy or hairy leaves — mallow, twin cress, storksbill, catsear.

None of the chemistry works on completely dormant or heat-stressed . Mild conditions with active growth produce the results. Spring and autumn are the best windows.

A dense, well-fed lawn at the right mowing height prevents most broadleaf weed establishment. The chemistry addresses the current problem; the cultural programme prevents the next one.
