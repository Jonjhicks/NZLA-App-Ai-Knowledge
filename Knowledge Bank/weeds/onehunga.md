# Onehunga Weed

Onehunga weed (Soliva sessilis) — also known as prickle weed, bindii, or jo-jo — is the weed that ruins the barefoot lawn experience. A single plant produces dozens of hard-spined seeds that lie flat on the soil surface and spike feet, children, and pets from spring onwards.

## Identification

Low, finely divided ferny leaves forming a flat rosette close to the soil surface. Looks a bit like a miniature carrot or parsley leaf — highly divided, feathery texture. Bright green in autumn and winter. Plants are inconspicuous until the seeds form.

The seeds are the giveaway: flat, disc-shaped, 3 to 5 mm across, with hard spines radiating from the edge. They develop in September and October and are painful to stand on by November. Once you've had barefoot prickles in summer, you'll look for the feathery rosettes in autumn.

Don't confuse with clover (three-leaflet oval leaves, very different) or other broadleaf . The ferny divided leaf structure of onehunga is distinctive.

## Lifecycle — why autumn timing is everything

Onehunga is a winter annual. Seeds germinate in late summer and autumn (February to April), the plant overwinters as a small rosette, then bolts and sets seed in spring (September to November). The seeds persist in the soil through summer and germinate again in the next autumn.

**Treat in autumn — this is non-negotiable.** Treatment in September or October, after the seeds have set, is already too late for that season. You're removing the plants but the spiny seeds are already in the soil and will cause the same problem through summer. The seed that falls in September will germinate in March.

Effective window: March to May, when the plants have germinated and are actively growing as rosettes, before they bolt and set seed in spring.

## Treatment

**NZLA BWC** at standard rate handles onehunga effectively in the autumn treatment window. NZLA Gold for heavy infestations or where BWC has had partial results in previous seasons.

Add NZLA Sticker to the spray solution — the finely divided leaves have limited surface area for uptake and Sticker improves coverage.

Apply when plants are actively growing and temperatures are mild (12 to 22°C). Visible damage within 7 to 14 days. Plants may need a second application 3 to 4 weeks later if population was heavy.

## Autumn treatment saves the following summer

The logic: treat in March to May → kill this season's plants before they produce seed in September → fewer seeds germinating next March → fewer plants to treat next season.

Over 2 to 3 years of consistent autumn treatment, onehunga populations can be reduced dramatically. Skip the autumn window, treat in October instead, and you're playing catch-up every year.

## Lawn density as prevention

Onehunga establishes in thin, sparse lawns. It cannot compete with a dense turf canopy — the seeds need bare or thin soil to germinate. A consistent NZLA All Seasons feeding programme and correct mowing height that maintains good canopy coverage is the long-term defence.

After controlling onehunga with herbicide, oversow thin areas in autumn with NZLA Perennial Rye or appropriate species. The grass fills before the next onehunga germination window.

## Kids and pets after treatment

Apply BWC or Gold following the standard 24-hour keep-off period. The spiny seeds remaining in the soil after treatment aren't a chemical risk — they're still physically painful to stand on that season regardless of treatment. The following summer, with the plants gone, the seed load will be reduced.

Bare feet before November on a known onehunga lawn: shoes are advisable regardless of treatment status, because seeds that set before treatment are still in the soil surface.
