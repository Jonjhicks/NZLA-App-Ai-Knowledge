# Oxalis

Oxalis is one of the most persistent and frustrating  in NZ lawns. It can be controlled but rarely eliminated in a single treatment, and understanding why makes the management approach clearer.

## Identification

Several oxalis species occur in NZ lawns. The key diagnostic features are consistent across species: heart-shaped leaves in groups of three (similar to clover but distinctly heart-shaped), and small five-petalled flowers. Leaf colour ranges from bright green to reddish-purple depending on species.

**Creeping oxalis (Oxalis corniculata):** the most common. Small bright green or purple-tinted leaves, low creeping stems that root at nodes, small yellow flowers. Spreads aggressively across the lawn surface by rooting at every node. Very hard to kill because stem fragments left in the soil resprout.

**Yellow oxalis (Oxalis stricta):** more upright habit, brighter green leaves, yellow flowers. Similar but slightly easier to control.

**Oxalis pes-caprae (Soursob/Bermuda buttercup):** large yellow flowers, clover-like leaves, grows from bulbils underground. Very difficult to eradicate — the bulbil chain in the soil persists for years. Less common in lawns but present in some NZ regions.

The clover comparison is common. Distinctions: oxalis leaves are heart-shaped with a notch at the outer edge; clover leaflets are oval with a white crescent marking on many varieties. Oxalis flowers are yellow; clover flowers are white or pink-purple.

## Why it's persistent

Two mechanisms make oxalis much harder to kill than standard broadleaf :

**Rooting stems (creeping oxalis).** Every node on the creeping stems can root into the soil. Herbicide kills the leaves but if the stem is still in contact with soil, new plants emerge from the nodes before the chemical kills the whole plant. Multiple applications are needed to progressively work through the root system.

**Bulbils.** Some oxalis species produce underground bulbils — small bulb-like structures that survive herbicide treatment. Each bulbil produces a new plant after the treated plant dies. This is why oxalis lawns can look clear after treatment then reinfest within weeks.

## Treatment

**NZLA Gold** (picloram + triclopyr) gives the best results on oxalis — better than BWC because the picloram component is more effective on persistent broadleaf  with strong vegetative reproduction systems.

**NZLA Sticker** is essential. Creeping oxalis and soursob both have waxy or hairy leaf surfaces that shed water-based spray. Without Sticker, coverage is poor and uptake is significantly reduced. Always add Sticker to Gold when spraying oxalis.

Apply to actively growing plants in mild conditions (15 to 25°C). Avoid spraying just before rain — wait for at least 2 hours of dry conditions after application.

**Expect to spray 2 to 4 times.** The first application kills the above-ground growth and visible plants. The second catches regrowth from rooting stems or sprouting bulbils. The third and fourth progressively deplete the bulbil and stem bank in the soil.

Space applications 3 to 4 weeks apart. Give each treatment time to fully translocate before assessing results.

## Timing

Autumn and spring are the best treatment windows — oxalis is actively growing and uptake is best. Summer heat reduces uptake. Winter dormancy slows it further.

For soursob/bulbil oxalis: treat in late autumn when the new season's plants have fully emerged and are actively growing. Treating before full emergence misses plants that emerge later.

## Realistic expectations

Creeping oxalis on a heavily infested lawn: 3 to 4 treatments across two seasons to achieve meaningful control. Not a one-spray fix.

Soursob with established bulbil banks: multi-year management. Each season's treatment reduces the viable bulbil population, but the seed and bulbil bank is persistent. Consistent annual treatment progressively reduces the population.

## Prevention

Dense, well-fed turf is the best long-term suppression mechanism. Oxalis establishes easily in thin, stressed, or low-fertility lawns. A consistent NZLA All Seasons feeding programme that drives canopy density creates a lawn that's much harder for oxalis to colonise.

Don't leave bare patches — oxalis moves in immediately. Oversow thin areas with appropriate grass seed before the oxalis gets there.
