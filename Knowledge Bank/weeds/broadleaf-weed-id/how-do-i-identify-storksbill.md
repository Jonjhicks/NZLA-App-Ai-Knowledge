# How do I identify storksbill?

Storksbill (Erodium cicutarium, also called heron's bill or filaree) is a low-spreading broadleaf weed with distinctive long-pointed seeds that resemble a bird's beak.

Identification:

Low-growing rosette of finely divided, fern-like leaves. Leaves are deeply lobed and feathery, 50 to 150 mm long. Light to mid green colour.

Small pink to purple flowers, 8 to 12 mm across, on short stems above the rosette. Flowers throughout much of the year in mild conditions.

Distinctive seed pods — long pointed structures 20 to 50 mm long that resemble a stork's or heron's beak (hence the name). When mature, the pods split into individual seeds that have spiral tails enabling them to "drill" into the soil and clothing.

Where it grows: throughout NZ lawns, particularly in dry exposed areas, thin lawns, and walked-on patches. Common in coastal regions and inland dry areas.

Tolerates drought, poor soil, and trampling — common in tough conditions where other broadleaf weeds struggle.

Lifecycle: annual or short-lived perennial depending on conditions. Multiple generations per year in mild climates.

Control:

Storksbill is one of the broadleaf weeds that often resists BWC and requires stronger chemistry.

NZLA BWC (MCPA + clopyralid + diflufenican) gives partial control of storksbill. Young plants respond better; established plants often survive single applications.

NZLA Gold (picloram + triclopyr) is the more effective option for storksbill. The triclopyr component handles tougher broadleaf weeds with waxy or fine-textured leaves.

Apply when actively growing in mild conditions. Storksbill is often actively growing through cooler months when other weeds are dormant — winter and early spring applications can be effective.

For individual mature storksbill in otherwise clean lawn:

Spot treatment with glyphosate plus NZLA Sticker. Kills completely; overseed the patch.

Cultural side:

Storksbill establishes in thin, dry, exposed lawn areas. The conditions favour it specifically because more competitive species struggle there.

Improving overall lawn density, fertility and irrigation reduces storksbill pressure substantially. Thick well-fed lawn at the right mowing height prevents most new establishment.

For lawns with established storksbill:

Spring application of Gold typically clears most of the population. New germinations may appear through summer from the seed bank — retreat as needed.

The seed dispersal mechanism (spiral-tailed seeds that drill into the soil and clothing) makes wide spread possible. Once a lawn has storksbill, ongoing low-level management is often realistic rather than complete eradication.

Watch the dog or kids walking through dried storksbill plants — the seeds attach readily to clothing and fur.
