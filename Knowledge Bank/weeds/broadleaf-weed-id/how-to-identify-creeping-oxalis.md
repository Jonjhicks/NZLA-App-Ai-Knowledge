# How do I identify and control creeping oxalis?

Identification:

Creeping oxalis (Oxalis corniculata) has small heart-shaped leaflets in groups of three, similar to clover but smaller and a different shape. Leaves are typically green but can have a purple-bronze tinge, especially in sun. Small yellow flowers (about 5 mm across) appear through most of the growing season.

The diagnostic feature is the growth habit — it creeps along the ground via runners (stolons) and roots at the nodes, forming a low spreading mat. Crush a leaf between fingers; it has a slight sour-lemon smell, which is the oxalic acid in the plant.

Distinguish from clover by leaf shape (oxalis leaflets are heart-shaped; clover leaflets are round to oval) and flower colour (oxalis yellow; clover white or pink).

Control:

Oxalis is one of the more persistent broadleaf weeds because of its rooting stolons. Killing the visible plant often leaves rooted nodes that regrow.

NZLA BWC (MCPA + clopyralid + diflufenican) at 50 mL per 100m² is the first tool. Apply when oxalis is actively growing in mild conditions. Expect damage within 2 weeks, full kill in 3 to 4 weeks. Multiple applications across a season usually needed for heavy infestations.

NZLA Gold (picloram + triclopyr) is the stronger option for oxalis that BWC hasn't cleared. The persistence of picloram in soil also gives some residual on new emerging seedlings.

Cultural side: oxalis loves bare patches, weak grass, and disturbed soil. A thick healthy lawn outcompetes it. Keep the lawn fed and dense to reduce the niche.

For oxalis on edges, in beds, or on hard surfaces, glyphosate spot treatment with NZLA Sticker as an adjuvant gives faster knockdown.
