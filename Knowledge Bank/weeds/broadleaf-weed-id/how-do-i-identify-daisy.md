# How do I identify daisy in my lawn?

Common lawn daisy (Bellis perennis) is one of the most easily recognised NZ lawn weeds. Most people know the distinctive small white flowers with yellow centres.

Identification:

Small rosette of leaves at ground level. Leaves are spoon-shaped, 20 to 50 mm long, with slightly rounded notched edges. Leaves are slightly hairy on both surfaces.

Small flowers 15 to 25 mm across on short individual stems 30 to 100 mm tall. White petals (sometimes pinkish on undersides) surrounding a bright yellow centre disk.

Each plant produces multiple flowers. Flowers throughout most of the growing season — spring through autumn, sometimes into winter in milder regions.

Spreads via short stoloniferous runners as well as seed. Forms small patches over time.

Where it grows: throughout NZ lawns, very common. Tolerates frequent mowing, low fertility, moderate compaction, and shade. One of the most adaptable lawn weeds in NZ.

Control:

NZLA BWC (MCPA + clopyralid + diflufenican) at 50 mL per 100m². Daisy is highly susceptible — among the easier broadleaf weeds to kill chemically.

Apply when daisy is actively growing and flowering — full uptake of the chemistry. Damage visible within a week, full kill within 2 to 3 weeks.

NZLA Gold also effective on daisy.

Cultural side:

Lawn daisy is more difficult to eliminate permanently than many lawn weeds because of its widespread seed bank and ability to spread by runners. New plants establish readily in any thin lawn area.

Steady fertility programme with proper mowing height reduces daisy pressure. A dense canopy at the right height crowds out new daisy establishment.

For lawns with light daisy presence:

Many people accept daisies as part of the lawn rather than fighting them. They're not aggressive enough to dominate, the flowers can be considered attractive, and they coexist with grass without major issues. The case for treatment is mostly aesthetic.

For lawns with heavy daisy infestation:

Single BWC application kills most daisies. Multiple applications across a season plus cultural improvement typically reduces the population significantly. Complete eradication is difficult because of seed bank and ease of new establishment from elsewhere.

For pure-grass aesthetic lawns:

Plan for ongoing daisy management. Twice-yearly broadleaf herbicide application keeps the population suppressed. Cultural intervention prevents new establishment.

Worth flagging to customers asking about daisy control — partial reduction is realistic; permanent elimination is not.
