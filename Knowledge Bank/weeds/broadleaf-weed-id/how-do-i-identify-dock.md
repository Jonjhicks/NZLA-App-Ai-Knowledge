# How do I identify dock?

Several dock species in NZ lawns — broadleaf dock (Rumex obtusifolius) and curled dock (Rumex crispus) are most common. Both controlled similarly.

Identification:

**Broadleaf dock.** Large oval to broadly heart-shaped leaves, 150 to 400 mm long. Wavy or crinkled leaf edges. Leaves have a prominent central vein. Grows as rosette initially, then sends up tall flowering stems.

**Curled dock.** Similar but leaves are narrower and more lance-shaped, with distinctly wavy or curled edges. Slightly more refined appearance than broadleaf dock.

Both develop deep taproots — sometimes 600 mm or more — that make hand removal extremely difficult.

Tall flowering stems develop in summer. Tiny green flowers in clusters, then rust-coloured seed heads with three-winged seeds (called fruits). Seed heads turn brown and persist on the plant through autumn.

A mature dock plant in a lawn produces thousands of seeds per year.

Where it grows: throughout NZ lawns, pastures, waste areas. Particularly common in lifestyle blocks, rural lawns, areas with disturbed soil. Tolerates a wide range of conditions.

Control:

Mature dock with established taproot is one of the harder weeds to control with chemistry.

NZLA BWC (MCPA + clopyralid + diflufenican) at 50 mL per 100m² hits dock but often requires multiple applications. Young dock plants (rosette stage, no flowering stem) respond well to single application.

NZLA Gold (picloram + triclopyr) is more effective on mature dock. The triclopyr component handles tougher broadleaf weeds with woody or established root structures.

Apply when actively growing in mild conditions. Young plants and early-season applications give best results.

For individual mature dock plants in an otherwise clean lawn, spot treatment with glyphosate plus NZLA Sticker as adjuvant is sometimes more effective than selective herbicide. Kills the dock and a small surrounding area; overseed the patch afterward.

Hand digging:

Sometimes attempted on individual mature dock plants but rarely fully successful — the deep taproot regrows from any fragment left. Use a long-bladed weeding tool to extract as much root as possible.

Cultural side:

Dock often establishes in disturbed or recently-cultivated areas. Avoiding soil disturbance reduces new establishment.

Mowing regularly prevents seed production. Mowing dock before flowering stems develop fully limits annual seed return to the soil.

For lawns with established dock populations, multiple seasons of consistent treatment are typically needed. Persistent — both the taproot and the seed bank in the soil resist single-season eradication efforts.
