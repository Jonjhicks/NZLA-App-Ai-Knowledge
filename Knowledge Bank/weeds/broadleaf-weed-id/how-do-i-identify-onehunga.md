# How do I identify onehunga weed?

Onehunga weed (Soliva sessilis) is famously identified by stepping on it barefoot — the prickly seed heads are unmistakable. Best to identify and control before that stage.

Early-stage identification:

Low-growing rosette of finely divided, fern-like or parsley-like leaves. Leaves are deeply lobed, finely segmented, 20 to 50 mm long.

The plant spreads low across the soil, often appearing in patches across thin lawn areas.

Tiny yellow flowers (almost inconspicuous) develop in the leaf axils.

Mature stage identification:

Distinctive prickly seed heads develop in late spring through summer. The seed heads are 5 to 10 mm in diameter, green initially, turning straw-coloured as they mature. They embed sharp spines that hurt when stepped on barefoot and stick to socks, shoes, dog paws and pet fur.

Once the seed heads have matured, the spines persist on the dead plant tissue for weeks afterward.

Where it grows: throughout NZ lawns, particularly the upper North Island where it's most common but spreading. Favours thin underfed lawns, walked-on areas, and lawns with bare patches for establishment.

Control:

Catch it before the seed heads form. Once you've felt the prickles in your feet, the seeds are already mature and dispersing — you've lost the season.

Best timing: late winter through early spring while the rosette is developing but before flowering and seed head formation. September through October typically.

NZLA BWC (MCPA + clopyralid + diflufenican) at 50 mL per 100m². Onehunga in clopyralid-susceptible populations responds well.

**Important resistance note.** Documented clopyralid-resistant onehunga populations exist in NZ — particularly north of Auckland, now spreading to Mount Maunganui and other regions. If BWC isn't working on onehunga in your area, you're likely dealing with resistance.

NZLA Gold contains picloram which has documented cross-resistance with clopyralid in resistant populations. Gold may also fail on resistant onehunga.

For resistant populations, the proven alternative is mecoprop (in mixtures with MCPA and dicamba). Mecoprop-containing products aren't currently in the NZLA range — flag this gap honestly to customers rather than substituting an unrelated active.

Cultural side:

Onehunga thrives in thin lawn areas. A dense well-fed lawn at the right mowing height substantially reduces onehunga establishment.

For lawns with established onehunga, kill the visible plants before seed set, then build the lawn density to prevent re-establishment.

Mowing before seed heads mature reduces but doesn't eliminate the seed production — flowers form low to the soil and can be missed by the mower.

Heavy infestations may take multiple seasons of treatment plus cultural improvement to clear.
