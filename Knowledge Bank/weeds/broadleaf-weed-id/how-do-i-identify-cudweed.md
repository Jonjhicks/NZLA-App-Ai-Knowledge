# How do I identify cudweed?

Cudweed (several species in NZ, primarily Gnaphalium species) is a low-growing rosette weed common in lawns and pastures, often confused with daisy and other rosette weeds.

Identification:

Flat rosette of small leaves at ground level. Leaves are spoon-shaped to oblong, 10 to 40 mm long.

Distinctive woolly or felted appearance — leaves are covered in fine white or grey hairs that give the plant a soft, slightly silvery look. This is the diagnostic feature. Run a finger across — the leaves feel soft and woolly.

Small flowering stems develop from the rosette, with clusters of tiny tubular yellow-cream flowers. Less showy than dandelion or catsear flowers.

Where it grows: throughout NZ lawns, particularly in low-fertility, dry, and exposed areas. Tolerates drought and poor soil where more demanding species struggle.

Common in:
- Underfed lawns
- Thin patches with bare or thin grass
- Exposed sunny areas
- Lawns on poor soils

How to distinguish from similar weeds:

**vs Daisy.** Daisy has smooth glossy leaves with white-petaled flowers. Cudweed has woolly hairy leaves with tubular yellow-cream flowers.

**vs Hawkbit, catsear.** Cudweed leaves are smaller and woollier than hawkbit or catsear. Different overall appearance.

**vs Other rosette weeds.** The woolly/felted leaf texture is the easiest diagnostic — most other rosette weeds have smooth or only slightly hairy leaves.

Control:

NZLA BWC (MCPA + clopyralid + diflufenican) at 50 mL per 100m² gives reasonable control of cudweed.

NZLA Gold for resistant infestations or large mature plants.

Apply when actively growing in mild conditions. The woolly leaves can reduce herbicide contact, so good coverage matters. Adding NZLA Sticker as adjuvant improves uptake.

Cultural side:

Cudweed indicates underfed and stressed lawns. Address the underlying fertility and conditions for long-term reduction.

A steady fertility programme with NZLA All Seasons substantially reduces cudweed pressure. Dense well-fed lawn at the right mowing height prevents most new establishment.

Sometimes cudweed is the first weed to colonise a struggling lawn — a sign that conditions favour the cudweed over the grass. Improving the lawn's overall health is more effective than chemistry alone.

For lawns with established cudweed:

Single BWC application kills most cudweed. Combined with fertility programme improvements, populations typically reduce substantially over a season.

Continued monitoring for new germinations from seed bank — annual or biennial cudweed produces significant seed quantities.

Cudweed isn't generally an aggressive lawn invader — it fills space where grass is struggling. Address why the grass is struggling, and cudweed becomes much less of an issue.
