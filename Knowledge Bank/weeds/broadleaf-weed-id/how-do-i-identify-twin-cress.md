# How do I identify twin cress?

Twin cress (Lepidium didymum, also called wartcress) is a low-spreading broadleaf weed that often resists standard broadleaf herbicide.

Identification:

Low-growing prostrate plant that creeps across the ground. Stems 100 to 400 mm long, multiple branches per plant.

Finely divided pinnate leaves — the leaves are split into multiple small segments along a central rachis. Each segment 5 to 15 mm long. The overall leaf appearance is fern-like.

Small inconspicuous white or greenish flowers in clusters at the stem tips and leaf axils.

Distinctive small two-lobed seed pods — the two seeds give the plant its name "twin cress." Seed pods are 1 to 2 mm across.

When crushed, the foliage has a strong cabbage or watercress smell — twin cress is in the Brassicaceae (mustard) family, related to actual cress.

Where it grows: throughout NZ lawns, particularly in disturbed soil, paths, driveways, lawn edges. Tolerates a range of conditions but particularly common in trampled and edge areas.

Annual or short-lived perennial depending on conditions. Multiple generations per year in mild climates.

Control:

Twin cress is one of the tough broadleaf weeds that often resists NZLA BWC. The combination of low growth habit (less spray contact) and waxy leaves (poor uptake) makes selective herbicide difficult.

NZLA Gold (picloram + triclopyr) is more effective on twin cress. The triclopyr component handles tough waxy-leafed broadleaf weeds.

Apply when actively growing in mild conditions. Young plants respond better than mature mats.

Adding NZLA Sticker as adjuvant improves penetration on waxy twin cress leaves.

For individual patches:

Spot treatment with glyphosate plus NZLA Sticker kills twin cress completely. Useful for isolated mats. Overseed the killed patches afterward.

For widespread twin cress:

NZLA Gold across the lawn at label rate. Multiple applications across the season often needed. Combined with cultural improvement.

Cultural side:

Twin cress establishes in disturbed thin lawn areas. Avoiding unnecessary soil disturbance and maintaining lawn density reduces new establishment.

Mow regularly. Prevents seed production and reduces the seed bank for next year.

Address compaction in patches where twin cress is concentrated. Core aeration opens up the soil and improves grass competition.

For lawns with persistent twin cress:

Multi-season programme with NZLA Gold combined with cultural improvement. Single-season eradication is rarely achievable with twin cress — plan for ongoing management.

Often a sign that lawn conditions could improve more broadly. Twin cress in healthy thick well-managed lawn is unusual; twin cress in struggling thin lawn is common.
