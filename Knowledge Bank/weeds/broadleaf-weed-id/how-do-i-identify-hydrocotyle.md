# How do I identify hydrocotyle?

Hydrocotyle (several species in NZ, also called pennywort) is a low-growing creeping weed identifiable by its distinctive round coin-shaped leaves.

Identification:

Small round to kidney-shaped leaves, 10 to 40 mm across, on long thin stems. The leaf shape gives the plant its common name "pennywort" — the leaves look like small coins.

Leaves have scalloped edges and a glossy bright green appearance.

Plant creeps along the ground via thin stems that root at the nodes wherever they contact soil. Forms dense mats over time in suitable conditions.

Tiny inconspicuous white or greenish flowers in small clusters.

Where it grows: damp areas, shaded patches, lawn edges near garden beds, and anywhere with consistent moisture. Hydrocotyle thrives in damp shaded conditions where most lawns struggle.

Common in:
- Lawns under trees
- Shaded north-facing patches against buildings
- Areas with poor drainage
- Near pond edges, irrigation systems, water features
- Lawn edges where garden beds are watered regularly

Control:

NZLA BWC (MCPA + clopyralid + diflufenican) at 50 mL per 100m² gives partial control of hydrocotyle. Young actively growing plants respond better.

NZLA Gold (picloram + triclopyr) is more effective on established hydrocotyle. The triclopyr handles tougher broadleaf weeds with waxy leaves and creeping habits.

Apply when actively growing in mild conditions.

For dense hydrocotyle mats:

The creeping rooting habit makes single-application chemistry challenging — even where the visible plants are killed, rooted nodes can regrow. Multiple applications across a season may be needed.

Spot treatment with glyphosate plus NZLA Sticker is often the most effective approach for established mats — kills everything in the spray area, overseed the patches afterward.

Cultural side:

Hydrocotyle's presence almost always indicates conditions favouring it over grass — shade, damp, or both. Address the underlying conditions for permanent control.

Reducing shade where possible. Improving drainage. NZLA Penetrate to move surface moisture down through the profile. Changing irrigation patterns to avoid keeping the area constantly damp.

Where shade and damp can't be changed substantially, hydrocotyle may be more realistic to accept than to fight. Ground cover plants suited to the conditions may produce a better outcome than grass.

For lawns with mild hydrocotyle in specific patches:

Treat the patches with selective herbicide. Improve drainage and shade where possible. Maintain dense canopy in surrounding lawn to prevent spread.

For lawns with widespread hydrocotyle:

Combined chemistry + condition improvement programme over a full season. New germinations from seed and from root fragments may continue for several seasons.

Hydrocotyle is more difficult than typical lawn weeds because of the favouring conditions. Cultural change is the long-term answer.
