# How do I identify and control plantain?

Two main species in NZ — broadleaf plantain and narrowleaf plantain. Both common, both controlled similarly.

**Broadleaf plantain (Plantago major).** Oval to broadly egg-shaped leaves, 50 to 200 mm long. Prominent parallel veins running from base to tip. Leaves arranged in flat rosette at ground level. Tall flower spikes 100 to 200 mm with tiny inconspicuous flowers along the stem. Common in compacted, wet, walked-on areas — paths, driveways, lawn edges.

**Narrowleaf plantain (Plantago lanceolata, also called ribwort plantain).** Narrow lance-shaped leaves up to 300 mm long. Same prominent parallel venation. Flower spikes shorter and more egg-shaped than broadleaf plantain. Common across lawn surfaces generally.

Both species can be told apart from grass and other weeds by the prominent parallel veins running the length of the leaf — the diagnostic feature.

Where they grow: throughout lawns, particularly in lower-traffic areas. Broadleaf plantain favours compacted areas. Narrowleaf plantain spreads more generally.

Control:

NZLA BWC (MCPA + clopyralid + diflufenican) at 50 mL per 100m². Plantain is highly susceptible to the auxin chemistry in BWC. Both species respond well.

Apply when actively growing in mild conditions. Damage visible within a week, full kill within 2 to 3 weeks.

NZLA Gold for resistant or mature plants.

Hand pulling works on individual rosettes — grab the leaf base and pull firmly. Plantain doesn't have the deep taproot dandelion does, so hand pulling is more effective.

Cultural side:

Broadleaf plantain particularly indicates compaction — its presence in patches suggests core aeration would help. Address the underlying compaction and the species becomes less competitive.

Both species prefer thin underfed lawns. A dense canopy at the right mowing height prevents new establishment.

For lawns with established plantain, one season of consistent BWC application plus aeration on compacted areas typically clears the population.

Plantain seed remains viable in the soil for years, so new germination from the seed bank can occur for several seasons after the visible plants are killed.
