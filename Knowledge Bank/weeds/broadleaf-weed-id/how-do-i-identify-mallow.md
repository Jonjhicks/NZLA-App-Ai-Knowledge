# How do I identify mallow?

Several mallow species in NZ — common mallow (Malva neglecta), small-flowered mallow (Malva parviflora), and high mallow (Malva sylvestris). All controlled similarly.

Identification:

Round to broadly heart-shaped leaves with shallow lobed edges. Leaves have prominent veins radiating from the leaf base. Leaves 20 to 80 mm across depending on species and conditions.

Leaves have a slightly velvety texture from fine hairs.

Pink, lavender or white flowers, 10 to 30 mm across, with five petals. Flower throughout much of the growing season.

Distinctive round seed pods that resemble miniature wheels or "cheeses" (some people call mallow "cheese plant" for this reason). Seeds are arranged in a ring inside the pod.

Plant habit varies by species — small-flowered mallow spreads low across the ground, common mallow grows more upright, high mallow can reach 1 to 2 metres tall.

Where it grows: lawns, gardens, waste areas, roadsides throughout NZ. Tolerates a wide range of soils. Common in compacted areas and lawn edges.

Control:

Mallow is one of the tougher broadleaf weeds for selective herbicide. The waxy leaves and woody stem structures resist some chemistries.

NZLA BWC (MCPA + clopyralid + diflufenican) gives partial control of mallow. Young plants respond better than mature plants.

NZLA Gold (picloram + triclopyr) is the more effective option. The triclopyr component handles waxy-leafed and tough broadleaf weeds that BWC sometimes leaves.

Apply when actively growing in mild conditions. Young rosette-stage mallow responds best.

Adding NZLA Sticker as adjuvant improves penetration on waxy mallow leaves.

For individual mature mallow plants:

Spot treatment with glyphosate plus NZLA Sticker. Kills mallow completely; overseed the patch.

Hand digging: mallow has a substantial taproot, particularly on mature plants. Removal requires extracting as much root as possible. Often regrows from root fragments.

Cultural side:

Mallow establishes in disturbed soil, edges, and stressed lawn areas. Avoiding soil disturbance and maintaining healthy lawn density reduces new establishment.

Mow regularly to prevent flowering and seed production. The "cheese" seed pods produce significant numbers of seeds that establish next year's pressure.

For lawns with established mallow:

Multi-season treatment with NZLA Gold typically reduces the population substantially. New germinations from seed bank continue for several seasons.

Mallow is rarely an aggressive lawn invader — it tends to grow in specific spots (edges, compacted areas, beds) rather than dominating large areas. Manage in the spots where it appears rather than chemistry across the whole lawn.

Watch garden bed edges where mallow seed can travel from neighbouring properties.
