# How do I identify and kill thistles in my lawn?

Several thistle species in NZ — Scotch thistle, nodding thistle, californian thistle, variegated thistle are common. Identifying by the species matters less than recognising thistles generally.

Identification:

Spiny leaves — the diagnostic feature. Thistle leaves carry distinct sharp spines along the edges and sometimes on the leaf surface. Painful to handle.

Tall flowering stems in summer — typically 500 to 1500 mm tall depending on species and conditions. Pink, purple, or sometimes white flower heads at the tops of the stems. Flower heads themselves are spiny.

Rosette of leaves at ground level early in the growing season before flowering stems develop. The rosette leaves carry the same spines as the older plant.

Some thistle species (particularly californian thistle) spread aggressively via underground rhizomes as well as seed.

Where they grow: throughout NZ lawns, pastures, waste areas. More common in rural and lifestyle block lawns than urban suburban lawns. Particularly common in newly broken ground, around stock yards, in pastures.

Control:

NZLA BWC (MCPA + clopyralid + diflufenican) at 50 mL per 100m² is effective on most thistle species. Clopyralid particularly handles the Asteraceae family thistles belong to.

Apply when thistles are at the rosette stage (early growing season) for best results. Once mature flowering stems are present, kill rates drop and multiple applications may be needed.

NZLA Gold for resistant species or large mature plants.

For californian thistle specifically (rhizomatous and persistent), even effective herbicide treatment may only kill above-ground growth — new shoots emerge from the underground rhizome system. Multiple applications across seasons may be needed for complete eradication.

For individual large thistles in an otherwise clean lawn:

Spot treatment with glyphosate plus NZLA Sticker as adjuvant works on persistent individuals. Kills the thistle plus a small surrounding area; overseed the patch.

Cutting off flowering stems before seed sets prevents annual seed production. Mowing manages this on lawns. For larger thistles in pasture-type areas, manual cutting may be needed.

Cultural side:

Thistles establish in disturbed or thin lawn areas. Dense lawn cover at the right mowing height prevents most thistle establishment.

Avoid soil disturbance where thistle seed bank may be high. New cultivation often brings up dormant seeds.

For lawns with established thistles, multi-season programmes combining herbicide, regular mowing-off of seed heads, and cultural improvement typically clear the population.

Wear gloves when handling thistle plants. The spines are sharp.
