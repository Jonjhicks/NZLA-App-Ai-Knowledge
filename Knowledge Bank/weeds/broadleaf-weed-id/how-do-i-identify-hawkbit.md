# How do I identify hawkbit?

Hawkbit (Leontodon taraxacoides, common name lesser hawkbit) is one of the yellow-flowered rosette weeds often confused with dandelion and catsear. Common across NZ lawns and pastures.

Identification:

Rosette of leaves at ground level. Leaves are narrow lance-shaped, often with shallow rounded lobes along the edges. 50 to 150 mm long.

Leaves are slightly hairy, but less so than catsear and more variable. Some plants have nearly smooth leaves, others noticeably bristly.

Yellow flowers similar to dandelion but smaller, 15 to 20 mm across. Single flower on each stem. Multiple stems per plant. Flower stems thin and wiry.

Seed heads form fluffy parachutes for wind dispersal, similar to other Asteraceae family members.

How to distinguish from similar weeds:

**vs Dandelion.** Smaller flowers, multiple flower stems per plant (dandelion has one stem per flower), leaves less deeply lobed.

**vs Catsear.** Catsear has thicker more branched flower stems with 2 to 4 flowers per stem. Hawkbit has thin single-flower stems.

**vs Hawksbeard.** Hawksbeard is taller, more branched, with more complex flower head structures.

Where it grows: throughout NZ lawns, particularly common in low-fertility lawns and exposed sites. Tolerates drought and poor soil better than dandelion.

Control:

Same chemistry as dandelion and catsear — NZLA BWC (MCPA + clopyralid + diflufenican) at 50 mL per 100m². Hawkbit is in the daisy family (Asteraceae) and the clopyralid component is highly effective.

Apply when actively growing in mild conditions. Smaller plants respond faster than large established rosettes.

NZLA Gold for tougher infestations.

Cultural side:

Hawkbit is highly resistant to drought and poor soil — its presence often indicates underlying issues with the lawn (low fertility, dry conditions, poor canopy).

A steady fertility programme combined with proper irrigation reduces hawkbit pressure over a season.

Mow regularly to prevent seed heads forming. Single flower stems are easy to mow off and the wind-dispersed seeds are a major source of new infestation.

For lawns where hawkbit is established, multiple herbicide applications across a season plus cultural improvement typically clears it.

Seed bank persists for several years, so monitor and retreat new germinations.
