# How do I identify sorrel?

Several sorrel species in NZ lawns — sheep sorrel (Rumex acetosella) and broad-leaved sorrel (Rumex acetosa) are the common ones.

Identification:

**Sheep sorrel** is the more common lawn weed. Small arrow-shaped or spear-shaped leaves, 20 to 60 mm long, with two distinct pointed lobes at the leaf base. Bright green leaves, sometimes with reddish tinge.

Spreads via creeping rhizomes underground as well as seed. Forms patches that gradually expand over time.

Tall thin flowering stems, 100 to 400 mm, with tiny green to reddish flowers in clusters. Mature seed heads turn rust-coloured and persist on the plant.

Plant has a slightly sour or lemon flavour (related to its oxalic acid content) — sorrel was historically used as a herb.

**Broad-leaved sorrel** is larger — leaves up to 200 mm long, more elongated arrow shape. Less common as a lawn weed but appears in pastures and waste areas.

Where it grows: throughout NZ lawns, particularly in acidic soils, low-fertility areas, and stressed lawn patches. Sorrel is often considered an indicator of acidic soil — its presence suggests the lawn pH may be below the ideal 5.5 to 7.0 range for cool-season grass.

Common in:
- Lawns on acidic soils (volcanic, pumice, some clay)
- Underfed lawns
- Lawns where natural rainfall is high (acid leaching effect)
- Thin lawn patches

Control:

NZLA BWC (MCPA + clopyralid + diflufenican) at 50 mL per 100m² gives reasonable control of sorrel. Young plants respond well.

NZLA Gold for established sorrel patches that BWC hasn't cleared.

Apply when actively growing in mild conditions. Young plants and early-season applications work best.

The rhizomatous spread of sheep sorrel means even effective chemistry may need multiple applications. Visible plants killed, but new shoots from underground rhizome system emerge over weeks following treatment. Re-treat as new shoots appear.

For individual sorrel patches in otherwise clean lawn:

Spot treatment with glyphosate plus NZLA Sticker kills the visible plants and a small surrounding area. Reseed the patch.

Cultural side:

Sorrel's presence often indicates acidic soil. A soil test confirms the pH. Lime application raises pH if needed — typical lawn lime application is 1 to 2 kg per 10m² of agricultural lime.

Address the underlying soil pH and sorrel pressure substantially reduces. Long-term solution rather than chemistry alone.

A fertility programme with NZLA All Seasons (which contains some calcium-supporting nutrients) supports lawn health and reduces sorrel pressure.

For lawns with established sorrel:

Combined approach — chemistry for visible plants, lime if soil test shows acidic pH, fertility programme to support competing grass. Multi-season programme for complete clearance.

Mowing height adjustment — lifting to 40 to 50 mm provides better canopy that crowds out sorrel establishment.
