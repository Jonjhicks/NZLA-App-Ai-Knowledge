# How do I identify hawksbeard?

Hawksbeard (Crepis capillaris, also called smooth hawksbeard) is another yellow-flowered weed in the daisy family. Often confused with hawkbit, catsear and dandelion but has distinct identifying features.

Identification:

Initial rosette of leaves at ground level. Leaves are lance-shaped with shallow irregular lobes along the edges. Leaves often have a slightly waxy appearance.

Tall flowering stems, 200 to 800 mm tall — significantly taller than hawkbit, catsear or dandelion. Stems are smooth (lacking the bristles of catsear) and branched at the top.

Multiple small yellow flowers per stem, in branched clusters. Each flower is 10 to 15 mm across — smaller than dandelion or catsear flowers.

Flower heads form characteristic narrow seed structures with parachute-like dispersal mechanisms.

How to distinguish from similar weeds:

**vs Dandelion, catsear, hawkbit.** Hawksbeard is generally taller (often 500+ mm), more branched at the top, with multiple smaller flowers per stem. Other yellow-flowered rosette weeds are more compact and lower-growing.

**vs Sow thistle.** Sow thistle has prickly leaves with spines, completely different texture. Hawksbeard leaves are smooth.

**vs other tall yellow-flowered weeds.** Hawksbeard has the multi-branched flowering structure and slim narrow leaves. Confirm by leaf shape and branching pattern.

Where it grows: lawns, pastures, gardens, waste areas throughout NZ. Adaptable to many conditions. Often appears in mowed lawns as a low rosette before sending up the tall flowering stems.

Control:

NZLA BWC (MCPA + clopyralid + diflufenican) at 50 mL per 100m². Hawksbeard is in the daisy family and responds well to clopyralid chemistry.

Apply when actively growing in mild conditions. Catch hawksbeard at the rosette stage before flowering stems develop — easier kill and less seed produced.

NZLA Gold for tougher established plants.

Cultural side:

Mow regularly to prevent flowering stems developing fully. The tall flowering stems produce significant seed quantities that establish next year's infestation.

Hawksbeard indicates the same conditions as other broadleaf rosette weeds — thin underfed lawn with gaps for establishment. Steady fertility programme reduces pressure.

For lawns with established hawksbeard, single application of BWC plus consistent mowing-off of flowering stems typically clears it within a season. New germinations from seed bank for several years afterward.
