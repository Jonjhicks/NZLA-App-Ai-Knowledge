# Grass Weed Identification and Control

Grass  are more challenging than broadleaf  because selective chemistry options are limited — you're trying to kill one grass without damaging another. The available tools have strict species restrictions, and some combinations simply don't have a selective option.

**NZLA product guide for grass :**

NZLA Blockade (prodiamine 476.2 g/L): pre-emergent, prevents germination of annual grass . NZ label registered for warm-season turf only (kikuyu, couch). Off-label use on cool-season turf cannot be officially recommended, though the chemistry is used routinely on cool-season turf internationally.

NZLA Etho (ethofumesate 200 g/L): post-emergent control of poa annua and other annual grasses in ryegrass and tall fescue lawns. **DO NOT use on fine fescue** — will damage or kill it. Also not for kikuyu or couch.

NZLA Meso (mesotrione 480 g/L): selective control of browntop and creeping bentgrass in cool-season turf. EPA cap at 2 applications per 12 months minimum 14 days apart.

NZLA Halo (haloxyfop-P-methyl): grass-in-grass kill for warm-season grasses (kikuyu, paspalum, couch, most others) in fine fescue lawns **only**. Hard rate cap 15 mL/100m² on fine fescue. **WILL KILL ryegrass, tall fescue, browntop, kikuyu, couch** at any rate — only use on fine fescue.

Spot glyphosate (with NZLA Sticker): non-selective. Kills all grasses in the spray area. Practical for isolated patches when selective options don't exist. Reseed afterward.

## Annual poa (Poa annua)

The most common grass weed in cool-season NZ lawns. Light bright green colour — paler than surrounding perennial ryegrass — is the first visual cue. Short stature if left to grow (50 to 150 mm). Fluffy white seed heads visible through most of autumn, winter and spring — poa flowers almost continuously in cool conditions.

Annual lifecycle: germinates in autumn, grows through winter and spring, sets prolific seed, dies or dramatically thins through summer heat.

**Perennial poa (Poa pratensis, Kentucky bluegrass) is different.** Darker green, more upright, spreads slowly via rhizomes, less aggressive. Not usually a problem in NZ home lawns.

Control:

Pre-em Blockade in autumn before soil temperatures drop into the poa germination window (18 to 22°C falling). February-March in Auckland, March-April in most of the country.

Post-em Etho for ryegrass and tall fescue lawns. Two applications 14 to 21 days apart. DO NOT use on fine fescue.

NZLA Paclo (paclobutrazol) for high-performance turf on a long-term programme. Selectively suppresses poa more than desirable grasses over multiple seasons, gradually shifting the competitive balance.

Cultural management is foundational. Poa annua exploits low mowing heights, thin canopy, and stressed lawns. Maintaining density, fertility and appropriate mowing height is the first line of defence.

## Summer grass and crabgrass (Digitaria species)

Summer grass (Digitaria ciliaris) and crabgrass (Digitaria sanguinalis) are closely related and controlled identically. Most NZ lawn owners use the names interchangeably.

Both are warm-season annuals: germinate in late spring when soils warm, grow aggressively through summer, produce seed, die in autumn frosts.

Identification: wide bright green leaves (5 to 10 mm wide), low spreading habit. The diagnostic feature is the distinctive finger-like seed heads in summer — 3 to 8 thin spikes radiating from a central point, like a small open hand.

Control is timing-dependent. The window is before germination.

NZLA Blockade at 10 to 30 mL/100m² applied 2 to 3 weeks before soil temperatures at 50 mm hit 12°C and hold. Auckland late August. Central NI mid-September. Canterbury late September to early October. Second application 90 to 120 days later extends the barrier through peak germination.

Activate with 6 mm of water within 7 days of application.

Note the NZ label restriction on Blockade — warm-season turf only. For cool-season lawns, this is an off-label use that can't be officially recommended.

Post-emergent control of established summer grass in cool-season turf: no fully selective option in the NZLA range. Spot glyphosate plus NZLA Sticker takes it out along with surrounding lawn; overseed the patches. Or accept the season's damage and rely on pre-em next spring.

## Browntop (Agrostis capillaris)

A fine-textured cool-season grass that invades ryegrass and fescue lawns. In a mowed lawn, browntop creates noticeably finer, denser patches with a slightly different colour and texture from surrounding grass. Leaf width 1 to 3 mm versus 3 to 6 mm for ryegrass.

Spreads via short rhizomes, forming patches that expand slowly. Distinctive open branched seed heads in summer with a brownish-purple tint — that colouring gives it the common name.

Source: browntop enters lawns via wind-borne seed from neighbouring pastures, contaminated grass seed mixes, or renovation seed that carried contamination.

Control: NZLA Meso (mesotrione 480 g/L) — two applications 14 days apart. The EPA cap is 2 applications per 12 months per area, so the full browntop programme uses the entire annual allowance. For lawns with heavy browntop, expect multi-year programmes.

For large established patches, spot glyphosate plus NZLA Sticker then reseed with NZLA Perennial Rye is sometimes more practical.

After Meso treatment, dead browntop areas show as bare or thin patches for 4 to 8 weeks. NZLA Starter plus overseed with appropriate ryegrass fills the patches.

## Creeping bentgrass (Agrostis stolonifera)

Very similar to browntop and often confused with it. The distinguishing feature is the growth habit: creeping bentgrass spreads via above-ground stolons (visible horizontal stems running across the soil surface that root at nodes), whereas browntop spreads more via underground rhizomes.

Forms low dense mats over time. Common in lawns near golf courses or sports turf operations — creeping bentgrass is the dominant species on most NZ golf greens, so proximity to these environments is a risk factor.

Control: same as browntop — NZLA Meso, two applications 14 days apart, EPA cap 2/year.

The selective control shows good efficacy — 39 to 88% clearance depending on rate and conditions. Multi-year programmes may be needed for heavy infestation.

Standard cool-season lawn maintenance (40 to 50 mm mowing height, moderate fertility) disadvantages creeping bentgrass compared to ryegrass. The grass prefers low-cut, high-fertility conditions — opposite of standard home lawn management.

## Kikuyu (Pennisetum clandestinum)

The dominant lawn species in the upper North Island, but a serious invader in cool-season lawns in cooler regions. Spreads aggressively via both stolons (above ground) and rhizomes (below ground). Distinctive pale green, wider leaves, and a different texture than ryegrass.

Selective control in cool-season lawns:

NZLA Halo on fine fescue lawns only (15 mL/100m²). Halo kills kikuyu while leaving fine fescue intact. Will damage or kill ryegrass, tall fescue, browntop and couch — this product is only for fine fescue lawns.

For kikuyu in ryegrass or tall fescue lawns: no selective option. Choices are spot glyphosate on encroachment patches (kills surrounding lawn too, then overseed) or full renovation for heavy infestation.

Physical barriers along property boundaries where kikuyu spreads from neighbouring lawns: 200 to 300 mm deep vertical barrier (galvanised steel or dedicated edging) stops rhizome spread permanently.

Regular boundary mowing at very low height (5 to 10 mm) suppresses kikuyu in the boundary zone — it can't establish at scalped heights.

Heavy kikuyu infestation in a cool-season lawn rarely has a quick fix. Full renovation (glyphosate kill, two passes, then re-establish) is the durable solution for serious cases.

## Paspalum (Paspalum dilatatum)

Coarse warm-season perennial grass with wide flat leaves (10 to 15 mm), distinctive seed heads with 2 to 3 "fingers" running along the stem in late summer. Creates obvious visual contrast in a cool-season lawn — the coarse texture stands out clearly.

Pre-em Blockade covers paspalum at the germinating seed stage (new plants from seed) but doesn't affect existing established plants. Note label restriction to warm-season turf.

NZLA Halo is effective on paspalum in fine fescue lawns at 15 mL/100m². Only fine fescue.

For paspalum in ryegrass or tall fescue lawns: spot glyphosate on established patches then overseed. For heavy infestation, full renovation.

## Browntop vs bentgrass: quick reference

Both are fine-textured, both show as patchy contrasting areas in a ryegrass lawn, both are controlled with NZLA Meso.

The practical difference: if you can see horizontal stolons running across the soil surface, it's creeping bentgrass. If patches are denser and more compact without visible surface runners, it's likely browntop. Control programme is the same either way.

## Yarrow in grass

Yarrow (Achillea millefolium) is technically a broadleaf weed but often categorised with grass  because its finely divided feathery leaves resemble grass at a distance. Close up: deeply pinnate leaves (many small segments along a central stem), strong aromatic smell when crushed, flat-topped white flower clusters in summer.

Controls: NZLA BWC at 50 mL/100m² gives reasonable results. Gold for established patches. The rhizomatous root system means multiple applications are typically needed. NZLA Sticker improves penetration on the slightly waxy fine leaves.

Yarrow thrives in low-fertility, dry and acidic conditions. Steady feeding programme reduces competitive advantage. See the broadleaf weed section for detailed control notes.

## Annual ryegrass vs perennial ryegrass

This is more a seed quality question than a weed identification question. Annual ryegrass (Lolium multiflorum, Italian ryegrass) looks similar to perennial ryegrass (Lolium perenne) but completes its lifecycle in a single season and dies.

The diagnostic field test: examine a young leaf at the base. Annual ryegrass has rolled vernation (new leaves emerge rolled). Perennial ryegrass has folded vernation (new leaves emerge folded along the midrib). Annual ryegrass seeds also have longer awns (bristle-like projections) than perennial.

Lawns that establish well in their first season but thin out in the second were likely sown with seed containing significant annual ryegrass. Overseed with NZLA Perennial Rye — certified straight perennial ryegrass — to rebuild the permanent population.

Use NZLA Perennial Rye for all lawn establishment. Cheap seed mixes often contain variable proportions of annual ryegrass without clear labelling.

## Mixed-species lawns

Most lawns over 5 years old contain multiple grass species. Wind-borne seed, soil seed bank, contaminated seed mixes, and spread from neighbouring properties all contribute. This is normal.

Mixed-species lawns are often more resilient than single-species lawns — different species tolerate different stresses. A lawn functioning well aesthetically and practically doesn't need intervention just because it contains multiple species.

Intervention is warranted when a specific invading species is causing visible problems (patches entry points, uneven appearance) or when the composition is shifting toward undesirable species domination.

## Most common grass  in NZ — quick reference

Poa annua: autumn pre-em Blockade; post-em Etho in ryegrass/tall fescue lawns; Paclo for fine turf management.

Summer grass/crabgrass: spring pre-em Blockade before 12°C soil temperature.

Browntop: Meso two applications 14 days apart; spot glyphosate on large patches.

Creeping bentgrass: Meso same programme as browntop.

Kikuyu in cool-season lawn: Halo on fine fescue only; spot glyphosate on other lawn types; physical barriers for neighbour spread.

Paspalum: Halo on fine fescue; spot glyphosate on other lawn types.

Cultural management is the foundation for all of these. A dense, well-fed, properly mowed lawn prevents most grass weed establishment.
