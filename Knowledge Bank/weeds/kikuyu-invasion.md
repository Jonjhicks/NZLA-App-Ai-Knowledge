# Kikuyu Invasion

Kikuyu spreading from a neighbour's property or from an adjacent area into a cool-season lawn is a persistent problem in the upper North Island. Kikuyu (Pennisetum clandestinum) spreads aggressively via both above-ground stolons and underground rhizomes, and once established in a ryegrass or fescue lawn it's difficult to remove selectively.

## Why kikuyu is so hard to control

Kikuyu spreads in two ways simultaneously. Above-ground stolons run across the surface and root at nodes — visible, fast-moving, obvious. Underground rhizomes penetrate the soil and produce new growth from below the surface — invisible, deeper, and harder to intercept.

Cut or spray the visible growth and the rhizomes survive to resprout. The re-invasion from rhizomes is the reason kikuyu keeps coming back after seemingly successful treatment.

In summer, kikuyu grows aggressively and is visually obvious — coarse leaf, different texture and colour from surrounding ryegrass. In winter, kikuyu slows and can be harder to see, but the root system is still there.

## Selective control options

The only selective product registered and appropriate for killing kikuyu in a fine fescue lawn is **NZLA Halo** (haloxyfop-P-methyl). Halo selectively kills most grass species except fine fescue. Hard rate cap: **15 mL per 100m² on fine fescue.**

Critical restriction: **Halo will kill ryegrass, tall fescue, browntop, and virtually all other grass species.** It can only be used where fine fescue is the desired lawn species. Using Halo on a ryegrass or tall fescue lawn to kill kikuyu will also kill the wanted grass. It is not an option for ryegrass/fescue lawns.

For **ryegrass and tall fescue lawns invaded by kikuyu**: there is no selective chemical option. The kikuyu and the ryegrass cannot be chemically separated — the same chemistry that kills kikuyu kills ryegrass.

## What can be done on ryegrass/tall fescue lawns

**Spot glyphosate + NZLA Sticker.** Non-selective — kills everything in the spray zone including the wanted grass. The approach: spray kikuyu patches with glyphosate, allow 10 to 14 days, oversow with NZLA Perennial Rye or appropriate species. This sacrifices the grass in that patch but removes the kikuyu, then re-establishes wanted grass.

Add NZLA Sticker to glyphosate for kikuyu — the waxy leaf surface reduces uptake significantly without an adjuvant. Sticker improves penetration and kill effectiveness.

For stolons running across the surface: cut them back to the boundary regularly. This doesn't eliminate the rhizome system but slows the spread and reduces the area needing treatment.

## Edge management and containment

**Physical barrier.** A deep root barrier (150 to 200 mm minimum depth) installed along the boundary prevents rhizome spread underground. Steel, root-barrier fabric, or concrete edging. Must be installed deep enough that kikuyu rhizomes can't grow under it. Effective if installed properly, but existing invasion already in the lawn isn't addressed by barrier alone.

**Regular mowing of the boundary.** Mowing kikuyu stolons along the fence line before they root into the cool-season lawn slows the advance. Consistent weekly edge trimming is part of containment.

**Edging.** A clean vertical cut along the lawn-kikuyu boundary with a half-moon edger or lawn edging tool severs any stolons that have crossed. Done every few weeks through the growing season, this is a practical ongoing management tool.

## Managing the relationship with the neighbour

In NZ, there's no legislation preventing kikuyu growing from one property to another. Communication is the first step — some neighbours with kikuyu lawns don't realise it's spreading and are happy to manage their boundary if asked.

Regular mowing and edge trimming on the neighbour's side significantly reduces the volume of stolon growth heading across the boundary.

## Realistic expectations

On a ryegrass or fescue lawn with established kikuyu invasion from rhizomes: managing the boundary and spot-treating advancing patches is the practical ongoing approach. Full elimination from a lawn with deep rhizome penetration is very difficult without killing and re-establishing the affected areas.

For a fine fescue lawn: Halo gives a genuine selective control option, but multiple treatments across seasons are needed to address rhizome regrowth.

The honest position: kikuyu invasion on ryegrass/fescue lawns is a management challenge that requires ongoing effort, not a one-time fix.
