# How to Thicken a Lawn: From Overseeding to Ongoing Management

A thin lawn rarely has one cause. It's usually a combination of things: underfeeding, infrequent mowing, thatch buildup, compaction, or just a lawn that's never been given the right conditions to fill in properly. Before reaching for seed, it's worth understanding what's actually keeping the lawn thin, because the fix depends on the cause.

That said, here's a practical approach that covers everything from worst-case thinning through to the ongoing management tools that keep a dense lawn looking good.

## When the lawn is genuinely thin: overseeding

If large areas are patchy, bare, or noticeably sparse, overseeding is the starting point. No amount of fertiliser or mowing management fills in genuine gaps. You need seed in the ground.

Autumn is the better window for overseeding: late February through to early April across most of New Zealand. Soil temperatures are still warm enough for germination, weed competition drops off, and the new seedlings have the whole cooler season to establish roots before their first summer. Spring works too (September through October) but the seedlings go straight into summer, which demands more irrigation and care.

Prep the surface properly before sowing. Mow the existing lawn short (around 20mm), rake or lightly scarify to expose soil between the existing plants, and apply NZLA Starter granular at 1.5 kg per 100m² to drive root development in the new seedlings. Sow at the recommended oversowing rate for your seed (typically lower than the new lawn rate, so check the bag or the NZLA seed guide). Rake the seed in lightly, water consistently for the first two weeks, and hold off mowing until the new seedlings reach 60 to 80mm.

Don't apply pre-emergent within four to six weeks of overseeding. It will kill the new seed.

## Mowing frequency and height

This is the most underrated tool for thickening a lawn and it costs nothing. Regular mowing at the right height drives tillering, the process by which grass plants produce new shoots laterally rather than just growing taller. More tillers per square metre equals a denser lawn.

The rule is simple: mow often enough that you're never removing more than a third of the leaf in one cut. A lawn that gets mowed weekly produces a significantly denser canopy over a season than the same lawn mowed every two to three weeks. Every time you cut the leaf tip, the plant responds by sending out new tillers. Miss mows and the plant's energy goes into height rather than spread.

Mowing height matters too. In summer, keep cool-season lawns at a moderate height and lift it higher in winter for more leaf area and light capture in low-light conditions. Check the recommended range for your specific grass variety. Going too short weakens the plant and opens the canopy to weeds and moss.

Sharp blades. Blunt blades tear rather than cut, damage the leaf, and create entry points for disease.

## Feeding consistently with Charger

A hungry lawn doesn't fill in. Thin lawns are often chronically underfed, and the plants simply don't have the resources to tiller and spread aggressively.

NZLA Charger as a fortnightly foliar between granular applications keeps the lawn in steady growth through the season. The nitrogen and iron combination drives both leaf growth and colour, and unlike a granular hit, it's controlled and low-risk on an already-thin lawn. Consistent feeding at a moderate rate through the season does more for density than occasional heavy applications.

Don't push high nitrogen in summer or heading into winter. Through summer, the risk is disease on soft growth. Heading into winter, high nitrogen invites Fusarium. Charger is the right tool through both periods because it keeps the lawn fed without the nitrogen load that causes problems.

## PGR: the density tool most home owners don't know about

NZLA PGR (trinexapac-ethyl) redirects the plant's energy from vertical growth into lateral spread, tillering and root development. Applied correctly through the growing season, it meaningfully increases canopy density and reduces how often you need to mow.

The PGR inhibits the hormone that drives upward growth. The plant keeps photosynthesising and producing biomass, but instead of going into tall stems and leaves, that energy goes into lateral shoots and deeper roots. The lawn gets thicker, mowing frequency drops, and the canopy holds its density better under pressure.

Apply at the label rate for your grass type during the active growing season. Don't apply to stressed, recovering, or dormant turf. Don't apply heading into winter as growth slows. Spring through to early autumn is the right window. There will be a rebound flush of growth when the effect wears off, so plan a mow for it.

## Topdressing

Topdressing improves density in two ways: it levels out surface unevenness that's causing uneven growth, and on clay soils it progressively improves drainage and soil structure, giving the grass plants better conditions to perform.

A light application of sandy loam at 5 to 10mm after core aeration or scarification fills the gaps in the surface and improves the seedbed for any overseeding. Do this after scarifying, not on an untouched lawn surface, as the material needs to work into the canopy rather than sitting on top.

## What the season changes

The tools available to you shift with the time of year.

In spring, all options are on the table. Overseed early in the window if the lawn needs it. Start Charger and All Seasons granular as growth fires up. Introduce PGR once the lawn is growing well and you want to start pushing density.

Through summer, focus on Charger foliar and PGR management. Overseeding in summer is high-risk for most home lawns. Hold off granular nitrogen in the hottest months to avoid disease on soft growth.

Heading into autumn, overseed if the lawn needs it: this is the best window. Get a granular All Seasons down before the soil cools. Wind the PGR back as growth slows toward winter.

Through winter, drop back to fortnightly Charger and Iron+ foliar. No PGR, no heavy granular nitrogen. Mow less often but keep mowing.
