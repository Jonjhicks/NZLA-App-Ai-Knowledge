# Damping-Off in New Lawns: Why Your Seedlings Are Dying

You sow the seed, the lawn germinates, and then patches of it collapse and die. The seedlings look fine one day and are gone the next. That's damping-off, and it's one of the most demoralising things to deal with when you're trying to establish a new lawn.

The good news is it's usually preventable, and in most cases the cause is straightforward.

## What you're seeing

Damping-off kills seedlings at or just below soil level. The stem of the seedling collapses, the plant topples over and dies. In the early stages you might see small patches of dead or dying seedlings with healthy seedlings surrounding them. As the disease progresses the patches spread. In humid conditions you may notice a fine white or grey fungal web at soil level in the affected areas in early morning.

It's worth distinguishing this from the other common reason seedlings die: drying out. Seedlings that dry out wilt and go crispy, usually in the afternoon during hot weather. Seedlings with damping-off collapse at the base — the stem fails before the leaf dies. If you look at a dead seedling and the stem is pinched or rotted at soil level, that's damping-off.

## What's causing it

The pathogens responsible are mostly Pythium species in warm conditions, with Rhizoctonia and Fusarium also capable of causing the same symptoms in cooler or wetter conditions. All of them exploit the same vulnerability: seedlings that stay wet for too long.

Overwatering is the primary contributor on home lawns. New lawn establishment instructions correctly say to keep the surface moist, and people follow that enthusiastically — often too enthusiastically. Multiple waterings a day in already-damp conditions or after overnight rain keeps the soil surface saturated and the leaf canopy wet for extended periods. That's exactly what these pathogens need.

High temperatures compound the problem. Pythium in particular is most active when soil and air temperatures are warm, which makes spring and early summer sowings the highest-risk window. A seeding done in October in Auckland with heavy irrigation on a warm humid week is a recipe for damping-off.

Dense seeding rates make it worse too. Thick populations of seedlings take longer to mature, stay wetter between waterings because the canopy holds moisture, and give the pathogen more material to work through. More is not better when it comes to seeding rate.

Excessive nitrogen at seeding is also a contributing factor. High-nitrogen starter products can push soft, lush growth in seedlings that's more susceptible to infection. A balanced starter fertiliser with good phosphorus — NZLA Starter at sowing — is the right profile, not a high-N push.

## Fixing it

The first response is to back off the irrigation immediately. Reduce watering frequency and let the surface partly dry between sessions. The seedlings need the surface to dry out between waterings, not stay constantly damp. In cool overcast conditions, seedlings may only need watering once a day or less. Watch the soil, not the schedule.

Improve airflow if the seedbed is in a sheltered spot. Still, humid air sitting over a new seeding creates the same leaf wetness problem that evening irrigation does.

If damping-off is already spreading, NZLA Azoxy is on label for seedling damping-off. Apply at label rate, reduce watering, and give the seedbed a chance to dry back. The healthy seedlings around the affected patches can be preserved if you act quickly.

Reseed the dead patches once conditions stabilise. Don't reseed into active disease pressure or saturated soil.

## Timing the sowing

Autumn is the lower-risk window for most of New Zealand. Soil temperatures are dropping, humidity is generally lower than late summer, and the seedlings don't face the same intense disease pressure as a spring sowing. Ryegrass and tall fescue both germinate well in autumn conditions.

Spring sowings aren't wrong, but they carry more risk if conditions turn warm and humid quickly. If you're repeatedly losing spring sowings to damping-off in the same spot, shifting to an autumn sowing often resolves it.

## Preventing it next time

Sow at the recommended rate. The tendency to oversow is understandable — it feels like insurance — but dense sowings are more vulnerable.

Water gently and consistently rather than heavily and frequently. The surface should be moist, not saturated.

Use NZLA Starter at sowing for the phosphorus and balanced nutrition that supports root development without pushing excessive top growth.

Avoid sowing directly before a forecast warm wet spell.
