# Not every lawn problem is a disease

I'm convinced a lot of what gets posted as a possible lawn fungus isn't even a lawn fungus at all. Yellow patches, brown spots, thinning, off-colour areas. The first instinct is to reach for a fungicide. Most of the time the cause is something else.

What looks like disease is often irrigation that misses or doubles up, soil compaction, shade, dry edges, fertiliser overlap, scalping, dull mower blades, herbicide drift, or even a dog burn or the neighbours cat.

Diagnosis is difficult because different problems can produce very similar symptoms. A Microdochium patch in winter and an irrigation issue in summer can both leave a yellow ring in the lawn. Without context, a photo alone usually isn't enough to tell the difference.

Healthy lawns are also full of fungi. Thousands of fungal species live alongside bacteria and the rest of the soil microbiome. Finding fungus in a lawn doesn't automatically mean fungus is causing the damage.

The pattern often tells you more than the colour. Is the damage in the shade? Does it follow an irrigation pattern? Is it where water sits after rain, or where people and pets regularly run? The shape and location of the damage can point you towards the cause faster than the colour ever will.

This is why a single close-up photo is rarely enough. It shows the symptom, but it doesn't show:

- Recent weather conditions
- Irrigation practices
- Soil conditions
- Fertiliser history
- Recent chemical applications
- Mowing height and frequency
- The progression of the problem over time

If you're asking for help in the NZLA group, include a close-up and a wide shot, your grass type, what products you've applied recently, how you're watering and mowing, how long the issue has been there, and whether it's changing.

That's also where the NZLA journal helps. When someone posts a problem, I can look back through their journal and see what they've actually been doing instead of guessing from a photo.

This morning someone posted a Microdochium question. My first thought was whether there had been a recent application of a cheap, fast-release granular fertiliser. A quick look at the journal showed no granular fertiliser logged for months, only foliar applications. It didn't answer the question, but it pointed me towards a better one.

Log the boring stuff. It becomes valuable the day you need help.

Stressed turf and a foliar feed or a herbicide on top of it can be a bad mix. The product cops the blame when the lawn collapses, but the stress was there before the spray went on. A herbicide that's safe on healthy, actively-growing turf can be rough on the same lawn coming out of waterlogging, or in the middle of a dry spell. Same goes for foliar feeds. If the lawn is already hurting, fix the conditions first and don't pile product on top.

Before reaching for a fungicide, walk the lawn and consider what's changed and what's stressing it. A lot of the time, the answer has nothing to do with a pathogen.
