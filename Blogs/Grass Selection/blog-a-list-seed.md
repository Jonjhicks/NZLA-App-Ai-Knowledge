# The Truth About A-List Seed in New Zealand

The A-LIST system is a legitimate turfgrass performance programme. The varieties that carry the logo have been through years of proper testing under low-input conditions, and a blend built around them properly is a good product. That's not the issue.

The issue is how A-List has been picked up as a marketing tool in New Zealand, often without any real transparency about what's actually in the bag.

## What A-List actually means

A-LIST is run by the Alliance for Low Input Sustainable Turf. It identifies grass cultivars that perform well under reduced nitrogen, lower irrigation, and better disease tolerance. The testing is rigorous. If a variety earns the A-List accreditation, it's genuinely earned it.

But an "A-List blend" doesn't mean every seed in the bag is an A-List variety.

Under the blend requirements, a product only needs to contain 65% A-List approved varieties. The remaining 35% just has to be certified turf varieties. Forage ryegrass, uncertified seed, and VNS (variety not stated) seed are excluded, so there are some guardrails. But certified filler is still filler, and which filler goes in can change from batch to batch depending on what's available and what's cheap at the time.

The bag you buy this spring might not be the same blend as the bag you buy next spring, even if the label looks identical.

## Context: where A-List seed mostly ends up in New Zealand

A lot of A-List seed in New Zealand goes into council sportsfields and public open spaces, and for that application, the batch-to-batch variation genuinely isn't a major problem. A sports field gets oversown regularly, it sees heavy wear, the turf is managed for coverage and performance rather than precise appearance, and a slightly different filler in one season just blends in with everything else.

That's a fair use of the product. For a council parks team managing large areas on a budget, ticking the A-List box means they're using proven varieties in the majority of the blend, and no one is scrutinising whether the filler shifted.

The home lawn is a different situation entirely. A lot of our customers are particular about their lawns, and they should be. When you've built something that looks consistently good, the last thing you want is to oversow a bare patch and end up with a slightly different shade of green or a leaf width that doesn't quite match. It's the kind of thing that bothers people for years. Consistency matters more in that context, and the 35% filler window is where that consistency can break down.

## Why that matters on a real lawn

In turf, consistency is everything. Texture, colour, growth habit, leaf width, winter activity. If you're overseeding an established lawn and the filler component shifts between purchases, you can end up with patches that don't match the rest of the lawn in colour or growth rate. Once that variation is in, it can take years to even out.

The A-List portion is solid. The variable is the rest.

## What to look for

Some suppliers run 100% A-List varieties in their blends, no filler, no rotation of cheap certified lines depending on the season. That's the standard worth looking for if you're maintaining a home lawn, because the composition stays consistent batch to batch.

If you're buying seed marketed as A-List, ask your supplier directly: is this 100% A-List varieties, or the minimum 65% with certified filler making up the rest? A supplier who knows their product can answer that question without hesitation.
